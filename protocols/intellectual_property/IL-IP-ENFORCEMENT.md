# Indian IP Law: Enforcement and Remedies

## Purpose
Comprehensive guidance on IP enforcement procedures (civil and criminal) across patents, copyrights, trademarks, designs, including infringement suits, remedies (injunctions, damages, account of profits), criminal prosecution, border measures (customs seizure), and practical enforcement strategies.

## Activation Triggers
Use when: IP infringement discovered | Civil suit for infringement | Criminal complaint filing | Interim injunction application | Anton Piller order (search/seizure) | John Doe order (against unknown infringers) | Customs recordation (border measures) | Domain name dispute | Online piracy enforcement | Enforcement strategy planning.

## Core Principles

### Civil vs Criminal Remedies

| Aspect | Civil Remedies | Criminal Remedies |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|
| **Purpose** | Compensate plaintiff, restrain infringement | Punish infringer, deter future violations |
| **Initiated By** | IP owner (plaintiff) | IP owner (complaint) OR Police (suo motu for cognizable offenses) |
| **Forum** | Civil Court (District Court/High Court) | Criminal Court (Magistrate Court) |
| **Remedies** | Injunction, Damages, Account of Profits, Delivery Up | Imprisonment, Fine, Seizure of infringing goods |
| **Standard of Proof** | Preponderance of probabilities (balance of probabilities) | Beyond reasonable doubt |
| **Availability** | All IP types | Copyright (strong), Trademarks (moderate), Patents/Designs (civil only for some) |

**Strategic Choice:**
- **Civil Suit:** For ongoing business competitor, seek injunction + damages
- **Criminal Complaint:** For blatant piracy/counterfeiting, seek deterrence

**Can File Both:** Simultaneously OR sequentially (civil + criminal)

## Civil Enforcement Procedures

### Phase 1: Pre-Litigation

**Step 1: Cease and Desist Letter**

**Purpose:**
- Put infringer on notice
- Attempt settlement before litigation
- Establish knowledge (for damages calculation)

**Contents:**
- IP rights owned by sender
- Evidence of infringement
- Demand to cease infringement
- Deadline for response (7-15 days)
- Consequences of non-compliance (litigation)

**Infringer's Possible Responses:**
1. **Comply:** Stop infringement, settle
2. **Deny:** Dispute infringement, challenge IP validity
3. **Ignore:** No response → Proceed to litigation

**Step 2: Evidence Collection**

**Critical for Successful Suit:**

**Documentary Evidence:**
- IP registration certificates
- Assignment/license agreements
- Infringing products/materials (purchase proof)
- Advertisements, websites (screenshots with timestamp)
- Sales invoices (defendant's sales)

**Expert Evidence:**
- Technical experts (for patents)
- Valuation experts (for damages)
- Survey evidence (for trademark confusion)

**Investigative Measures:**
- Market survey (purchase infringing goods)
- Online monitoring (e-commerce, social media)
- Test purchases (notarized purchase invoices)

**Step 3: Assess Forum (Jurisdiction)**

**Jurisdiction for IP Suits:**

**Patents:**
- High Court (commercial division) OR District Court (where infringement occurred/plaintiff resides)
- Section 104 Patents Act

**Copyrights:**
- District Court having jurisdiction (where plaintiff resides OR infringement occurred)
- Section 62 Copyright Act

**Trademarks:**
- District Court having jurisdiction (where plaintiff resides/carries business OR defendant resides/carries business OR infringement occurred)
- Section 134 Trade Marks Act

**Designs:**
- District Court having jurisdiction (where proprietor OR defendant resides/carries business)
- Section 22 Designs Act

**Commercial Courts Act 2015:**
- For specified value (Rs. 3 lakhs+): Commercial Division of High Court OR Commercial Courts
- Expedited procedure (strict timelines)

### Phase 2: Filing Suit

**Plaint Contents (Order VII CPC):**

1. **Jurisdiction Facts** - Establish court's jurisdiction
2. **Plaintiff's Title** - Ownership/exclusive license
3. **IP Details** - Registration number, date, class
4. **Infringing Acts** - Detailed description (who, what, when, where)
5. **Damages/Account of Profits** - Quantification (if claiming)
6. **Prayer** - Injunction (interim + permanent), Damages, Delivery Up, Costs

**Documents to File:**
- Plaint + Affidavit verifying plaint
- IP registration certificate (certified copy)
- Evidence of infringement (samples, invoices, screenshots)
- Valuation certificate (for court fees)

**Court Fees:**
- Based on valuation OR ad valorem (for injunction - notional value)
- Varies by state (0.5% to 2% of valuation)

### Phase 3: Interim Injunction (Order XXXIX CPC)

**Purpose:** Prevent ongoing infringement pending trial (may take 2-5 years)

**Application:** IA under Order XXXIX Rule 1&2 CPC

**Test for Interim Injunction:**

**Three-Fold Test (ALL Must Be Satisfied):**

1. **Prima Facie Case** - Plaintiff shows likelihood of success on merits
2. **Balance of Convenience** - Irreparable injury to plaintiff > hardship to defendant
3. **Irreparable Injury** - Damages inadequate remedy (cannot be compensated by money)

**Example:**
- Plaintiff owns registered trademark "XYZ" for footwear
- Defendant using "XYZ" for footwear (infringing)
- Prima facie case: Registered mark + identical use ✓
- Irreparable injury: Goodwill damage, consumer confusion (cannot be quantified) ✓
- Balance of convenience: Plaintiff's business damaged > Defendant can use different mark ✓
- Interim injunction granted

**Timeline:**
- Hearing usually within 2-4 weeks of filing
- Order within 1-2 months (if expedited)

**Ex-Parte Interim Injunction:**
- In extreme urgency (imminent launch, trade fair)
- Without notice to defendant
- Rare (courts reluctant)

### Phase 4: Anton Piller Order (Search and Seizure)

**Purpose:** Prevent destruction of evidence by defendant

**Requirements:**

1. **Extremely strong prima facie case**
2. **Serious potential/actual damage** to plaintiff
3. **Clear evidence** that defendant has incriminating documents/materials
4. **Real possibility** that defendant will destroy evidence before discovery

**Procedure:**
- Ex-parte application (defendant not notified)
- Court appoints Commissioner
- Commissioner searches defendant's premises (with Court Officer)
- Seize infringing goods, documents, evidence
- Inventory prepared (panchnama)

**Safeguards:**
- Defendant's counsel can be present (if available)
- Search during business hours
- No excessive force
- Female commissioner for search of female/residential premises

**Example:**
- Copyright owner suspects pirate DVD manufacturing unit
- Evidence suggests massive piracy operation
- Risk of evidence destruction if notice given
- Anton Piller order obtained
- Surprise search → Seize pirate DVDs, stampers, invoices

### Phase 5: John Doe Order (Against Unknown Infringers)

**Purpose:** Injunction against unknown/unnamed defendants

**Applicable When:**
- Infringers not identified (anonymous online infringers)
- Large-scale infringement (multiple unknown parties)

**Procedure:**
- Describe class of defendants (e.g., "all persons infringing plaintiff's copyright in Film X")
- Court issues order against "John Doe" defendants
- Advertise order publicly
- Any person infringing falls within injunction scope

**Example:**
- Movie release imminent
- Pirated copies may be distributed online by unknown persons
- John Doe order against "all persons uploading/distributing pirated copies"
- ISPs, hosting platforms put on notice

**Requirements:**
- Strong prima facie case
- Irreparable harm
- Infringement by unidentified persons likely

### Phase 6: Trial

**Stages:**

1. **Written Statement** - Defendant's defense (30-90 days from summons)
2. **Replication** - Plaintiff's reply (if needed)
3. **Framing of Issues** - Court identifies contested questions of fact/law
4. **Evidence** - Plaintiff's evidence → Cross-examination → Defendant's evidence → Cross-examination
5. **Arguments** - Oral/written submissions
6. **Judgment** - Court decides

**Timeline:**
- Normal: 2-5 years (High Court), 3-7 years (District Court)
- Commercial Courts: 1-2 years (expedited - strict timelines under Commercial Courts Act)

### Phase 7: Judgment and Remedies

**Remedies Decreed:**

**1. Permanent Injunction (Section 55 Copyright Act; Section 135 Trade Marks Act):**
- Restrain defendant from infringing permanently
- Failure to comply → Contempt of court

**2. Damages (Compensatory):**

**Calculation Methods:**

**A. Lost Profits:**
- Plaintiff's lost sales due to infringement
- Formula: (Units plaintiff would have sold - Units actually sold) × Profit per unit

**B. Reasonable Royalty:**
- What defendant should have paid for license
- Expert evidence on industry royalty rates

**C. Defendant's Profits (Account of Profits - alternative to damages):**
- Plaintiff elects: Damages OR Account of Profits (not both)
- Account of Profits: Defendant's revenue from infringing goods - Costs
- Discovery: Defendant must disclose sales, costs

**Example:**
- Defendant sold 10,000 infringing units at Rs. 500 each = Rs. 50 lakhs revenue
- Costs: Rs. 20 lakhs
- Profits: Rs. 30 lakhs
- Plaintiff entitled to Rs. 30 lakhs (account of profits)

**3. Delivery Up (Section 58 Copyright Act; Section 135 Trade Marks Act):**
- Infringing goods/materials delivered to plaintiff for destruction
- Plates, molds, masters destroyed

**4. Costs:**
- Litigation costs awarded to successful party
- Typically 20-40% of actual costs

**Punitive/Exemplary Damages:**
- Rare in India (only if malicious/fraudulent infringement)

## Criminal Enforcement

### Copyright - Criminal Provisions (Section 63-70)

**Section 63 - Infringement as Offense:**

**Penalties:**
- **Imprisonment:** Minimum 6 months, maximum 3 YEARS
- **Fine:** Minimum Rs. 50,000, maximum Rs. 2,00,000

**Second Offense (Section 63A):**
- **Imprisonment:** Minimum 1 year, maximum 3 years
- **Fine:** Minimum Rs. 1,00,000, maximum Rs. 2,00,000

**Cognizable Offense (Section 63B):**
- For second/subsequent offense: Police can arrest without warrant
- First offense: Non-cognizable (police cannot arrest without warrant)

**Section 64 - Enhanced Penalty for Second Offense**

**Section 65 - Seizure of Infringing Copies:**
- Police can seize infringing copies without warrant (if satisfied reasonable grounds)

**Procedure:**

1. **Complaint by IP Owner** - To Police OR Magistrate
2. **FIR Registered** - Police investigates
3. **Seizure** - Police seizes infringing goods (Section 65)
4. **Charge Sheet Filed** - After investigation
5. **Trial** - Magistrate Court
6. **Conviction** - Imprisonment + Fine

**Timeline:** 1-3 years (summary trial for first offense)

### Trademarks - Criminal Provisions (Section 103-105, 107-114)

**Section 103 - Penalty for Applying False Trademark:**
- **Imprisonment:** Minimum 6 months, maximum 3 years
- **Fine:** Minimum Rs. 50,000, maximum Rs. 2,00,000

**Section 104 - Penalty for Selling Goods with False Trademark:**
- Same as Section 103

**Section 105 - Enhanced Penalty for Second Offense:**
- **Imprisonment:** Minimum 1 year, maximum 3 years
- **Fine:** Minimum Rs. 1,00,000, maximum Rs. 2,00,000

**Cognizable Offense:**
- Police can arrest with warrant

**Defenses (Section 107-112):**
- Bona fide use of own name
- Innocent infringer (no knowledge, reasonable grounds)

**Procedure:** Similar to copyright criminal prosecution

### Designs - Criminal Provisions (Section 22A)

**Section 22A - Piracy of Design:**
- **Imprisonment:** Up to 3 years
- **Fine:** Up to Rs. 50,000

**First offense:** Summary trial

## Border Measures (Customs Seizure)

### Intellectual Property Rights (Imported Goods) Enforcement Rules 2007

**Purpose:** Prevent import of infringing goods

**Procedure:**

**Step 1: Recordation with Customs**

IP owner records IP with Customs authorities (online application)

**Information:**
- IP details (registration number, certificate)
- Description of genuine goods
- Particulars of authorized importers/distributees
- Infringing goods description (how to identify)

**Step 2: Customs Surveillance**

Customs officers monitor imports

**Step 3: Detention of Suspected Goods**

If Customs suspects goods infringing → Detain goods

**Notice to:**
- Importer
- IP owner

**Step 4: Verification (10 Days)**

IP owner inspects detained goods

**If Infringing:**
- File application for confiscation
- Provide indemnity bond (to protect importer if goods genuine)

**If Not Infringing:**
- Goods released

**Step 5: Adjudication**

Customs adjudicates

**If Infringing:** Goods confiscated, destroyed

**Benefits:**
- Prevents infringing goods entering market
- Cost-effective (no need to sue each importer)
- Deterrent effect

## Online/Digital Enforcement

### ISP Liability and Notice-Takedown (Section 52(1)(c) Copyright Act; IT Act Section 79)

**Section 52(1)(c) Copyright Act - ISP Safe Harbor:**

ISP NOT liable if:
1. Mere intermediary (hosting/transmitting)
2. No actual knowledge of infringement
3. On obtaining knowledge, expeditiously removes/disables access

**Procedure:**

**Step 1: Notice to ISP**

Copyright/trademark owner sends takedown notice to ISP

**Contents:**
- Owner's details, IP rights
- Infringing content URL
- Request to remove/disable access
- Declaration (content infringing, notice in good faith)

**Step 2: ISP Action**

ISP must promptly (24-48 hours) remove/disable access

**Step 3: Counter-Notice (If User Disputes)**

User claims content non-infringing → Counter-notice

**Step 4: Restoration OR Court Proceeding**

IP owner must file court suit within reasonable time OR ISP may restore content

**Failure to Takedown:**
- ISP loses safe harbor protection
- Liable for infringement (contributory infringement)

### Domain Name Disputes

**INDRP (India Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy) - .IN domains**

**Forum:** .IN Registry (Arbitration)

**Grounds:**
- Domain name identical/confusingly similar to complainant's trademark
- Registrant has no legitimate interest
- Registered/used in bad faith

**Remedy:**
- Transfer domain to complainant OR
- Cancel domain registration

**Timeline:** 2-4 months

**UDRP (Uniform Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy) - .COM, .NET, etc.**

**Forum:** WIPO, National Arbitration Forum

**Similar procedure to INDRP**

## Enforcement Strategy

### 1. Pre-Enforcement Assessment

**Questions to Consider:**

1. **Strength of IP Rights:**
   - Registered? Valid? Renewable?
   - Prior art issues (patents)?
   - Descriptive mark (trademarks)?

2. **Strength of Infringement Case:**
   - Clear infringement OR colorable imitation?
   - Evidence quality?
   - Defenses likely?

3. **Infringer Profile:**
   - Business competitor OR fly-by-night pirate?
   - Financial status (can pay damages)?
   - Repeat offender?

4. **Commercial Considerations:**
   - Cost of litigation vs potential recovery
   - Business relationship (supplier, distributor)?
   - Publicity impact (Streisand effect)?

### 2. Remedies Selection

**Injunction Focus:** When ongoing business harm, need to stop immediately

**Damages Focus:** When infringement ceased, seek compensation

**Criminal Prosecution:** For blatant piracy/counterfeiting (deter others)

**Multiple Remedies:** Injunction + Damages + Criminal (parallel proceedings)

### 3. Settlement Negotiations

**Often Better than Litigation:**
- Faster resolution
- Cost-effective
- Certainty of outcome
- Preserve business relationships

**Settlement Terms:**
- Cease infringement
- Destroy infringing stock
- Pay compensation (lump sum OR royalty for past use)
- License for future use (if appropriate)
- Confidentiality clause

### Common Mistakes

**1. Delay in Enforcement** → Laches defense, damages limitation

**2. Insufficient Evidence Collection** → Weak case, injunction refused

**3. Not Seeking Interim Injunction** → Infringement continues during trial (years)

**4. Underestimating Defendant's Resources** → Prolonged litigation, high costs

**5. Not Considering Settlement** → Litigation costs exceed recovery

**6. Filing Only Civil OR Criminal (Not Both)** → Leave deterrence/compensation on table

**7. Not Recordating IP with Customs** → Infringing imports enter market unchecked

**8. Ignoring Online Infringement** → Piracy proliferates on internet

**9. Not Monitoring Market** → Late discovery of infringement (damages escalate)

**10. Inadequate Damages Quantification** → Under-compensation
