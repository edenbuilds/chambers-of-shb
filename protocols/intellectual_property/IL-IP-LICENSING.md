# Indian IP Law: Licensing and Assignment of Intellectual Property

## Purpose
Comprehensive guidance on licensing and assignment of patents, copyrights, trademarks, designs across Indian IP laws, covering exclusive/non-exclusive licenses, technology transfer, royalty structures, termination, compulsory licensing, and IP transaction due diligence.

## Activation Triggers
Use when: IP license negotiation/drafting | IP assignment transaction | Technology transfer agreement | Franchise agreement (trademark license) | Copyright licensing (publishing, software) | Patent licensing (cross-licensing, technology access) | Royalty dispute | License termination | Compulsory license | IP due diligence for M&A | International IP licensing.

## Core Principles

### Assignment vs License - Fundamental Distinction

| Aspect | Assignment | License |
|--------|------------|---------|
| **Ownership Transfer** | YES (transferee becomes owner) | NO (licensor retains ownership) |
| **Duration** | Permanent (or as specified) | Temporary (specified term) |
| **Rights Transferred** | All rights OR specified rights (permanently) | Permission to use (not ownership) |
| **Consideration** | Lump sum OR royalty | Royalty/license fee |
| **Reversion** | Generally NO (unless condition) | YES (at end of term) |
| **Standing to Sue** | Assignee can sue for infringement | Exclusive licensee can sue (usually) |
| **Formality** | Must be in writing (for all IP) | Writing required for exclusive license |

**Example (Assignment):**
- Inventor assigns patent to Company X for Rs. 50 lakhs
- Company X becomes patent owner
- Inventor has NO further rights (unless assignment limited)

**Example (License):**
- Patentee licenses patent to Company Y for 10 years, royalty 5%
- Patentee remains owner
- After 10 years, rights revert to patentee

## Patents - Licensing and Assignment

### Section 67-71: Assignment and Licenses

**Section 68 - Form and Effect of Assignments:**

**Requirements:**
1. **In Writing** - Must be documented
2. **Signed by Assignor** OR authorized agent
3. **Registered** - Must be registered with Patent Office (within 6 months)

**If Not Registered:**
- Assignment NOT effective against subsequent assignee/licensee
- Not admissible as evidence

**Section 69 - Effect of Non-Registration:**
- Unregistered assignment void against subsequent registered assignment/license

**Example:**
- 1st March: A assigns patent to B (not registered)
- 1st June: A assigns same patent to C (registered)
- Priority: C > B (C prevails)

**Lesson:** Always register assignment promptly

**Section 70 - Compulsory License Cannot Be Assigned:**
- Compulsory license (Section 84) non-assignable
- Tied to licensee (prevents commercialization of compulsory license)

### Patent License Types

**1. Exclusive License:**
- Only licensee can practice patent
- Patentee cannot grant to others (or use himself - if truly exclusive)

**Requirements:**
- Must be in writing
- Must be registered

**Example:**
- Pharmaceutical company licenses patented drug to Manufacturer A exclusively for India
- No other manufacturer can produce (including patentee)

**2. Non-Exclusive License:**
- Multiple licensees permitted
- Patentee can also practice OR license to others

**Example:**
- Software patentee licenses to Company A, Company B, Company C simultaneously

**3. Sole License:**
- Only licensee and patentee can practice
- Patentee cannot grant to others (but can use himself)

**Cross-Licensing:**
- Two patentees license patents to each other
- Common in technology industries (avoid infringement suits)

**Example:**
- Company A has Patent X
- Company B has Patent Y
- Both needed for Product Z
- Cross-license: A licenses X to B; B licenses Y to A

### Patent Royalty Structures

**1. Lump Sum (Upfront Payment):**
- One-time payment for license
- No ongoing royalty

**Example:** Rs. 1 crore upfront for 10-year license

**2. Running Royalty (Percentage of Sales):**
- Percentage of net sales/revenue
- Typical: 2-10% (varies by industry, exclusivity)

**Example:** 5% of net sales of licensed products

**3. Minimum Guarantee + Running Royalty:**
- Minimum annual payment (guaranteed) + royalty if sales exceed threshold

**Example:** Rs. 10 lakhs minimum per year + 3% royalty

**4. Milestone Payments:**
- Payments on achieving milestones (development stages, regulatory approval, sales targets)

**Example:**
- Rs. 50 lakhs on regulatory approval
- Rs. 1 crore on first Rs. 10 crore sales
- 2% royalty thereafter

**Factors Affecting Royalty Rate:**
- Exclusivity (exclusive = higher royalty)
- Territory (worldwide vs India only)
- Field of use (broad vs narrow)
- Patent strength (strong patent = higher royalty)
- Market size, competitive landscape

## Copyright - Assignment and Licensing

### Section 18-19: Assignment of Copyright

**Section 19 - Mode of Assignment:**

**Requirements (ALL Mandatory):**

1. **In Writing** - Must be written document signed by assignor
2. **Specific Rights** - Identify rights assigned (reproduction, distribution, etc.)
3. **Territorial Extent** - India, worldwide, specific countries
4. **Period/Term** - Duration (5 years, 10 years, full term, etc.)

**Section 19(4) - Default Term if Not Specified: 5 YEARS**

**Example:**
- Author assigns copyright to Publisher
- Agreement silent on duration
- Assignment deemed for 5 years only (copyright reverts to author after 5 years)

**Moral Rights (Section 57):**
- **CANNOT Be Assigned** - Inalienable
- Remain with author (even if copyright assigned)
- Can be waived (in writing)

**Section 18 - First Owner of Copyright in Special Cases:**

**Work Made for Hire (Section 17(a)):**
- Employer owns copyright (not employee)
- But can assign back to employee (if agreed)

**Commissioned Works:**
- Photographer/painter commissioned → Commissioner owns (Section 17(b), (c))
- Unless contract states otherwise

### Copyright License Types

**1. Exclusive License (Section 30):**
- Must be in writing
- Only licensee can exercise rights
- Copyright owner excluded from exercising rights
- Licensee can sue for infringement (Section 55)

**2. Non-Exclusive License:**
- Can be oral OR written (written preferable)
- Multiple licensees permitted
- Copyright owner can also exercise rights

**Example (Publishing):**
- Author licenses book to Publisher A (exclusive print rights - India) for 5 years
- Author licenses to Publisher B (exclusive digital rights - worldwide) for 3 years
- Different rights, different licensees, both exclusive in respective fields

### Copyright Licensing Scenarios

**Software Licensing:**
- End-user license agreements (EULA)
- Site license (organization-wide)
- OEM license (bundled with hardware)

**Publishing:**
- Print rights, digital rights, translation rights, film adaptation rights (separately licensable)

**Music:**
- Mechanical license (reproduction)
- Performance license (public performance)
- Synchronization license (use in film/video)

**Royalty Structures (Copyright):**

**Publishing:**
- 10-15% of net sales (typical for books)
- Higher for established authors

**Software:**
- Per-unit license fee OR subscription fee

**Music:**
- Mechanical royalty: Fixed per copy (or streaming)
- Performance royalty: Based on usage (radio play, concerts)

## Trademarks - Assignment and Licensing

### Section 37-48: Assignment and Transmission

**Section 37 - Trademark Assignment with/without Goodwill:**

**With Goodwill:**
- Trademark + associated business/goodwill transferred
- Assignee can use mark immediately

**Without Goodwill:**
- Only trademark transferred (no business)
- Risky (may confuse consumers)
- Registrar scrutinizes (may refuse if deceptive)

**Restrictions (Section 38):**
- Cannot assign part of mark only (e.g., cannot assign "COCA" retaining "COLA")
- Cannot assign for some goods only (if would cause confusion)

**Registration of Assignment (Section 45):**
- Assignment effective only after registration with Trademark Registry
- Application within 6 months

### Trademark License (Registered User - Section 48-54)

**Old Law: Registered User System (Abolished 2010)**

**New Law (Post-2010):**
- No "registered user" requirement
- Licensor can license freely
- BUT must maintain quality control (to prevent deception)

**Permitted Use (Section 2(1)(r)):**
```
"Permitted use means use of trademark by registered proprietor OR registered 
user OR person authorized by proprietor (subject to control of proprietor)"
```

**Quality Control Requirement:**
- Licensor must control quality of licensed goods/services
- Prevents deceptive use (consumer protection)

**Example:**
- McDonald's licenses trademark to franchisees
- McDonald's must ensure franchisees maintain quality standards (food quality, service, hygiene)
- Failure to control → May lose trademark rights

**Types:**

**1. Exclusive License:**
- Only licensee can use mark in specified territory/field
- Licensor cannot grant to others (or use himself in that field/territory)

**2. Non-Exclusive License:**
- Multiple licensees permitted
- Licensor can also use

**3. Sole License:**
- Only licensee and licensor can use
- No third-party licenses

**Franchise Agreement:**
- Comprehensive trademark license + business system
- Franchisee uses franchisor's trademark + follows operating procedures
- Royalty: Initial fee + ongoing royalty (% of sales)

**Example:**
- Subway franchise
- Franchisee pays upfront fee + 8% ongoing royalty
- Uses SUBWAY trademark, follows prescribed menu, quality standards

## Designs - Assignment and Transmission

### Section 30: Assignment and Transmission

**Similar to Patents/Trademarks:**
- Assignment must be in writing
- Registered with Design Office

**License:**
- Design Act silent on licensing
- But proprietor can grant permission to use design (contractual license)

**Exclusive vs Non-Exclusive:**
- As per agreement
- No statutory formalities (unlike patent exclusive license)

## Technology Transfer and Cross-Border Licensing

### Foreign Exchange Management Act (FEMA) Considerations

**Royalty Payments to Foreign Licensor:**

**Automatic Route (No RBI Approval Required):**
- Lump sum payment: Up to USD 2 million
- Royalty on domestic sales: Up to 5% (without minimum)
- Royalty on exports: Up to 8%
- For franchise: 2% (domestic), 5% (export)

**Government Route (RBI Approval Required):**
- Exceeding above limits
- Sensitive sectors (defense, atomic energy)

**Restrictions:**
- Non-compete clauses (limited to duration of agreement)
- Restrictions on export (prohibited in most cases)
- Mandatory technology transfer clauses (in some sectors)

### International Licensing - Key Clauses

**1. Territory:**
- Define geographical scope (India only, Asia, worldwide)

**2. Field of Use:**
- Limit to specific applications/industries

**Example:** Patent license for medical devices (not industrial uses)

**3. Exclusivity:**
- Exclusive, sole, non-exclusive

**4. Term:**
- Duration (typically 5-10 years for technology)
- Renewal options

**5. Royalty:**
- Rate, payment terms, currency, withholding tax

**6. Improvement Grants:**
- Grant-back of improvements (licensee's improvements to licensed technology)
- Exclusive OR non-exclusive grant-back

**7. Quality Control:**
- For trademarks: Mandatory quality control provisions

**8. Termination:**
- Grounds (breach, insolvency, force majeure)
- Notice period
- Reversion of rights
- Sell-off period (for inventory)

**9. Governing Law and Jurisdiction:**
- Indian law vs foreign law
- Arbitration clause (ICC, SIAC, Delhi arbitration)

**10. Confidentiality:**
- Protection of trade secrets, know-how disclosed during license

## Termination of License

### Grounds for Termination

**1. Expiry of Term:**
- License expires on end date
- Rights revert to licensor

**2. Breach:**
- Non-payment of royalty
- Quality control violations (trademarks)
- Unauthorized sublicensing
- Infringement of licensor's other IP

**3. Insolvency:**
- Licensee becomes insolvent
- License may terminate automatically (if clause included)

**4. Mutual Agreement:**
- Parties agree to early termination

**5. Force Majeure:**
- Supervening impossibility (rare)

### Consequences of Termination

**Obligations on Termination:**

**Licensee Must:**
- Cease use of licensed IP
- Return confidential information
- Sell remaining inventory (within agreed period - "sell-off period")
- Pay outstanding royalties

**Licensor Can:**
- Reclaim all rights
- Sue for infringement if licensee continues use

**Survival Clauses:**
- Confidentiality (survives termination)
- Royalty payment obligations (for pre-termination use)
- Indemnification

## IP Due Diligence (M&A, Licensing)

### Due Diligence Checklist

**1. Title Verification:**
- Verify registered owner (search IP registers)
- Confirm no third-party claims
- Review chain of title (assignments, previous licenses)

**2. Validity:**
- Patent: Still in force? (annual fees paid?)
- Trademark: Renewed? Not removed for non-use?
- Copyright: Within term?
- Design: Valid registration?

**3. Encumbrances:**
- Existing licenses (exclusive/non-exclusive)
- Security interests (IP pledged as collateral)
- Litigation (pending/threatened)

**4. Infringement Risks:**
- Freedom-to-operate analysis (does licensed IP infringe third-party rights?)
- Prior art search (for patents)

**5. Geographic Scope:**
- Where is IP protected? (India only OR multiple countries?)
- Filing status in key markets

**6. Restrictions:**
- Non-compete clauses in prior agreements
- Field-of-use restrictions
- Government approvals required (for sensitive sectors)

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Not Registering Assignment/Exclusive License** → Not effective against subsequent transferees

**2. Ambiguous License Scope** → Disputes over territory, field of use, exclusivity

**3. No Quality Control (Trademark License)** → Risk of losing trademark (naked license)

**4. Not Specifying Term (Copyright Assignment)** → Defaults to 5 years (Section 19(4))

**5. Assigning Moral Rights (Copyright)** → Impossible (moral rights inalienable)

**6. Exceeding FEMA Limits Without Approval** → Royalty payments blocked, penalties

**7. Unclear Termination Provisions** → Disputes on termination, sell-off periods

**8. Not Conducting IP Due Diligence** → Acquiring invalid/encumbered IP

**9. Granting Worldwide Exclusive Rights Prematurely** → Limits future expansion opportunities

**10. No Improvement Grant-Back Clause** → Licensee benefits from improvements, licensor does not
