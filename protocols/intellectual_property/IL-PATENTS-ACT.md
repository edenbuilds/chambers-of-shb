# Indian Intellectual Property Law: Patents Protection

## Purpose
Comprehensive guidance on Patents Act 1970 and Patents Rules 2003, covering patentability criteria (novelty, inventive step, industrial application), patent application procedure, examination, opposition, term and maintenance, patent rights and infringement, compulsory licensing, and practical patent strategy for inventions in India.

## Activation Triggers
Use when: Client has invention requiring patent protection | Patent infringement dispute | Patentability assessment | Patent application filing | Pre-grant/post-grant opposition | Compulsory license under Section 84 | Patent validity challenge | Prior art search | Freedom-to-operate analysis | Patent portfolio strategy | Pharmaceutical patents (Section 3(d) analysis) | Patent licensing/assignment | International patent filing (PCT, Paris Convention).

## Core Principles

### Section 2(1)(j): "Invention" Defined

**"Invention means a new product or process involving an inventive step and capable of industrial application"**

**Three Essential Requirements (ALL Must Be Satisfied):**

1. **NOVELTY** - New (not anticipated by prior art)
2. **INVENTIVE STEP** - Non-obvious to person skilled in art  
3. **INDUSTRIAL APPLICATION** - Capable of being made/used in industry

### Section 3: What is NOT Patentable

**Excluded Subject Matter:**

**(d) Discovery of Scientific Principle** - Mere discovery (not invention)

**(e) Mere Arrangement/Rearrangement of Known Devices** - No inventive step

**(f) Method of Agriculture/Horticulture** - Growing plants/animals

**(i) Algorithms, Computer Programs Per Se** - Software generally not patentable (unless technical effect beyond computer)

**(j) Presentation of Information** - Mere information display

**(k) Topography of Integrated Circuits** - Protected separately (not under patents)

**(l) Traditional Knowledge** - Indigenous knowledge

**(m) Inventions Contrary to Public Order/Morality** - Harmful inventions

**(n) Plants and Animals (Except Microorganisms)** - Living organisms

**(o) Biological Process for Production of Plants/Animals** - Natural breeding

**(p) Mathematical/Business Methods** - Abstract methods

**Critical - Section 3(d): Pharmaceutical Patents**

```
"Mere discovery of new form of known substance (not resulting in enhancement 
of known efficacy) OR mere discovery of new property/use of known substance 
OR mere use of known process unless such process results in new product or 
employs at least one new reactant, is NOT patentable"
```

**Novartis v. Union of India (SC 2013):**
- Imatinib mesylate (Gleevec) beta crystalline form
- Mere new form of known substance (imatinib)
- No enhanced efficacy
- NOT patentable under Section 3(d)

**Effect:**
- Prevents evergreening (minor modifications to extend patent term)
- Requires substantial improvement in efficacy

## Patentability Criteria

### 1. NOVELTY (Section 2(1)(l) - Prior Art)

**Invention is NEW if:**
- Not published in any document anywhere in the world (absolute novelty)
- Not used in India (prior public use)
- Not claimed in earlier patent application with earlier priority date

**Prior Art (Section 2(1)(j)):**
- Everything made available to public before priority date
- Worldwide publications (papers, patents, online)
- Public use in India

**Destroying Novelty:**
- Publication before filing → Destroys novelty
- Public demonstration → Destroys novelty

**Grace Period:**
- NO grace period in India (unlike US)
- Any publication before filing destroys novelty

**Example:**
- Inventor publishes paper describing invention on 1st January
- Files patent on 1st March
- Publication on 1st January = Prior art
- Patent rejected for lack of novelty

**Solution:**
- File patent BEFORE any publication/disclosure

### 2. INVENTIVE STEP (Section 2(1)(ja))

**"Feature that makes invention non-obvious to person skilled in art"**

**Test (Problem-Solution Approach):**

1. Identify closest prior art (most similar to invention)
2. Determine technical problem solved
3. Would skilled person, starting from closest prior art, arrive at claimed invention in obvious manner?

**If YES (Obvious) → NO inventive step**  
**If NO (Non-obvious) → Inventive step present**

**Factors Indicating Inventive Step:**
- Solves long-standing problem
- Unexpected result
- Commercial success (secondary consideration)
- Addresses unmet need

**Example:**
- Prior art discloses drug A for disease X
- Inventor discovers dosage modification improving efficacy 2x with reduced side effects
- Non-obvious solution (specific dosage not suggested in prior art)
- Inventive step present ✓

**Obvious Variation:**
- Prior art suggests dosage range 10-100mg
- Inventor claims 50mg (midpoint)
- Obvious to try → NO inventive step ✗

### 3. INDUSTRIAL APPLICATION (Section 2(1)(ac))

**"Invention capable of being made or used in industry"**

**Requirement:**
- Practical utility
- Reproducible
- Not abstract/theoretical

**Examples:**

**Industrial Application ✓:**
- Pharmaceutical composition (can be manufactured)
- Manufacturing process (can be practiced)
- Mechanical device (can be made)

**NO Industrial Application ✗:**
- Perpetual motion machine (impossible)
- Mathematical theorem (abstract)
- Human diagnostic method on living body (Section 3(i))

## Patent Application Procedure

### Phase 1: Pre-Filing

**Step 1: Prior Art Search**
- Search patent databases (Indian, global)
- Search scientific literature
- Identify closest prior art
- Assess patentability

**Step 2: Draft Specification**

**Types of Specification:**

**1. Provisional Specification (Section 9(1)):**
- Brief description (not complete disclosure)
- Filed first to secure priority date
- 12 months to file complete specification

**2. Complete Specification (Section 9(2)):**
- Full disclosure enabling person skilled in art to practice invention
- Claims defining scope of monopoly

**Complete Specification Contents:**

1. **Title** - Concise title
2. **Field of Invention** - Technical field
3. **Background** - Prior art, problem to be solved
4. **Summary** - Brief summary
5. **Detailed Description** - Full disclosure (enabling)
6. **Claims** - Define scope (independent + dependent claims)
7. **Abstract** - 150 words summary
8. **Drawings** - If necessary

**Claim Drafting:**

**Independent Claim:**
- Broadest claim (essential features only)
- Example: "A pharmaceutical composition comprising compound X"

**Dependent Claim:**
- Narrows independent claim (additional features)
- Example: "The composition of claim 1, wherein concentration of X is 10-50mg"

**Claim Strategy:**
- Broad independent claim (maximum protection)
- Narrow dependent claims (fallback if broad claim invalid)

**Step 3: File Application**

**Forms:**
- Form 1: Application for patent
- Form 2: Provisional/complete specification
- Form 3: Statement and undertaking (Section 8 - foreign applications)
- Form 5: Declaration (if applicable)

**Fee:**
- Natural person: Rs. 1,600
- Small entity: Rs. 4,000
- Entity other than small: Rs. 16,000

**Priority Date:**
- Date of filing = Priority date
- If provisional filed → Priority date is provisional filing date
- Complete specification due within 12 months

### Phase 2: Examination

**Request for Examination (Section 11B):**

**Timeline:**
- Must request examination within 48 MONTHS of priority date
- If not requested → Application deemed withdrawn

**Fee:**
- Natural person: Rs. 4,000
- Small entity: Rs. 10,000
- Other: Rs. 20,000

**Examination Process:**

1. **Patent Examiner Reviews Application**
   - Novelty search
   - Inventive step assessment
   - Industrial application check
   - Compliance with formalities

2. **First Examination Report (FER) Issued**
   - Objections raised (if any)
   - Prior art cited
   - Timeline: 12-18 months from request for examination

3. **Response to FER**
   - Applicant has 12 MONTHS to respond (6 months + 6 months extension)
   - Amend claims, argue patentability
   - Submit evidence, affidavits

4. **Hearing (If Required)**
   - If objections not resolved by written response
   - Oral hearing before Controller

5. **Grant OR Refusal**
   - If satisfied: Patent granted
   - If not satisfied: Application refused

**Timeline:**
- Total: 2-5 years from filing to grant (approximately)

### Phase 3: Grant and Publication

**Publication of Application (Section 11A):**
- Application published after 18 months from priority date
- Published in Patent Office Journal
- Public can access

**Grant of Patent:**
- Certificate of patent issued
- Patent No. assigned
- Publication in journal
- Rights effective from date of patent

**Rights on Grant:**
- Exclusive rights to make, use, sell, import patented invention (Section 48)
- 20 years from filing date (non-extendable)

## Patent Rights and Infringement

### Section 48: Rights of Patentee

**Exclusive Rights:**

**For Product Patent:**
- Prevent others from making, using, selling, offering for sale, importing

**For Process Patent:**
- Prevent others from using process
- Prevent others from using, selling, offering for sale, importing product directly obtained by process

**Example:**
- Patent on pharmaceutical compound X
- Third party manufactures compound X without license → Infringement
- Third party imports compound X from abroad → Infringement

### Section 104: Burden of Proof (Process Patents)

**Reverse Burden for Process Patents:**

If patented process for new product:
- Defendant proved to have manufactured same product
- Presumed defendant used patented process (unless proved otherwise)
- Burden on defendant to prove different process used

**Rationale:**
- Difficult for plaintiff to prove defendant's process (trade secret)
- Shifts burden to defendant

### Section 107: Infringement Exceptions

**Acts NOT Constituting Infringement:**

**(a) Private/Non-Commercial Use** - Personal experimentation, research

**(b) Bona Fide Use Before Priority Date** - Prior user rights (if used before patent filed)

**(c) Experimental Purposes** - Research on patented invention

**(d) Regulatory Approval** - Manufacturing for regulatory approval (Bolar provision)

**(e) Transit Through India** - Goods passing through India (not infringement if not sold in India)

**Section 107A(b) - Parallel Import Allowed:**
- Exhaustion of rights
- Patented product sold by patentee abroad → Can be imported into India without infringement

### Remedies for Infringement (Section 108)

**Civil Remedies:**

1. **Injunction** - Restrain infringement
2. **Damages** - Compensatory damages OR account of profits
3. **Delivery Up** - Infringing products/materials destroyed

**Criminal Remedies:**
- NO criminal remedies (unlike copyright)
- Only civil suit for infringement

## Opposition to Patents

### Section 25(1): Pre-Grant Opposition

**Who Can Oppose:** Any person (from publication to grant)

**Grounds:**
- Lack of novelty, inventive step, industrial application
- Not patentable under Section 3, 4
- Insufficient disclosure
- Fraud/misrepresentation

**Procedure:**
- File representation (Form 7A)
- Controller considers opposition
- Hearing (if required)
- Decision before grant

**Effect:**
- If opposition successful → Application refused
- If unsuccessful → Patent granted

### Section 25(2): Post-Grant Opposition

**Who Can Oppose:** Any interested person (within 12 months of grant)

**Grounds (Similar to Pre-Grant):**
- Section 25(2)(a)-(k): Novelty, inventive step, Section 3/4 exclusions, etc.

**Procedure:**
- File opposition (Form 7)
- Evidence filed
- Counter-evidence by patentee
- Hearing
- Decision by Controller

**Effect:**
- If opposition successful → Patent revoked
- If unsuccessful → Patent upheld

**Timeline:**
- Decision usually within 18-24 months

## Compulsory Licensing (Section 84-92)

### Section 84: Compulsory License (After 3 Years)

**After 3 YEARS from grant**, any interested person can apply for compulsory license on grounds:

**Grounds (Any of Following):**

**(a) Reasonable Requirements of Public Not Satisfied**
- Patented invention not available at reasonable price
- Insufficient quantity
- Not worked in India

**(b) Patented Invention Not Available at Reasonably Affordable Price**

**(c) Patented Invention Not Worked in India**
- Manufacturing not done in India (importation not sufficient)

**Example:**
- Pharmaceutical patent for life-saving drug
- Patentee only imports drug (not manufacturing in India)
- Drug priced at Rs. 1 lakh per month (unaffordable)
- Generic manufacturer applies for compulsory license
- Controller may grant license if:
  - Public requirement not met
  - Price unreasonable
  - Not worked in India

**Natco v. Bayer (First Compulsory License - 2012):**
- Cancer drug (Sorafenib) patented by Bayer
- Price: Rs. 2.8 lakhs per month
- Only 2% of patients could access
- Natco applied for compulsory license
- Controller granted license
- Royalty: 6% to Bayer
- Natco to sell at Rs. 8,800 per month

**Conditions for Grant:**

1. Applicant attempted to obtain voluntary license on reasonable terms (but failed)
2. Applicant capable of working invention
3. Public interest served

**Terms of Compulsory License:**

- Non-exclusive (patentee can also exploit)
- Predominantly for domestic market (not export-oriented, except specific cases)
- Royalty payable to patentee (determined by Controller, usually 4-7%)
- License can be revoked if conditions not met

### Section 92: Compulsory License in National Emergency

**Controller Can Grant Compulsory License Without Application:**

**Grounds:**
- Circumstances of national emergency
- Extreme urgency
- Public non-commercial use

**Example:**
- COVID-19 pandemic
- Government can grant compulsory license for vaccine/drug manufacturing
- No need to negotiate with patentee first

**Section 92A: Compulsory License for Export to Countries with Insufficient Manufacturing Capacity:**

- Pharmaceutical products
- Export to least-developed countries/importing countries with public health crisis
- TRIPS flexibility (Doha Declaration)

## Integration and Strategies

### Patent Portfolio Strategy

**1. File Early, File Often:**
- Secure priority date before publication
- File provisional (if invention developing)
- File complete within 12 months

**2. Broad + Narrow Claims:**
- Broad independent claims (maximum protection)
- Narrow dependent claims (fallback if challenged)

**3. Freedom-to-Operate (FTO) Analysis:**
- Before commercialization, search for patents covering similar technology
- Assess infringement risk
- Obtain licenses OR design around

**4. International Filing:**
- PCT application (international filing under Patent Cooperation Treaty)
- 30 months to enter national phase (India, US, Europe, etc.)
- Paris Convention (12-month priority claim)

### Pharmaceutical Patents - Section 3(d) Strategy

**Challenge:**
- Section 3(d) prevents evergreening
- Minor modifications not patentable

**Strategy for Innovators:**
- Demonstrate enhanced efficacy (not just new form)
- Therapeutic advantage (clinical data)
- Unexpected properties

**Strategy for Generic Manufacturers:**
- Challenge patents under Section 3(d)
- File pre-grant/post-grant opposition
- Prior art search

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Publishing Before Filing** → Destroys novelty (no grace period in India)

**2. Not Requesting Examination** → Application deemed withdrawn after 48 months

**3. Not Responding to FER Within 12 Months** → Application deemed abandoned

**4. Claiming Software "Per Se"** → Not patentable (need technical effect)

**5. Not Filing Complete Specification Within 12 Months of Provisional** → Priority date lost

**6. Over-Broad Claims** → Challenged for lack of support/enablement

**7. Not Considering Compulsory License Risk** → Especially for essential medicines

**8. Not Working Invention in India** → Vulnerable to compulsory license under Section 84

**9. Ignoring Prior Art During Drafting** → Claims too broad, easily invalidated

**10. Not Maintaining Patent (Annual Fees)** → Patent lapses if fees not paid
