# Indian Intellectual Property Law: Copyright Protection

## Purpose
This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on the Copyright Act 1957 and Copyright Rules 2013, covering copyrightable works, ownership, duration, moral rights, exceptions and limitations (fair dealing), assignment and licensing, infringement remedies, digital copyright issues (ISP liability, technological protection measures), and practical considerations for copyright protection and enforcement in India.

## Activation Triggers
Use this protocol when:
- Client wants to protect original literary, artistic, musical, or cinematographic work
- Copyright infringement dispute (unauthorized copying, reproduction, distribution)
- Copyright ownership determination (work for hire, joint authorship)
- Assignment or exclusive license of copyright
- Fair dealing/fair use defense analysis
- Moral rights violation (attribution, integrity)
- Digital copyright issues (online piracy, ISP liability, takedown notices)
- Duration of copyright calculation
- Copyright registration application
- International copyright protection (Berne Convention, TRIPS)
- Software copyright protection
- Database copyright protection
- Compulsory licensing under Section 31, 31A, 31D

## Core Principles

### 1. Section 13: What is Protected by Copyright

**"Copyright Subsists in Original Literary, Dramatic, Musical and Artistic Works, Cinematograph Films and Sound Recordings"**

**Categories of Copyrightable Works (Section 13):**

**A. Literary Works (Section 2(o)):**
- Books, pamphlets, poems, articles
- Computer programs (explicitly included)
- Databases (if original selection/arrangement)
- Lyrics, scripts
- Compilation (if original)

**NOT Literary Works:**
- Ideas, concepts, themes (only expression protected)
- Facts (but arrangement may be)
- Titles (usually too short)

**B. Dramatic Works (Section 2(h)):**
- Plays, scripts for films/television
- Choreographic works (if fixed in writing/notation)
- Pantomimes

**C. Musical Works (Section 2(p)):**
- Musical compositions (melody, harmony)
- Songs (music only, not lyrics - lyrics = literary work)

**D. Artistic Works (Section 2(c)):**
- Paintings, drawings, sculptures
- Photographs
- Architectural works (buildings, models)
- Works of artistic craftsmanship

**E. Cinematograph Films (Section 2(f)):**
- Movies, films, video recordings
- Any visual recording (including documentaries, advertisements)

**F. Sound Recordings (Section 2(xx)):**
- Audio recordings (music, speech, sounds)
- Distinct from musical/literary work recorded

**Example:**
- Song has 3 copyrights:
  1. Lyrics (literary work - lyricist owns)
  2. Music (musical work - composer owns)
  3. Sound recording (sound recording - producer owns)

### 2. Section 13: Requirement of ORIGINALITY

**"Copyright Subsists ONLY in ORIGINAL Works"**

**Originality Test (Indian Law):**

**NOT Novelty/Uniqueness Required:**
- Work need not be novel or unique
- Multiple people can create similar works independently

**Sufficient Originality:**
- Work originated from author (not copied)
- Minimum degree of skill, judgment, labor
- Author's own intellectual creation

**Eastern Book Company v. D.B. Modak (SC 2008):**
- "Originality in copyright refers to original expression of thought"
- Minimum creativity required (not mere mechanical work)
- Sweat of brow NOT sufficient (departed from earlier law)

**Example (Original):**
- Photograph of common scene (Taj Mahal) → Original (photographer's skill, angle, lighting)
- Compilation of data (if selection/arrangement shows judgment) → Original

**Example (NOT Original):**
- Exact copy of existing work → Not original
- Trivial alteration → Not original (derivative, needs permission)
- Mere mechanical reproduction (photocopy) → Not original

### 3. Section 14: Meaning of Copyright (Exclusive Rights)

**Copyright = Bundle of Exclusive Rights**

**For Literary/Dramatic/Musical/Artistic Works:**

1. **Reproduction** - Right to make copies
2. **Issuance of Copies to Public** - Distribution right
3. **Performance in Public** - Public performance right
4. **Communication to Public** - Broadcast, transmission
5. **Adaptation** - Translation, conversion, modification
6. **Commercial Rental** - Rent copies (for computer programs, sound recordings)

**For Cinematograph Films:**
- Same as above + Right to make copies of film

**For Sound Recordings:**
- Reproduction, issuance, communication to public, commercial rental

**"Exclusive" Means:**
- ONLY copyright owner can do OR authorize
- Others cannot without permission (license)
- Violation = Infringement

## Ownership of Copyright

### Section 17: First Ownership of Copyright

**General Rule: Author is First Owner**

**Author = Creator of Work**
- Writer (literary work)
- Composer (musical work)
- Artist (artistic work)
- Photographer (photograph)
- Director (cinematograph film)
- Producer (sound recording)

**Exceptions (Section 17):**

**Exception 1: Work for Hire (Section 17(a))**

```
"Where a work is made in the course of employment, the employer is the first 
owner of copyright (in absence of agreement to contrary)"
```

**Conditions:**
1. Work made in course of employment
2. Under contract of service (not independent contractor)

**Example:**
- Software engineer employed by Company A
- Engineer writes software during employment
- Copyright: Company A (employer)

**Freelancer/Independent Contractor:**
- NOT employee
- Freelancer retains copyright (unless assigned)

**Example:**
- Company B hires freelance graphic designer for logo
- No employment relationship
- Copyright: Designer (unless contract assigns to Company)

**Exception 2: Government Work (Section 17(c))**
- Work made by/under direction of Government
- Copyright: Government

**Exception 3: Photographs/Paintings/Portraits Commissioned (Section 17(b), (c))**
- Photograph taken OR painting drawn under commission
- Copyright: Person who commissioned (paid for)

**Exception 4: Journalism (Section 17(b))**
- Article in newspaper/magazine/periodical
- Copyright: Proprietor of publication (absent agreement)
- Author retains right to publish in book form

**Exception 5: Works Made in Course of Employment by Public Undertakings (Section 17(cc))**
- Copyright vests in public undertaking

**Joint Ownership (Section 2(z)):**
- Work produced by collaboration
- Contribution of each author inseparable
- Both/all are joint authors
- Joint ownership (all must consent for exploitation)

## Duration of Copyright

### Section 22-29: Term of Protection

**A. Literary, Dramatic, Musical, Artistic Works (Section 22):**

**Lifetime of Author + 60 Years**
- Counted from end of calendar year of author's death

**Example:**
- Author dies on 15th March 2025
- Copyright expires: 31st December 2085 (60 years after 2025)

**B. Anonymous/Pseudonymous Works (Section 23):**
- 60 years from end of calendar year of first publication

**C. Photographs, Cinematograph Films, Sound Recordings (Section 25-27):**
- **Photographs:** 60 years from year of publication
- **Cinematograph Films:** 60 years from year of publication
- **Sound Recordings:** 60 years from year of publication

**D. Government Works (Section 28):**
- 60 years from year of publication

**E. Works of International Organizations (Section 28A):**
- 60 years from year of publication

**After Copyright Expires:**
- Work enters PUBLIC DOMAIN
- Anyone can use freely (no permission, no payment)

## Rights of Copyright Owner

### Economic Rights (Section 14)

**1. Reproduction Right**
- Make copies (print, photocopy, scan, digitize)

**2. Adaptation Right**
- Translate, convert format
- Dramatization (novel to film)
- Version for children

**3. Public Performance Right**
- Perform play, recite poem, screen film

**4. Communication to Public**
- Broadcast on TV/radio
- Internet streaming, upload

**5. Distribution Right**
- Sell, rent, lend copies

**Example:**
- Publishing house wants to publish book
- Needs reproduction + distribution rights
- Must obtain license from author

### Moral Rights (Section 57)

**Section 57:**
```
"Independently of author's copyright, author has right:
(a) to claim authorship (paternity right), AND
(b) to restrain/claim damages for distortion, mutilation, modification, or 
    other action prejudicial to honor or reputation (integrity right)"
```

**Moral Right 1: PATERNITY (Attribution)**
- Right to be identified as author
- Name must appear on copies, credits

**Violation Example:**
- Publisher publishes book without author's name → Violation

**Moral Right 2: INTEGRITY**
- Right to object to distortion/mutilation/modification
- Work must not be altered prejudicially

**Violation Example:**
- Film producer cuts scenes from director's film, distorting message → Violation

**Characteristics of Moral Rights:**
- INALIENABLE (cannot be transferred/sold)
- Personal to author (not transferable, even if copyright assigned)
- Persist even after economic rights assigned
- But can be WAIVED

**Duration of Moral Rights:**
- Same as copyright duration (lifetime + 60 years)
- After author's death: Legal representatives can enforce

## Assignment and Licensing

### Section 18-19A: Transfer of Copyright

**Two Methods to Transfer Rights:**

### A. Assignment (Section 18)

**Assignment = Transfer of Ownership of Copyright**

**Requirements:**
1. **In Writing** - Signed by assignor (copyright owner)
2. **Specific Rights** - Identify rights assigned (reproduction, distribution, etc.)
3. **Territorial Extent** - India, worldwide, specific countries
4. **Period** - Duration (5 years, perpetual, etc.)
5. **Royalty** - If applicable (amount, percentage)

**Section 19 - Assignment Not Valid Unless:**
- In writing
- Signed by assignor
- Specifies rights, territory, period
- If no period specified: Assignment deemed for 5 YEARS only

**Example - Assignment Clause:**

```markdown
The Author hereby assigns to the Publisher the entire copyright in the Work, 
including the exclusive rights of reproduction, distribution, adaptation, 
and communication to public, throughout the world, for the full term of 
copyright, in consideration of royalty of 10% of net sales.
```

**Types of Assignment:**

**1. Complete Assignment:**
- All rights transferred
- Assignee becomes owner

**2. Partial Assignment:**
- Specific rights only (e.g., print rights, not film rights)
- Assignor retains other rights

**3. Exclusive Assignment:**
- Only assignee can exercise rights
- Even assignor cannot exercise

**4. Non-Exclusive Assignment:**
- Assignee can exercise rights
- Assignor can also license to others

### B. License (Section 30)

**License = Permission to Use (Ownership NOT Transferred)**

**Exclusive License (Section 2(j)):**
- Only licensee can exercise rights
- Copyright owner cannot exercise OR grant to others
- Must be in writing (Section 30)

**Non-Exclusive License:**
- Licensee can use
- Copyright owner can also use AND license to multiple others
- Can be oral OR written (but written preferable)

**Implied License:**
- Arises from conduct/circumstances
- Example: Hiring photographer for wedding → Implied license to use photos

**Section 30 - Exclusive License Requirements:**
- Must be in writing
- Signed by copyright owner OR authorized agent

**Difference:**

| Aspect | Assignment | License |
|--------|------------|---------|
| **Ownership** | Transferred | Retained by licensor |
| **Exclusivity** | Can be exclusive/non-exclusive | Can be exclusive/non-exclusive |
| **Formality** | MUST be in writing | Exclusive: Must be in writing; Non-exclusive: Can be oral |
| **Rights** | All or specified rights | Permission to use |
| **Standing to Sue** | Assignee can sue | Exclusive licensee can sue (Section 55) |

## Exceptions and Limitations (Fair Dealing)

### Section 52: Acts Not Constituting Infringement

**Fair Dealing Provisions (Section 52(1)):**

**1. Private or Personal Use (Section 52(1)(a))**
- Making single copy for private use
- Research, criticism, review

**Conditions:**
- Must be fair dealing
- No commercial purpose
- One copy only

**Example (Fair):**
- Photocopy chapter from library book for study → Fair dealing
- Photocopy entire textbook to avoid buying → NOT fair dealing (excessive)

**2. Criticism or Review (Section 52(1)(a))**
- Using work to critique/review
- Must acknowledge source

**Example:**
- Book reviewer quotes paragraphs in review → Fair dealing
- Film critic shows clips in review → Fair dealing

**3. Reporting Current Events (Section 52(1)(b))**
- Newspaper/magazine/broadcast reporting news

**Example:**
- News report using photograph from original source (with attribution) → Fair

**4. Educational Use (Section 52(1)(i))**
- Reproduction by teacher/pupil during instruction
- In course of educational activities

**Conditions:**
- Not for commercial purpose
- Extent reasonable

**5. Library/Research (Section 52(1)(n), (o))**
- Library making copies for research/private study
- One article from periodical OR reasonable portion of work

**6. Judicial Proceedings (Section 52(1)(q))**
- Reproduction for judicial proceedings
- Report of judicial proceedings

**Fair Use Test (Derived from Case Law):**

**Four Factors:**

1. **Purpose and Character of Use**
   - Commercial vs educational/research?
   - Transformative use?

2. **Nature of Copyrighted Work**
   - Factual or creative?
   - Published or unpublished?

3. **Amount and Substantiality**
   - How much used? (percentage)
   - Qualitatively important?

4. **Effect on Market**
   - Substitute for original?
   - Harm commercial value?

**Example Analysis:**

**Scenario:** Student photocopies 3 chapters (out of 20) from textbook for exam preparation

Factor 1: Educational purpose ✓  
Factor 2: Published textbook (factual) ✓  
Factor 3: 15% of work (reasonable) ✓  
Factor 4: No market harm (temporary use) ✓

**Likely:** Fair dealing

**Scenario:** Business photocopies entire technical manual for all 50 employees to avoid buying copies

Factor 1: Commercial purpose ✗  
Factor 2: Published work  
Factor 3: 100% (entire work) ✗  
Factor 4: Substitutes for purchasing → Market harm ✗

**Likely:** NOT fair dealing (infringement)

## Copyright Infringement

### Section 51: Infringement Defined

**Infringement = Doing, Without License, Act that Copyright Owner Has Exclusive Right to Do**

**Direct Infringement:**
- Reproducing work without permission
- Distributing copies
- Public performance without permission
- Communicating to public (e.g., uploading to internet)

**Indirect Infringement (Section 51(a)(ii)):**
- Permitting place to be used for infringing performance
- Selling, distributing infringing copies
- Importing infringing copies

**Example:**
- A copies B's novel, publishes 1000 copies, sells them
- Infringements: Reproduction (copying) + Distribution (selling)

**Knowledge Requirement (Commercial Dealings):**
- For selling/distributing: Must know OR have reason to believe copies are infringing
- Section 51(b)

**Innocent Infringer Defense:**
- If defendant did not know and had no reasonable grounds to know work was protected
- Damages may be reduced (but injunction still granted)

### Remedies for Infringement (Section 55)

**Civil Remedies:**

**1. Injunction (Section 55(1)(a))**
- Permanent injunction (restraining future infringement)
- Interim/temporary injunction (pending trial)

**Example:**
- Copyright owner discovers pirate copies circulating
- Files suit for injunction to stop further distribution

**2. Damages (Section 55(1)(a))**
- Compensatory damages (actual loss)
- Account of profits (defendant's profit from infringement)

**Calculation:**
- Plaintiff elects: Damages OR Account of profits (not both)

**Damages Measure:**
- Lost sales (how many copies plaintiff could have sold)
- License fee (what defendant should have paid)

**3. Delivery Up (Section 55(1)(b))**
- Infringing copies delivered to plaintiff for destruction
- Plates, masters used for infringement destroyed

**Example:**
- Pirate CDs seized and delivered to copyright owner for destruction

**4. Conversion Damages (Section 55(1)(b))**
- Damages for conversion (wrongful possession of infringing goods)

**Criminal Remedies (Section 63):**

**Infringement as Criminal Offense:**
- Imprisonment: Minimum 6 months, maximum 3 YEARS
- Fine: Minimum Rs. 50,000, maximum Rs. 2,00,000

**Cognizable Offense:**
- Police can arrest without warrant (for repeat offenders)
- First offense: Non-cognizable (police cannot arrest without warrant)

**Section 64 - Enhanced Penalty for Second Offense:**
- Imprisonment: Minimum 1 year, maximum 3 years
- Fine: Minimum Rs. 1,00,000, maximum Rs. 2,00,000

**Section 65 - Seizure of Infringing Copies:**
- Police can seize infringing copies
- Without warrant (if satisfied infringement)

## Digital Copyright Issues

### Section 65A-65B: Technological Protection Measures and Rights Management Information

**Section 65A - Protection of Technological Measures:**

```
"Prohibition Against Circumvention of Effective Technological Measures"
```

**Technological Protection Measure (TPM):**
- Technology/device/component preventing unauthorized access/use
- Examples: Encryption, password protection, DRM, geo-blocking

**Section 65A:**
- Circumventing TPM = Offense
- Manufacturing/selling circumvention devices = Offense

**Example:**
- DVD encrypted with region code (TPM)
- Breaking encryption to play DVD from another region → Violation of Section 65A

**Penalty:**
- Imprisonment up to 2 years
- Fine up to Rs. 3,00,000

**Section 65B - Protection of Rights Management Information:**

**Rights Management Information (RMI):**
- Information identifying work, author, owner, terms of use
- Copyright notice, author name, watermark

**Section 65B:**
- Removing/altering RMI with intent to conceal infringement = Offense
- Distributing work with RMI removed = Offense

**Example:**
- Photograph with photographer's watermark
- Removing watermark before posting online → Violation of Section 65B

**Penalty:**
- Imprisonment up to 2 years
- Fine up to Rs. 3,00,000

### Section 52(1)(c) - Internet Service Provider Safe Harbor

**ISP Liability for User Infringement:**

**General Rule:**
- ISP may be liable for hosting infringing content uploaded by users

**Exception - Safe Harbor (Section 52(1)(c)):**

ISP NOT liable if:
1. ISP is mere intermediary (hosting/transmitting)
2. ISP has NO actual knowledge of infringement
3. On obtaining knowledge, ISP expeditiously removes/disables access
4. ISP does not financially benefit directly from infringement

**Notice and Takedown:**
- Copyright owner sends notice to ISP (informing of infringement)
- ISP must promptly remove content OR disable access
- If ISP complies: No liability

**Example:**
- User uploads pirated movie to video-sharing platform
- Platform (ISP) unaware initially
- Copyright owner sends takedown notice
- Platform removes video within 24 hours
- Platform NOT liable (safe harbor protection)

**Failure to Takedown:**
- If ISP ignores notice and does not remove content
- Safe harbor lost
- ISP liable for infringement (contributory infringement)

## Compulsory Licensing (Section 31, 31A, 31D)

### Section 31 - Compulsory License for Published Works

**After 3 Years from Publication:**

Any person can apply to Copyright Board for compulsory license if:
1. Work published in India OR outside India
2. 3 years elapsed since publication
3. Copies not available to public at reasonable price OR work withheld from public

**Copyright Board May Grant License:**
- If satisfied work not available OR withheld
- Applicant must pay royalty (fixed by Board)
- License NON-EXCLUSIVE

**Example:**
- Academic textbook published 5 years ago
- Out of print, not available in market
- Educational publisher applies for compulsory license
- Board grants license, fixes royalty at 5%

### Section 31A - Compulsory License for Benefit of Disabled

**For Works in Accessible Format:**

Any person can apply for compulsory license to produce work in format accessible to disabled persons

**Formats:**
- Braille
- Audio books
- Large print
- Digitized formats

**Conditions:**
- Work not already available in accessible format
- License for non-commercial purposes
- Royalty fixed by Board (may be nominal)

### Section 31D - Statutory License for Broadcasting

**Cover Versions of Sound Recordings:**

Once sound recording of musical work published:
- Any person can make new sound recording (cover version)
- By giving notice to copyright owner + paying royalty

**Royalty Rate:**
- As per Copyright Board OR as agreed

**Conditions:**
- Original recording must be published in India
- Cover version for same musical work
- Cannot alter lyrics/music fundamentally
- Notice given to owner
- Royalty paid

**Example:**
- Original song by Singer A released by Music Label B
- Singer C wants to record cover version
- C gives notice to B + pays royalty
- C can record and release cover (statutory license)

## Registration of Copyright (Section 45)

**Copyright Registration is OPTIONAL**

**Copyright Arises Automatically:**
- Upon creation and fixation
- Registration NOT required for protection

**Benefits of Registration:**

1. **Prima Facie Evidence (Section 48)**
   - Certificate of registration = Prima facie evidence of:
     - Ownership
     - Particulars in certificate

2. **Evidential Value:**
   - Shifts burden of proof to challenger
   - Useful in infringement suits

3. **Public Notice:**
   - Entry in Copyright Register
   - Third parties can search

4. **Statutory Damages:**
   - Easier to claim statutory damages if registered

**Registration Procedure:**

1. **Application** - Form XIV (literary/dramatic/musical/artistic) or Form XV (films/sound recordings)
2. **Documents** - Copies of work, proof of ownership, applicant's identity
3. **Fee** - Prescribed fee (varies by work category)
4. **Diary Number** - Application given diary number
5. **Examination** - Copyright Office examines (30-60 days)
6. **Objections** - If any discrepancies, objections issued
7. **Reply** - Applicant replies within 30 days
8. **Registration** - If satisfied, registration granted
9. **Certificate** - Certificate of registration issued
10. **Extract in Copyright Journal** - Published in Copyright Office Journal

**Timeline:**
- Usually 6-12 months (if no objections)

**Grounds for Refusal:**
- Work not original
- Work not copyrightable
- Ownership disputed
- Previous registration exists

## International Copyright Protection

### Berne Convention for Protection of Literary and Artistic Works

**India is Member of Berne Convention**

**Key Principles:**

**1. National Treatment:**
- Works of foreign authors (from Berne member countries) protected in India
- Indian authors' works protected in all Berne member countries
- Same rights as nationals

**Example:**
- US author's book automatically protected in India (no formality)
- Indian author's book automatically protected in US, UK, France, etc.

**2. Automatic Protection:**
- No formality required (no registration)
- Copyright arises on creation

**3. Minimum Term:**
- Berne Convention minimum: Lifetime + 50 years
- India provides: Lifetime + 60 years (exceeds minimum)

**4. Moral Rights:**
- Paternity and integrity rights recognized

### TRIPS Agreement (Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights)

**India is WTO Member → TRIPS Obligations**

**TRIPS Requires:**
- Copyright protection for computer programs (literary works)
- Copyright protection for databases (if original)
- Rental rights for computer programs, cinematograph films
- Term: Minimum 50 years for most works (India provides 60)

**TRIPS Article 9:**
- Incorporates Berne Convention substantive provisions

## Common Scenarios and Solutions

### Scenario 1: Employee Creates Software - Who Owns Copyright?

**Facts:**
- Software engineer employed by Company A
- Engineer develops software application during office hours
- Using company resources

**Legal Analysis:**

**Section 17(a) - Work for Hire:**
- Software created in course of employment
- Under contract of service

**Ownership:**
- Company A owns copyright (employer)
- Engineer has NO copyright (absent contrary agreement)

**If Freelancer:**
- Not employee (independent contractor)
- Freelancer owns copyright (unless assignment)

**Recommendation:**
- Companies should have employment agreements with express copyright assignment clauses

---

### Scenario 2: Fair Dealing Defense - Student Photocopies Textbook

**Facts:**
- Student photocopies 5 chapters (out of 10) from textbook
- For exam preparation
- Publisher sues for infringement

**Student's Defense:**

**Section 52(1)(a) - Fair Dealing for Private Study:**
- Private or personal use exception

**Fair Dealing Test:**
1. Purpose: Educational ✓
2. Nature: Published textbook
3. Amount: 50% (substantial) ✗
4. Market Effect: Substitutes for purchase ✗

**Likely Outcome:**
- 50% is excessive
- Defeats publisher's market
- NOT fair dealing (infringement)

**If Only 1-2 Chapters (10-20%):**
- Likely fair dealing ✓

---

### Scenario 3: Photographer Hired for Wedding - Photo Ownership

**Facts:**
- Bride and groom hire Photographer X for wedding
- Photographer takes 500 photos
- Delivers album to couple
- Later, photographer uses photos in advertising
- Couple objects

**Legal Analysis:**

**Section 17(b) - Commissioned Photographs:**
```
"In case of photograph taken under commission, the person who commissioned 
the photograph is the first owner of copyright"
```

**Ownership:**
- Bride and groom (commissioners) own copyright
- Photographer does NOT own copyright

**Photographer's Use in Advertising:**
- Without couple's permission → Infringement

**Couple's Remedies:**
- Injunction to stop advertising use
- Damages

**If Photographer Wants to Use:**
- Must obtain written permission from couple
- OR contract at outset to retain copyright

**Modern Practice:**
- Photography contracts often reserve photographer's right to use for portfolio/advertising
- Express clause: "Photographer retains right to use photos for advertising, subject to privacy"

---

### Scenario 4: Copyright Infringement on Social Media - ISP Liability

**Facts:**
- User uploads pirated movie to video-sharing platform
- Copyright owner discovers infringement
- Sends takedown notice to platform
- Platform takes 15 days to remove video
- Meanwhile, video viewed 100,000 times

**Legal Analysis:**

**ISP Liability:**

**Section 52(1)(c) - Safe Harbor:**
- Platform is intermediary
- Conditions for safe harbor:
  - No actual knowledge (initially) ✓
  - On obtaining knowledge, expeditiously removes ✓ (15 days reasonable?)
  - No financial benefit directly from this video ✓

**Expeditiously Removed?**
- 15 days may be excessive (should be 24-48 hours)
- If unreasonable delay → Safe harbor questionable

**Outcome:**
- If court finds 15 days unreasonable → Platform liable (contributory infringement)
- If 15 days reasonable (considering manual review) → Platform NOT liable

**Best Practice for Platforms:**
- Automated notice-and-takedown system
- Remove within 24 hours of notice
- Counter-notice procedure (if user disputes)

---

### Scenario 5: Moral Rights Violation - Film Editing

**Facts:**
- Director A makes film, assigns copyright to Producer B
- Producer edits film heavily, removing key scenes
- Director's artistic vision distorted
- Director sues for moral rights violation

**Legal Analysis:**

**Section 57 - Moral Rights:**

**Right of Integrity:**
- Director has right to object to distortion/mutilation/modification prejudicial to honor/reputation

**Assignment ≠ Moral Rights Waived:**
- Economic rights assigned to Producer (copyright)
- Moral rights remain with Director (inalienable)

**Director's Remedies:**
- Injunction to restrain release of edited version
- Damages for violation of integrity right
- Mandatory injunction to restore original version

**Producer's Defense:**
- Edits necessary for commercial viability
- Director consented (if written waiver exists)
- Changes not prejudicial to honor/reputation

**Waiver of Moral Rights:**
- Director could have waived moral rights in writing
- Common in film industry contracts
- But must be express and clear

---

### Scenario 6: Compulsory License - Out-of-Print Textbook

**Facts:**
- Classic textbook published in 1995
- Author deceased (2005)
- Copyright held by Legal Heirs
- Book out of print for 8 years
- Educational publisher wants to reprint

**Legal Analysis:**

**Section 31 - Compulsory License:**

**Conditions Satisfied:**
1. Work published (1995) ✓
2. 3+ years since publication ✓
3. Copies not available to public OR withheld ✓

**Procedure:**
1. Publisher applies to Copyright Board
2. Notice to copyright owner (legal heirs)
3. Hearing
4. If Board satisfied work not available
5. Grant compulsory license
6. Fix royalty (e.g., 5-10%)

**Terms of License:**
- Non-exclusive (heirs can also publish)
- Territory (India only OR worldwide - as determined by Board)
- Period (5-10 years)
- Royalty payable to heirs

**Heirs' Position:**
- Can oppose (argue work available OR in process of reprint)
- If unsuccessfully oppose → Receive royalty

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Assuming Ideas are Protected
**Consequence:** No copyright protection for ideas/concepts
**Solution:** Understand only expression protected, not ideas

### 2. Not Obtaining Written Assignment
**Consequence:** Ownership disputes; assignment invalid (Section 19)
**Solution:** Always execute written assignment/license agreement

### 3. Assuming Registration Required for Protection
**Consequence:** Delay in enforcing rights
**Solution:** Copyright arises automatically; registration optional but advisable

### 4. Work for Hire - Employee Retains Copyright
**Consequence:** Employer does not own work created by employee
**Solution:** Section 17(a) - Employer owns (but have express clause in employment contract)

### 5. Excessive Fair Dealing Claim
**Consequence:** Infringement liability despite fair use defense
**Solution:** Limit copying to reasonable portion; consider four factors

### 6. Ignoring Moral Rights in Assignment
**Consequence:** Author can still claim integrity/attribution despite assignment
**Solution:** Obtain written waiver of moral rights (if necessary)

### 7. ISP Not Acting on Takedown Notice
**Consequence:** Loss of safe harbor; contributory infringement liability
**Solution:** Promptly remove content on receiving valid notice (24-48 hours)

### 8. Failing to Specify Rights in License
**Consequence:** Ambiguity, disputes over scope
**Solution:** Clearly specify: Rights, territory, period, exclusivity, royalty

### 9. Circumventing TPMs
**Consequence:** Criminal liability under Section 65A
**Solution:** Respect technological protection measures; obtain authorization

### 10. Not Conducting Copyright Search Before Use
**Consequence:** Unknowing infringement
**Solution:** Search Copyright Register; verify ownership; obtain permission
