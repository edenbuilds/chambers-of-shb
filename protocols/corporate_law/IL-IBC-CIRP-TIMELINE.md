# IBC - CIRP Timeline and Process (180-330 Days)

## Overview

### CIRP Process Summary

The **Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process (CIRP)** is a **strict time-bound** mechanism under IBC 2016 to resolve corporate insolvency within **180-330 days**. Unlike pre-IBC era (BIFR/DRT cases pending for 10-15 years), IBC mandates strict timelines at every stage.

**CIRP Timeline Framework**:
- **Mandatory Period**: 180 days from CIRP commencement (admission date)
- **First Extension**: +90 days (with CoC approval + NCLT approval) = 270 days total
- **Second Extension**: +60 days (pending NCLT approval of resolution plan) = 330 days maximum
- **No Further Extensions**: After 330 days, if no resolution plan approved → **Mandatory Liquidation** (Section 33)

**Key Milestones**:
1. **Day 0**: NCLT admission, Moratorium, IRP appointment
2. **Day 0-3**: Public announcement (Section 13)
3. **Day 0-30**: Claims submission, verification, CoC constitution
4. **Day 30-45**: First CoC meeting, RP appointment
5. **Day 45-120**: Information Memorandum, Expression of Interest (EOI), due diligence
6. **Day 120-180**: Resolution plans submission, evaluation, CoC voting
7. **Day 180-210**: NCLT confirmation hearing, approval order
8. **Day 210-330**: Implementation (payments to creditors, asset transfers)

**Creditor Control Hierarchy**:
- **Committee of Creditors (CoC)**: Financial creditors ONLY (100% voting power)
- **Operational Creditors**: NO voting rights (can attend meetings, give suggestions, but cannot vote)
- **Shareholders/Equity Holders**: NO rights (last in priority, typically get zero in insolvency)

**Success vs Failure**:
- **Success** (Resolution Plan Approved): Corporate debtor continues as going concern, creditors recover 40-60% of dues (average), jobs preserved
- **Failure** (No Plan Approved): Mandatory liquidation (Section 33), assets sold piecemeal, creditors recover 20-30% (liquidation value), jobs lost

## Legal Framework - Key Sections

### Section 12 - CIRP Period (180-330 Days Maximum)

**Section 12(1)**:
> "The corporate insolvency resolution process shall be completed within a period of one hundred and eighty days from the insolvency commencement date."

**Section 12(2)** (First Extension):
> "The resolution professional shall file an application to the Adjudicating Authority to extend the period of the corporate insolvency resolution process beyond one hundred and eighty days, if instructed to do so by a resolution passed at a meeting of the committee of creditors by a vote of sixty-six per cent of voting share."

**Section 12(3)** (Maximum Period):
> "The corporate insolvency resolution process shall mandatorily be completed within a period of three hundred and thirty days from the insolvency commencement date, including any extension of the period of corporate insolvency resolution process granted under sub-section (2) and the time taken in legal proceedings in relation to such resolution process."

**Timeline Breakdown**:
- **180 Days Mandatory**: From CIRP commencement (admission date)
- **+90 Days Extension** (Total 270 days): Requires CoC approval (66% vote) + NCLT order approving extension
- **+60 Days Judicial Time** (Total 330 days): Time taken for NCLT to approve resolution plan (hearings, objections, final order)
- **330 Days = ABSOLUTE MAXIMUM**: No further extensions (unless Supreme Court intervention in exceptional cases)

**Consequences of 330-Day Breach**:
- **Automatic Liquidation**: If no resolution plan approved by Day 330 → Corporate debtor goes into liquidation (Section 33(2))
- **No Exceptions**: Even if resolution plan is 99% ready, Day 331 = liquidation (Jet Airways case - CoC cannot seek indefinite extensions)

### Section 21 - Committee of Creditors (CoC) Constitution

**Section 21(2)** - **CoC Composition**:
> "The committee of creditors shall comprise all financial creditors of the corporate debtor."

**Key Principle**: **ONLY Financial Creditors Vote**
- **Included**: Banks, NBFCs, bondholders, debenture holders, ECB lenders, homebuyers (Section 5(8)(f))
- **Excluded**: Operational creditors (suppliers, employees, government), shareholders, promoters

**Voting Share Calculation** (Section 21(2)):
```
Each Financial Creditor's Voting Share (%) =
(Financial Creditor's Admitted Claim ÷ Total Financial Creditors' Admitted Claims) × 100
```

**Example**:
- Total Financial Creditors' Claims Admitted: ₹1,000 crore
  - ABC Bank: ₹500 crore (50% voting share)
  - DEF Bank: ₹300 crore (30% voting share)
  - Bondholders: ₹200 crore (20% voting share)
- **Operational Creditors' Claims Admitted**: ₹200 crore (suppliers, employees, government)
  - **Voting Share**: 0% (no voting rights, can only attend CoC meetings as invitees)

**CoC Meetings** (Section 21(8)):
- **Quorum**: 33% of voting share (1/3rd financial creditors' votes must be present)
- **Frequency**: As often as required (typically monthly during CIRP)
- **First Meeting**: Within 7 days of CoC constitution (Day 30-37 typically)
- **Agenda**: Appointment of RP, approval of interim finance, evaluation of resolution plans, approval of final plan

**Operational Creditors' Rights** (Limited):
- Attend CoC meetings (as invitees, no voting)
- Make representations to CoC (suggestions on resolution plan terms)
- Object to resolution plan at NCLT (if unfair distribution)
- **Cannot Block Resolution Plan**: Even if 100% operational creditors oppose, if 66% financial creditors approve → plan goes through (K. Sashidhar case)

### Section 29A - Ineligibility Criteria for Resolution Applicants

**Section 29A** lists **12 categories** of persons who **CANNOT submit resolution plans** (even if willing to pay highest amount):

**29A(a) - Undischarged Insolvent**:
- Person who is adjudicated insolvent (personal insolvency under IBC Part III)

**29A(b) - Willful Defaulter**:
- Person classified as "willful defaulter" by RBI
- **Definition** (RBI Master Circular): Borrower who (i) defaults despite ability to pay, OR (ii) diverts funds, OR (iii) siphons off funds, OR (iv) does not cooperate in recovery
- **Example**: Vijay Mallya (Kingfisher Airlines - classified willful defaulter by RBI → cannot bid for any IBC assets)

**29A(c) - NPA Account (>1 Year)**:
- Person with account classified as NPA (Non-Performing Asset) by bank/FI for **>1 year** before CIRP commencement
- **NPA Definition**: Loan overdue >90 days (RBI classification)
- **ArcelorMittal case (2018)**: ArcelorMittal initially had NPA account (Uttam Galva) overdue >1 year → ineligible, had to clear NPA debt (₹7,000cr) before bidding for Essar Steel

**29A(d) - Convicted for Offence**:
- Person convicted for offence punishable with ≥2 years imprisonment
- **Includes**: Fraud (Section 447 Companies Act - 10 years), Cheating (Section 420 IPC - 7 years), Corruption

**29A(e) - Disqualified Director**:
- Person disqualified under Section 164 Companies Act (e.g., DIN suspended, convicted fraud)

**29A(f) - Prohibited by SEBI/RBI**:
- Person prohibited by SEBI (from securities market) or RBI (from banking/NBFC operations)

**29A(g) - Related to Disqualified Person**:
- Person related to any of the above ineligible persons (promoter, director, KMP of ineligible company)
- **Connected Person** (Section 29A Explanation II): Promoter/director/KMP of ineligible company, or 100% subsidiary/holding company

**29A(h) - Avoided Transaction Beneficiary**:
- Person who received benefit from preferential/undervalued/fraudulent transaction (Sections 43, 45, 46, 49, 50)
- **Example**: Promoter transferred ₹100cr land to personal name 6 months before CIRP (undervalued transfer) → land recovered by RP, promoter ineligible to bid

**29A(j) - Corporate Debtor's Promoter/Connected (Limited Exception)**:
- Corporate debtor's **promoters** generally ineligible (cannot bid for their own company)
- **Exception**: MSMEs (Micro, Small, Medium Enterprises) - promoters CAN bid (Section 240A, added 2020 amendment)
  - MSME Definition: Investment in plant ≤₹50cr AND turnover ≤₹250cr
  - Rationale: Allow small businesses to retain ownership (avoid loss of livelihood for small entrepreneurs)

**Section 29A Application**:
- RP must verify Section 29A compliance before accepting resolution plan
- Any bidder found ineligible → plan rejected automatically (even if highest amount)
- **Effect**: Prevents willful defaulters/NPAs from buying back their own companies at distressed valuations (moral hazard prevention)

### Section 30 - CoC Approval of Resolution Plan (66% Voting Share)

**Section 30(4)**:
> "The committee of creditors may approve a resolution plan by a vote of not less than sixty-six per cent of voting share of the financial creditors."

**66% Threshold**:
- **Supermajority**: More than 2/3rds financial creditors (by value, not number) must approve
- **Binds ALL Creditors**: Once 66% approve, **dissenting 34% + operational creditors + shareholders are bound** (must accept plan terms)
- **No Court Override**: NCLT cannot modify CoC's commercial decision (Section 31(1), Essar Steel case - "commercial wisdom of CoC is paramount")

**Resolution Plan Contents** (Section 30(2)):
Must include:
1. **Payment Terms**: Amount to be paid to each class of creditors (financial, operational, workmen, secured, unsecured)
2. **Timeline**: Payment schedule (upfront payment at implementation + deferred payments over 3-5 years typical)
3. **Management**: New management structure, board composition post-resolution
4. **Business Plan**: Turnaround strategy, revenue projections, capex plan (next 3-5 years)
5. **Workmen Protection**: Treatment of employees (retention, retrenchment compensation if any, salary arrears payment)
6. **Statutory Dues**: Payment to government (pending GST, income tax, provident fund dues)

**NCLT's Limited Review** (Section 31(1)):
NCLT can reject plan ONLY if:
- Does not comply with Section 30(2) requirements (missing payment terms, business plan incomplete)
- Violates any law (e.g., environmental clearances not obtained, labor laws violated)
- Contravenes provisions of IBC
- **Cannot Reject on Merits**: NCLT cannot say "recovery too low for operational creditors" if CoC (66%) approved (Essar Steel case)

### Section 31 - NCLT Approval Timeline

**Section 31(1)**:
> "If the Adjudicating Authority is satisfied that the resolution plan as approved by the committee of creditors under sub-section (4) of section 30 meets the requirements as referred to in sub-section (2) of section 30, it shall by order approve the resolution plan."

**NCLT Approval Process**:
1. RP files resolution plan (approved by CoC 66%) with NCLT
2. NCLT issues notice to stakeholders (operational creditors, dissenting financial creditors, employees, government)
3. Objections filed (typically 14 days from notice)
4. Hearing(s) conducted (objectors present arguments, RP/CoC defend plan)
5. NCLT passes order (approve/reject)

**Timeline**: 30-60 days (included in 330-day maximum)

**NCLT Cannot**:
- Override CoC's 66% decision on commercial terms (amount, distribution)
- Modify plan sua sponte (on its own) without CoC approval
- Reject plan merely because operational creditors get lower recovery (liquidation waterfall Section 53 justifies differential treatment)

**NCLT Must Reject If**:
- Section 30(2) non-compliance (e.g., plan does not provide for priority payments - workmen dues per Section 53)
- Section 29A violation (bidder is ineligible - willful defaulter/NPA)
- Legal violations (environmental laws, FEMA violations for foreign bidders)

## CIRP Timeline - Day-by-Day Breakdown

### Phase 1: NCLT Admission to IRP Appointment (Day 0-1)

**Day 0 - NCLT Admission Order**:
- NCLT admits Section 7/8/9 application
- **Moratorium Declared** (Section 14): All suits, SARFAESI actions, asset transfers STAYED
- **IRP Appointed**: Proposed IRP becomes Interim Resolution Professional
- **Management Suspension**: Board of Directors suspended, KMPs report to IRP (Section 17)

**IRP's Immediate Actions** (Day 0-1):
1. **Take Physical Control**: Visit corporate debtor's registered office, factory, warehouses (take keys, change locks if necessary to prevent asset removal)
2. **Secure Assets**: Appoint security guards, inventory all movable assets (machinery, inventory, vehicles, cash)
3. **Bank Account Control**: Inform banks to freeze accounts, IRP becomes sole signatory (prevent unauthorized withdrawals)
4. **Employee Communication**: Address employees (assure continuity, explain CIRP process, prevent mass resignations)
5. **Customer/Supplier Assurance**: Contact key customers/suppliers (ensure business continuity, prevent supply disruptions)

### Phase 2: Public Announcement and Claims Invitation (Day 1-30)

**Day 1-3 - Public Announcement** (Section 13):

**Form B** (Public Announcement Format):
```
PUBLIC ANNOUNCEMENT OF CORPORATE INSOLVENCY RESOLUTION PROCESS
under Section 13 of the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016

[Company Logo]

CORPORATE DEBTOR: [Company Name], CIN: [CIN], Registered Office: [Address]

NOTICE is hereby given that the National Company Law Tribunal, [Bench], vide order dated
[Date] has admitted the application for initiation of Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process
(CIRP) against [Company Name].

INTERIM RESOLUTION PROFESSIONAL: [IRP Name], IBBI Registration No. [XXX], Email: [Email], Phone: [Phone]

CREDITORS' CLAIMS: All creditors (financial creditors, operational creditors, employees,
workmen) of the corporate debtor are required to submit their claims with proof to the
Interim Resolution Professional on or before [Date] (within 14 days from this announcement).

CLAIM SUBMISSION:
- Online: www.[companyCIRP].com
- Email: [IRP Email]
- Postal: [IRP Address]

FORMS:
- Financial Creditors: Form C (Regulation 9, CIRP Regulations 2016)
- Operational Creditors: Form D (Regulation 10)
- Employees/Workmen: Form E (Regulation 11)

DOCUMENTS REQUIRED:
- Proof of debt (loan agreement/invoices/salary slips)
- Account statements
- Correspondence with corporate debtor

Last Date for Submission of Claims: [Date] (Day 14 typically)

[IRP Signature]
[Date]
```

**Advertisement**:
- **Newspapers**: Publish in (i) English national daily (Economic Times), (ii) Regional language daily (state where registered office located)
- **IBBI Website**: Upload on www.ibbi.gov.in (public announcements section)
- **Corporate Debtor Website**: Upload on company website (if operational)
- **Registered Office**: Affix physical copy at registered office notice board

**Day 1-30 - Claims Submission and Verification**:

**Creditors Submit Claims**:
- **Financial Creditors** (Banks, NBFCs, Bondholders): Form C
  - Attach: Loan agreements, account statements, security documents, board resolutions
  - Claim: Principal ₹_____ + Interest ₹_____ + Penal interest ₹_____ = Total ₹_____
- **Operational Creditors** (Suppliers, Service Providers): Form D
  - Attach: Invoices, delivery challans, purchase orders, acceptance certificates
  - Claim: Invoice amounts + Interest (if agreed) = Total ₹_____
- **Employees**: Form E
  - Attach: Employment contract, salary slips, provident fund statements
  - Claim: Unpaid salaries (last 12 months) + Provident fund dues + Gratuity = Total ₹_____

**IRP Verification** (Section 18(1)(b)):
- Verify authenticity of claims (cross-check with corporate debtor's books, bank statements)
- **Admit** genuine claims (issue admission certificate)
- **Reject** fraudulent/inflated claims (issue rejection notice with reasons, claimant can appeal to NCLT)
- Prepare **List of Claims** (admitted claims by category - financial, operational, workmen, secured, unsecured)

**Example - Claims Verification**:
- Bank A claims ₹500cr (principal ₹400cr + interest ₹100cr)
  - IRP verifies: Loan agreement shows ₹400cr sanctioned, account statement shows ₹350cr disbursed
  - **Admitted Claim**: ₹350cr principal + ₹90cr interest (recalculated as per agreement) = ₹440cr
  - **Rejected**: ₹60cr (inflated claim, bank overclaimed)

### Phase 3: CoC Constitution and RP Appointment (Day 30-45)

**Day 30 - CoC Constitution** (Section 21):

**IRP Constitutes CoC**:
- Includes ALL financial creditors with admitted claims (banks, NBFCs, bondholders, homebuyers)
- Calculate voting shares (each financial creditor's claim ÷ total financial creditors' claims × 100)
- **Exclude** operational creditors (suppliers, employees, government - no voting rights)

**CoC Composition Example**:
```
Committee of Creditors (CoC) - [Company Name]

Financial Creditors (Voting Members):
1. ABC Bank - Admitted Claim ₹440 crore (44% voting share)
2. DEF Bank - Admitted Claim ₹300 crore (30% voting share)
3. GHI NBFC - Admitted Claim ₹150 crore (15% voting share)
4. Bondholders (represented by Debenture Trustee) - Admitted Claim ₹110 crore (11% voting share)

Total Financial Creditors' Claims: ₹1,000 crore (100% voting share)

Operational Creditors (Non-Voting Invitees):
1. Suppliers - Admitted Claims ₹80 crore
2. Employees - Admitted Claims ₹10 crore
3. Government (GST dues) - Admitted Claims ₹5 crore

Total Operational Creditors' Claims: ₹95 crore (0% voting share, can attend meetings as invitees)
```

**Day 30-37 - First CoC Meeting**:

**Agenda**:
1. **Introduction**: IRP presents corporate debtor's financial position, business operations, asset valuation
2. **Appointment of Resolution Professional (RP)** (Section 22):
   - Option 1: Confirm IRP as RP (most common - 70% of cases)
   - Option 2: Replace IRP with different RP (if CoC unhappy with IRP's performance)
   - **Voting**: Requires 66% CoC approval
3. **Interim Finance Approval** (if required):
   - If corporate debtor needs working capital to continue operations (pay salaries, buy raw materials)
   - CoC approves interim finance (up to ₹__cr), lender gets super-priority (Section 5(15A) - repaid before all other debts)
4. **Timeline Discussion**: RP presents proposed timeline for resolution plan process (EOI, due diligence, plan submission)

**RP Appointment**:
- **If IRP Confirmed**: IRP continues as RP till CIRP ends (resolution plan approved or liquidation ordered)
- **If Different RP Appointed**: IRP hands over to new RP within 7 days (all documents, bank accounts, asset custody transferred)

### Phase 4: Information Memorandum and EOI (Day 45-120)

**Day 45-60 - Information Memorandum (IM) Preparation** (Regulation 37):

**IM Contents** (100-300 pages typically):
1. **Corporate Debtor Profile**:
   - History, business segments, products/services, market position
   - Organizational structure, subsidiaries, associates, joint ventures
2. **Financial Information**:
   - Last 5 years audited financials (P&L, balance sheet, cash flow)
   - Monthly management accounts (last 12 months)
   - Aging analysis (receivables, payables)
3. **Assets Details**:
   - Fixed assets (land, buildings, plant, machinery - location, age, condition, valuation)
   - Current assets (inventory, receivables, cash)
   - Intangible assets (patents, trademarks, licenses, goodwill)
4. **Liabilities Details**:
   - Secured debts (lenders, security details, outstanding amounts)
   - Unsecured debts (operational creditors, employees, government)
   - Contingent liabilities (pending litigation, tax demands, bank guarantees)
5. **Legal Due Diligence**:
   - Title deeds (ownership verification for all properties)
   - Pending litigation (civil suits, criminal cases, tax disputes, labor disputes)
   - Regulatory approvals (environmental clearances, factory licenses, drug licenses)
6. **Valuation Reports**:
   - Liquidation value (if assets sold piecemeal in liquidation - Section 53 waterfall basis)
   - Going concern value (if business continues operations, DCF-based valuation)
   - Fair market value (each asset class valued independently)
7. **Business Plan** (Prepared by RP/Management):
   - Revenue projections (next 3-5 years, segment-wise)
   - Capex requirements (plant upgrades, technology investments)
   - Turnaround strategy (cost reduction, market expansion, product diversification)

**Example - Valuation Summary**:
```
Valuation of [Company Name]

1. Liquidation Value (Piecemeal Sale): ₹800 crore
   - Land & Buildings: ₹300cr (forced sale, 60% of market value)
   - Plant & Machinery: ₹150cr (auction, 30% of cost)
   - Inventory: ₹50cr (distress sale, 50% of book value)
   - Receivables: ₹100cr (50% realization)
   - IP/Goodwill: ₹100cr (brand value)
   - Cash: ₹100cr

2. Going Concern Value (DCF): ₹1,500 crore
   - Future cash flows (₹200cr EBITDA × 7.5x EV/EBITDA multiple)

3. Fair Market Value (Asset-by-Asset): ₹1,200 crore
   - Each asset valued at current market value (not distress sale)

RP's Recommendation: Aim for Going Concern Sale (₹1,200-1,500cr) to maximize creditor recovery
```

**Day 60-90 - Expression of Interest (EOI) Invitation**:

**EOI Advertisement**:
```
EXPRESSION OF INTEREST (EOI)
for submission of Resolution Plan for [Company Name]

The Resolution Professional invites Expression of Interest from prospective Resolution Applicants
for submission of Resolution Plan for [Company Name] (under CIRP).

CORPORATE DEBTOR: [Company Name], Business: [Manufacturing/Services], Turnover: ₹__cr,
Employees: [X], Assets: [Land, Plant, Inventory worth ₹__cr]

INFORMATION MEMORANDUM: Available at www.[companyCIRP].com (register to download, password-protected)

EOI SUBMISSION:
- Last Date: [Date] (Day 90)
- Format: Online submission via CIRP portal
- Processing Fee: ₹[5-10 lakh] (refundable to shortlisted bidders)

ELIGIBILITY:
- Must NOT be ineligible under Section 29A IBC (willful defaulters, NPA accounts >1 year, etc.)
- Demonstrate financial capability (net worth ≥₹__cr OR turnover ≥₹__cr OR consortium with financial partner)
- Submit undertaking of Section 29A compliance

SHORTLISTING: RP will shortlist EOI respondents based on eligibility, financial capability,
experience in similar businesses. Shortlisted applicants will be invited for due diligence.

Contact: [RP Name], [Email], [Phone]

[RP Signature]
[Date]
```

**Who Responds to EOI**:
- **Strategic Buyers**: Competitors, industry players looking to acquire (consolidation play)
- **Financial Buyers**: Private equity funds, distressed asset funds (turnaround investment)
- **Asset Reconstruction Companies (ARCs)**: Banks' ARCs bidding for NPAs (recover on behalf of selling banks)
- **Promoters** (if MSME): Existing promoters can bid for MSMEs (Section 240A exception to Section 29A(j))

**EOI Examples**:
- EOI 1: XYZ Steel Ltd. (Strategic Buyer, competitor, turnover ₹5,000cr, wants to acquire for capacity expansion)
- EOI 2: ABC Private Equity Fund (Financial Buyer, AUM $2bn, turnaround specialists)
- EOI 3: Promoter Group (if MSME, wants to retain business, willing to infuse fresh equity)

**Day 90-95 - EOI Shortlisting**:
- RP evaluates EOIs (10-20 received typically)
- **Section 29A Verification**: Check each bidder against 12 ineligibility criteria (willful defaulter status, NPA accounts, convicted persons, disqualified directors)
- **Financial Capability**: Verify net worth, turnover, funding sources (equity + debt)
- **Shortlist**: 3-5 serious bidders (rejected: ineligible under Section 29A, lack of financial capability, not serious)

**Day 95-120 - Due Diligence by Shortlisted Bidders**:
- **Physical Due Diligence**: Shortlisted bidders visit plants, inspect machinery, verify inventory
- **Data Room Access**: RP provides access to detailed documents (contracts, licenses, employee records, pending litigation files)
- **Management Presentations**: Corporate debtor's management presents to bidders (business operations, customer relationships, future prospects)
- **Q&A**: Bidders submit clarification questions to RP (RP coordinates responses from management/auditors)

### Phase 5: Resolution Plan Submission and Evaluation (Day 120-180)

**Day 120-150 - Resolution Plans Submission** (Section 28):

**Resolution Plan Format** (Regulation 38):

**Part A - Bidder Information**:
- Resolution applicant name, address, PAN, CIN (if company)
- Group structure (if consortium - equity partners, debt partners, strategic partners)
- Financial statements (last 3 years audited accounts, demonstrating capability to pay)
- Section 29A declaration (not ineligible, attach supporting documents - CIBIL report showing no NPA, no willful defaulter status)

**Part B - Resolution Plan Summary**:
```
RESOLUTION PLAN for [Company Name]

Submitted by: [Resolution Applicant Name]
Date: [Date]

1. UPFRONT PAYMENT (On Plan Approval):
   - Financial Creditors: ₹[__] crore ([__%] recovery)
   - Operational Creditors: ₹[__] crore ([__%] recovery)
   - Workmen Dues (Priority): ₹[__] crore (100% payment mandatory per Section 30(2)(b))
   - Total Upfront: ₹[__] crore

2. DEFERRED PAYMENTS (Over 3-5 Years):
   - Year 1: ₹[__] crore
   - Year 2: ₹[__] crore
   - Year 3: ₹[__] crore
   - Total Deferred: ₹[__] crore

3. TOTAL CONSIDERATION: ₹[__] crore (Upfront + Deferred)

4. SOURCES OF FUNDS:
   - Equity: ₹[__] crore (bidder's own funds)
   - Debt: ₹[__] crore (bank loans, Annexure: financing commitment letters)
   - Asset Sale: ₹[__] crore (non-core assets to be sold post-acquisition)

5. BUSINESS PLAN:
   - Turnaround Strategy: [Cost reduction, capacity utilization improvement, new product launch]
   - Revenue Projections: Year 1 ₹__cr, Year 2 ₹__cr, Year 3 ₹__cr
   - Capex Plan: ₹[__] crore (plant modernization, technology upgrade)
   - Job Retention: [__] employees retained ([__%] of total)

6. MANAGEMENT STRUCTURE:
   - Board Composition: [X] directors (Resolution Applicant nominees [X], Independent Directors [X])
   - CEO: [Name] (experience, background)
   - CFO: [Name]
   - Key Management Team: [Details]

7. COMPLIANCE:
```

- Audited financials (bidder's last 3 years)
- Funding commitment letters (from banks/equity partners, confirming ₹__cr available)

- Business plan (detailed, 50-100 pages)
- Valuation reports (justifying offer price)

**Day 150-170 - CoC Evaluation**:

**CoC Meetings** (2-3 meetings typically):
- **Meeting 1**: RP presents all submitted plans, initial comparison (amount, upfront vs deferred, business plan credibility)
- **Meeting 2**: Shortlisted bidders present plans to CoC (management presentations, Q&A with financial creditors)
- **Meeting 3**: Final deliberations, voting

**Evaluation Criteria** (CoC Considers):
1. **Recovery Amount**: Higher the better (but not the ONLY factor - Essar Steel case)
2. **Upfront vs Deferred**: Higher upfront preferred (certainty, time value of money)
3. **Business Plan Credibility**: Realistic revenue projections, proven management team, capex adequacy
4. **Job Retention**: CoC considers social impact (though not legally binding to choose highest job retention)
5. **Operational Creditors' Treatment**: Fair distribution (NCLT may scrutinize if operational creditors get <liquidation value)
6. **Funding Certainty**: Firm financing commitments (bank loans sanctioned, equity committed), not conditional
7. **Section 29A Compliance**: No ineligibility issues (RP verified, CoC double-checks)

**CoC's Commercial Wisdom** (Essar Steel Principle):
- CoC can choose lower-amount plan if it has higher upfront, better business plan, stronger bidder
- **Example**: CoC may prefer Plan B (₹1,000cr, 70% upfront) over Plan C (₹900cr, 56% upfront) even though Plan C is highest → CoC's commercial judgment, NCLT cannot override

**Day 170-180 - CoC Voting** (Section 30(4)):

**Voting Process**:
- RP puts resolution plan(s) to vote (typically one plan at a time - if first plan gets 66%, no need to vote on others)
- Each financial creditor votes (FOR/AGAINST)
- Count votes (by value, not number - sum of voting shares of FOR votes)

**Approval Threshold**: ≥66% of voting share

**Example - Voting**:
```

DEF Bank                30%             FOR         Prefer upfront cash, low execution risk
GHI NBFC                15%             AGAINST     Wants higher recovery, prefers Plan B
Bondholders             11%             FOR         Management team credible, job retention

TOTAL FOR:              44% + 30% + 11% = 85% ✅ (>66% threshold)
TOTAL AGAINST:          15%

RESULT: Resolution Plan A APPROVED by Committee of Creditors (85% voting share)

Date: Day 180
RP Signature: [Signature]
```

**If No Plan Gets 66%**:
- RP can invite modified plans (bidders revise terms, increase amounts, change payment structure)
- CoC can extend CIRP period (+90 days, requires NCLT approval under Section 12(2))
- **If No Approval by Day 330** → **Mandatory Liquidation** (Section 33(2))

### Phase 6: NCLT Confirmation and Implementation (Day 180-330)

**Day 181-190 - Resolution Plan Filing with NCLT** (Section 31):

**RP Files**:
- Resolution plan document (approved by 66% CoC)
- CoC voting record (Form showing 66% approval, each creditor's vote)
- Section 29A compliance certificates (bidder's affidavits, RP's verification report)
- Business plan, funding commitment letters, valuation reports

**NCLT Issues Notice**:
- To all stakeholders: Dissenting financial creditors (34% who voted against), operational creditors, employees, government, shareholders
- **Objection Period**: 14-21 days (stakeholders can file written objections)

**Day 190-200 - NCLT Hearing**:

**Objections** (Typical):
1. **Operational Creditors**: "We're getting only 65% recovery while financial creditors get 100%, unfair distribution"
   - **RP's Response**: "Liquidation waterfall (Section 53) gives financial creditors priority, 65% is MORE than operational creditors would get in liquidation (estimated 30%), hence fair"
2. **Dissenting Financial Creditor** (GHI NBFC): "We wanted Plan B (higher recovery for us due to security), but CoC chose Plan A"
   - **RP's Response**: "66% majority (85%) approved Plan A, dissenting 15% is bound, commercial wisdom of CoC is supreme per Essar Steel"
3. **Employees' Union**: "100 employees being retrenched, violates labor laws"
   - **Bidder's Response**: "Full retrenchment compensation being paid (₹2cr), 400 jobs retained (80%), necessary for business viability"
4. **Government** (Tax Department): "Our GST dues (₹5cr) being paid only 50%, we're priority creditor"
   - **RP's Response**: "Government dues treated as operational debt (Section 53(1)(b)(ii)), getting same recovery as other operational creditors (65%), liquidation waterfall priority respected"

**NCLT's Review** (Section 31(1)):
- Does plan comply with Section 30(2)? (Payment terms specified, business plan included, workmen dues 100% paid)
- Does plan violate any law? (Labor laws, environmental laws, FEMA for foreign bidders)
- Is Section 29A complied? (Bidder not ineligible)
- Is liquidation waterfall (Section 53) respected? (Secured creditors getting more than unsecured, operational creditors getting at least liquidation value)

**If Satisfied → NCLT Approves**:

   - Operational Creditors: ₹180cr paid to suppliers, government, professional fees
   - Employees: ₹20cr paid upfront (within 30 days - priority payment)

2. **Share Transfer**:

   - Resolution Applicant becomes 100% shareholder (new equity issued, old equity cancelled)

## Landmark Cases

### 1. Essar Steel India Ltd. Committee of Creditors case (2019 Supreme Court - Constitutional Bench)

**Facts**:
- Essar Steel (steel manufacturer, ₹50,000cr debt) under CIRP
- 2 resolution plans submitted:
  - **ArcelorMittal**: ₹42,000cr (financial creditors ₹41,000cr, operational creditors ₹1,000cr = 2.4% recovery for operational creditors)
  - **Numetal**: ₹40,000cr (financial creditors ₹37,000cr, operational creditors ₹3,000cr = 7.3% recovery for operational creditors)
- CoC approved ArcelorMittal plan (higher amount, despite lower recovery for operational creditors)
- **Operational Creditors Challenged**: "ArcelorMittal plan violates Section 30(2)(b) - discriminates against operational creditors, we're getting only 2.4% while financial creditors get 100%"

**Issue**: Can CoC approve plan giving differential treatment to operational creditors vs financial creditors? Can NCLT reject plan to protect operational creditors?

**Held** (Supreme Court - 3:0 Unanimous, Upheld CoC's Decision):
1. **CoC's Commercial Wisdom is Supreme** (Section 30(4)):
   - Once 66% CoC approves, NCLT cannot override on commercial merits
   - **"The commercial wisdom of creditors is to be preferred over the wisdom of courts"** (famous quote from judgment)
   - NCLT can only check: (i) Section 30(2) compliance, (ii) Law violations, (iii) IBC contraventions
2. **Differential Treatment Allowed**:
   - IBC distinguishes financial creditors (risk capital, voting rights) vs operational creditors (no voting rights)
   - Liquidation waterfall (Section 53) gives secured financial creditors priority over operational creditors
   - If operational creditors getting **more than liquidation value**, they have no grievance (here, liquidation value was 0-1%, getting 2.4% is better)
3. **Operational Creditors' Rights are Procedural, Not Substantive**:
   - Operational creditors can: (i) Attend CoC meetings, (ii) Make representations, (iii) Object at NCLT
   - But **cannot block resolution plan** approved by 66% financial creditors
4. **NCLT Cannot Redistribute**:
   - NCLT cannot modify plan to increase operational creditors' share (even if it seems "fair")
   - Any modification requires CoC re-approval (66% vote), NCLT cannot impose sua sponte

**Principle**:
- **Financial Creditors > Operational Creditors** in IBC hierarchy (voting power, recovery priority)
- **CoC's commercial decision is final** (NCLT's role is limited to legal/procedural review, not commercial review)
- Operational creditors protected if getting ≥liquidation value (Section 53 waterfall), no right to equal treatment with financial creditors

**Impact**:
- Settled law: Operational creditors cannot challenge CoC-approved plans on "unfair distribution" grounds (unless getting <liquidation value)
- Financial creditors can approve plans maximizing their recovery, operational creditors bound to accept

### 2. ArcelorMittal India Pvt. Ltd. v. Satish Kumar Gupta (2018 Supreme Court)

**Facts**:
- ArcelorMittal (global steel giant, revenue $70bn) wanted to bid for Essar Steel (under CIRP)
- **Problem**: ArcelorMittal's India subsidiaries (Uttam Galva Steel, KSS Petron) were NPA accounts (classified NPA by banks, overdue >1 year)
- Section 29A(c): Person with NPA account >1 year is **ineligible** to be resolution applicant
- ArcelorMittal argued: "Uttam Galva is separate company (subsidiary), not ArcelorMittal itself, Section 29A(c) should not apply"
- **Satish Kumar Gupta** (Resolution Professional for Essar Steel) rejected ArcelorMittal's EOI citing Section 29A(c)

**Issue**: Does Section 29A(c) ineligibility apply to parent company if subsidiary has NPA account >1 year?

**Held** (Supreme Court - Arcelar Mittal Ineligible Unless NPAs Cleared):
1. **Section 29A(c) Applies to "Connected Persons"**:
   - Section 29A Explanation II defines "connected person" includes: Promoter, director, KMP, holding company, subsidiary
   - ArcelorMittal (parent) is "connected person" of Uttam Galva (subsidiary) and KSS Petron (subsidiary)
   - If Uttam Galva/KSS Petron are NPAs, ArcelorMittal is ineligible under Section 29A(c)
2. **Purpose of Section 29A(c)**: Prevent NPA defaulters from buying back their own or related companies at distressed valuations
   - "Would defeat the object of the Code if a person could escape ineligibility by routing investment through holding company"
3. **Cure Available**: ArcelorMittal can become eligible by **clearing NPA dues**
   - If ArcelorMittal pays all overdue amounts for Uttam Galva + KSS Petron (make accounts "standard"), Section 29A(c) no longer applies

**Outcome**:
- ArcelorMittal paid ₹7,000 crore to clear Uttam Galva and KSS Petron NPAs (made accounts current)
- Post-payment, ArcelorMittal became eligible, resubmitted bid for Essar Steel
- CoC approved ArcelorMittal plan (₹42,000cr), NCLT confirmed, Arcelar Mittal acquired Essar Steel

**Principle**:
- **Section 29A covers "connected persons"** (parent, subsidiary, promoters of subsidiary)
- NPA defaulters MUST clear dues before bidding (no escape via corporate structuring)
- **Cure Available**: Pay off NPA dues → become eligible (redemption opportunity)

**Impact**:
- Forced many PE funds/strategic buyers to clear related-party NPAs before bidding (₹50,000+ crore NPA dues cleared by bidders 2018-2020)
- Increased recovery for banks (defaulters paid up to become eligible for other auctions)

## Compliance Checklist

### IRP/RP Checklist (Day 0-30)

**Immediate Actions (Day 0-3)**:
- [ ] Take physical control of corporate debtor's premises (registered office, factories, warehouses)
- [ ] Secure all movable assets (machinery, inventory, vehicles, cash, documents)
- [ ] Freeze bank accounts (IRP becomes sole signatory, prevent unauthorized withdrawals)
- [ ] Collect all books of accounts, financial records, contracts, licenses from management
- [ ] Address employees (explain CIRP process, assure salary continuity, prevent mass exodus)
- [ ] Contact key customers and suppliers (assure business continuity, prevent supply disruptions)

**Public Announcement (Day 1-3)**:
- [ ] Prepare Form B (public announcement format per Regulation 7)
- [ ] Publish in newspapers: (i) English national daily (Economic Times/Business Standard), (ii) Regional language daily (state of registered office)
- [ ] Upload on IBBI website (www.ibbi.gov.in) and corporate debtor website (if operational)
- [ ] Affix physical copy at registered office notice board
- [ ] Send notice to known creditors (email + registered post)

**Claims Invitation and Verification (Day 1-30)**:
- [ ] Set up online claims portal (or email/postal address for claim submission)
- [ ] Receive claims (Form C - financial creditors, Form D - operational creditors, Form E - employees)
- [ ] Verify each claim: Cross-check with corporate debtor's books, bank statements, contracts
- [ ] Admit genuine claims (issue admission certificate with admitted amount)
- [ ] Reject inflated/fraudulent claims (issue rejection notice with reasons)
- [ ] Prepare List of Claims (admitted claims by category - financial, operational, workmen)

**CoC Constitution (Day 30)**:
- [ ] Identify all financial creditors with admitted claims
- [ ] Calculate voting shares (each financial creditor's claim ÷ total financial creditors' claims × 100)
- [ ] Prepare CoC membership list (names, addresses, voting shares, contact details)
- [ ] Send CoC constitution notice to all financial creditors

**First CoC Meeting (Day 30-37)**:
- [ ] Agenda: (i) IRP presentation on corporate debtor's status, (ii) RP appointment voting, (iii) Interim finance discussion, (iv) Timeline approval
- [ ] Prepare presentation (financial position, asset valuation, business operations, proposed timeline)
- [ ] Conduct voting on RP appointment (requires 66% approval)
- [ ] If confirmed as RP: Continue for full CIRP period
- [ ] If different RP appointed: Hand over all documents, assets, bank accounts to new RP within 7 days

### RP Checklist (Day 30-180)

**Information Memorandum Preparation (Day 45-60)**:
- [ ] Collect corporate debtor's last 5 years financials (audited accounts, tax returns)
- [ ] Commission asset valuation (registered valuer for liquidation value, going concern value, fair market value)
- [ ] Conduct legal due diligence (title verification for all properties, pending litigation search, license/approval status)
- [ ] Prepare business plan (turnaround strategy, revenue projections, capex requirements)
- [ ] Compile Information Memorandum (100-300 pages, Regulation 37 format)
- [ ] Upload IM on CIRP portal (password-protected, accessible only to registered EOI respondents)

**EOI Process (Day 60-90)**:
- [ ] Advertise EOI in newspapers (English + regional) and IBBI website
- [ ] Set up EOI portal (online submissions, processing fee payment ₹5-10L)
- [ ] Receive EOIs (typical: 10-20 respondents)
- [ ] Verify Section 29A compliance for each EOI (check CIBIL reports, RBI willful defaulter list, court records for convictions, disqualification orders)
- [ ] Shortlist 3-5 serious bidders (eligible under Section 29A, financially capable, industry experience)
- [ ] Reject ineligible bidders (issue rejection notice with Section 29A violation details)

**Due Diligence by Bidders (Day 90-120)**:
- [ ] Set up data room (physical or virtual, 500-1000 documents typical)
- [ ] Coordinate plant visits (escorted tours, safety protocols)
- [ ] Arrange management presentations (CEO/CFO present to bidders)
- [ ] Respond to bidder queries (Q&A clarifications, additional document requests)
- [ ] Issue Resolution Plan invitation (format per Regulation 38, deadline Day 120-150)

**Resolution Plan Evaluation (Day 150-170)**:
- [ ] Receive resolution plans (2-5 plans typical)
- [ ] Verify Section 29A compliance AGAIN (final check before presenting to CoC)
- [ ] Prepare comparison matrix (amount, upfront vs deferred, recovery %, business plan, job retention)
- [ ] Convene CoC meetings (2-3 meetings for evaluation, bidder presentations, deliberations)
- [ ] Present RP's assessment and recommendation to CoC

**CoC Voting (Day 170-180)**:
- [ ] Put resolution plan(s) to vote (one at a time, start with RP's recommended plan)
- [ ] Record votes (Form with creditor names, voting shares, FOR/AGAINST, signatures)
- [ ] Count votes (sum voting shares of FOR votes, check if ≥66%)
- [ ] If ≥66% approval: Plan approved, proceed to NCLT filing
- [ ] If <66%: Options - (i) Vote on alternative plan, (ii) Invite modified plans, (iii) Extend CIRP (+90 days with NCLT approval), (iv) If Day 330 → Liquidation (Section 33(2))

**NCLT Confirmation (Day 180-210)**:
- [ ] File approved resolution plan with NCLT (Section 31)
- [ ] Attach: (i) Resolution plan document, (ii) CoC voting record (66% proof), (iii) Section 29A compliance certificates, (iv) Business plan, (v) Funding commitment letters
- [ ] NCLT issues notice to stakeholders (dissenting creditors, operational creditors, employees, government)
- [ ] Respond to objections (written replies to NCLT, legal arguments at hearing)
- [ ] Attend NCLT hearing (present resolution plan, defend against objections, request approval)
- [ ] Obtain NCLT approval order (certified copy)

**Implementation Monitoring (Day 210-330)**:
- [ ] Coordinate resolution applicant's payments to creditors (₹1,100cr to financial creditors within 90 days)
- [ ] Verify share transfer (old shares cancelled, new shares issued to resolution applicant)
- [ ] Verify Board reconstitution (old Board removed, new Board appointed per plan)
- [ ] Monitor business continuity (ensure operations continue smoothly, no disruption)
- [ ] File compliance report with NCLT (Resolution Plan fully implemented, attach payment proofs, share certificates, Board resolution)
- [ ] Obtain NCLT's final order closing CIRP
