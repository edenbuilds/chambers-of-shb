## Overview

**Director's Duties** = Legal obligations directors owe to company (not shareholders) arising from their position as fiduciaries.

**Legal Framework**:
- **Section 166**: General duties of directors (codification of common law fiduciary duties)
- **Section 184**: Disclosure of interest in contracts/arrangements
- **Section 185-188**: Loans to directors, related party transactions
- **Section 166(5)**: Duty to attend board meetings
- **Section 339**: Liability for fraudulent conduct

**Key Principle**: Directors are **trustees** of company's assets, **agents** of company (not individual shareholders). They must act in **company's best interest**, not personal interest or individual shareholder's interest.

---

## Fiduciary Duties Overview (Section 166)

### Statutory Codification

**Section 166**: Every director shall:
1. Act in accordance with company's articles (Section 166(1))
2. Act in good faith to promote company's objects (Section 166(2))
3. Exercise duties with due and reasonable care, skill, and diligence (Section 166(3))
4. Not involve in situation creating conflict of interest (Section 166(4))
5. Not achieve undue gain or advantage (Section 166(5))
6. Not assign office (Section 166(6))

**Penalty for Breach** (Section 166(7)):
- Director liable to company for:
  - Restitution of profits made (disgorgement)
  - Compensation for losses caused to company
  - Criminal prosecution (if fraud involved)

---

## 1. Duty to Act in Accordance with AOA (Section 166(1))

### Scope

**Rule**: Directors must act within powers conferred by **Articles of Association**.

**Examples**:
✅ If AOA empowers board to borrow up to ₹50 lakh → Board can borrow ≤₹50 lakh (without shareholder approval)
❌ If board borrows ₹1 crore (beyond AOA limit) → **Ultra vires directors**, breach of duty

**Rationale**: AOA is company's constitution (approved by shareholders). Directors cannot exceed powers granted by shareholders via AOA.

---

### Proper Purpose Doctrine

**Rule**: Directors must exercise powers for **proper purpose** (purpose for which power conferred), not collateral/improper purpose.

**Landmark Case: Howard Smith Ltd v. Ampol Petroleum Ltd (1974)**

**Facts**:
- Company had 2 major shareholders: Ampol (55%) and Bulkships (45%)
- Board issued new shares to Howard Smith (diluting Ampol to 37%, Bulkships to 31%, Howard Smith 32%)
- Purpose claimed: Raise capital for company
- Actual purpose: Dilute Ampol's majority control (Ampol opposed takeover, board wanted Howard Smith as partner)

**Held**:
- Share issuance power must be exercised for **proper purpose** (raising capital)
- Using share issuance to alter control = **Improper purpose** (even if directors believed it was in company's interest)
- Share issuance set aside (invalid)

**Principle**: Directors cannot use share issuance power to manipulate control/voting, even if they believe it's in company's interest. Power must be used for purpose for which conferred (raising capital, not altering control).

**Application**: If directors issue shares to:
✅ Raise capital for expansion → Proper purpose (valid)
✅ Fund acquisition (genuine business need) → Proper purpose (valid)
❌ Dilute hostile takeover bidder → Improper purpose (invalid, defensive tactic)
❌ Reward friendly investors, punish opponents → Improper purpose (invalid)

## 2. Duty to Act in Good Faith (Section 166(2))

### Subjective vs Objective Test

**Rule**: Directors must act in **good faith** to promote company's objects, for benefit of members as a whole.

**Subjective Test**: What directors **honestly believed** was in company's interest
**Objective Test**: What **reasonable director** would believe was in company's interest

**Indian Law**: Predominantly **subjective** (if directors honestly believed act was in company's interest, good faith satisfied), BUT courts apply **objective reasonableness** check (honesty alone insufficient if decision patently unreasonable).

---

### Benefit of Company vs Individual Shareholders

**General Rule**: Directors owe duty to **company** (legal entity), not individual shareholders.

**Exception**: In specific situations, directors must consider **shareholders as a whole**:
- Winding up (company insolvent, shareholders' interests priority shifts to creditors)
- Takeover situations (shareholders' interests in receiving fair price)

**Landmark Case: Percival v. Wright (1902)**
**Facts**: Directors purchased shares from shareholders at ₹12.50 without disclosing company was in takeover talks (shares worth ₹30).
**Held**: Directors owed no duty to disclose to individual shareholders. Duty owed to company, not individual members.
**Principle**: No fiduciary relationship between directors and individual shareholders (unless special circumstances like takeover).

**Exception**: Modern law recognizes directors must consider **creditors' interests** when company nearing insolvency (wrongful trading provisions).

---

## 3. Duty to Exercise Independent Judgment (Section 166(3))

### No Fettering of Discretion

**Rule**: Directors must exercise **independent judgment**, not blindly follow instructions from shareholders, promoters, or external parties.

**Prohibited**:
❌ Director acts as "puppet" of controlling shareholder (rubber-stamps all decisions)
❌ Director signs blank board resolutions (allowing others to fill in later)
❌ Director abdicates decision-making to CEO/promoter (doesn't apply mind)

**Allowed**:
✅ Director seeks advice from experts (lawyers, CAs, consultants) before deciding
✅ Director considers shareholder views BUT makes independent assessment
✅ Nominee director represents nominating party's views BUT exercises independent judgment (owes duty to company)

**Nominee Directors**: Despite being nominated by specific shareholder/lender, nominee director owes fiduciary duty to **company** (not just nominating party). Must exercise independent judgment.

**Landmark Case: Scottish Co-operative Wholesale Society v. Meyer (1959)**
**Facts**: Nominee directors (appointed by parent company) acted solely in parent's interest, ignoring minority shareholders' rights.
**Held**: Nominee directors breached fiduciary duty. Despite being nominees, they owe duty to **company**, must exercise independent judgment.

## 4. Duty of Care, Skill, and Diligence (Section 166(3))

### Standard of Care

**Section 166(3)**: Director shall exercise duties with **due and reasonable care, skill, and diligence**, exercising **independent judgment**.

**Test** (Dual Standard):
1. **Subjective Test**: Care, skill expected from director based on their **actual knowledge, skill, experience**
2. **Objective Test**: Care, skill expected from **reasonably diligent person** in director's position

**Landmark Case: Re City Equitable Fire Insurance (1925)**

**Facts**: Company's fire insurance business collapsed (heavy losses). Directors claimed they relied on managing director, didn't scrutinize accounts.

**Held**: Directors' duty of care assessed on:
1. **Subjective Test**: Director need only exhibit care, skill they **actually possess** (not expected to have skills they don't have)
2. **Objective Test**: Director expected to exhibit care **reasonable person** in their position would show

**Three Principles**:
1. **Skill**: Director need not exhibit greater skill than they actually possess. Finance expert held to higher standard than non-expert director.
2. **Delegation**: Directors can delegate to officers/committees (need not personally perform every task). BUT must exercise general supervision.
3. **Attendance**: Directors not bound to attend every board meeting. BUT regular attendance expected (absenting for 12 months = vacation of office u/s 167).

**Modern Trend**: Indian law adopts **higher objective standard** - Directors expected to be **reasonably informed**, apply business judgment. Cannot claim ignorance of basic financial health, major transactions.

---

### Specific Care Duties

**a. Duty to Attend Board Meetings** (Section 166(5)):
- Directors must attend board meetings regularly
- Repeated absence (12 months) = Automatic vacation of office (Section 167(1)(b))

**b. Duty to Be Informed**:
- Read board papers, financial statements, proposals
- Ask questions, seek clarifications
- Cannot blindly sign resolutions without understanding

**c. Duty to Monitor**:
- Oversee management performance
- Review financial position regularly
- Ensure compliance with laws, regulations

**d. Duty to Act Timely**:
- Take decisions without undue delay
- Address red flags, warning signs promptly

---

### Business Judgment Rule

**Definition**: Courts defer to directors' **business decisions** (made in good faith, with reasonable information, without conflict of interest), even if decision ultimately fails.

**Rationale**: Directors are business experts, courts are not. Hindsight should not be used to judge business decisions. Protects directors from liability for honest errors of judgment.

**Requirements for Protection**:
1. ✅ **Good Faith**: Decision made honestly, believing it was in company's interest
2. ✅ **Informed Basis**: Directors reasonably informed (reviewed proposals, sought expert advice)
3. ✅ **No Conflict of Interest**: Decision not tainted by self-interest
4. ✅ **Rational Basis**: Decision has rational business justification (not arbitrary)

**Landmark Case: Smith v. Van Gorkom (Delaware, 1985)**

**Facts**:
- Board approved company sale at $55/share in 2-hour meeting (without reviewing valuation, expert fairness opinion, or negotiating price)
- Shareholders sued directors for breach of duty of care (inadequate deliberation)

**Held**:
- Directors breached duty of care (grossly negligent)
- Business judgment rule NOT applicable (directors not reasonably informed)
- Insufficient deliberation (2-hour meeting for $690 million sale), no valuation analysis, no expert opinion

**Principle**: Business judgment rule protects directors ONLY if decision made on **informed basis**. Directors must:
- Review materials (proposals, valuations, expert reports)
- Deliberate sufficiently (2-hour meeting inadequate for major transaction)
- Document decision-making process (board minutes, resolutions)

**Impact**: Post-Van Gorkom, boards conduct thorough deliberations for major transactions (fairness opinions, independent valuations, extended board meetings, detailed minutes).

---

### Indian Application of Business Judgment Rule

**Indian Courts**: Recognize business judgment rule **implicitly** (courts reluctant to second-guess commercial decisions).

**Example**: Court will not interfere if board decides to:
- Enter new market (even if venture fails)
- Acquire company (even if acquisition underperforms)
- Divest business unit (even if buyer profits later)

**Provided**: Decision made in good faith, with reasonable information, without conflict.

**Exception**: Courts intervene if:
- Decision made fraudulently, in bad faith
- Self-dealing (directors benefited personally)
- Gross negligence (no reasonable basis for decision)

## 5. Duty to Avoid Conflicts of Interest (Section 166(4))

### No-Conflict Rule

**Rule**: Director must not place themselves in position where personal interest **conflicts** with company's interest.

**Examples of Conflicts**:
❌ Director has personal interest in contract with company (owns supplier company, company buying from supplier)
❌ Director competes with company (starts rival business)
❌ Director takes corporate opportunity (company had opportunity, director diverts to self)

---

### Disclosure of Interest (Section 184)

**Mandatory Disclosure**:
- Director must disclose **nature of interest** in contracts/arrangements (at board meeting)
- Disclosure required BEFORE contract entered, OR at first board meeting after interest arises
- Form MBP-1: Register of interests maintained

**Example**:
- Director A owns 50% in Supplier Co.
- Company proposes to buy goods from Supplier Co.
- Director A must disclose: "I hold 50% shares in Supplier Co. I am interested in this transaction."
- Board (excluding interested director) votes on transaction

**Non-Interested Directors**: Only **non-interested directors** can approve related party transaction. Interested director cannot vote (Section 184(2)).

**Penalty for Non-Disclosure** (Section 184(3)):
- Fine: ₹1 lakh - ₹5 lakh
- Contract voidable at company's option

---

### Corporate Opportunity Doctrine

**Rule**: Director cannot usurp **corporate opportunity** (opportunity belonging to company) for personal benefit.

**Test for Corporate Opportunity**:
1. Was opportunity **in line of company's business**?
2. Did company have **financial ability** to pursue opportunity?
3. Did director learn of opportunity **through directorship** (using company's resources/information)?

**If YES to above** → Corporate opportunity. Director must:
- Offer opportunity to company first
- If company declines (after full disclosure), director may pursue personally

**Landmark Case: Regal (Hastings) Ltd v. Gulliver (1942)**

**Facts**:
- Regal Ltd wanted to acquire cinema lease (needed £5,000 capital)
- Regal couldn't raise capital, directors subscribed personally, acquired lease through separate company
- Later, Regal sold cinema business (including directors' company) at profit
- Directors made personal profit

**Held**:
- Directors liable to account for profit to Regal (even though Regal couldn't have pursued opportunity)
- No-profit rule: Directors cannot profit from position (even if company couldn't have profited)
- Directors must offer opportunity to company first, obtain informed board approval before pursuing personally

**Principle**: **No-profit rule** is strict. Directors cannot retain profit made using:
- Company's property, information, or opportunity
- Position as director
Even if company could not have exploited opportunity, director must account for profit.

**Exception**: Directors can pursue corporate opportunity if:
- Full disclosure to board (all material facts)
- Board (of non-interested directors) approves director pursuing opportunity
- Company genuinely unable/unwilling to pursue (documented in board minutes)

## 6. Duty Not to Achieve Undue Gain (Section 166(5))

### No-Profit Rule

**Section 166(5)**: Director shall not, directly or indirectly:
- Achieve or attempt to achieve any **undue gain or advantage** to themselves or associates
- Cause or attempt to cause any **undue loss** to company

**Examples**:
❌ Insider trading (director trades shares based on unpublished price-sensitive information)
❌ Director awards contract to own company at inflated prices
❌ Director receives commission/kickback from third party (without disclosure)
❌ Director diverts company's business to own entity

**Penalty** (Section 166(7)):
- **Liable to company** for:
  - Restitution of gain made
  - Compensation for loss caused to company
- **Fine**: ₹1 lakh - ₹5 lakh
- **Imprisonment**: Up to 1 year (if fraudulent intent)

---

### Secret Profits

**Rule**: Director cannot retain **secret profit** (profit made through directorship without company's consent).

**Examples**:
- Director receives commission from supplier (for awarding contract to supplier) → Secret profit (must disgorge to company)
- Director receives shares from acquirer (as inducement to support merger) → Secret profit (unless disclosed + approved by board)

**Remedy**: Company can:
- Sue director for **account of profits** (director must pay profit to company)
- **Rescind contract** (if secret commission/bribe tainted transaction)

## 7. Business Judgment Rule (Expanded)

### Application in India

**Indian Courts** recognize business judgment rule, but less developed than US Delaware law.

**Key Cases**:

**Tata Consultancy Services v. State of Andhra Pradesh (2004)**
- Supreme Court held: Courts should not interfere with **commercial decisions** of companies/directors
- Directors have discretion to make business decisions (courts not substitute their judgment)

**Principles**:
1. Directors are elected by shareholders to manage company (expertise presumed)
2. Courts lack business expertise (should not second-guess commercial decisions)
3. Risk-taking inherent in business (directors not liable for honest errors)

**Limitations**:
- Business judgment rule does NOT protect:
  - Fraud, self-dealing, bad faith
  - Gross negligence (reckless decisions, no reasonable basis)
  - Conflict of interest (directors personally benefited)
  - Oppression of minority shareholders

## 8. Director Liability and Penalties

### Personal Liability Scenarios

#### a. Section 339: Fraudulent Conduct

**Liability**: If company's business conducted with **intent to defraud creditors/members**, directors **personally liable** for company's debts.

**Elements**:
1. Company's business conducted fraudulently
2. Director **knew** OR ought to have known fraudulent conduct
3. Director actively participated OR turned blind eye

**Penalty**:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to 10 years
- **Fine**: ₹20 lakh - ₹300 crore (or 3 times defrauded amount, whichever higher)
- **Personal Liability**: Director liable for company's debts (corporate veil lifted)

**Example**:
- Company takes deposits from public (promising high returns)
- Company uses deposits to fund promoters' personal expenses (Ponzi scheme)
- Company insolvent, depositors unpaid
- **Directors personally liable** for depositors' losses (fraud u/s 339)

---

#### b. Section 447: Fraud

**Definition** (Section 447): Fraud includes any act, omission, concealment of fact, abuse of position:
- With intent to deceive company, members, creditors, public
- To gain undue advantage
- To injure interests of company, members, creditors, public

**Penalty**:
- **Imprisonment**: 6 months - 10 years
- **Fine**: ≥₹50 lakh (may extend to 3 times profit OR loss caused)
- **Disqualification**: Disqualified as director for 5 years (Section 164)

**Examples of Fraud**:
- Falsifying financial statements (inflating assets, concealing liabilities)
- Siphoning company funds (related party transactions at unfair prices)
- Insider trading (trading on UPSI - Unpublished Price Sensitive Information)

---

#### c. Criminal Liability (Other Offenses)

**Section 628**: Misapplication of company's property
- Director uses company property/money for personal benefit
- Imprisonment: Up to 5 years + fine

**Section 629**: Retaining wrongful gains
- Director retains property obtained wrongfully
- Imprisonment: Up to 2 years + fine

**IPC Offenses**: Directors can be prosecuted under:
- **Section 405-409 IPC**: Criminal breach of trust (director misappropriates company assets)
- **Section 420 IPC**: Cheating (director defrauds company/shareholders)
- **Section 409 IPC**: Criminal breach of trust by public servant/agent (applicable to directors)

---

### Vicarious Liability (Officer in Default)

**Concept**: If company commits offense (e.g., non-filing of returns), **every officer in default** liable.

**Officer in Default** (Section 2(60)) includes:
- Managing director, whole-time director
- Director charged with responsibility for compliance
- Any director who knowingly permitted non-compliance

**Example**:
- Company fails to file annual return (Section 92)
- **Company penalty**: ₹100/day (max ₹5 lakh)
- **Every officer in default penalty**: ₹100/day (max ₹5 lakh)
- Typically: MD, CEO, CFO, Company Secretary liable (if they were responsible for filing)

**Defense**: Officer can avoid liability if:
- They did not know about default (lacked knowledge)
- They exercised due diligence to prevent default
- Default occurred despite their efforts

## 9. Lifting of Corporate Veil

### General Principle: Limited Liability

**Salomon v. Salomon & Co (1897)**: Company is separate legal entity. Shareholders/directors enjoy **limited liability** (not personally liable for company's debts).

**Corporate Veil**: Legal separation between company and its members/directors.

---

### When Veil Lifted (Directors Personally Liable)

**Statutory Provisions**:

**1. Section 339 (Fraudulent Trading)**:
- If business conducted to defraud creditors → Directors personally liable

**2. Section 7(7) (Holding-Subsidiary Relationship)**:
- Holding company liable for subsidiary's debts (in certain circumstances)

**3. Section 399 (Oppression and Mismanagement)**:
- NCLT can lift veil if directors acted oppressively

**Judicial Grounds** (Common Law):

**a. Fraud or Improper Conduct**:
- Company used as façade to commit fraud
- Example: Director forms company to evade tax, conducts illegal business
- Court lifts veil, holds director personally liable

**b. Agency**:
- Company acts as agent of director/parent company
- Example: Subsidiary has no independent existence, merely acts as parent's agent
- Parent company liable for subsidiary's acts

**c. Group Enterprise**:
- Multiple companies in group treated as single economic entity
- Veil lifted to prevent corporate structure abuse

**d. Evasion of Legal Obligations**:
- Company formed to evade legal obligations (tax, contractual, statutory)
- Court lifts veil, looks at substance over form

**Landmark Case: Dinshaw Maneckjee Petit (1927)**
**Facts**: Shareholder held majority shares in multiple companies. Treated companies as personal property, intermingled funds.
**Held**: Corporate veil lifted. Shareholder personally liable for companies' debts (companies used as alter ego).

## 10. Directors & Officers (D&O) Insurance

### Purpose

**D&O Insurance** protects directors/officers from **personal liability** for acts done in capacity as director/officer.

**Coverage**:
- Legal defense costs (lawyers, court fees)
- Settlements, judgments (if director held liable)
- Investigation costs (regulatory inquiries, shareholder suits)

**Exclusions** (Typically):
- **Fraud**: Intentional fraud, dishonesty not covered
- **Personal Gain**: Claims arising from director's personal profit
- **Criminal Penalties**: Fines, penalties for criminal acts not covered
- **Known Claims**: Claims known at policy inception

---

### Why D&O Insurance Important

**High-Risk Areas for Director Liability**:
- Securities litigation (shareholder class actions for stock price drop)
- Regulatory investigations (SEBI, ROC, CCI, RBI)
- Employment disputes (wrongful termination, discrimination)
- M&A disputes (breach of fiduciary duty in sale/merger)
- Bankruptcy (wrongful trading, preference payments)

**Cost of Defense**: ₹50 lakh - ₹5 crore+ (even if director ultimately not liable)

**D&O Insurance**: Transfers this risk to insurer (company pays premium, insurer covers director's defense costs + liability).

---

### Section 197(13): Company Can Indemnify Directors

**Section 197(13)**: Company can purchase D&O insurance OR indemnify directors for liability incurred in discharge of duty (except fraud, willful default).

**Indemnification Clauses** (in AOA):
- Company indemnifies directors to fullest extent permitted by law
- Covers: Legal costs, settlements, judgments (for acts in good faith)
- Excludes: Fraud, willful misconduct, acts causing loss to company

## Best Practices for Directors

### Risk Mitigation

✅ **Informed Decisions**: Review board materials thoroughly, ask questions, seek expert advice (lawyers, CAs, consultants)
✅ **Board Minutes**: Ensure board minutes accurately reflect deliberations, dissents (if director disagrees with decision, record dissent)
✅ **Disclosure**: Disclose all conflicts of interest (Form MBP-1), abstain from voting on conflicted transactions
✅ **Attendance**: Attend board meetings regularly (≥75% attendance benchmark), participate actively
✅ **D&O Insurance**: Ensure company has adequate D&O insurance (coverage ≥₹50 crore for listed companies)
✅ **Compliance**: Monitor compliance (annual return, financial statements, tax filings, regulatory approvals)
✅ **Red Flags**: Raise concerns about red flags (deteriorating financials, related party transactions, non-compliance)

---

### When to Dissent

**Director Should Dissent If**:
- Decision violates law, regulations, AOA
- Decision involves conflict of interest (self-dealing by other directors)
- Decision lacks reasonable basis (arbitrary, irrational)
- Insufficient information provided (board asked to decide without adequate materials)

**How to Dissent**:
- State dissent **during board meeting** (voice dissent, request recorded in minutes)
- If not present at meeting, send written dissent within 7 days of receiving minutes
- Consider resigning if repeated dissents ignored (continuing as director may imply acquiescence)

---

### When to Seek Legal Advice

**Seek Legal Advice Before**:
- Major transactions (M&A, capital raising, asset sales >10% of company's assets)
- Related party transactions (involving directors, promoters, their relatives)
- Regulatory investigations (SEBI, RBI, CCI, ROC)
- Shareholder disputes (oppression claims, derivative suits)
- Material non-compliance (missed filings, tax defaults)

---

## Conclusion

Directors owe comprehensive **fiduciary duties** to company - duty of loyalty, duty of care, duty to act in good faith, duty to avoid conflicts.

**Key Takeaways**:

1. ✅ **Section 166**: Codifies fiduciary duties (good faith, care/skill/diligence, avoid conflicts, no undue gain)
2. ✅ **Business Judgment Rule**: Protects directors for honest errors IF decision made in good faith, on informed basis, without conflict
3. ✅ **No-Profit Rule**: Directors cannot retain profit made through directorship (Regal Hastings case)
4. ✅ **Disclosure**: Mandatory disclosure of interests (Section 184), non-interested directors approve transactions
5. ✅ **Personal Liability**: Directors personally liable for fraud (Section 339), criminal breach of trust, wrongful trading
6. ✅ **Lifting Veil**: Corporate veil lifted for fraud, improper conduct, evasion of legal obligations
7. ✅ **D&O Insurance**: Essential risk mitigation (covers legal costs, settlements for acts in good faith)

**Strategic Advice**:

- **For Directors**: Attend meetings, stay informed, disclose conflicts, dissent when appropriate, ensure D&O insurance, seek legal advice for major decisions
- **For Companies**: Conduct board training (fiduciary duties, compliance), maintain D&O insurance (≥₹50 crore for listed), document board deliberations thoroughly
- **For Shareholders**: Monitor director performance (attendance, related party transactions), use oppression remedy (Section 241) if directors breach duties
