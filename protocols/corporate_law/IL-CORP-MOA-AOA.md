## Overview

**Memorandum of Association (MOA)** and **Articles of Association (AOA)** are the constitutional documents of a company.

**Legal Status**:
- **MOA**: Company's charter, defines scope of activities (Section 4)
- **AOA**: Company's internal regulations, defines management rules (Section 5)
- **Relationship**: MOA is superior to AOA. In case of conflict, MOA prevails.

**Statutory Requirement**:
- Companies Act 2013, Section 4-7
- Companies (Incorporation) Rules 2014
- Schedule I: Model MOA/AOA forms

---

## Memorandum of Association (MOA)

### Definition (Section 4)

**MOA** = Memorandum of Association subscribed by persons forming company, containing:
1. Name Clause
2. Registered Office Clause
3. Objects Clause
4. Liability Clause
5. Capital Clause
6. Association/Subscription Clause

**Purpose**: Defines the **scope** of company's activities. Company cannot act beyond MOA (ultra vires).

---

### 1. Name Clause (Section 4(1)(a))

**Content**:
- Company name followed by "Limited" or "Private Limited" or "OPC"
- Example: "ABC Technologies Private Limited"

**Legal Requirements**:
- Must include suffix based on company type:
  - Public Company: "Limited"
  - Private Company: "Private Limited"
  - OPC: "One Person Company" or "OPC"
  - Section 8 Company: Can omit "Limited" if Central Government license obtained

**Restrictions** (Rule 8):
- Cannot be identical/similar to existing company name
- Cannot suggest government patronage without approval (National, Government, President, etc.)
- Cannot violate trademarks
- Cannot contain obscene, illegal, communal words

**Alteration** (Section 13):
- Special resolution in general meeting
- ROC approval required
- Form INC-24 (within 30 days of special resolution)
- **Grounds**: Name change for rebranding, trademark conflict resolution, government direction

---

### 2. Registered Office Clause (Section 4(1)(b))

**Content**:
- State where registered office is situated
- Example: "The registered office of the company will be situated in the State of Delhi"

**Purpose**:
- Determines ROC jurisdiction (e.g., Delhi ROC for Delhi companies)
- Determines tax jurisdiction
- Venue for service of legal notices

**Actual Address**:
- Complete registered office address filed separately in Form INC-22 (within 30 days of incorporation)
- Must have proof of ownership (rent agreement/sale deed) + utility bill

**Change of Registered Office** (Section 12):
- **Within Same City**: Board resolution + Form INC-22 (within 30 days)
- **Outside City but Same State**: Board resolution + Special resolution + Form INC-22
- **Outside State**: Special resolution + Central Government approval (3-6 months) + ROC confirmation

---

### 3. Objects Clause (Section 4(1)(c))

**Content**:
- **Main Objects**: Primary business activities
- **Objects Incidental to Main Objects**: Ancillary activities necessary for main objects
- **Other Objects**: Additional business activities company may undertake in future

**Example**:
```
MAIN OBJECTS:
1. To carry on business of software development, web development, mobile application development.
2. To provide IT consulting, system integration, digital transformation services.

OBJECTS INCIDENTAL TO MAIN OBJECTS:
1. To acquire, purchase, or lease computer hardware, software, intellectual property.
2. To enter into partnerships, joint ventures with other companies for software projects.

OTHER OBJECTS:
1. To carry on business of e-commerce, online marketplace, digital payments.
2. To provide cloud computing, data analytics, artificial intelligence services.
```

**Significance**:
- Company can ONLY undertake activities listed in objects clause
- Any act beyond objects = **Ultra Vires** (void, cannot be ratified even by unanimous shareholder approval)

**Alteration** (Section 13):
- Special resolution in general meeting
- ROC filing: Form MGT-14 (within 30 days)
- **Grounds**: Expansion to new business, abandoning old business, restructuring

---

### 4. Liability Clause (Section 4(1)(d))

**Content**:
- Statement that liability of members is limited
- **Limited by Shares**: "The liability of the members is limited to the unpaid amount on shares held by them."
- **Limited by Guarantee**: "The liability of the members is limited to the amount they have undertaken to contribute to the assets of the company in the event of winding up."

**Types of Companies**:
1. **Company Limited by Shares** (Most Common):
   - Member liability = Unpaid amount on shares
   - Example: If shareholder holds 100 shares of ₹10 each (₹1,000 total), paid ₹600, liability = ₹400 (unpaid portion)

2. **Company Limited by Guarantee** (NGOs, Clubs, Associations):
   - No share capital
   - Member liability = Guarantee amount (typically ₹100 - ₹10,000 per member)
   - Used for Section 8 companies (non-profit)

3. **Unlimited Company** (Rare):
   - No limit on member liability (members liable for all company debts)
   - Rarely used (only 0.01% of companies)

**Alteration**: Cannot alter liability clause (except converting unlimited → limited via special procedure - Section 18)

---

### 5. Capital Clause (Section 4(1)(e))

**Content**:
- **Authorized Share Capital**: Maximum capital company is authorized to raise
- **Division**: Number of shares, face value per share

**Example**:
```
The Authorized Share Capital of the company is ₹10,00,000 (Rupees Ten Lakh Only) divided into 1,00,000 (One Lakh) equity shares of ₹10 (Rupees Ten) each.
```

**Key Terms**:
- **Authorized Capital**: Maximum capital (as per MOA)
- **Issued Capital**: Capital issued to shareholders (≤ Authorized Capital)
- **Subscribed Capital**: Capital subscribed by shareholders (≤ Issued Capital)
- **Paid-up Capital**: Actual amount paid by shareholders (≤ Subscribed Capital)

**Alteration** (Section 61):
- **Increase Authorized Capital**: Ordinary resolution (simple majority) + Form SH-7
- **Decrease Authorized Capital**: Not allowed (must do capital reduction u/s 66 via NCLT)
- **Stamp Duty**: Payable on increase (state-wise variation)

---

### 6. Association/Subscription Clause (Section 4(1)(f))

**Content**:
- Declaration by subscribers (initial shareholders) stating:
  - Name, father's name, occupation, address of each subscriber
  - Number of shares subscribed by each
  - Signature of each subscriber
  - Witness name, signature, address

**Minimum Subscribers**:
- Private Company: 2 subscribers
- Public Company: 7 subscribers
- OPC: 1 subscriber

**Example**:
```
We, the several persons whose names and addresses are subscribed below, are desirous of being formed into a company in pursuance of this Memorandum of Association and we respectively agree to take the number of shares in the capital of the company set opposite our respective names.

Name         | Father's Name | Address           | Occupation | Shares | Signature
-------------|---------------|-------------------|------------|--------|----------
Amit Sharma  | Raj Sharma    | Delhi             | Engineer   | 50     | [Signature]
Priya Gupta  | Suresh Gupta  | Mumbai            | CA         | 50     | [Signature]

Witness:
Name: Ramesh Kumar
Address: Bangalore
Signature: [Signature]
```

**Legal Effect**:
- Subscribers become first shareholders on incorporation
- Subscribers must pay for shares subscribed (cannot be unpaid)
- Subscribers sign MOA in presence of witness

## Articles of Association (AOA)

### Definition (Section 5)

**AOA** = Articles of Association containing regulations for management of company, including:
1. Share capital and variation of rights
2. Issue of share certificates, transfer and transmission of shares
3. Alteration of capital
4. Calls on shares, forfeiture of shares
5. Conversion of shares into stock
6. Directors: Number, appointment, retirement, removal, powers
7. Board meetings: Quorum, notice, voting, minutes
8. General meetings: AGM, EGM, notice, quorum, voting (show of hands, poll, proxy)
9. Dividends: Declaration, payment
10. Accounts, audit
11. Winding up
12. Borrowing powers

**Purpose**: Defines **internal management** and relationships between company, shareholders, directors.

---

### Model AOA vs Customized AOA

#### Model AOA (Table F, Schedule I)

**Section 5(2)**: If company limited by shares, can adopt Model AOA (Table F) in its entirety or with modifications.

**Table F**: Standard AOA prescribed by MCA for companies limited by shares
- **Advantage**: Simple, no need to draft from scratch, MCA-approved
- **Disadvantage**: Generic, may not suit specific needs (investor rights, special voting, ESOP, etc.)

**When to Use Model AOA**:
✅ Small company, 2-3 shareholders, no external investors
✅ Standard shareholding structure (equal rights)
✅ No special provisions needed (tag-along, drag-along, anti-dilution)

---

#### Customized AOA

**When Required**:
✅ Startup planning to raise funding (investor rights clauses needed)
✅ Multiple shareholder classes (equity, preference, DVR shares)
✅ Special voting rights, reserved matters (board composition, exit rights)
✅ ESOP schemes

**Key Customizations**:
1. **Board Composition**: Investor nomination rights (e.g., "Series A investor entitled to 1 board seat")
2. **Reserved Matters**: Matters requiring investor approval (e.g., "Merger/acquisition requires 75% shareholder approval including Series A investor")
3. **Pre-emptive Rights**: Right of first refusal on new share issuance
4. **Tag-Along / Drag-Along**: Exit rights for minority/majority shareholders
5. **Anti-Dilution**: Protection against dilution in down-rounds
6. **Dividend Policy**: Preference share dividend priority
7. **ESOP**: ESOP pool allocation, vesting, exercise

---

### Key AOA Clauses

#### 1. Share Capital & Variation of Rights

**Initial Share Capital**:
- Number of shares, face value, issued capital, paid-up capital
- Example: "The authorized share capital is ₹10 lakh divided into 1 lakh equity shares of ₹10 each."

**Variation of Rights** (Section 48):
- Procedure to vary rights attached to shares (voting, dividend, redemption)
- Requires: 75% approval of affected class + special resolution of company

**Example**:
```
Article 10: Variation of Rights
The rights attached to any class of shares may be varied with the consent in writing of holders of 3/4th of the issued shares of that class or with the sanction of a special resolution passed at a separate general meeting of the holders of shares of that class.
```

---

#### 2. Transfer & Transmission of Shares

**Transfer of Shares** (Section 56):
- Voluntary transfer by shareholder (sale, gift)
- Requires share transfer form (SH-4) + stamp duty
- Board approval required if AOA imposes restrictions (private companies often restrict transfers)

**Transmission of Shares**:
- Involuntary transfer by operation of law (death, insolvency, court order)
- Nominee/legal heir submits death certificate + succession certificate/will
- Board registers transmission (no approval needed unless AOA restricts)

**Restrictions on Transfer** (Common in Private Companies):
```
Article 15: Right of First Refusal
No shareholder shall transfer shares without first offering them to existing shareholders pro rata to their shareholding. Existing shareholders have 30 days to accept offer. If not accepted, shareholder may transfer to third party with board approval.
```

---

#### 3. Directors: Appointment, Retirement, Removal

**Number of Directors** (Section 149):
- Private Company: Minimum 2
- Public Company: Minimum 3
- Maximum: 15 (can be increased by special resolution up to any number)

**Appointment**:
- First directors: Named in MOA/AOA
- Subsequent directors: Appointed in general meeting (ordinary resolution)
- Casual vacancy: Board can appoint director (holds office till next AGM)

**Retirement by Rotation** (Section 152):
- Public companies: 2/3rd directors retire by rotation (every 3 years)
- Private companies: Can opt out of retirement by rotation (if AOA excludes Section 152)

**Removal** (Section 169):
- Ordinary resolution in general meeting (simple majority)
- Special notice (14 days before meeting) to company
- Director has right to be heard

**Example AOA Clause**:
```
Article 25: Appointment of Directors
The company shall have minimum 2 and maximum 15 directors. Directors shall be appointed by ordinary resolution in general meeting. In case of casual vacancy (death, resignation), remaining directors may appoint a director who shall hold office till the next Annual General Meeting.
```

---

#### 4. Board Meetings

**Quorum** (Section 174):
- 1/3rd of total directors OR 2 directors, whichever is higher
- Example: If 6 directors, quorum = 2 (1/3rd of 6 = 2)

**Notice** (Section 173):
- 7 days notice (can be shorter with consent)
- Agenda circulated
- Video conferencing allowed (except for sensitive matters like approval of annual accounts, audit report)

**Voting**:
- One director, one vote
- Chairman has casting vote (second vote) in case of tie

**Example AOA Clause**:
```
Article 30: Board Meeting Quorum
The quorum for board meeting shall be 1/3rd of total strength or 2 directors, whichever is higher. If quorum not present within 30 minutes of scheduled time, meeting stands adjourned to same day next week at same time and place.
```

---

#### 5. General Meetings

**Annual General Meeting (AGM)** (Section 96):
- Mandatory for all companies (except private companies where all shareholders are directors)
- Within 6 months of financial year end (first AGM within 9 months of first FY end)

**Extraordinary General Meeting (EGM)** (Section 100):
- Called by board for urgent matters (capital increase, alteration of AOA, director removal)
- 21 days notice required (can be shorter with 95% consent)

**Quorum** (Section 103):
- Private Company: 2 members personally present
- Public Company: 5 members personally present
- If quorum not present, meeting adjourned

**Voting**:
- **Show of Hands**: One member, one vote (irrespective of shares held) - Default method
- **Poll**: One share, one vote (proportional to shareholding) - Demanded by 10% shareholders OR 5 members

**Proxy** (Section 105):
- Shareholder can appoint proxy to attend and vote on their behalf
- Proxy must file proxy form 48 hours before meeting
- Proxy cannot speak at meeting (only vote)

**Example AOA Clause**:
```
Article 50: General Meeting Quorum
For Private Company: 2 members personally present shall constitute quorum.
For Public Company: 5 members personally present shall constitute quorum.
If quorum not present within 30 minutes, meeting stands adjourned to same day next week at same time and place.
```

---

#### 6. Dividends

**Declaration** (Section 123):
- Dividend declared in AGM on board recommendation
- Cannot exceed amount recommended by board
- Paid only out of profits (current year profit OR accumulated reserves)

**Interim Dividend**:
- Board can declare interim dividend between two AGMs
- Paid out of current year profits or reserves

**Preference Dividend Priority**:
- Preference shares: Fixed dividend (e.g., 8% per annum)
- Equity shares: Variable dividend (after preference dividend paid)

**Example AOA Clause**:
```
Article 60: Dividend Declaration
The company in general meeting may declare dividends, but no dividend shall exceed the amount recommended by the board. The board may declare interim dividends. All dividends shall be paid in proportion to the amount paid-up on each share.
```

### Alteration of AOA (Section 14)

**Procedure**:
1. **Special Resolution**: 75% majority in general meeting
2. **Form MGT-14**: Filed with ROC within 30 days of special resolution
3. **Fee**: Based on authorized capital (₹200 - ₹2,000)

**Restrictions on Alteration**:
❌ Cannot alter to increase shareholder liability without consent
❌ Cannot alter to convert public company to private company without NCLT approval (Section 14(2))
❌ Cannot alter to violate Companies Act provisions (e.g., cannot reduce quorum below statutory minimum)

**Situations Requiring Alteration**:
✅ Adding new business objects (main objects in MOA)
✅ Changing share transfer restrictions (ROFR → free transferability)
✅ Modifying board composition (increase max directors from 10 to 15)
✅ Introducing ESOP clauses
✅ Adding investor protection clauses (tag/drag, anti-dilution)

## Conflicts Between MOA and AOA

### Rule: MOA Prevails

**Legal Principle**: If MOA and AOA conflict, **MOA prevails** (MOA is superior to AOA).

**Rationale**:
- MOA defines scope (what company can do)
- AOA defines procedure (how company does it)
- Scope > Procedure

**Example Conflict**:
- **MOA**: "Company can carry on business of software development"
- **AOA**: "Company can invest in real estate"
- **Result**: Company CANNOT invest in real estate (MOA doesn't permit, AOA cannot override)

**Remedy**: Alter MOA first (add real estate to objects clause), then alter AOA.

---

### Doctrine of Constructive Notice

**Principle**: Anyone dealing with company is deemed to have notice of MOA and AOA (public documents filed with ROC).

**Legal Effect**:
- Third parties cannot claim ignorance of MOA/AOA restrictions
- If company acts ultra vires (beyond MOA objects), third party contract is void
- Example: If MOA doesn't allow borrowing, loan taken from bank is void (bank deemed to have constructive notice)

**Case Law**: **Royal British Bank v. Turquand (1856)**
- Exception to constructive notice: **Indoor Management Rule**
- Third parties can assume company's internal procedures (board resolutions, shareholder approvals) were properly followed
- Need not verify minutes, resolutions (only MOA/AOA on public record)

## Ultra Vires Doctrine

### Definition

**Ultra Vires** = Beyond powers (Latin)

**Types**:
1. **Ultra Vires the Company** (beyond MOA objects)
2. **Ultra Vires the Directors** (beyond board powers per AOA)
3. **Ultra Vires the Act** (beyond Companies Act provisions)

---

### 1. Ultra Vires the Company (Beyond MOA Objects)

**Rule**: Any act beyond MOA objects clause is **void ab initio** (void from the beginning).

**Legal Effects**:
❌ Act cannot be ratified even by unanimous shareholder approval
❌ Contract is void, unenforceable
❌ Company cannot sue or be sued on ultra vires contract
❌ Directors personally liable for ultra vires acts if they knew/should have known

**Landmark Case: Ashbury Railway Carriage v. Riche (1875)**

**Facts**:
- Ashbury Railway Carriage Company's MOA objects: "To make and sell railway carriages and wagons"
- Company entered contract to finance construction of railway line in Belgium
- Shareholders later passed resolution ratifying the contract
- Company refused to perform contract, arguing it was ultra vires

**Held**:
- Contract ultra vires MOA (financing railway ≠ making railway carriages)
- Contract void, cannot be enforced
- Shareholder ratification irrelevant (even unanimous approval cannot validate ultra vires act)

**Principle**: Company cannot act beyond MOA objects. Ultra vires acts are void and cannot be ratified.

**Application**: Before entering significant contract, verify MOA objects permit the transaction.

---

**Exceptions to Ultra Vires Doctrine**:

**1. Incidental Powers** (Attorney General v. Great Eastern Railway Co):
- Company has implied powers incidental to main objects
- Example: Software company can hire employees, rent office, buy computers (incidental to software development)
- But cannot open restaurant (not incidental)

**2. Charitable Donations** (Section 181):
- Company can make charitable donations up to 5% of average net profits (last 3 years)
- Deemed incidental power (even if not in objects)

**3. Estoppel**:
- If company already performed its part of contract, it cannot later claim ultra vires
- Example: Company received goods, used them, cannot refuse payment claiming ultra vires

---

### 2. Ultra Vires the Directors (Beyond Board Powers)

**Rule**: Acts beyond board powers (per AOA) are voidable (can be ratified by shareholders).

**Example**:
- AOA: "Board can borrow up to ₹50 lakh without shareholder approval"
- Board borrows ₹1 crore (beyond authority)
- Act ultra vires directors, but **not** ultra vires company (if borrowing is in MOA objects)
- **Remedy**: Shareholders can ratify in general meeting (ordinary/special resolution)

**Distinction from Ultra Vires Company**:
- Ultra vires company: Void, cannot be ratified
- Ultra vires directors: Voidable, can be ratified by shareholders

---

### 3. Ultra Vires the Act (Beyond Companies Act)

**Rule**: Acts violating Companies Act provisions are void.

**Examples**:
❌ Allotting shares at discount (Section 53 prohibits)
❌ Giving loans to directors without shareholder approval (Section 185)
❌ Buying back shares beyond statutory limits (Section 68)

**Remedy**: None (violates statute, void ab initio)

## Practical Considerations

### MOA Drafting Tips

✅ **Broad Objects Clause**: Include main objects + incidental objects + other objects (future flexibility)
✅ **Avoid Over-Specificity**: "Software development" better than "Mobile app development for Android OS"
✅ **Include Ancillary Powers**: "To acquire IP, enter partnerships, raise capital" (avoid ultra vires issues)
✅ **Check Industrial Codes**: Ensure objects align with NIC (National Industrial Classification) codes for SPICe+ filing

❌ **Avoid**: Illegal objects (lottery, gambling), ambiguous wording, overly narrow scope

---

### AOA Drafting Tips (For Startups Raising Funding)

✅ **Investor Nomination Rights**: "Series A investors entitled to appoint 1 director"
✅ **Reserved Matters**: List of actions requiring investor approval (M&A, capex >₹1 crore, new funding rounds)
✅ **Pre-emptive Rights**: Investors have right to participate pro rata in future rounds (anti-dilution)
✅ **Tag-Along**: If founder sells shares, minority investors can "tag along" and sell proportionately
✅ **Drag-Along**: If 75% shareholders approve sale, minority must also sell (facilitates exit)
✅ **Liquidation Preference**: Preference shareholders get 1x-2x investment back before equity shareholders (in exit/liquidation)
✅ **ESOP Pool**: Reserve 10-15% shares for ESOP (attract talent)

---

### Alteration Strategy

**When to Alter MOA**:
- Expanding to new business vertical (add to objects clause)
- Increasing authorized capital (Section 61)
- Changing company name (rebranding, trademark conflict)

**When to Alter AOA**:
- Raising funding (add investor protection clauses)
- Introducing ESOP (add ESOP clauses)
- Modifying board size (increase max directors)
- Changing share transfer restrictions (ROFR → free transferability post-listing)

**Alteration Costs**:
- Special resolution in general meeting: ₹5,000 - ₹10,000 (convening meeting, CA/CS fees)
- ROC filing (Form MGT-14): ₹200 - ₹2,000 (based on authorized capital)
- Stamp duty (for capital increase): State-wise variation (₹1,000 - ₹5,000 per ₹1 lakh increase)

## Conclusion

**MOA** and **AOA** are constitutional documents defining company's scope (MOA) and internal management (AOA).

**Key Takeaways**:
1. ✅ MOA superior to AOA (in case of conflict, MOA prevails)
2. ✅ Ultra vires doctrine: Acts beyond MOA objects are void
3. ✅ Customized AOA essential for startups raising funding (investor rights, tag/drag, anti-dilution)
4. ✅ Alteration requires special resolution (75% majority) + ROC filing

**Strategic Importance**:
- **For Founders**: Broad MOA objects (flexibility), clear AOA governance (avoid disputes)
- **For Investors**: Protective AOA clauses (board seats, reserved matters, exit rights)
- **For Public Companies**: Strict adherence to statutory AOA provisions (retirement by rotation, AGM, dividends)
