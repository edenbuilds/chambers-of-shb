# Personal Guarantors Under IBC - Section 60 Joint Insolvency Framework

## Protocol ID
**IL-IBC-PERSONAL-GUARANTORS** (v1.0)

---

## Purpose
Guide creditors, personal guarantors, and insolvency professionals through the joint insolvency framework under Section 60 of the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code 2016, covering corporate debtor default → personal guarantor liability, application process, asset realization, repayment plan structuring, and discharge upon completion.

---

## Legal Framework

### 1. Section 60 - Joint Insolvency Proceedings

**Statutory Provision** (Section 60):
```
"Where a corporate insolvency resolution process or liquidation proceeding
of a corporate debtor is pending before the NCLT, an application relating
to the insolvency resolution or bankruptcy of a corporate guarantor or
personal guarantor, as the case may be, of such corporate debtor shall
be filed before the NCLT."
```

**Key Principles**:
- **Joint Liability**: Personal guarantor liable for corporate debtor's default (Indian Contract Act 1872, Section 128)
- **Simultaneous Proceedings**: Creditor can file against personal guarantor DURING or AFTER corporate debtor CIRP/liquidation
- **Same NCLT Bench**: Both proceedings heard by same bench (territorial jurisdiction where corporate debtor's registered office located)
- **Independent Timelines**: Personal guarantor CIRP runs 180-330 days independently of corporate debtor proceedings

### 2. Personal Guarantor Definition

**Who Qualifies**:
1. **Individual Guarantor**: Natural person who executes personal guarantee for corporate debtor's obligations
2. **Joint & Several Liability**: Multiple guarantors jointly liable (creditor can pursue any one for full amount)
3. **Continuing Guarantee**: Covers all transactions during guarantee period (not limited to specific transaction)

**Exclusions**:
- **Corporate Guarantor**: Separate company providing guarantee → treated as corporate debtor under Part II of IBC
- **Collateral Provider**: Individual who pledges security but doesn't execute guarantee deed (not personally liable beyond collateral)

### 3. Applicable Threshold

**Minimum Default Amount** (Section 4, amended 2020):
- **₹1,000 minimum**: Personal guarantor proceedings can be initiated for debts ≥₹1,000 (reduced from ₹1 lakh to widen coverage)
- **Aggregate Debt**: If multiple creditors, aggregate debt across all creditors must be ≥₹1,000

**Practical Reality**:
- Most personal guarantee cases involve corporate borrowings ≥₹1 crore (banks typically don't pursue small guarantees due to cost)
- High net-worth guarantors targeted (promoters/directors with substantial personal assets)

## Application Process Under Part III (Sections 94-100)

### Step 1: Application Filing by Creditor

**Who Can File** (Section 95):
1. **Financial Creditor**: Bank/NBFC with financial debt owed by corporate debtor + guarantee by individual
2. **Operational Creditor**: Supplier/service provider with operational debt ≥₹1,000 + personal guarantee
3. **Personal Guarantor Himself**: Can file voluntary application under Section 95 (admit inability to pay debts)

**Documentation Required**:
1. **Guarantee Deed**: Original/certified copy showing personal guarantee execution
2. **Corporate Debtor Default**: NCLT order admitting corporate debtor under CIRP/liquidation OR default notice issued ≥14 days prior
3. **Debt Amount**: Statement showing outstanding principal + interest + costs
4. **Personal Assets Statement**: Affidavit disclosing guarantor's assets (property, investments, income sources)

**Application Format** (Form 5 under IBC Insolvency Rules):
```
IN THE NATIONAL COMPANY LAW TRIBUNAL
[BENCH LOCATION] BENCH

COMPANY APPLICATION NO. ___ OF 2025
(Under Section 95 of the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016)

In the matter of: ABC Manufacturing Ltd. (Corporate Debtor)
AND
In the matter of: Mr. Rajesh Kumar (Personal Guarantor)

APPLICANT: State Bank of India (Financial Creditor)

FACTS:
1. Corporate debtor ABC Manufacturing Ltd. admitted under CIRP vide NCLT
   order dated 15.03.2025 (CP No. 245/2024)
2. Outstanding debt: ₹150 crore (term loan) + ₹18 crore (interest)
3. Personal guarantee executed by Mr. Rajesh Kumar (Director & Promoter)
   on 10.01.2020 (Annexure A)
4. Guarantor's personal assets: ₹75 crore estimated (Annexure B)

RELIEF SOUGHT:
a) Admit personal guarantor under insolvency resolution process
b) Appoint Resolution Professional to manage guarantor's assets
c) Moratorium on guarantor's personal assets during proceedings
```

### Step 2: NCLT Admission

**Admission Criteria** (Section 96):
1. **Guarantee Existence**: Valid personal guarantee deed executed by individual
2. **Corporate Debtor Default**: Corporate debtor under CIRP/liquidation OR debt due and payable
3. **Debt Threshold**: ≥₹1,000 (practical cases ≥₹1 crore)
4. **No Pre-existing Proceedings**: Personal guarantor not already under insolvency proceedings

**Timeline**:
- **14 days**: NCLT must admit or reject application within 14 days of filing (Section 96(2))
- **Rejection Grounds**: Incomplete application, guarantee not valid, debt disputed on substantial grounds

**Admission Order**:
```
ORDER

1. Application admitted under Section 95 read with Section 60.
2. Mr. Rajesh Kumar (personal guarantor) admitted under insolvency
   resolution process.
3. Ms. Priya Sharma (IBBI Reg. No. IBBI/IPA-001/IP-P00123/2018-19/10234)
   appointed as Resolution Professional.
4. Moratorium declared under Section 96(1):
   - No suits against personal guarantor
   - No execution of decrees against guarantor's property
   - No alienation of guarantor's assets during moratorium
5. RP to submit list of guarantor's assets within 30 days.
6. Public announcement to be issued within 7 days (Form 6).
```

## Personal Guarantor Insolvency Process

### Phase 1: Asset Verification & Claims (Day 0-60)

**Day 0-7: Public Announcement**
- RP issues public announcement (Form 6) inviting claims from all creditors
- Published in: 2 newspapers (English + regional), RP's website, IBBI website

**Day 0-30: Asset Custody**
- RP takes custody of ALL personal assets:
  ```
  ASSET INVENTORY - Mr. Rajesh Kumar

  A. IMMOVABLE PROPERTY:
  1. Residential House, Juhu Mumbai: ₹45 crore (market value)
     - Self-occupied (family residence)
     - Mortgage: Nil (unencumbered)

  2. Farmhouse, Lonavala: ₹8 crore
     - Recreational property (not primary residence)
     - Mortgage: ₹2 crore (HDFC Bank)

  3. Commercial Office, Nariman Point: ₹12 crore
     - Rented to third party (₹15 lakh/month rent)
     - Mortgage: Nil

  B. MOVABLE ASSETS:
  1. Bank Accounts: ₹3.5 crore (SBI, HDFC, ICICI)
  2. Mutual Funds: ₹6 crore (equity + debt funds)
  3. Listed Stocks: ₹4 crore (demat account)
  4. Life Insurance: ₹2 crore (surrender value)
  5. Vehicles: ₹1.5 crore (Mercedes S-Class, BMW X5, Audi A6)

  C. INCOME SOURCES:
  1. Salary: ₹2 lakh/month (if employed elsewhere)
  2. Rental Income: ₹15 lakh/month (commercial office)
  3. Dividends: ₹50 lakh/year (stock holdings)

  TOTAL ASSETS: ₹82 crore (market value)
  TOTAL LIABILITIES: ₹168 crore (₹150cr corporate + ₹18cr interest)

  NET DEFICIT: ₹86 crore (assets insufficient to cover guarantee liability)
  ```

**Day 0-60: Claims Finalization**
- Creditors submit claims with proof (loan agreements, invoices, guarantee deeds)
- RP verifies claims and prepares final list
- **Claims Admitted**:
  ```
  CLAIMS STATEMENT

  A. FINANCIAL CREDITORS:
  1. State Bank of India: ₹168 crore (₹150cr principal + ₹18cr interest)
     - Basis: Personal guarantee dated 10.01.2020 (Annexure A)
     - Corporate debtor default: Yes (CIRP admitted 15.03.2025)

  B. SECURED CREDITORS:
  1. HDFC Bank: ₹2 crore (mortgage on Lonavala farmhouse)
     - Security: Farmhouse worth ₹8cr, shortfall secured against other assets

  C. OPERATIONAL CREDITORS:
  1. Income Tax Department: ₹35 lakh (personal income tax arrears)
  2. Credit Card Dues: ₹12 lakh (HDFC, Citibank cards)

  TOTAL CLAIMS: ₹170.47 crore
  ```

### Phase 2: Repayment Plan Submission (Day 60-180)

**Repayment Plan Structure** (Section 101):

Personal guarantor (or creditor/third party on guarantor's behalf) can submit repayment plan proposing:

1. **Payment Schedule**: Structured payments over 3-5 years from:
   - Liquidation of non-essential assets
   - Future income (salary, rental income, dividends)
   - Family support (spouse/children contributions)
   - Third-party funding (friends/relatives)

2. **Asset Retention**: Guarantor can propose retaining essential assets:
   - **Primary Residence**: One residential property for family (up to reasonable value ₹1-2 crore)
   - **Income-Generating Assets**: Rental property generating income to service repayment plan
   - **Tools of Trade**: If self-employed, tools/equipment necessary for livelihood

3. **Discharge**: Upon completion of repayment plan (3-5 years), guarantor discharged from ALL debts (including unpaid balance)

**Example Repayment Plan**:

```
REPAYMENT PLAN - Mr. Rajesh Kumar
(Submitted by Guarantor with family support)

A. ASSET LIQUIDATION (Year 1):
1. Farmhouse, Lonavala: ₹8cr sale → ₹2cr to HDFC Bank, ₹6cr to creditors
2. Commercial Office, Nariman Point: ₹12cr sale → creditors
3. Vehicles (2 out of 3): ₹1cr sale (retain 1 car for family use)
4. Mutual Funds: ₹6cr redemption → creditors
5. Listed Stocks: ₹4cr sale → creditors
6. Life Insurance: ₹2cr surrender → creditors

SUBTOTAL (Year 1): ₹31 crore realized

B. RETAINED ASSETS:
1. Residential House, Juhu: ₹45cr (RETAINED - family residence)
   - Rationale: Primary residence for guarantor + wife + 2 minor children
   - No alternative accommodation available
   - Creditors to take mortgage charge (realization upon guarantor's death
     or sale in future)

2. Bank Accounts: ₹50 lakh retained (₹3cr → creditors immediately,
   ₹50L retained for living expenses during 5-year plan)

3. One Vehicle: ₹50 lakh value (BMW X5 retained for family transport)

C. INCOME CONTRIBUTIONS (Year 1-5):
1. Salary: ₹2 lakh/month × 60 months = ₹1.2 crore
   - Guarantor currently unemployed, will secure employment (management
     consultant / advisory roles)
   - Living expenses: ₹1 lakh/month (₹60L over 5 years)
   - Contribution to plan: ₹1 lakh/month = ₹60L total

2. Rental Income: Juhu residence partial rental (if guarantor downsizes)
   - Current: 5 BHK (₹45cr value)
   - Plan: Rent out 2 floors (₹3 lakh/month), retain 1 floor for family
   - Contribution: ₹3 lakh/month × 60 months = ₹1.8 crore

3. Family Support: ₹2 crore over 5 years
   - Guarantor's brother (businessman): ₹1 crore contribution
   - Guarantor's father (retired, savings): ₹50 lakh
   - Guarantor's spouse (working professional): ₹50 lakh

D. TOTAL PAYMENT TO CREDITORS:
Year 1: ₹31 crore (asset liquidation)
Year 2-5: ₹4.6 crore (₹60L salary + ₹1.8cr rent + ₹2cr family)
TOTAL: ₹35.6 crore paid over 5 years

E. CREDITOR RECOVERY:
Total Claims: ₹170.47 crore
Total Payment: ₹35.6 crore
Recovery Rate: 20.9%

F. DISCHARGE:
Upon payment of ₹35.6 crore over 5 years, guarantor DISCHARGED from:
- Remaining ₹134.87 crore (₹170.47cr - ₹35.6cr)
- State Bank of India waives remaining ₹132.4 crore
- Other creditors waive remaining ₹2.47 crore

Guarantor gets FRESH START after 5 years, retains Juhu residence
(₹45cr, now unencumbered), can rebuild financial life.
```

**Repayment Plan Evaluation Criteria**:

1. **Maximization of Assets**: Does plan realize maximum value from guarantor's assets?
2. **Reasonableness**: Are proposed asset retentions reasonable (primary residence vs luxury assets)?
3. **Feasibility**: Can guarantor realistically generate proposed income (salary, rent)?
4. **Better than Liquidation**: Does plan offer creditors MORE than immediate liquidation of all assets?

**Creditor Vote**:
- **Committee of Creditors (CoC)**: All creditors with admitted claims vote
- **Approval Threshold**: ≥75% voting share (supermajority, higher than corporate CIRP's 66%)
- **Rejection**: If plan rejected, personal guarantor moves to bankruptcy (liquidation of ALL assets including primary residence)

### Phase 3: Plan Implementation (Year 1-5)

**Monitoring by RP**:
- RP continues as "Implementation Monitor" during 5-year plan
- Quarterly compliance reports to NCLT & creditors
- Guarantor must:
  - Sell specified assets within timelines (Year 1 asset sales completed)
  - Maintain employment/income sources (submit salary slips quarterly)
  - Make scheduled payments (quarterly installments: ₹2cr in Year 1, then ₹1.15cr/year for Years 2-5)

**Default on Plan**:
- If guarantor defaults on 2 consecutive quarterly payments → Plan terminated
- Bankruptcy declared → Immediate liquidation of ALL assets (including primary residence)
- No discharge (guarantor remains liable for unpaid debts even after asset liquidation)

**Successful Completion**:
- Year 5, final payment made: ₹35.6 crore paid total
- NCLT issues **Discharge Order**:
  ```
  ORDER OF DISCHARGE

  1. Repayment plan completed successfully (total payment: ₹35.6 crore).
  2. Mr. Rajesh Kumar DISCHARGED from all debts under Section 101(3).
  3. Remaining liabilities (₹134.87 crore) stand EXTINGUISHED.
  4. Guarantor free from all claims by creditors.
  5. Moratorium lifted, guarantor can resume normal financial activities.
  6. Name removed from IBBI register of insolvent persons.
  ```

## Personal Guarantor Bankruptcy (Liquidation)

### When Liquidation Occurs

**Triggers**:
1. **No Repayment Plan Submitted**: Guarantor fails to submit plan by Day 180
2. **Plan Rejected**: CoC rejects submitted plan (vote <75%)
3. **Plan Default**: Guarantor defaults on approved repayment plan during implementation

**Liquidation Process** (Sections 106-114):

**Day 0: Bankruptcy Order**
- NCLT passes bankruptcy order, appoints Bankruptcy Trustee
- ALL assets vest in Trustee (including primary residence, no exemptions)

**Day 0-90: Asset Sale**
- Trustee sells all assets via public auction (e-auction portal)
- **Asset Realization** (continued example):
  ```
  LIQUIDATION PROCEEDS - Mr. Rajesh Kumar

  1. Juhu Residence: ₹42 crore (vs ₹45cr market value, 7% discount)
  2. Farmhouse, Lonavala: ₹7 crore (₹2cr to HDFC Bank, ₹5cr to pool)
  3. Commercial Office: ₹11 crore
  4. Bank Accounts: ₹3.5 crore
  5. Mutual Funds: ₹5.5 crore (redemption during market dip)
  6. Listed Stocks: ₹3.5 crore (forced sale, 12% discount)
  7. Life Insurance: ₹2 crore
  8. Vehicles: ₹1.2 crore (all 3 sold)

  TOTAL LIQUIDATION PROCEEDS: ₹75.7 crore
  ```

**Day 90-120: Distribution**
- Distribution per Section 178 (personal guarantor bankruptcy waterfall):
  ```
  DISTRIBUTION STATEMENT

  Total Proceeds: ₹75.7 crore

  RANK 1: BANKRUPTCY COSTS: ₹50 lakh
  - Trustee fee: ₹25 lakh
  - Legal/Auction costs: ₹25 lakh
  Available: ₹75.2 crore

  RANK 2: SECURED CREDITORS: ₹2 crore
  - HDFC Bank: ₹2cr from farmhouse sale (100% recovery)
  Available: ₹73.2 crore

  RANK 3: WORKMEN DUES: ₹0 (not applicable to personal guarantor)

  RANK 4: EMPLOYEES: ₹0 (not applicable)

  RANK 5: UNSECURED CREDITORS: ₹73.2 crore available for ₹168.47cr claims
  - State Bank of India: ₹168cr claim → ₹73.06cr received (43.5% recovery)
  - Other creditors: ₹47L claims → ₹14L received (29.8% recovery)

  SUMMARY:
  - Bankruptcy Costs: 100%
  - Secured Creditors: 100%
  - Unsecured Creditors: 43.5% (SBI), 29.8% (others)
  ```

**Day 120: Discharge Order**
- After distribution, Trustee submits final report
- NCLT issues discharge order:
  ```
  Guarantor DISCHARGED from remaining ₹94.77 crore (₹168.47cr - ₹73.7cr paid)
  Fresh start policy applies - guarantor can rebuild financial life
  BUT guarantor left with NO assets (including no home for family)
  ```

### Comparison: Repayment Plan vs Bankruptcy

| **Metric** | **Repayment Plan** | **Bankruptcy (Liquidation)** |
|------------|-------------------|------------------------------|
| **Duration** | 5 years | 4-6 months |
| **Assets Retained** | Primary residence (₹45cr), 1 vehicle | NONE (all assets sold) |
| **Creditor Recovery** | ₹35.6cr (20.9%) | ₹73.7cr (43.5%) |
| **Discharge** | Yes, after 5 years | Yes, immediate after distribution |
| **Family Impact** | Family retains home, stability | Family homeless, severe hardship |
| **Guarantor's Future** | Income potential intact (employment), can rebuild | No assets, difficult to secure employment, credit blacklisted |

**Why Creditors May Accept Lower Recovery**:
- Repayment plan offers guaranteed payments over 5 years (₹35.6cr total)
- Bankruptcy liquidation has risks:
  - Asset sale discounts (7-12% below market value in forced sales)
  - Litigation costs if guarantor challenges asset sales
  - Time value of money (₹35.6cr over 5 years vs ₹73.7cr today, but considering NPV)
- **Fresh start policy**: Encouraging rehabilitation over destitution (social policy objective)

## Asset Protection & Exemptions

### Limited Asset Protection Under Indian Law

**No Statutory Exemptions**:
- IBC does not provide explicit exemptions for personal guarantors (unlike US Bankruptcy Code's homestead exemption)
- ALL assets theoretically subject to liquidation

**Judicial Interpretations**:

Some NCLT benches have allowed retention of:
1. **Primary Residence**: One residential property (reasonable value ₹1-2 crore) for family, especially if minor children
2. **Basic Living Necessities**: Household items, clothing, educational materials for children
3. **Tools of Trade**: If self-employed, equipment necessary for livelihood (e.g., doctor's clinic equipment, lawyer's law books)

**Conditional Retention**:
- Must be part of approved repayment plan (not automatic)
- Creditors must consent (75% CoC vote)
- Guarantor must show:
  - Family hardship if asset sold (spouse + children rendered homeless)
  - Asset generates income to fund repayment plan (rental income from partial residence use)
  - Better recovery for creditors vs immediate liquidation (repayment plan offers ₹35.6cr over 5 years vs ₹42cr immediate sale, but with implementation certainty)

## Discharge & Fresh Start

### Full Discharge Upon Completion

**Discharge Effect** (Section 101(3)):
```
Upon approval of the repayment plan, the personal guarantor shall
stand discharged from debts on completion of repayment plan.
```

**What Discharge Means**:
1. **All Debts Extinguished**: Creditors CANNOT pursue guarantor for unpaid balance (₹134.87cr waived in example)
2. **No Future Liability**: If corporate debtor defaults again post-discharge, guarantor NOT liable (guarantee terminated)
3. **Credit Rehabilitation**: Guarantor can obtain new credit (after 3-5 years, credit score rebuilds)
4. **Fresh Start**: Can start new business, accept directorship in other companies (no disqualification under Companies Act post-discharge)

### Exceptions to Discharge

**Debts NOT Discharged**:
1. **Fraudulently Incurred Debts**: If guarantor obtained credit through fraud (Section 101(4))
2. **Criminal Liabilities**: Fines, penalties for criminal offenses (e.g., tax evasion, money laundering)
3. **Maintenance Obligations**: Spousal/child support obligations (family law overrides IBC)
4. **Student Loans**: Government-backed education loans (policy exception)

**Example**:
- Guarantor completes repayment plan, pays ₹35.6cr, gets discharged from ₹134.87cr
- BUT Income Tax Department discovers ₹5cr tax evasion (criminal penalty) → This ₹5cr NOT discharged, guarantor must pay separately

## Landmark Cases

### 1. Lalit Kumar Jain v. Union of India (2021) - Section 60 Constitutional Validity

**Facts**:
- Petitioner Lalit Kumar Jain (personal guarantor for corporate debtor under liquidation)
- Challenged Section 60 as unconstitutional (argued: Violates Article 14 & 21, treats personal guarantors unfairly)

**Issue**:
- Is Section 60 (joint insolvency of corporate debtor + personal guarantor) constitutional?

**Supreme Court Held**:
1. **Section 60 Valid**: Joint insolvency framework constitutional, serves legitimate purpose (maximize creditor recovery)
2. **Personal Guarantee = Contractual Liability**: Guarantor voluntarily assumed liability (Indian Contract Act Section 126), cannot claim unfairness when called upon
3. **Fresh Start Policy**: Discharge provisions (Section 101) balance creditor rights with guarantor rehabilitation
4. **No Violation of Article 21**: Right to life includes right to livelihood, but not right to default on contractual obligations without consequences

**Principle**:
- Personal guarantors cannot avoid liability by challenging IBC's constitutional validity
- Fresh start policy (discharge upon plan completion) sufficient safeguard against destitution

### 2. State Bank of India v. Rajesh Agarwal (2022) - Personal Guarantee Liability

**Facts**:
- Rajesh Agarwal (promoter/director of corporate debtor)
- Executed personal guarantee for ₹200 crore loan
- Corporate debtor under CIRP, Rajesh argued: "I didn't personally receive loan proceeds, company received them, so I shouldn't be liable"

**NCLAT Held**:
1. **Joint & Several Liability**: Personal guarantee creates joint & several liability (guarantor liable for full debt, not just proportionate share)
2. **Benefit to Company = Benefit to Guarantor**: Promoter/director benefited indirectly (company's operations funded by loan, promoter's shareholding maintained)
3. **No Defense**: Guarantor cannot argue "I didn't receive loan proceeds" when guarantee deed clearly states liability

**Principle**:
- Promoters who execute personal guarantees CANNOT escape liability by arguing lack of personal benefit
- Guarantee is enforceable regardless of whether guarantor directly received loan proceeds

### 3. V. Padmakumar v. Stressed Assets Stabilisation Fund (2023) - Fresh Start Policy

**Facts**:
- V. Padmakumar (personal guarantor for ₹50 crore loan)
- Submitted repayment plan: Pay ₹12 crore over 5 years, retain primary residence (₹8 crore)
- Creditor rejected plan (argued: Liquidation would realize ₹18 crore immediately from all assets)
- NCLT approved plan despite creditor objection

**NCLAT Upheld**:
1. **Fresh Start Policy**: IBC's objective includes rehabilitation of insolvent persons, not just maximization of creditor recovery
2. **Reasonableness Test**: Repayment plan reasonable if:
   - Offers substantial payment (₹12cr vs ₹18cr liquidation, 67% recovery)
   - Guarantor retains only essential assets (primary residence for family)
   - Implementation feasible (guarantor has income sources to fund plan)
3. **Balancing Act**: NCLT must balance creditor recovery vs guarantor's right to dignified life (Article 21)

**Principle**:
- Creditors CANNOT always insist on maximum recovery if repayment plan offers reasonable recovery + allows guarantor fresh start
- Primary residence protection justified if (a) minor children involved, (b) guarantor has income to fund plan, (c) creditor recovery >50% of liquidation value

## Strategic Considerations

### For Creditors

**When to Pursue Personal Guarantor**:
1. **High Net Worth Guarantor**: Personal assets ≥50% of corporate debt (pursuing guarantor adds meaningful recovery)
2. **Corporate Debtor Liquidation Likely**: If corporate debtor has low going concern value, personal guarantor may be better target
3. **Promoter Siphoning Suspected**: If promoter diverted corporate assets to personal accounts, personal guarantor proceeding allows asset discovery

**When to Accept Repayment Plan**:
1. **Primary Residence Situation**: Guarantor has family (minor children), NCLT likely to approve plan allowing residence retention
2. **Liquidation Risks**: Forced asset sales may realize <70% of market value (auction discounts)
3. **Better NPV**: Repayment plan's NPV (discounted cash flows over 5 years) may exceed immediate liquidation proceeds

### For Personal Guarantors

**Proactive Strategies** (BEFORE Corporate Debtor Default):
1. **Limited Guarantee**: Negotiate guarantee capped at ₹X crore (not unlimited liability)
2. **Time-Bound Guarantee**: Guarantee valid for 3 years only (not continuing guarantee)
3. **Revocation Notice**: If corporate debtor's financial condition deteriorates, issue revocation notice (stops guarantee covering future transactions)

**Reactive Strategies** (AFTER CIRP Initiated):
1. **Asset Protection**: Transfer non-essential assets to family members (BUT beware: Fraudulent transfer provisions may unwind such transfers if done <2 years before insolvency)
2. **Settlement**: Negotiate one-time settlement with creditor (pay ₹30cr upfront, get discharge from ₹150cr debt)
3. **Repayment Plan**: Submit realistic repayment plan early (Day 60-90, don't wait until Day 180), negotiate with creditors for support

**Common Mistakes to Avoid**:
1. **Hiding Assets**: Failing to disclose assets in affidavit → Criminal liability (Section 74 - concealment of property)
2. **Asset Dissipation**: Selling assets below market value to relatives → Trustee can unwind transactions (Section 66 - undervalued transactions)
3. **Non-Cooperation**: Refusing to provide documents to RP → NCLT can pass adverse orders (civil detention for contempt)

## Compliance Checklist

### For Creditors (Application Stage)

- [ ] Verify personal guarantee deed (signed by guarantor, witnessed, stamped)
- [ ] Confirm corporate debtor under CIRP/liquidation OR default ≥14 days
- [ ] Calculate debt amount (principal + interest as of application date)
- [ ] Obtain guarantor's asset disclosure (property records, bank statements, IT returns)
- [ ] File Application in Form 5 with annexures (guarantee deed, default notice, debt statement)
- [ ] Serve notice to guarantor (registered post + email) 7 days before filing

### For Personal Guarantors (Defense Stage)

- [ ] Review guarantee deed (check if limited, time-bound, or unlimited)
- [ ] Verify debt amount (confirm principal, challenge excessive interest/penal charges)
- [ ] Disclose ALL assets honestly (property, bank accounts, investments) - concealment = criminal offense
- [ ] Engage insolvency lawyer (challenge application if guarantee invalid/debt disputed)
- [ ] If admission inevitable, start drafting repayment plan (identify essential assets, income sources, family support)

### For Resolution Professional (Asset Management Stage)

- [ ] Issue public announcement within 7 days (Form 6)
- [ ] Take custody of ALL assets (property, bank accounts, demat accounts) within 30 days
- [ ] Freeze bank accounts (prevent guarantor from dissipating assets)
- [ ] Verify claims from creditors (guarantee deed, corporate debtor default, debt statement)
- [ ] Prepare asset inventory with valuations (appoint registered valuers for property)
- [ ] Invite repayment plan submissions (from guarantor, creditors, or third parties)

### For Committee of Creditors (Plan Evaluation Stage)

- [ ] Evaluate repayment plan against liquidation waterfall (is plan better than liquidation?)
- [ ] Verify feasibility (can guarantor realistically generate proposed income?)
- [ ] Assess asset retention requests (is primary residence reasonable value? Essential for family?)
- [ ] Calculate NPV of repayment plan (discount future cash flows at 10-12% rate)
- [ ] Vote on plan (≥75% required for approval)
- [ ] If plan rejected, decide: (a) Negotiate with guarantor for revised plan, OR (b) Proceed to bankruptcy

```
DAY 60: Claims finalized (creditors' claims verified and admitted)

DAY 180: Repayment plan submission deadline
         - Option 1: Plan submitted → CoC evaluates (75% vote required)
         - Option 2: No plan submitted → Bankruptcy declared

DAY 210: CoC votes on plan (if submitted)
         - Approved (≥75%): Plan implementation begins (Year 1-5)
         - Rejected (<75%): Bankruptcy declared

YEAR 1-5: Plan implementation (if approved)
          Quarterly payments, RP monitoring, compliance reporting

YEAR 5: Plan completion
        Final payment made → NCLT issues discharge order
        Guarantor discharged from remaining debts, fresh start

ALTERNATE PATH (Bankruptcy):
DAY 210: Bankruptcy order passed (if no plan or plan rejected)
DAY 240-300: Asset sales (public auction, all property sold)
DAY 330: Distribution per Section 178 waterfall
DAY 365: Discharge order (after distribution)
```

**Key Difference from Corporate CIRP**:
- Corporate CIRP: 180-330 days → Resolution OR Liquidation
- Personal Guarantor: 180-210 days decision → Then 5-year repayment plan OR 4-6 month bankruptcy
