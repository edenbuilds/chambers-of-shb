## Overview

**Shareholder Rights** = Rights conferred on shareholders by virtue of shareholding in company.

**Sources of Rights**:
1. **Companies Act 2013**: Statutory rights (Sections 88-250)
2. **MOA & AOA**: Company-specific rights
3. **Shareholder Agreements (SHA)**: Contractual rights between shareholders
4. **SEBI Regulations**: For listed companies (takeover code, substantial acquisition)

**Key Principle**: **Majority Rule** - Company managed by majority shareholders/directors. Minority shareholders protected via statutory remedies (oppression/mismanagement, derivative actions, class actions).

## Fundamental Shareholder Rights

### 1. Right to Vote (Section 47)

**General Rule**: 1 Share = 1 Vote (for equity shares)

**Voting Entitlement**:
✅ Equity Shares: Voting rights on all resolutions
✅ Preference Shares: Generally no voting rights EXCEPT:
- Resolutions affecting their rights (class rights variation)
- Resolutions directly affecting them (winding up, capital reduction)
- If preference dividend in arrears for 2+ years (voting rights restored till arrears cleared)

❌ DVR Shares: Differential voting (lesser votes per share, e.g., 0.1 vote per share)

---

### 2. Right to Attend General Meetings (Section 105)

**Types of Meetings**:
- **AGM** (Annual General Meeting): Section 96
  - Held once a year (within 6 months of financial year end)
  - Mandatory for all companies (except private companies where all shareholders are directors)
  - Agenda: Adoption of financial statements, dividend declaration, director appointment/retirement, auditor appointment

- **EGM** (Extraordinary General Meeting): Section 100
  - Called for urgent matters (capital increase, director removal, alteration of MOA/AOA, special resolutions)
  - Convened by: Board OR Directors (on requisition by 10% shareholders OR 100 shareholders whichever lower) OR NCLT

**Participation Methods**:
✅ Personal attendance
✅ Through proxy (shareholder appoints proxy via Form MGT-11, proxy cannot speak but can vote)
✅ Through authorized representative (for body corporate shareholder)
✅ Through video conferencing (if company provides facility, prevalent post-COVID)

---

### 3. Right to Dividend (Section 123)

**Dividend Declaration**:
- Board recommends dividend amount
- Shareholders approve in AGM (cannot exceed board recommendation, can approve less)
- Paid only out of **profits** (current year OR accumulated reserves)
- Cannot pay dividend out of capital (violates capital maintenance doctrine)

**Interim Dividend**:
- Board can declare interim dividend (between two AGMs) out of current year profits
- No shareholder approval needed (board decision)

**Dividend Entitlement**:
- **Preference Shares**: Fixed dividend (e.g., 8% per annum), paid before equity dividend
- **Equity Shares**: Variable dividend (based on profitability), paid after preference dividend

**Unpaid Dividend**:
- Transferred to **Unpaid Dividend Account** within 30 days of declaration
- After 7 years, transferred to **Investor Education and Protection Fund (IEPF)**
- Shareholders can claim from IEPF (online application)

---

### 4. Pre-emptive Rights (Section 62)

**Definition**: Right of existing shareholders to subscribe to new shares **before** company offers to outsiders.

**Statutory Provision** (Section 62(1)(a)):
- New shares offered to existing equity shareholders **in proportion to their existing shareholding**
- Example: Shareholder holds 10% equity → Entitled to 10% of new shares
- Notice period: Minimum 15 days to accept offer

**Waiver**:
- Shareholders can waive pre-emptive rights via **special resolution** (75% majority)
- Example: To enable preferential allotment to strategic investor (without offering to all shareholders)

**Exceptions** (No Pre-emptive Rights):
❌ ESOP (Employee Stock Option Plan) shares
❌ Sweat equity shares
❌ Bonus shares (already distributed pro rata)
❌ Shares issued to underwriters/vendors for consideration other than cash

---

### 5. Right to Inspect Books (Section 94)

**Inspection Rights**:
✅ Register of Members (shareholder list)
✅ Register of Debenture Holders
✅ Copies of balance sheet, profit & loss account, auditor's report (filed with ROC)
✅ Minutes of general meetings (within 7 days of request)

❌ Cannot inspect: Board meeting minutes, management accounts, contracts (unless director/auditor)

**Procedure**:
- Shareholder submits written request
- Company provides inspection/copies within 7 days
- Fee: Nominal (₹50 - ₹100 per document)

---

### 6. Right to Transfer Shares (Section 44, 58)

**General Principle**: Shares are freely transferable UNLESS restrictions imposed by AOA.

**Transfer Restrictions** (Common in Private Companies):
- **AOA Clause**: "No share shall be transferred without first offering to existing shareholders (ROFR - Right of First Refusal)"
- **Board Approval**: Board can refuse transfer (if AOA permits)
- **Lock-in Period**: Shares may have lock-in (e.g., promoter shares locked for 3 years in IPO)

**Refusal of Transfer**:
- Board must communicate refusal within **30 days** (Section 58(4))
- If no communication, transfer deemed approved
- Shareholder can appeal to NCLT if refusal unfair/arbitrary

---

### 7. Right to Participate in Surplus Assets (on Winding Up)

**Distribution Priority** (Section 53 IBC, 2016):
1. **Secured Creditors**: Mortgages, charges (first claim)
2. **Workmen Dues**: Salaries/wages for 24 months (up to ₹24,000 per worker)
3. **Unsecured Creditors**: Trade creditors, unsecured loans
4. **Preference Shareholders**: Return of capital + arrears of dividend
5. **Equity Shareholders**: Residual assets (after all above paid)

**Equity Shareholder Risk**: Last in priority (may receive nothing if company insolvent)

## Voting Rights and Procedures

### Types of Resolutions

#### 1. Ordinary Resolution (Section 114)

**Majority Required**: Simple majority (>50% of votes cast)

**Matters Requiring Ordinary Resolution**:
- Adoption of financial statements
- Declaration of dividend (on board recommendation)
- Appointment of auditor
- Appointment of directors (except independent directors)
- Increase in authorized capital (Section 61)

---

#### 2. Special Resolution (Section 114)

**Majority Required**: 75% of votes cast

**Matters Requiring Special Resolution**:
- Alteration of MOA/AOA (Sections 13, 14)
- Change of company name (Section 13)
- Change of registered office (outside state - Section 12)
- Buyback of shares (Section 68)
- Issue of sweat equity shares (Section 54)
- Preferential allotment (Section 62(1)(c))
- Private placement (Section 42)
- Loans to directors (Section 185)

---

### Voting Methods

#### 1. Show of Hands (Section 107)

**Method**: Each member present (physically/proxy) gets **1 vote**, irrespective of shares held

**Use**: Default voting method in general meetings (unless poll demanded)

**Example**:
- Shareholder A: 1,000 shares (10%)
- Shareholder B: 100 shares (1%)
- Both vote on show of hands: Each gets 1 vote (equal voting despite unequal shareholding)

---

#### 2. Poll (Section 109)

**Method**: Voting proportional to shareholding (1 share = 1 vote)

**When Demanded**:
- Chairman of meeting can order poll (anytime)
- OR Demanded by:
  - 5 members present (having voting rights)
  - OR Member(s) holding 10% voting power
  - OR Member(s) holding shares with aggregate paid-up capital ≥₹5 lakh

**Example** (Same as above):
- Shareholder A: 1,000 shares × 1 vote = 1,000 votes (90.9%)
- Shareholder B: 100 shares × 1 vote = 100 votes (9.1%)
- Poll result: Reflects actual shareholding

**Strategic Use**: Minority shareholders can demand poll to prevent majority's show-of-hands dominance (if they hold ≥10% voting power or ₹5 lakh paid-up capital).

---

#### 3. Postal Ballot (Section 110)

**Method**: Voting by post/electronic mode (without physical meeting)

**Mandatory for** (Rule 22):
- Alteration of objects clause (MOA)
- Change of registered office (outside state)
- Buyback of shares
- Issue of shares with differential voting rights
- Sale of whole/substantially whole of undertaking

**Procedure**:
- Notice sent to shareholders (physical + email)
- Voting period: Minimum 30 days
- Scrutinizer appointed to count votes
- Result declared within 2 days of ballot closing

---

#### 4. E-Voting (Section 108)

**Mandatory For** (Listed Companies + Companies with ≥1,000 shareholders):
- All general meeting resolutions (AGM/EGM)
- E-voting facility open 3 days before meeting, closes at meeting start
- Shareholders vote online (NSDL/CDSL e-voting portal)

**Purpose**: Wider shareholder participation (especially small shareholders who cannot attend physically)

## Dividend Rights

### Dividend Declaration Process (Section 123)

**Step 1: Board Recommendation**
- Board recommends dividend amount (in board meeting)
- Based on profitability, cash flow, future capex needs

**Step 2: Shareholders' Approval (AGM)**
- Ordinary resolution (simple majority)
- Cannot approve more than board recommendation (can approve less)

**Step 3: Payment** (within 30 days of declaration)
- Dividend credited to shareholder's bank account (registered with company)
- Unpaid dividend (if bank details missing/incorrect) → Unpaid Dividend Account

---

### Dividend Policy Considerations

**Residual Dividend Theory**:
- Pay dividend from **residual profits** (after reinvestment needs)
- Example: Profit ₹10 lakh, capex requirement ₹6 lakh → Dividend from ₹4 lakh residual

**Dividend Yield**:
- Dividend Yield = (Dividend per share / Market price per share) × 100
- Example: Dividend ₹5, market price ₹100 → Yield 5%

**Preference vs Equity Dividend**:
1. **Preference Dividend**: Fixed (e.g., 8% of face value), paid first
2. **Equity Dividend**: Variable, paid from remaining profit

---

### Dividend Restrictions

**Section 123(1)**: Dividend can be paid only out of:
✅ Profits of current year (after depreciation)
✅ Accumulated reserves (from previous years)
✅ Money provided by Central/State Government (for dividends on shares capital provided by them)

❌ Cannot pay dividend out of:
- Capital
- Borrowed funds
- Sale of assets

**Depreciation Requirement** (Section 123(1)(a)):
- Depreciation must be provided **before** declaring dividend
- Ensures profits computed after accounting for asset wear & tear

**Example**:
- Revenue: ₹50 lakh
- Expenses: ₹30 lakh
- Depreciation: ₹5 lakh
- Profit before depreciation: ₹20 lakh
- **Profit after depreciation**: ₹15 lakh ← Dividend paid from this (not ₹20 lakh)

## Minority Shareholder Protection

### 1. Class Action Suits (Section 245)

**Definition**: Suit filed by shareholders/creditors seeking relief for company's wrongful acts.

**Who Can File**:
- ≥100 members OR
- ≥10% of total members (whichever lower) OR
- Member(s) holding ≥10% issued share capital OR ₹5 lakh paid-up capital

**Grounds for Class Action**:
- Company conducted affairs **against public interest**
- Directors acted **oppressively** against any class of shareholders/creditors
- Fraudulent/illegal acts
- Material change in company management/control affecting shareholders adversely

**Remedies Available** (Section 245(3)):
- Restrain company from committing illegal act
- Direct company to take action (to protect shareholders/creditors)
- Award damages to affected parties
- Any other order NCLT deems fit

**Example**:
- Company diverts funds to sister concern (controlled by promoters)
- Minority shareholders (holding 12% shares) file class action at NCLT
- NCLT can order: Return of diverted funds, removal of directors, appointment of independent directors

**Landmark Case: LIC Housing Finance v. Shiv Darshan CHS (2012)**
- Class action filed by flat buyers against builder
- NCLT held: Class action maintainable for consumer protection

---

### 2. Oppression and Mismanagement (Section 241-246)

**Definition**:
- **Oppression**: Acts that unfairly prejudice shareholders' interests
- **Mismanagement**: Affairs conducted in manner prejudicial to company/shareholders/public interest

**Who Can File** (Section 244):
- ≥100 members OR
- ≥10% of total members (whichever lower) OR
- Member(s) holding ≥10% issued share capital OR ₹2 lakh paid-up capital

**Grounds for Oppression** (Section 241):
- Company affairs conducted in oppressive manner against shareholders
- Material change in company management/control affecting shareholders prejudicially
- Company acted fraudulently, illegally

**Examples of Oppression**:
- Exclusion of minority director from board meetings
- Non-declaration of dividends despite profits (to starve minority shareholders)
- Diversion of company funds to promoter entities
- Refusal to register share transfer (without valid reason)
- Denial of inspection rights

**Examples of Mismanagement**:
- Company assets sold below market value (to promoter-controlled entity)
- Excessive remuneration paid to directors
- Non-compliance with statutory provisions (repeatedly)
- Company's business conducted contrary to its objects (MOA)

---

### Oppression & Mismanagement Procedure

**Step 1: Filing Petition at NCLT** (Form NCLT-1)
- Jurisdiction: NCLT where company's registered office situated
- Fee: ₹5,000 - ₹10,000
- Documents: Petition, affidavit, shareholding proof, evidence of oppression/mismanagement

**Step 2: NCLT Issues Notice**
- Notice to company, respondents (directors/majority shareholders)
- Company files reply (30 days)

**Step 3: NCLT Hearing**
- Petitioner presents case (oppression/mismanagement acts)
- Respondents defend
- NCLT examines evidence

**Step 4: NCLT Order** (Section 242)
- If oppression/mismanagement proved, NCLT can order:

**Remedies**:
1. **Regulate Company Conduct**: Direct company to cease oppressive acts
2. **Purchase Shares**: Order majority shareholders to buy out minority shares (at fair value determined by registered valuer)
3. **Reduce Share Capital**: Cancel/reduce shares held by oppressive shareholders
4. **Remove Directors**: Terminate directors responsible for oppression
5. **Appoint Directors**: Appoint independent directors (to protect minority)
6. **Appoint Managing Director**: Appoint professional MD (if company mismanaged)
7. **Modify AOA**: Alter AOA to prevent future oppression
8. **Winding Up**: Order winding up (if just and equitable)
9. **Any Other Order**: NCLT has wide powers to grant relief

**Timeline**: 6-24 months (depending on case complexity)

**Landmark Case: Shanti Prasad Jain v. Kalinga Tubes (1965)**

**Facts**:
- Minority shareholder (Jain) held 25% shares in Kalinga Tubes
- Majority shareholders (Bennett Coleman group) excluded Jain from management
- Dividends not declared despite profits
- Jain filed petition for oppression

**Held**:
- Oppression proved (exclusion of minority shareholder who was also managing director)
- Supreme Court ordered: Majority to buy out Jain's shares at fair valuation
- Valuation determined by independent valuer

**Principle**: Oppression = Acts that unfairly prejudice minority shareholders' reasonable expectations (expectation to participate in management, receive dividends).

---

### 3. Derivative Action (Section 245 read with Common Law)

**Definition**: Suit filed by shareholder **on behalf of company** to enforce company's rights (when company fails to take action).

**Rule in Foss v. Harbottle (1843)**:
- General Rule: Company (not individual shareholder) is proper plaintiff for wrongs done to company
- **Rationale**: Majority rule - If majority shareholders don't want to sue, minority cannot override

**Exceptions** (When Derivative Action Allowed):
1. **Ultra Vires Acts**: Act beyond company's powers (MOA objects)
2. **Fraud on Minority**: Majority shareholders committing fraud, controlling company to prevent action
3. **Irregular Acts**: Acts requiring special resolution passed as ordinary resolution
4. **Personal Rights**: Shareholder's individual membership rights violated

**Example**:
- Directors divert ₹10 lakh company funds to personal account
- Company (controlled by same directors) doesn't sue directors
- Minority shareholder files derivative action (on behalf of company) to recover ₹10 lakh
- If successful, recovery goes to company (not individual shareholder)

**Landmark Case: Burland v. Earle (1902)**:
- Directors sold company assets undervalue to themselves
- Shareholders filed derivative action
- Court held: Derivative action maintainable (fraud on minority exception)

---

### 4. Personal Action (Individual Rights)

**Distinction from Derivative Action**:
- **Derivative Action**: Enforce **company's** rights (recovery to company)
- **Personal Action**: Enforce **shareholder's individual** rights (remedy to shareholder)

**Examples of Personal Actions**:
- Right to vote violated → Shareholder sues for right to vote
- Dividend declared but not paid → Shareholder sues for dividend payment
- Share transfer wrongly refused → Shareholder sues for registration of transfer
- Name wrongly removed from Register of Members → Shareholder sues for restoration

## Strategic Considerations

### Minority Shareholder Protection Strategies

**1. Shareholder Agreement (SHA)** (Most Effective):
- Contractual rights beyond AOA
- Clauses: Tag-along (forced exit with majority), drag-along (forced sale by minority), ROFR, anti-dilution, board seats, veto rights on reserved matters

**2. Nominee Directors**:
- Investor appoints director on board (ensures representation)
- Nominee director owes fiduciary duty to company (not just investor), but provides information flow

**3. Class Rights** (Section 47):
- Issue preference shares with class rights (fixed dividend, voting rights on specific matters)
- Class rights can be varied only with 75% approval of that class

**4. Reserved Matters in AOA**:
- List of decisions requiring minority shareholder approval (merger, capex >₹1 crore, related party transactions)
- Example: "Any merger/acquisition requires approval of 75% shareholders including 51% of Preference shareholders"

---

### Majority Shareholder Protections

**Drag-Along Rights**:
- If 75% shareholders approve sale of company, minority must also sell (cannot block exit)
- **Example**: Founder holds 60%, investor holds 20%, employee holds 20%
  - Founder + investor (80%) agree to sell to acquirer
  - Drag-along clause: Employee must also sell 20% (cannot block deal)

**Deadlock Resolution**:
- If 50-50 shareholding leads to deadlock (cannot pass special resolution)
- **Remedies**: Buy-sell clause (one party offers to buy other at X valuation, other chooses to sell OR buy at same valuation), arbitration, NCLT winding up (just and equitable ground)

## Shareholder Rights in Listed Companies

### Takeover Code (SEBI Substantial Acquisition Regulations)

**Substantial Acquisition** (5% or more shares/voting rights):
- Disclose to stock exchange within 2 working days (Form PIT-2)

**Mandatory Open Offer** (Regulation 3):
- If acquirer crosses 25% shares/voting rights → Mandatory open offer to acquire 26% from public shareholders
- **Purpose**: Public shareholders get exit opportunity at fair price
- **Offer Price**: Higher of (a) Negotiated price, (b) Highest price paid in last 52 weeks, (c) Volume-weighted average market price (VWAP) for 60 trading days

**Creeping Acquisition**:
- Acquirer holding 25-75% can acquire additional **5% per financial year** (without open offer)

---

### Insider Trading Prohibition (SEBI Insider Trading Regulations)

**Definition**: Trading in company securities based on Unpublished Price Sensitive Information (UPSI)

**Prohibited**:
❌ Directors, key managerial personnel, promoters cannot trade during **trading window closure** (before quarterly results, material events)
❌ Sharing UPSI with others (tipping)

**Penalty**: ₹25 crore or 3 times profit made (whichever higher) + imprisonment up to 10 years

## Conclusion

Shareholder rights in India are comprehensively protected via:

1. ✅ **Statutory Rights**: Voting, dividends, pre-emptive rights, inspection, transfer (Companies Act 2013)
2. ✅ **Minority Protection**: Oppression/mismanagement remedy (Section 241), class action (Section 245), derivative action
3. ✅ **Contractual Rights**: Shareholder agreements (SHA) for investor protection
4. ✅ **Listed Company Rights**: Takeover code, insider trading prohibition, SEBI regulations

**Key Takeaways**:
- **Majority Rule**: Generally prevails, but minority protected against oppression
- **Voting**: Show of hands (1 member = 1 vote) vs Poll (1 share = 1 vote) - Minority can demand poll (if 10% voting power)
- **Remedies**: NCLT for oppression/mismanagement (6-24 months), class action for fraud, derivative action for company's wrongs

**Strategic Advice**:
- **Investors**: Secure protection via SHA (tag-along, board seats, veto rights, anti-dilution)
- **Minorities**: Know remedies (oppression petition, class action, derivative action) + exercise voting rights (demand poll)
- **Companies**: Transparent governance (dividends, disclosures, shareholder communications) to avoid oppression claims
