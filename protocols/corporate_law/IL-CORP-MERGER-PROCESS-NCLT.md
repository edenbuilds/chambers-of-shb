# MERGER PROCESS AND NCLT SCHEMES - INDIAN CORPORATE LAW

## OVERVIEW

A merger (or amalgamation) is a corporate restructuring transaction where two or more companies combine into one entity. The transferor company (merging company) ceases to exist, and its assets, liabilities, and shareholders transfer to the transferee company (surviving company). Mergers in India are governed by the Companies Act 2013 and require NCLT (National Company Law Tribunal) approval through a Scheme of Arrangement.

**MERGER vs ACQUISITION**:

| Parameter | Merger | Acquisition |
|---|---|---|
| **Structure** | Two companies combine into one (Transferor → Transferee) | Buyer acquires target (remains separate or becomes subsidiary) |
| **Legal Existence** | Transferor company dissolves | Target company continues (as subsidiary) |
| **Consideration** | Share swap (shareholders of transferor receive transferee shares) | Cash or shares (paid to target shareholders) |
| **Approval** | NCLT scheme approval (court-driven) | Shareholders approve share purchase (simpler) |
| **Timeline** | 6-12 months (NCLT process) | 2-4 months (contractual) |
| **Cost** | High (legal, valuation, NCLT fees) | Moderate (legal, DD) |

**LEGAL FRAMEWORK**:
- **Companies Act 2013**: Sections 230-240 (Compromises, Arrangements, Amalgamations)
- **NCLT Rules 2016**: Procedure for scheme applications
- **Income Tax Act 1961**: Sections 2(1B) (Amalgamation), 47(vi) (Tax-neutral treatment)
- **Competition Act 2002**: Merger control (CCI approval if thresholds crossed)
- **SEBI (LODR) Regulations 2015**: Listed company merger disclosures

## 1. TYPES OF MERGERS

### 1.1 Classification by Structure

**1. Absorption (Forward Merger)**:
```
Structure: Subsidiary merges into Holding Company
Example: Tata Steel acquires Corus → Corus (transferor) merges into Tata Steel (transferee)
Result: Corus shareholders receive Tata Steel shares, Corus ceases to exist
```

**2. Consolidation (Formation of New Entity)**:
```
Structure: Two companies merge to form New Co (both transferors, new entity created)
Example: Company A + Company B → New Co AB (A and B cease to exist, AB is new entity)
Result: A and B shareholders receive AB shares
```

**3. Triangular Merger (Subsidiary Absorption)**:
```
Structure: Target merges into Acquirer's subsidiary (not acquirer itself)
Example: Acquirer creates Sub Co → Target merges into Sub Co → Sub Co issues shares to Target shareholders (exchangeable for Acquirer shares)
Result: Target becomes part of Acquirer group (as subsidiary)
```

### 1.2 Classification by Business Relationship

**1. Horizontal Merger**: Competitors in same industry (e.g., HDFC Bank + Kotak Mahindra Bank)

**2. Vertical Merger**: Supplier-customer relationship (e.g., Auto Manufacturer + Tire Supplier)

**3. Conglomerate Merger**: Unrelated businesses (e.g., Reliance Industries + Network18 Media)

**Example - Horizontal Merger**:
```
Company A: E-commerce (₹500 cr revenue, 40% market share)
Company B: E-commerce (₹300 cr revenue, 25% market share)
Post-Merger: Combined entity (₹800 cr revenue, 65% market share)

Synergies:
- Eliminate duplicate costs (₹50 cr admin, ₹30 cr logistics overlap)
- Cross-sell (A customers buy B products, vice versa)
- Bargaining power with suppliers (combined scale)
- Market dominance (65% market share)

Competition Concern:
- 65% market share may trigger anti-trust review (CCI investigation)
- If CCI finds monopoly concerns, may block merger or impose conditions (divest some operations)
```

## 2. NCLT SCHEME PROCESS (Sections 230-232)

### 2.1 Pre-Scheme Preparation (60-90 days)

**Step 1: Due Diligence**:
- Legal DD: Shareholding, contracts, litigation, compliance
- Financial DD: Assets, liabilities, contingent liabilities, off-balance-sheet items
- Tax DD: Tax disputes, advance tax, MAT credit, carry-forward losses
- Operational DD: Employees, real estate, IP, customers, suppliers

**Step 2: Valuation**:
- Independent registered valuer prepares valuation report (Section 232(2)(c))
- Methods: NAV (Net Asset Value), DCF (Discounted Cash Flow), Market Multiples
- Determine Share Exchange Ratio (SER): How many Transferee shares for 1 Transferor share

**Step 3: Scheme Drafting**:
- Comprehensive document (50-100 pages typical)
- Sections: Recitals, Definitions, Appointed Date, Effective Date, Transfer of Assets/Liabilities, Share Exchange, Accounting Treatment, Approvals, Implementation

**Example - Share Exchange Ratio**:
```
Transferor: Company A (1 crore shares, valuation ₹100 cr, ₹100/share)
Transferee: Company B (2 crore shares, valuation ₹400 cr, ₹200/share)

Fair SER Calculation:
Value per share ratio: ₹100/share (A) ÷ ₹200/share (B) = 0.5
SER: 1 share of A = 0.5 shares of B (or 1:0.5 or 2:1)

Post-Merger:
- A shareholders: 1 crore shares × 0.5 = 0.5 crore B shares (worth ₹100 cr)
- Existing B shareholders: 2 crore shares (worth ₹400 cr)
- Total B shares post-merger: 2.5 crore shares (worth ₹500 cr = ₹100 cr + ₹400 cr)
- A shareholders ownership: 0.5 cr ÷ 2.5 cr = 20%
- Existing B shareholders ownership: 2 cr ÷ 2.5 cr = 80%
```

### 2.2 NCLT Application and Approval (180-240 days)

**Step 4: Board Approval**:
- Transferor Board approves scheme
- Transferee Board approves scheme
- Audit Committee reviews valuation, fairness opinion

**Step 5: NCLT Application (Form NCLT-1)**:
- File application with NCLT (jurisdiction where registered office located)
- Documents: Scheme, Board resolutions, valuation report, financial statements (3 years), list of creditors/shareholders
- Fees: ₹5 lakh - ₹10 lakh (based on company size)

**Step 6: NCLT Directions (First Hearing)**:
- NCLT admits application (14-21 days)
- Directions: Convene meetings of shareholders and creditors (for voting)
- NCLT orders: Notice to shareholders, creditors, regulatory authorities (ROC, IT, SEBI, Stock Exchanges)

**Step 7: Meetings (Shareholders and Creditors)**:
- **Shareholders Meeting**: 75% majority (in value and 50% in number) required to approve scheme
- **Creditors Meeting**: 75% majority (in value and 50% in number) required to approve scheme
- Notice: 21 days before meeting
- Chairperson: NCLT-appointed chairperson (or company proposes, NCLT approves)

**Example - Meeting Voting**:
```
Transferor: Company A (100 shareholders, 1 crore shares outstanding)

Shareholders Meeting:
- Present: 80 shareholders holding 90 lakh shares (90% of total shares)
- Voted YES: 70 shareholders holding 75 lakh shares
- Voted NO: 10 shareholders holding 15 lakh shares
- Abstained: 20 shareholders holding 10 lakh shares (not counted)

Approval Check:
- Value: 75 lakh ÷ 90 lakh = 83.3% (>75% ✓)
- Number: 70 ÷ 80 = 87.5% (>50% ✓)
Result: Shareholders APPROVE scheme

Creditors Meeting:
- Total unsecured creditors: ₹50 crore (100 creditors)
- Present: 80 creditors (₹45 crore)
- Voted YES: 65 creditors (₹38 crore)
- Voted NO: 15 creditors (₹7 crore)

Approval Check:
- Value: ₹38 cr ÷ ₹45 cr = 84.4% (>75% ✓)
- Number: 65 ÷ 80 = 81.3% (>50% ✓)
Result: Creditors APPROVE scheme

Overall: Both meetings approved → Scheme proceeds to final NCLT hearing
```

**Step 8: Regulatory Approvals (Parallel with Meetings)**:
- **ROC**: No-objection certificate (no pending statutory dues, filings up-to-date)
- **Income Tax Department**: Clearance (no pending tax disputes or tax will be adjusted in scheme)
- **SEBI** (if listed): No-objection, observation letter (scheme complies with SEBI regulations)
- **Stock Exchanges** (if listed): In-principle approval for listing merged entity shares
- **CCI** (Competition Commission of India): If assets/turnover thresholds crossed (see Section 9)
- **Sectoral Regulators**: RBI (banks, NBFCs), IRDAI (insurance), SEBI (stock exchanges/clearing houses)

**Step 9: Final NCLT Hearing**:
- NCLT reviews: Meeting results (75% approval ✓), regulatory approvals (ROC, IT, SEBI clearances ✓)
- NCLT may hear objections (dissenting shareholders, creditors, employees, regulatory authorities)
- NCLT passes order: Approves scheme with "effective date" (date on which scheme becomes operative)

**Step 10: ROC Filing and Implementation**:
- File NCLT order with ROC (Form INC-28 within 30 days)
- Transferor company dissolved (struck off from ROC register)
- Transferee company issues shares to transferor shareholders (per SER)
- Transfer assets/liabilities from transferor to transferee
- Update contracts, licenses, registrations (transferee replaces transferor)

**Timeline**:
```
Day 0: Board approves scheme
Day 30: NCLT application filed
Day 60: NCLT first hearing (directions for meetings)
Day 120: Shareholders/creditors meetings held (21-day notice + meeting)
Day 180: Regulatory approvals obtained (parallel process)
Day 240: NCLT final hearing (approve scheme)
Day 270: ROC filing, implementation (shares issued, assets transferred)

Total: 270-365 days (9-12 months typical)
```

## 3. DISSENTING SHAREHOLDERS (Section 230(11))

### 3.1 Objection Rights

**Dissenting Shareholders**: Shareholders who vote AGAINST scheme (or abstain) have right to object at NCLT final hearing.

**Objection Grounds**:
- Unfair share exchange ratio (valuation undervalues transferor)
- Non-disclosure of material facts (hidden liabilities, related party transactions)
- Scheme prejudicial to class interests (preference shareholders' rights diluted)
- Fraud, oppression, mismanagement

**NCLT Powers**:
- Hear objections (shareholders present arguments, company responds)
- Modify scheme (adjust SER, add protective clauses)
- Reject scheme (if objections valid, scheme prejudicial)

**Example - Dissenting Shareholder Objection**:
```
Merger: Company A (transferor) → Company B (transferee)
SER: 1 share of A = 0.5 shares of B

Shareholder X (holds 5% of A, voted AGAINST):
Objection: SER undervalues A
- A's NAV: ₹120/share (per independent valuer)
- B's NAV: ₹200/share
- Fair SER: 120 ÷ 200 = 0.6 shares of B (vs proposed 0.5)
- Shareholder X argues: 20% undervaluation (should get 0.6, getting 0.5)

Company Response:
- A's valuation based on DCF (future cash flows discounted), not NAV (book value)
- DCF reflects A's declining business (lower future cash flows → lower valuation)
- NAV overstates value (includes obsolete inventory ₹20 cr, unrecoverable receivables ₹10 cr)

NCLT Decision:
- Reviews valuation methodology (DCF vs NAV)
- If DCF justified (A's business declining): SER upheld (0.5)
- If NAV more appropriate (DCF assumptions unreasonable): SER modified (0.55 compromise)
```

### 3.2 Appraisal Rights (Exit Offer)

**No Statutory Appraisal Rights in India**: Unlike US/UK, Indian law does NOT grant dissenting shareholders right to exit at fair value (demand company/acquirer buy shares).

**Workaround**: Scheme can include "exit offer" clause (optional):
```
Clause: Dissenting shareholders can elect to receive cash (at FMV) instead of transferee shares
Payment: Transferee company or promoters purchase dissenting shareholders' shares

Example:
SER: 1 share of A = 0.5 shares of B
Exit Offer: Dissenting shareholders of A can elect cash (₹100/share) instead of B shares

Shareholder Y (holds 1 lakh A shares, dissents):
Option 1: Accept B shares (1 lakh × 0.5 = 50,000 B shares worth ₹100 lakh at ₹200/share)
Option 2: Exit for cash (1 lakh × ₹100 = ₹100 lakh cash)

Shareholder Y chooses cash → Transferee/promoters pay ₹100 lakh, Y exits
```

## 4. COMPETITION COMMISSION OF INDIA (CCI) APPROVAL

### 4.1 Merger Control Thresholds (Section 5, Competition Act)

**Mandatory CCI Approval if**:
```
DOMESTIC THRESHOLDS (both parties in India):
- Assets: Combined India assets >₹2,000 crore OR
- Turnover: Combined India turnover >₹6,000 crore

OR

TARGET THRESHOLDS:
- Assets: Target India assets >₹750 crore OR
- Turnover: Target India turnover >₹2,250 crore

If thresholds NOT crossed: No CCI approval required (exemption for small deals)
```

**Example - CCI Threshold Check**:
```
Company A (Transferor): Assets ₹1,200 cr, Turnover ₹3,000 cr
Company B (Transferee): Assets ₹1,500 cr, Turnover ₹4,500 cr

Combined Assets: ₹1,200 cr + ₹1,500 cr = ₹2,700 crore (>₹2,000 cr threshold)
Combined Turnover: ₹3,000 cr + ₹4,500 cr = ₹7,500 crore (>₹6,000 cr threshold)

Result: CCI approval MANDATORY (thresholds crossed)

Alternative Scenario:
Company A: Assets ₹500 cr, Turnover ₹1,000 cr
Company B: Assets ₹600 cr, Turnover ₹1,500 cr
Combined: Assets ₹1,100 cr (<₹2,000 cr), Turnover ₹2,500 cr (<₹6,000 cr)

Result: CCI approval NOT required (thresholds not crossed, de minimis exemption)
```

### 4.2 CCI Review Process (210 days max)

**Phase I (30 days)**:
- CCI assesses: Does merger cause Appreciable Adverse Effect on Competition (AAEC)?
- Factors: Market share post-merger, barriers to entry, likelihood of collusion, impact on consumers
- If no AAEC: CCI approves (90% cases approved in Phase I)
- If potential AAEC: CCI refers to Phase II (detailed investigation)

**Phase II (180 days + optional 30-day extension)**:
- Detailed investigation: Market definition, market share analysis, competitive effects
- CCI can: (a) Approve unconditionally, (b) Approve with conditions (behavioral/structural remedies), (c) Prohibit merger

**Remedies**:
- **Behavioral**: Promise not to abuse market dominance (e.g., no predatory pricing, no tying/bundling)
- **Structural**: Divest overlapping businesses (e.g., sell one division to reduce market share)

**Example - CCI Conditional Approval**:
```
Merger: Telecom A (30% market share) + Telecom B (25% market share)
Combined Market Share: 55% (dominant position)

CCI Concern: Market concentration (3-player market → 2-player post-merger)

Remedies Proposed by Parties:
1. Divest 5% market share (₹500 cr spectrum) to third party (reduce to 50%)
2. No bundling of services (cannot force customers to buy multiple services)
3. Price cap (cannot increase prices >10% per annum for 3 years)

CCI Decision: Approve subject to conditions (divestiture + price cap + no bundling)
```

## 5. ACCOUNTING TREATMENT

### 5.1 Pooling of Interests vs Purchase Method

**Pooling of Interests (Merger of Equals)**:
```
Criteria: Merging parties are comparable size (neither is acquirer, both are equals)
Treatment: Assets/liabilities recorded at book value (no goodwill)
Advantage: No goodwill impairment (clean balance sheet)
```

**Purchase Method (Acquisition)**:
```
Criteria: One party clearly acquires the other (transferee is acquirer, transferor is target)
Treatment: Assets/liabilities recorded at fair value (excess over book value = goodwill)
Goodwill: Amortized over useful life (Ind AS) or annually tested for impairment
```

**Example - Accounting Treatment Comparison**:
```
Transferor: Company A
- Book Value of Net Assets: ₹100 crore
- Fair Value of Net Assets: ₹150 crore (includes ₹30 cr brand value, ₹20 cr real estate appreciation)
- Consideration paid by Transferee: 0.5 crore shares (worth ₹180 crore at ₹360/share)

POOLING METHOD:
Transferee Balance Sheet:
Dr. Assets ₹100 cr (book value)
Cr. Share Capital ₹5 cr (face value ₹10 × 0.5 cr shares)
Cr. Reserves ₹95 cr (plug)

Result: No goodwill, assets at historical cost

PURCHASE METHOD:
Transferee Balance Sheet:
Dr. Assets ₹150 cr (fair value)
Dr. Goodwill ₹30 cr (₹180 consideration - ₹150 FV net assets)
Cr. Share Capital ₹5 cr (face value)
Cr. Securities Premium ₹175 cr (₹180 cr - ₹5 cr face value)

Result: ₹30 cr goodwill (tested annually for impairment, if impaired → P&L charge)
```

## 6. TAX IMPLICATIONS

### 6.1 Tax-Neutral Amalgamation (Section 2(1B))

**Definition of "Amalgamation"** (Section 2(1B), Income Tax Act):
```
Conditions for tax-neutral treatment:
1. ALL properties of amalgamating company (transferor) transferred to amalgamated company (transferee)
2. ALL liabilities of transferor transferred to transferee
3. Shareholders holding ≥75% value of shares in transferor become shareholders of transferee (continuity of shareholder interest)
4. Transferor wound up (dissolved, ceases to exist)

If conditions met: Amalgamation is tax-neutral (no capital gains tax on transfer of assets)
```

**Benefits of Tax-Neutral Amalgamation**:
- Transferor: No capital gains tax on asset transfer (Section 47(vi) exemption)
- Transferee: Inherits transferor's tax attributes (carry-forward losses, MAT credit, depreciation) (Section 72A)
- Shareholders: No capital gains tax on share exchange (cost of acquisition of new shares = cost of old shares)

**Example - Tax-Neutral Amalgamation**:
```
Transferor: Company A (assets book value ₹100 cr, fair value ₹200 cr)
Capital Gain (if A sold assets): ₹200 cr - ₹100 cr = ₹100 cr (taxable @ 25% = ₹25 cr tax)

Tax-Neutral Merger:
- A merges into B (Section 2(1B) conditions satisfied)
- Section 47(vi): Asset transfer exempt from capital gains tax (₹0 tax vs ₹25 cr if sold)
- Section 72A: B inherits A's carry-forward losses (₹50 cr losses, can set off against future profits)

Shareholder: Mr. X (holds 1 lakh A shares, cost ₹50/share, receives 50,000 B shares per SER 1:0.5)
- Cost of B shares: ₹50 × 2 = ₹100/share (adjusted for SER, maintains original cost)
- No capital gains tax on share exchange (Section 47(vii))
- Future sale of B shares: Capital gain = Sale price - ₹100 (cost basis preserved)
```

### 6.2 Transfer Pricing in Mergers (Section 92B)

**Related Party Merger**: If transferor and transferee are related parties (same promoter/group), transfer pricing rules apply.

**Arms-Length Valuation**: SER must be at arms-length (fair market value). If not, tax authorities can adjust.

**Example - Transfer Pricing Adjustment**:
```
Merger: Company A (loss-making, promoter-owned) → Company B (profitable, same promoter)
SER: 1 share of A = 1 share of B (generous to A shareholders, undervalues B)

Fair SER (per independent valuer): 1 share of A = 0.5 shares of B
Overvaluation of A: 2x (getting 1 B share vs deserved 0.5)

Transfer Pricing Risk:
- Tax officer: SER not arms-length (A overvalued to benefit promoter shareholders of A)
- Adjustment: ₹100 cr income added to A (deemed transfer pricing adjustment)
- A tax liability: 25% × ₹100 cr = ₹25 cr (penalty 100-300% of tax = ₹25-75 cr additional)

Mitigation: Obtain independent valuation, document SER rationale (Vodafone Essar Gujarat case - transfer pricing scrutiny in mergers)
```

## 7. CROSS-BORDER MERGERS (Section 234)

### 7.1 Inbound Merger (Foreign Company → Indian Company)

**Permitted** (Section 234 allows foreign company to merge into Indian company).

**Conditions**:
- Foreign company from notified jurisdiction (16 countries including US, UK, Singapore, UAE)
- RBI prior approval (FDI policy compliance)
- Valuation by registered valuer (both companies)
- NCLT approval (Indian company files scheme)

**Example**:
```
Foreign Co (Singapore): Subsidiary of Indian Parent
Indian Parent: Wants to merge Singapore subsidiary back into India

Process:
1. RBI approval (FDI rules: No sectoral caps violated)
2. Valuation: Singapore Co (₹200 cr), Indian Parent (₹1,000 cr)
3. SER: 1 Singapore share = 0.2 Parent shares
4. NCLT application (Indian Parent jurisdiction)
5. NCLT approval (scheme sanctioned)
6. Singapore Co dissolved, assets/liabilities transferred to Indian Parent
```

### 7.2 Outbound Merger (Indian Company → Foreign Company)

**Restricted**: Not explicitly permitted under Section 234 (no provision for Indian company to merge into foreign company).

**Workaround**: Liquidation + Asset Sale (Indian company sells assets to foreign company, winds up domestically).

## 8. FAST-TRACK MERGER (Section 233)

### 8.1 Simplified Process (No NCLT Approval)

**Eligibility**:
```
1. Holding-Subsidiary Merger:
   - Holding company holds ≥90% shares in subsidiary → Subsidiary merges into holding
   OR
   - Two or more subsidiaries (100% owned by same holding) merge

2. Small Companies Merger:
   - Both companies are "small companies" (paid-up capital ≤₹2 crore, turnover ≤₹20 crore)
```

**Process**:
```
Step 1: Board approval (both companies)
Step 2: Shareholder approval (75% majority) - No need for separate creditors meeting
Step 3: File scheme with ROC (Form FTM-1) + Central Government approval (ROC review)
Step 4: ROC approves (30 days) OR objects (if objections, refer to NCLT)
Step 5: Implementation (after 30 days of ROC filing, if no objections)

Timeline: 60-90 days (vs 270-365 days for regular NCLT merger)
```

**Example - Holding-Subsidiary Fast-Track Merger**:
```
Holding: Parent Ltd (owns 95% of Sub Ltd)
Subsidiary: Sub Ltd (wholly-owned subsidiary for practical purposes, 5% held by Parent employees)

Fast-Track Merger: Sub → Parent
- No NCLT approval (holding owns >90%)
- Board approves scheme (Parent and Sub boards)
- Parent shareholder approval: 75% majority (routine approval, no dilution)
- Sub shareholder approval: 75% majority (Parent votes 95%, guaranteed approval)
- ROC filing: Form FTM-1
- Effective: 30 days after filing (if no objections)

Cost Savings: ₹5 lakh (fast-track) vs ₹20 lakh (NCLT process)
Time Savings: 60 days vs 270 days
```

## 9. POST-MERGER INTEGRATION

### 9.1 Operational Integration

**Day 1 (Effective Date)**:
- Legal merger effective (transferor dissolved, transferee survives)
- Shares issued to transferor shareholders
- Assets/liabilities transferred

**Day 1-100 (Integration)**:
- **Employees**: Transfer employment contracts, harmonize salaries/benefits
- **Customers**: Notify customers (transferee replaces transferor in contracts)
- **Suppliers**: Update contracts, vendor master
- **IT Systems**: Integrate ERP, CRM, email, intranet
- **Branding**: Rebrand (single brand vs dual brand transition)
- **Operations**: Consolidate offices, manufacturing plants, warehouses
- **Culture**: Integration workshops, town halls (address employee concerns)

**Example - Integration Challenges**:
```
Merger: Company A (startup culture, 500 employees, ₹50 lakh average salary) + Company B (traditional, 2,000 employees, ₹30 lakh average salary)

Post-Merger Issues:
1. Salary Disparity: A employees earning 66% more than B employees (same roles)
   - Solution: Harmonize over 2 years (freeze A salaries, increase B salaries 15% per year)

2. Layoffs: Duplicate functions (both companies have HR, Finance, IT departments)
   - Solution: Retain best talent (50% from A, 50% from B), lay off redundant (₹10 cr severance cost)

3. Culture Clash: A employees (equity-driven, risk-taking) vs B employees (salary-driven, conservative)
   - Solution: Extend ESOP to B employees (align incentives), leadership alignment workshops

4. Customer Attrition: A customers prefer A brand (worried about B's service quality)
   - Solution: Retain dual brands for 1 year (transition gradually), invest in customer success team
```

## STRATEGIC CONSIDERATIONS

### When to Choose Merger vs Acquisition

**Choose Merger When**:
- Tax-neutral treatment required (preserve carry-forward losses, avoid capital gains tax)
- Regulatory approvals easier (merger = less antitrust scrutiny vs cash acquisition)
- Shareholder alignment (both sets of shareholders want to participate in combined upside)
- Holding-subsidiary simplification (fast-track merger, eliminate subsidiary)

**Choose Acquisition (Share Purchase) When**:
- Speed critical (2-3 months vs 9-12 months for merger)
- Minority shareholders hostile (acquisition can use drag-along, merger requires 75% approval)
- Target liabilities uncertain (acquisition can use W&I insurance, indemnities; merger = assume ALL liabilities)
- Seller wants cash exit (merger = share swap, acquisition = cash payment)

## COMPLIANCE CHECKLIST

**Pre-Application**:
- [ ] Board approvals (transferor and transferee)
- [ ] Due diligence (legal, financial, tax, operational)
- [ ] Valuation report (registered valuer, both companies)
- [ ] Draft scheme of arrangement (comprehensive, 50-100 pages)
- [ ] Fairness opinion (if listed, merchant banker opines on SER fairness)

**NCLT Application**:
- [ ] File Form NCLT-1 with NCLT (jurisdiction of registered office)
- [ ] Attach: Scheme, board resolutions, valuation reports, financials (3 years), shareholder/creditor lists
- [ ] Pay NCLT fees (₹5-10 lakh)
- [ ] Await first hearing (14-21 days, NCLT issues directions)

**Meetings**:
- [ ] Convene shareholders meeting (21-day notice, 75% in value + 50% in number required)
- [ ] Convene creditors meeting (21-day notice, same voting threshold)
- [ ] Appoint chairperson (NCLT-approved or company proposes)
- [ ] File meeting results with NCLT (Form CA-4 within 7 days)

**Regulatory Approvals**:
- [ ] ROC no-objection (statutory filings up-to-date, no defaults)
- [ ] Income Tax clearance (no pending assessments or tax adjusted in scheme)
- [ ] SEBI observation letter (if listed, scheme complies with LODR)
- [ ] Stock exchange in-principle approval (if listed)
- [ ] CCI approval (if thresholds crossed, Phase I/II process)
- [ ] Sectoral regulator (RBI for banks, IRDAI for insurance, etc.)

**Final NCLT Hearing**:
- [ ] File all regulatory approvals with NCLT
- [ ] Respond to objections (dissenting shareholders, if any)
- [ ] NCLT passes order (approves scheme, specifies effective date)
- [ ] Obtain certified copy of NCLT order

**Implementation**:
- [ ] File Form INC-28 with ROC (NCLT order + scheme within 30 days)
- [ ] Transferor struck off (ROC removes from register)
- [ ] Issue shares to transferor shareholders (per SER, demat credit)
- [ ] Transfer assets/liabilities (deed of conveyance, assignment agreements)
- [ ] Update contracts, licenses, registrations (transferee replaces transferor)
- [ ] File Form PAS-3 (return of allotment for new shares issued)
