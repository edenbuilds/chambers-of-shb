# IBC - Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process (CIRP) Initiation

## Overview

### What is CIRP?

**Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process (CIRP)** is a time-bound (180-330 days) insolvency resolution mechanism under the **Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code 2016 (IBC)** that aims to:
1. **Rescue Distressed Companies**: Revive financially stressed but commercially viable businesses (going concern value > liquidation value)
2. **Maximize Asset Value**: Ensure creditors recover maximum value (resolution plan vs liquidation)
3. **Balance Interests**: Protect creditors' rights while allowing promoters/management opportunity to revive business
4. **Time-Bound Process**: Strict timelines to prevent prolonged litigation (unlike pre-IBC era: BIFR/DRT took 10-15 years)

**Key Principles**:
- **Creditor-in-Control**: Committee of Creditors (CoC) controls process, approves resolution plan (66% vote)
- **Time-Bound**: 180 days + 90-day extension max = 330 days (vs pre-IBC unlimited delays)
- **Moratorium**: Automatic stay on all legal proceedings upon CIRP admission (Section 14)
- **Management Suspension**: Promoter directors suspended, Interim Resolution Professional (IRP) takes control

### IBC 2016 - Paradigm Shift from Pre-IBC Era

**Pre-IBC Era (SICA/BIFR/DRT) - Problems**:
1. **Debtor-in-Possession**: Promoters retained control (could delay, asset stripping, tunneling)
2. **Prolonged Timelines**: Average resolution 10-15 years (BIFR cases pending for decades)
3. **Low Recovery**: Banks recovered 20-25% of dues (liquidation value after years of value erosion)
4. **Multiple Forums**: BIFR (sick companies), DRT (debt recovery), Company Court (winding up) - forum shopping, conflicting orders

**Post-IBC Era (2016 Onwards) - Solutions**:
1. **Creditor-in-Control**: Management suspended, CoC (financial creditors) controls process
2. **Strict Timelines**: 180-330 days mandatory (NCLT must admit within 14 days for Section 7)
3. **Higher Recovery**: Improved to 40-45% (going concern sale vs liquidation)
4. **Single Forum**: NCLT exclusive jurisdiction (Section 60), no parallel proceedings

### Who Can Initiate CIRP?

**Three Categories** (Sections 7, 8, 10):

1. **Financial Creditor** (Section 7):
   - Banks, NBFCs, bondholders, debenture holders, ECB lenders
   - **Threshold**: Default ≥₹1 crore
   - **Timeline**: NCLT must decide within 14 days of application
   - **Advantage**: Fastest route, no pre-existing dispute defense (Innoventive Industries case)

2. **Operational Creditor** (Section 8):
   - Trade suppliers, vendors, employees (unpaid salaries/dues), government (unpaid taxes)
   - **Pre-Requirement**: Demand notice (10 days for payment/dispute)
   - **Threshold**: Default ≥₹1 crore
   - **Timeline**: NCLT must decide within 14 days (if no pre-existing dispute)
   - **Disadvantage**: Must prove no pre-existing dispute (burden on operational creditor)

3. **Corporate Applicant/Debtor** (Section 10):
   - Corporate debtor itself (voluntary insolvency)
   - **Pre-Requirement**: Board resolution (3/4th majority) OR shareholders resolution (special resolution 75%)
   - **No Threshold**: No minimum default amount required (voluntary)
   - **Timeline**: NCLT decides based on facts (no statutory 14-day limit)

## Legal Framework

### Section 4 - Minimum Default Threshold

**Statutory Provision** (IBC 2016):
> "This Code shall apply where the minimum amount of the default is one lakh rupees or such other amount, as may be notified by the Central Government."

**Notification History**:
- **Original Threshold** (December 2016): ₹1 lakh (too low, flood of frivolous applications)
- **Revised Threshold** (March 2018): ₹1 crore (reduced frivolous cases)
- **COVID Relief** (April 2020 - March 2021): ₹1 crore suspended, no COVID-related defaults eligible
- **Current Threshold** (Since April 2021): **₹1 crore** (₹10 million)

**Rationale for ₹1 Crore Threshold**:
- Avoid frivolous applications (small trade disputes converting into insolvency)
- Focus NCLT resources on material defaults (₹1cr+ debt = significant financial stress)
- Allow small operational creditors to pursue DRT/civil courts (less draconian remedies)

**Example**:
- Supplier owed ₹75 lakh for goods supplied → **Cannot file Section 8 application** (below ₹1cr threshold)
- Bank owed ₹1.2 crore term loan installment (overdue 90 days) → **Can file Section 7 application** (above ₹1cr threshold)

### Section 5 - Key Definitions

**Financial Debt** (Section 5(8)):
> "A debt along with interest, if any, which is disbursed against the consideration for the time value of money"

**Meaning**: Money borrowed where lender expects return purely for parting with capital (time value of money = interest/returns)

**Includes** (Section 5(8) list):
- Bank loans, term loans, working capital
- Debentures, bonds, NCDs
- Inter-corporate deposits (ICDs)
- External Commercial Borrowings (ECB)
- Delayed payment of purchase price (if consideration for time value)
- **Homebuyers' deposits** (Section 5(8)(f) - added 2018 amendment after real estate crisis)

**Excludes**:
- Trade credit (goods supplied, 30-60 day payment terms = operational debt, NOT financial debt)
- Security deposits (refundable deposit, no time value component)
- Advance payments (for future goods/services, not loan)

**Operational Debt** (Section 5(21)):
> "A claim in respect of the provision of goods or services including employment or a debt in respect of the repayment of dues arising under any law for the time being in force and payable to the Central Government, any State Government or any local authority"

**Includes**:
- Trade payables (suppliers, vendors)
- Employee salaries, provident fund, gratuity (unpaid)
- Government dues (unpaid taxes - GST, income tax, customs duty)
- Professional fees (lawyers, consultants, auditors - unpaid)

**Financial Creditor** (Section 5(7)):
- Person to whom financial debt is owed
- **Includes**: Banks, NBFCs, bondholders, debenture holders, ARC (Asset Reconstruction Companies), homebuyers (Pioneer Urban case)

**Operational Creditor** (Section 5(20)):
- Person to whom operational debt is owed
- **Includes**: Suppliers, employees, government (for taxes), service providers

## Section 7 - Initiation by Financial Creditor

### Who Can File Section 7?

**Eligible Financial Creditors**:
1. **Banks**: Scheduled commercial banks (SBI, ICICI, HDFC, etc.)
2. **NBFCs**: Registered with RBI (Bajaj Finance, HDFC Ltd., etc.)
3. **All India Financial Institutions**: SIDBI, NABARD, EXIM Bank
4. **Asset Reconstruction Companies (ARCs)**: After purchasing NPAs from banks (Section 5(7)(a))
5. **Bondholders/Debenture Trustees**: On behalf of debenture holders (collective filing)
6. **Foreign Lenders**: ECB lenders (DBS Bank, HSBC, etc.)
7. **Homebuyers**: For real estate projects (Section 5(8)(f), Pioneer Urban case 2019)

**Threshold**: Default ≥₹1 crore (principal + interest)

### Section 7 Application Requirements

**Form 1** (CIRP Regulations 2016, Regulation 3):

**Part A - Applicant Details**:
- Financial creditor name, address, PAN
- Nature of creditor (bank/NBFC/bondholder/ARC)
- If ARC: Assignment deed details (purchased NPA from whom, assignment date, consideration paid)

**Part B - Corporate Debtor Details**:
- Company name, CIN, registered office
- Nature of business (manufacturing/services/trading)
- Authorized capital, paid-up capital

**Part C - Debt Details**:
- **Amount of Claim**: ₹_____ crore (principal + interest till application date)
- **Date of Default**: [Date when payment became overdue, e.g., EMI due date not paid]
- **Nature of Financial Debt**: Term loan/working capital/debentures/bonds/ECB/ICD
- **Evidence of Debt**:
  - Loan agreement (certified copy)
  - Sanction letter (certified copy)
  - Account statements (showing principal disbursed, EMIs due, EMIs paid, outstanding balance)
  - Demand notice (if sent to corporate debtor, though NOT mandatory for Section 7)
  - Board resolution (if lender is company, authorizing filing)

**Part D - Default Evidence**:
- Statement of account: Disbursement ₹100cr, Repayments ₹30cr, Outstanding ₹70cr + interest ₹10cr = ₹80cr total default
- Overdue period: First EMI missed on [Date], application filed on [Date] = [X] months overdue
- Recovery efforts: Demand letters sent (dates), meetings held (minutes), OTS offered (rejected)

**Part E - Proposed Interim Resolution Professional (IRP)**:
- IRP name, IBBI registration number
- IRP consent letter (signed, agreeing to act as IRP if appointed)
- IRP declaration (no conflict of interest, not related to corporate debtor/promoters within 2 years)
- IRP CV (experience, successful resolution cases handled)

**Attachments** (Typically 15-25 Documents):
1. **Loan Agreement** (certified copy, original + all amendments)
2. **Sanction Letter** (sanctioned amount, terms, repayment schedule)
3. **Account Statements** (from disbursement to application date, highlighting defaults)
4. **Security Documents** (hypothecation deed, mortgage deed, personal guarantees)
5. **Board Resolution** (if financial creditor is company, authorizing Section 7 filing)
6. **Certificate of Incorporation** (financial creditor + corporate debtor)
7. **PAN Card** (financial creditor + corporate debtor)
8. **Financial Statements** (corporate debtor's last 3 years audited accounts, showing deteriorating financials)
9. **CIBIL Report** (corporate debtor's credit report showing defaults)
10. **Records of Default** (RBI CRILC data, showing NPA classification)
11. **IRP Consent** (Form 2, signed by proposed IRP)
12. **IRP CV & Registration Certificate** (from IBBI)
13. **Affidavit** (by financial creditor's authorized representative, verifying facts)
14. **Power of Attorney** (authorizing signatory to file application on behalf of financial creditor)

### NCLT Admission Process - Section 7

**Timeline** (Section 7(5)):
> "The Adjudicating Authority shall, within fourteen days of the receipt of the application under sub-section (2), by an order—
> (a) admit such application and communicate such decision to the financial creditor and the corporate debtor; or
> (b) reject such application."

**14-Day Mandatory Timeline**: NCLT **MUST** decide within 14 days of filing (unlike Section 8 where 14 days is for admission, actual decision may take 30-60 days)

**Admission Criteria** (Section 7(5) - Innoventive Industries case clarification):

**NCLT Must Admit If**:
1. ✅ **Default Exists**: Financial debt ≥₹1 crore is unpaid/overdue
2. ✅ **Evidence Satisfactory**: Loan agreement + account statements prove debt and default
3. ✅ **No Repayment/Dispute**: Corporate debtor has not paid within notice period (if notice given)

**NCLT Cannot Reject On Grounds**:
- ❌ **Pre-Existing Dispute**: Unlike Section 8, financial creditor applications CANNOT be defeated by pre-existing dispute defense (Innoventive Industries SC judgment)
  - Rationale: Financial creditors (banks) have robust loan documentation (loan agreements, security, board approvals), unlike operational creditors (invoice-based)
- ❌ **Solvency**: NCLT cannot reject saying "company is solvent, can pay later" (irrelevant if default exists)
- ❌ **Debt is Disputed**: Debt dispute is NOT a defense for Section 7 (corporate debtor can raise in resolution process, but cannot prevent admission)

**NCLT Can Reject If**:
1. **Incomplete Application**: Missing documents (loan agreement, account statements, IRP consent)
2. **Below Threshold**: Default <₹1 crore (add all dues - principal + interest + penalties)
3. **Not Financial Debt**: Debt is operational debt (e.g., trade payables misclassified as financial debt)
4. **Debt Barred by Limitation**: Debt is >3 years old (limitation period expired, though courts differ on whether limitation applies to IBC)
5. **Already Undergone CIRP**: Corporate debtor already completed CIRP once (cannot file again within 1 year unless fresh default)

## Section 8 - Initiation by Operational Creditor

### Who Can File Section 8?

**Eligible Operational Creditors**:
1. **Trade Suppliers/Vendors**: Suppliers of goods (raw materials, inventory, machinery)
2. **Service Providers**: Consultants, professionals (lawyers, CAs, engineers), contractors
3. **Employees**: Unpaid salaries, provident fund, gratuity (≥₹1cr aggregate for all employees combined typically)
4. **Government**: Unpaid taxes (GST, income tax, customs duty, excise - if ≥₹1cr)
5. **Utility Providers**: Electricity, water, telecom bills (if ≥₹1cr, rare but possible for large corporate debtors)

**Threshold**: Operational debt ≥₹1 crore

### Pre-Requirement - Demand Notice (Section 8(1))

**Unlike Section 7 (financial creditor - no demand notice required), Section 8 (operational creditor) REQUIRES demand notice before NCLT filing**

**Demand Notice Contents** (Section 8(1)):
```
DEMAND NOTICE
under Section 8(1) of the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016

To,
[Corporate Debtor Name]
[Registered Office Address]

Date: [Date]

Dear Sirs,

RE: DEMAND NOTICE FOR PAYMENT OF OPERATIONAL DEBT OF ₹_____ CRORES

1. We, [Operational Creditor Name], supplied goods/rendered services to your company as per the following:
   - Invoice No. [XXX] dated [Date] for ₹_____ (Due Date: [Date])
   - Invoice No. [YYY] dated [Date] for ₹_____ (Due Date: [Date])
   [List all unpaid invoices]

   Total Outstanding: ₹_____ crores (Principal ₹_____ + Interest ₹_____)

2. As per our records, the above invoices are overdue by [X] days and remain unpaid despite repeated follow-ups.

3. This constitutes DEFAULT under Section 3(12) of the Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016.

4. You are hereby DEMANDED to:
   a) PAY the outstanding amount of ₹_____ crores within 10 (TEN) DAYS from the date of this notice; OR
   b) NOTICE of dispute with reasons and supporting documents if you dispute the debt.

5. If payment is not received OR notice of dispute is not given within 10 days, we shall be constrained to initiate Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process (CIRP) under Section 9 of the IBC, 2016 before the National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT).

6. This notice is without prejudice to our rights and remedies under law.

Yours faithfully,

[Authorized Signatory]
[Operational Creditor Name]
[PAN: XXXXX1234X]

Enclosures:
1. Copy of Invoices (with proof of delivery/acceptance)
2. Copy of Purchase Orders (if any)
3. Copy of Agreements (if any)
4. Account Statement (showing outstanding balance)
```

**Dispatch Method**:
- Registered Post/Speed Post (AD - Acknowledgment Due)
- Courier with tracking (FedEx, DHL, Bluedart - obtain proof of delivery)
- Email to corporate debtor's registered email (as per MCA records)

**10-Day Period** (Section 8(2)):
- Corporate debtor has 10 days from notice receipt to:
  - **Pay**: Clear the outstanding amount (full/partial payment proves willingness, may defeat application)
  - **Dispute**: Reply stating debt is disputed, provide reasons/documents (pre-existing dispute defeats application)
  - **Ignore**: No response = default established, operational creditor can file Section 9 application

### Section 9 - Application by Operational Creditor

**Form 5** (CIRP Regulations 2016, Regulation 5):

**Part A - Applicant Details**:
- Operational creditor name, address, PAN
- Nature of operational debt (goods/services/employment/government dues)

**Part B - Corporate Debtor Details**:
- Company name, CIN, registered office

**Part C - Debt Details**:
- **Amount of Claim**: ₹_____ crore (invoices + interest if applicable)
- **Date of Default**: [Invoice due date not paid]
- **Nature of Operational Debt**: Goods supplied/services rendered/unpaid salary/government dues
- **Evidence of Debt**:
  - Invoices (original + copies)
  - Purchase orders (if issued by corporate debtor)
  - Delivery challans/proof of delivery (goods received by corporate debtor)
  - Service agreements (if services)
  - Acceptance certificates (if applicable, e.g., project completion accepted)
  - Account statements (showing outstanding balance)

**Part D - Demand Notice Compliance**:
- **Demand Notice**: Copy of notice sent (Section 8(1))
- **Proof of Dispatch**: Postal receipt/courier tracking/email delivery confirmation
- **Date of Dispatch**: [Date]
- **10-Day Period Completion**: [Date] (must be ≥10 days before Section 9 filing)
- **Response from Corporate Debtor**: None received (attach proof if replied, showing no valid dispute)

**Part E - Pre-Existing Dispute (Critical for Operational Creditor)**:

**Declaration** (Operational Creditor Must Certify):
```
"I/We hereby declare that:
1. There is NO PRE-EXISTING DISPUTE between the Operational Creditor and the Corporate Debtor
   regarding the debt claimed in this application.
2. The Corporate Debtor has NOT raised any dispute in response to the demand notice dated [Date].
3. No suit/arbitration/adjudication is pending between the parties regarding this debt.
4. If the Corporate Debtor has raised a dispute, supporting documents proving the dispute is
   frivolous/afterthought are attached."
```

**Why Pre-Existing Dispute Matters**:
- **Mobilox Innovations case (2017)**: Operational creditor must prove NO pre-existing dispute
- If corporate debtor disputes debt BEFORE demand notice (e.g., quality issues raised 6 months ago, email trail), NCLT will reject Section 9 application
- Rationale: Operational debt often based on invoices (may have genuine disputes - quality defects, quantity shortfalls, service not rendered satisfactorily), unlike financial debt (bank loans have robust documentation)

**Part F - Proposed Interim Resolution Professional (IRP)**:
- Same as Section 7 (IRP name, consent, registration, CV)

**Attachments** (Typically 20-30 Documents):
1. **Invoices** (original + copies, with due dates, amounts)
2. **Purchase Orders** (issued by corporate debtor, if available)
3. **Delivery Challans** (proof of goods delivered + received by corporate debtor)
4. **Acknowledgments** (corporate debtor's receiving officer signed delivery challan/gate pass)
5. **Service Agreements** (if services, with scope, deliverables, payment terms)
6. **Acceptance Certificates** (for services, corporate debtor certified completion)
7. **Email Correspondence** (showing debt acknowledgment, e.g., "We will pay by [Date]")
8. **Ledger Statements** (showing transactions, outstanding balance)
9. **Demand Notice** (copy sent under Section 8(1))
10. **Proof of Dispatch** (postal receipt/courier tracking)
11. **No-Dispute Certificate** (self-declaration by operational creditor)
12. **Certificate of Incorporation** (operational creditor + corporate debtor)
13. **PAN Cards** (operational creditor + corporate debtor)
14. **Board Resolution** (if operational creditor is company, authorizing Section 9 filing)
15. **IRP Consent** (Form 2)
16. **IRP CV & Registration**
17. **Affidavit** (verifying facts)
18. **Power of Attorney** (authorizing signatory)

### NCLT Admission/Rejection - Section 9

**Timeline** (Section 9(5)):
> "The Adjudicating Authority shall, within fourteen days of the receipt of the application, by an order—
> (a) admit the application and communicate such decision to the operational creditor and the corporate debor; or
> (b) reject the application and communicate such decision to the operational creditor and the corporate debtor."

**14-Day Timeline**: NCLT should decide within 14 days (though in practice, may take 30-60 days if corporate debtor files detailed reply)

**Admission Criteria**:

**NCLT Must Admit If**:
1. ✅ **Operational Debt ≥₹1 Crore**: Unpaid invoices total ≥₹1cr
2. ✅ **Demand Notice Sent**: Notice under Section 8(1) sent and 10 days elapsed
3. ✅ **No Pre-Existing Dispute**: Corporate debtor did not raise any dispute before/in response to demand notice
4. ✅ **Evidence Satisfactory**: Invoices + delivery proof + acceptance = debt established

**NCLT Must Reject If**:
1. **Pre-Existing Dispute** (Mobilox Innovations case):
   - Corporate debtor raised dispute BEFORE demand notice (e.g., quality issues email 3 months ago)
   - Corporate debtor replied to demand notice with valid dispute (e.g., goods rejected due to defects, delivery challan shows "subject to inspection")
   - Suit/arbitration pending between parties regarding this debt

   **Example** (Dispute Defeats Application):
   - Supplier claims ₹2cr for goods supplied (100 tons steel)
   - Corporate debtor replied to demand notice: "Only 80 tons received, 20 tons short-supplied, deduct ₹40L, will pay balance ₹1.6cr"
   - **Pre-existing dispute exists** (quantity dispute), NCLT will reject Section 9 (supplier must first resolve dispute in civil court/arbitration)

2. **Below Threshold**: Operational debt <₹1 crore

3. **Frivolous/Afterthought Dispute**:
   - If corporate debtor raises dispute ONLY after Section 9 filing (never disputed before demand notice), NCLT may hold "afterthought" and admit application
   - Operational creditor must prove: No dispute raised in past 12 months, no emails/letters showing disagreement, sudden dispute after CIRP filing = mala fide

**Corporate Debtor's Reply** (Section 9 Application):

**If Corporate Debtor Wants to Defeat Section 9**:
1. File detailed reply within 7-10 days of receiving NCLT notice
2. **Prove Pre-Existing Dispute**:
   - Attach emails showing quality complaints raised 6 months ago
   - Attach letters rejecting goods/services (before demand notice)
   - Attach arbitration notice sent to operational creditor (dispute already in arbitration)
3. **Prove Debt Paid/Settled**:
   - Attach payment proof (bank statement showing transfer)
   - Attach settlement agreement (debt compromised/waived)
4. **Prove Debt Below ₹1 Crore**:
   - Detailed reconciliation showing: Invoices ₹2cr, Payments ₹1.2cr, Debit notes ₹50L (quality defects), Net outstanding ₹30L (<₹1cr threshold)

### Admission Order - Section 9 (Same as Section 7)

Once admitted, Section 9 admission order is identical to Section 7:
- Moratorium declared (Section 14)
- IRP appointed
- Public announcement (Section 13)
- CoC constitution within 30 days
- Resolution plan within 180-330 days

## Section 14 - Moratorium (Automatic Upon CIRP Admission)

### What is Moratorium?

**Moratorium** = Legal stay/freeze on ALL legal proceedings, suits, and asset transfers against the corporate debtor during CIRP period (180-330 days)

**Purpose**:
1. **Preserve Asset Value**: Prevent creditors from seizing assets (going concern value > liquidation value)
2. **Level Playing Field**: All creditors participate in CIRP equally (no preferential payments to aggressive creditors)
3. **Allow Breathing Space**: Corporate debtor can continue operations under IRP, focus on revival (not defending multiple lawsuits)
4. **Prevent Asset Stripping**: Promoters cannot sell/transfer assets during CIRP (IRP controls)

### Section 14(1) - Prohibited Actions During Moratorium

**Section 14(1) Comprehensive List**:

**a) Suits/Proceedings Stay** (Section 14(1)(a)):
> "No suits or proceedings shall be instituted, continued or pending against the corporate debtor"

**Includes**:
- Civil suits (breach of contract, recovery suits, property disputes)
- Criminal proceedings (Section 138 cheque bounce cases stayed till CIRP ends)
- Arbitration proceedings (cannot continue, CIRP takes precedence)
- Execution proceedings (decree obtained, cannot execute during moratorium)
- Tax recovery proceedings (income tax/GST recovery stayed)

**Exceptions** (Section 14(3)):
- ✅ Suits BY corporate debtor (corporate debtor can sue others to recover dues)
- ✅ Supply of essential goods/services (electricity, water - cannot be disconnected under Section 14(2A))

**b) Asset Seizure Stay** (Section 14(1)(b)):
> "No action to foreclose, recover or enforce any security interest created by the corporate debtor"

**Includes**:
- SARFAESI Act actions (banks cannot invoke SARFAESI to seize assets during CIRP)
- Mortgage foreclosure (lenders cannot foreclose property mortgaged to them)
- Hypothecation enforcement (lenders cannot seize machinery/inventory hypothecated)
- Pledge enforcement (lenders cannot sell shares/securities pledged)

**Example**:
- Bank has ₹50cr term loan secured by mortgage of corporate debtor's factory land
- Pre-CIRP: Bank issued SARFAESI notice (Section 13(2)), 60-day period running
- CIRP admitted: **SARFAESI proceedings AUTOMATICALLY STAYED** (moratorium under Section 14(1)(b))
- Bank cannot seize factory, must participate in CIRP and vote on resolution plan as financial creditor

**c) Asset Transfers Stay** (Section 14(1)(c)):
> "No action to recover any property of the corporate debtor held by a third party"

**Example**:
- Corporate debtor leased equipment to Customer X (equipment worth ₹10cr, customer defaulting on lease rent)
- CIRP admitted: IRP can recover equipment from Customer X (asset belongs to corporate debtor)
- Customer X cannot claim "I have right to keep equipment" - moratorium allows IRP to recover ALL corporate debtor assets

**d) Asset Disposal Prohibition** (Section 14(1)(d)):
> "No sale, transfer, encumbrance, alienation or disposal of any of the assets or any legal right or beneficial interest of the corporate debtor"

**Includes**:
- Promoters CANNOT sell corporate debtor's assets (land, machinery, IP, inventory)
- Promoters CANNOT create fresh security (mortgage, hypothecation) on corporate debtor's assets
- Promoters CANNOT transfer shares (corporate debtor's shareholding in subsidiary)

**IRP Control**: Only IRP can sell assets (with CoC approval) for going concern operations or as part of resolution plan

**e) Payment Prohibition** (Section 14(2)):
> "The supply of essential goods or services to the corporate debtor as specified shall not be terminated or suspended or interrupted during moratorium period"

**Example**:
- Electricity provider (operational creditor) owed ₹5cr pre-CIRP dues
- CIRP admitted: **Electricity provider CANNOT disconnect supply** (essential service under Section 14(2A))
- However, electricity provider can demand cash payment for post-CIRP supply (no credit allowed)

### Section 14 Duration

**Moratorium Period**:
- **Starts**: Date of NCLT admission order
- **Ends**: Earliest of:
  - Approval of resolution plan (CoC approves, NCLT confirms)
  - Rejection of resolution plan + liquidation order (CIRP failed, liquidation begins)
  - 330 days from CIRP commencement (180 days + 90-day extension + 60 days if pending approval)

**Maximum**: 330 days moratorium protection

## Section 16 & 17 - Appointment and Powers of Interim Resolution Professional (IRP)

### Who is IRP?

**Interim Resolution Professional (IRP)** = Insolvency professional (registered with IBBI) who takes over management of corporate debtor during CIRP

**Qualifications** (Insolvency Professional Regulations 2016):
- Chartered Accountant / Company Secretary / Cost Accountant / Advocate (with 15 years experience), OR
- Graduate/Post-graduate with 10 years experience in management, business, finance, law
- Passed IBBI examination (Insolvency Professional exam)
- Registered with Insolvency Professional Agency (IPA - ICAI, ICSI, ICMAI)
- No conflict of interest (not related to corporate debtor/promoters/creditors within 2 years)

### IRP Appointment Process

**Proposed IRP** (Section 16(3)):
- **Section 7**: Financial creditor proposes IRP in application (Form 1)
- **Section 8**: Operational creditor proposes IRP in application (Form 5)
- **Section 10**: Corporate debtor proposes IRP in application (Form 6)

**NCLT Appointment**: NCLT appoints proposed IRP in admission order (same day as CIRP admission)

**IRP Tenure**: Till CoC appoints Resolution Professional (RP) in first CoC meeting (typically 30-45 days after CIRP commencement)
- If CoC confirms IRP as RP → IRP continues as RP for full CIRP period
- If CoC appoints different RP → IRP hands over to RP within 7 days

### Section 17 - Management Vesting in IRP

**Section 17(1)**:
> "On the appointment of the interim resolution professional, the management of the affairs of the corporate debtor shall vest in the interim resolution professional."

**Meaning**:
- **Board of Directors SUSPENDED**: Promoter directors, independent directors, nominee directors - ALL powers suspended
- **KMPs Suspended**: CEO, CFO, Company Secretary - reporting powers suspended (remain as employees, but decision-making by IRP)
- **IRP Controls**: All business decisions, contracts, asset disposal, fund deployment controlled by IRP

**IRP Powers** (Section 17(2)):

**Business Operations**:
- Continue business operations (manufacturing, sales, services)
- Manage day-to-day affairs (approve purchase orders, sales invoices, salary payments)
- Enter into new contracts (with customers/suppliers, if essential for going concern operations)

**Asset Management**:
- Protect and preserve assets (prevent theft, damage, unauthorized removal)
- Sell assets (with CoC approval, if required for working capital or resolution plan)
- Create security (with CoC approval, if required to raise interim finance - Section 5(15A))

**Financial Management**:
- Operate bank accounts (but cannot make pre-CIRP debt payments - moratorium prohibits)
- Raise interim finance (Section 5(15A) - super-priority status, with CoC approval)
- Pay operational creditors (for post-CIRP supplies, cash-and-carry basis)

**Corporate Debtor's Obligations**:
- Board/KMPs must provide ALL documents, records, books to IRP (Section 17(3))
- Failure to cooperate = Contempt of NCLT (imprisonment up to 3 years under Section 70)

## Practical Examples

### Example 1 - Bank Files Section 7 (Financial Creditor) Against Manufacturing Company

**Background**:
- **Corporate Debtor**: XYZ Steel Ltd. (steel manufacturing), ₹500cr turnover, 500 employees
- **Financial Creditor**: ABC Bank (term loan sanctioned ₹200cr for plant expansion in 2020)
- **Default**: EMI due quarterly ₹12cr (principal ₹10cr + interest ₹2cr), 4 EMIs overdue = ₹48cr default

**Timeline**:

**Day 0** - ABC Bank files Section 7 application:
- Form 1 prepared: Claim ₹248cr (principal ₹200cr + interest ₹35cr + penal interest ₹13cr)
- Attached: Loan agreement, sanction letter, account statements (4 EMIs missed), demand letters
- Proposed IRP: Mr. Ravi Kumar (CA, IBBI registration IP-12345)
- Filed with NCLT Mumbai bench (XYZ Steel's registered office in Mumbai)

**Day 7** - NCLT issues notice to XYZ Steel:
- "Reply within 7 days, hearing on Day 14"

**Day 10** - XYZ Steel files reply:
- "Debt not disputed, but temporary cash flow stress due to COVID, expect to normalize in 3 months, request NCLT to not admit"
- **Bank's Rebuttal**: "XYZ Steel has been NPA for 18 months (since June 2022), not COVID issue, resolution required"

**Day 14** - NCLT First Hearing:
- Bank argues: Default established, ₹248cr > ₹1cr threshold, evidence sufficient
- XYZ Steel argues: Temporary stress, give 6 months time, not insolvency
- **NCLT's View**: "Default exists, magnitude ₹248cr is significant NPA, temporary stress not a defense under Section 7, application ADMITTED"

**Day 14** - NCLT Admission Order:
```
ORDER

This application under Section 7 is ADMITTED.

1. MORATORIUM declared under Section 14 (effective from today).
2. Mr. Ravi Kumar appointed as Interim Resolution Professional (IRP).
3. Management of XYZ Steel Ltd. vests in IRP (Board suspended).
4. IRP to make public announcement within 3 days, calling for creditor claims.
5. IRP to constitute Committee of Creditors (CoC) within 30 days.
6. Resolution plan to be submitted within 180 days (extendable by 90 days).
```

**Day 15-17** - Public Announcement (Section 13):
- IRP publishes notice in Economic Times (English) + Loksatta (Marathi)
- IBBI website publication (Form B)
- Content: "CIRP admitted against XYZ Steel Ltd., creditors must file claims by [Day 30], forms available at www.xyz steel-cirp.com"

**Day 30** - Claims Received:
- Financial Creditors: ABC Bank ₹248cr, DEF Bank ₹150cr, Bondholders ₹100cr = ₹498cr total
- Operational Creditors: Suppliers ₹80cr, Employees ₹5cr, Government (GST dues) ₹10cr = ₹95cr total
- **Total Claims**: ₹593cr

**Day 45** - First CoC Meeting:
- CoC constitution: ABC Bank (49.8% vote), DEF Bank (30.1% vote), Bondholders (20.1% vote) = 100% financial creditor voting rights
- **Operational creditors**: No voting rights (can attend, give suggestions, but cannot vote)
- CoC confirms Mr. Ravi Kumar as Resolution Professional (RP) for full CIRP period

**Day 90** - Resolution Plan Invitation:
- RP publishes Expression of Interest (EOI): "Interested resolution applicants to submit EOI for acquiring XYZ Steel Ltd."
- 5 companies respond: PQR Steel (competitor), LMN Investment Fund (PE fund), Promoter (existing promoter - own plan)

**Day 150** - Resolution Plans Submitted:
- PQR Steel: Offers ₹400cr cash (80% recovery for financial creditors), retain all 500 employees
- LMN Fund: Offers ₹350cr cash (70% recovery), retrench 100 employees
- Promoter: Offers ₹300cr (60% recovery), infuse ₹50cr fresh equity

**Day 180** - CoC Voting:
- PQR Steel plan put to vote: ABC Bank FOR (49.8%), DEF Bank FOR (30.1%), Bondholders AGAINST (20.1%) = 79.9% FOR (>66% required)
- **PQR Steel Plan APPROVED**

**Day 200** - NCLT Confirmation Hearing:
- RP presents approved plan to NCLT
- Operational creditors object: "Only ₹10cr for operational creditors (10% recovery vs 80% for financial creditors)"
- **NCLT View**: "Financial creditors' commercial wisdom under Section 30(4), operational creditors getting liquidation waterfall equivalent (Section 53 priority), plan APPROVED"

**Day 210** - NCLT Approval Order:
- Resolution plan approved, XYZ Steel transfers to PQR Steel
- PQR Steel pays ₹400cr to financial creditors within 90 days
- CIRP concluded, moratorium lifted, XYZ Steel continues operations under PQR Steel

**Outcome**:
- Financial Creditors: Recovered 80% (vs 25% typical liquidation recovery)
- Operational Creditors: Recovered 10% (better than liquidation where often zero)
- Employees: 500 jobs saved (vs liquidation = 500 job losses)
- XYZ Steel: Continues as going concern (vs liquidation = asset stripping, closure)

## Landmark Cases

### 1. Innoventive Industries Ltd. v. ICICI Bank (2017 Supreme Court)

**Facts**:
- ICICI Bank (financial creditor) filed Section 7 application against Innoventive Industries (corporate debtor)
- Claim: ₹50cr term loan default
- **Innoventive's Defense**: "Pre-existing dispute exists (interest rate was renegotiated, bank's claim includes excess interest), Section 7 should be rejected like Section 8/9"

**Issue**: Can corporate debtor defeat Section 7 application by raising pre-existing dispute (like Section 8/9)?

**Held** (Supreme Court - Favored ICICI Bank):
- **NO, pre-existing dispute is NOT a defense for Section 7** (financial creditor applications)
- Section 8/9 (operational creditor) explicitly requires "no dispute" (Section 8(2)(a)), but **Section 7 has NO such requirement**
- **Rationale**: Financial debts (bank loans) have robust documentation (loan agreements, security documents, board approvals), unlike operational debts (invoices, may have quality disputes)
- Financial creditor only needs to prove: (1) Debt exists, (2) Default exists, (3) Debt ≥₹1cr → NCLT must admit

**Principle**:
- **Financial Creditor > Operational Creditor**: Financial creditors get faster, dispute-proof admission
- **Section 7 vs Section 8/9 Dichotomy**: Pre-existing dispute defeats Section 8/9 (operational creditor) but NOT Section 7 (financial creditor)

**Impact**: Post-Innoventive, banks use Section 7 extensively (over 60% of CIRP cases are Section 7 filed by financial creditors), as it's fastest route with no dispute defense

### 2. Swiss Ribbons Pvt. Ltd. v. Union of India (2019 Supreme Court - Constitutional Bench)

**Facts**:
- Multiple petitions challenging constitutional validity of IBC 2016:
  - "IBC violates Article 14 (equality) - why financial creditors get 100% voting rights in CoC, operational creditors get zero?"
  - "IBC violates Article 19(1)(g) (right to business) - promoters lose management control arbitrarily"
  - "IBC violates Article 300A (right to property) - creditors can approve resolution plan distributing promoters' shares without consent"

**Issue**: Is IBC 2016 constitutionally valid? Does it unfairly favor financial creditors over operational creditors/promoters?

**Held** (Supreme Court - Upheld IBC 2016):
- **IBC is constitutional**, reasonable classification exists
- **Financial Creditors vs Operational Creditors**:
  - Classification is based on **nature of debt** (financial creditors provide capital for business operations vs operational creditors provide goods/services)
  - Financial creditors take higher risk (loan may not be repaid if business fails), operational creditors take lower risk (goods consumed/services rendered)
  - **Reasonable to give financial creditors control** (they have maximum stake in revival)
- **Promoters' Rights**:
  - Promoters are not creditors, no vested right to control company during insolvency
  - IBC's time-bound resolution (180-330 days) serves public interest (earlier BIFR/DRT took 10-15 years, value erosion)
- **Operational Creditors' Rights**:
  - Operational creditors protected: (i) liquidation waterfall priority (Section 53), (ii) can object to unfair resolution plans (NCLT review under Section 30(2))

**Principle**:
- **IBC's Primacy**: IBC overrides prior insolvency laws (SICA, BIFR, DRT), time-bound resolution is constitutional mandate
- **Creditor-in-Control**: Paradigm shift from debtor-in-possession (pre-IBC) to creditor-in-control (post-IBC) is valid
- **Financial Creditors' Commercial Wisdom**: CoC's decision on resolution plan is final (Section 30(4)), courts cannot second-guess commercial decision

**Impact**: Settled constitutional challenges to IBC, reinforced financial creditors' primacy in resolution process

## Compliance Checklist

### Section 7 (Financial Creditor) Checklist

**Pre-Filing Preparation**:
- [ ] Verify default amount ≥₹1 crore (principal + interest + penalties)
- [ ] Calculate claim: Principal + Interest (simple/compound as per agreement) + Penal interest + Other charges = Total claim
- [ ] Identify IRP: Contact IBBI-registered IP, obtain consent (Form 2), verify no conflict of interest
- [ ] Gather documents: Loan agreement, sanction letter, account statements (from disbursement to application date), demand letters

**Form 1 Preparation**:
- [ ] Part A: Financial creditor details (name, PAN, address, nature - bank/NBFC/bondholder)
- [ ] Part B: Corporate debtor details (CIN, registered office, business nature)
- [ ] Part C: Debt details (amount, default date, financial debt nature, loan agreement date)
- [ ] Part D: Evidence (attach loan agreement, account statements, demand letters, security documents)
- [ ] Part E: Proposed IRP (name, IBBI registration, consent letter, CV)

**Attachments Checklist** (15-25 documents):
- [ ] Loan Agreement (certified copy, all amendments)
- [ ] Sanction Letter
- [ ] Account Statements (disbursement to application date, highlighting defaults)
- [ ] Security Documents (mortgage/hypothecation deeds, personal guarantees)
- [ ] Board Resolution (financial creditor's board authorizing Section 7 filing)
- [ ] Certificate of Incorporation (financial creditor + corporate debtor)
- [ ] PAN Cards (financial creditor + corporate debtor)
- [ ] Financial Statements (corporate debtor's last 3 years audited accounts)
- [ ] CIBIL Report (corporate debtor's credit report showing defaults)
- [ ] IRP Consent (Form 2, signed by IRP)
- [ ] IRP CV & Registration Certificate (IBBI)
- [ ] Affidavit (financial creditor's authorized representative verifying facts)
- [ ] Power of Attorney (authorizing signatory to file on behalf of financial creditor)

**NCLT Filing**:
- [ ] E-file Form 1 on NCLT e-filing portal (https://nclt.gov.in)
- [ ] Pay filing fee (as per NCLT fee schedule, typically ₹5,000-10,000)
- [ ] Submit hard copies at NCLT registry (3 sets) within 7 days of e-filing
- [ ] Obtain acknowledgment with case number (CP(IB) No. ___/___/2024)

**Post-Filing**:
- [ ] Serve copy of application on corporate debtor (registered office + email)
- [ ] Serve copy on corporate debtor's directors (individual notice to each director)
- [ ] Attend NCLT hearing (Day 14 typically), argue for admission
- [ ] If admitted: Coordinate with IRP for CoC constitution, claims verification

### Section 9 (Operational Creditor) Checklist

**Pre-Filing Preparation**:
- [ ] Verify operational debt ≥₹1 crore (unpaid invoices + interest if applicable)
- [ ] Prepare demand notice under Section 8(1) (10-day payment/dispute demand)
- [ ] Dispatch demand notice (registered post AD/courier with tracking/email)
- [ ] Wait 10 days from notice receipt (if no payment/dispute, proceed to Section 9)
- [ ] Identify IRP: Contact IBBI-registered IP, obtain consent (Form 2)

**Demand Notice Checklist**:
- [ ] Addressed to corporate debtor (registered office as per MCA)
- [ ] List all unpaid invoices (invoice no., date, amount, due date)
- [ ] Total outstanding ≥₹1 crore
- [ ] Demand: Pay within 10 days OR give notice of dispute
- [ ] Warning: Section 9 application will be filed if no payment/dispute
- [ ] Dispatch proof: Postal receipt/courier tracking/email delivery confirmation
- [ ] Wait 10 days (calculate from receipt date, not dispatch date)

**Form 5 Preparation**:
- [ ] Part A: Operational creditor details (name, PAN, nature - supplier/employee/government)
- [ ] Part B: Corporate debtor details (CIN, registered office)
- [ ] Part C: Debt details (invoices, default dates, operational debt nature)
- [ ] Part D: Demand notice compliance (copy of notice, dispatch proof, 10-day period completion)
- [ ] Part E: Pre-existing dispute declaration (**CRITICAL** - declare NO dispute exists, or prove dispute is frivolous)
- [ ] Part F: Proposed IRP (name, IBBI registration, consent, CV)

**Pre-Existing Dispute Evidence Checklist** (Critical for Section 9):
- [ ] **Search Past Correspondence**: Review emails, letters, WhatsApp for 12-24 months (any quality complaints, quantity disputes, service issues?)
- [ ] **If NO Dispute Found**:
   - [ ] Self-declaration: "No pre-existing dispute, corporate debtor never raised any issue before demand notice"
   - [ ] Attach email trail showing: Goods delivered → Acknowledgment received → Payment promised → No complaints raised
- [ ] **If Dispute Found (by Corporate Debtor)**:
   - [ ] Prove Frivolous: "Dispute raised AFTER Section 9 filing (afterthought), never mentioned before demand notice"
   - [ ] Attach: Delivery challans (signed by corporate debtor's officer, no remarks), Acceptance certificates (no defects noted)

**Attachments Checklist** (20-30 documents):
- [ ] Invoices (original + copies, all unpaid invoices)
- [ ] Purchase Orders (issued by corporate debtor)
- [ ] Delivery Challans (signed by corporate debtor's receiving officer)
- [ ] Acceptance Certificates (if services/projects)
- [ ] Email Correspondence (debt acknowledgment, payment promises)
- [ ] Ledger Statements (transactions, outstanding balance)
- [ ] Demand Notice (Section 8(1), copy sent)
- [ ] Proof of Dispatch (postal receipt/courier tracking)
- [ ] No-Dispute Certificate (self-declaration by operational creditor)
- [ ] Certificate of Incorporation (operational creditor + corporate debtor)
- [ ] PAN Cards
- [ ] Board Resolution (operational creditor's board authorizing Section 9 filing)
- [ ] IRP Consent (Form 2)
- [ ] IRP CV & Registration
- [ ] Affidavit (verifying facts)
- [ ] Power of Attorney (authorizing signatory)

**NCLT Filing & Post-Filing**: (Same as Section 7)
