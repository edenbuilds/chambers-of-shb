# Indian Consumer & Misc Law: Environmental Law and Pollution Control

## Purpose
Comprehensive guidance on Indian environmental law framework covering Environmental Protection Act 1986, Pollution Control Acts (Air, Water, Noise), National Green Tribunal (NGT), environmental clearances, EIA requirements, pollution penalties, public interest litigation, and remedies for environmental violations.

## Activation Triggers
Use when: Industrial pollution (air/water/noise) | Environmental clearance required for project | NGT petition filing | River/groundwater contamination | Illegal construction in eco-sensitive zone | Forest clearance (diversion) | E-waste/hazardous waste disposal | Public interest environmental litigation | Violation of environmental norms | Sustainable development disputes.

## Core Principles

### Constitutional Foundation

**Article 21: Right to Life Includes Right to Healthy Environment**

**M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (1987):** "Right to clean environment is fundamental right under Article 21"

**Article 48A (DPSP):** State shall endeavor to protect and improve environment

**Article 51A(g) (Fundamental Duty):** Duty of every citizen to protect and improve natural environment

### Precautionary Principle

**"When activity raises threats of harm to environment/human health, precautionary measures taken even if cause-effect not fully established scientifically"**

**Vellore Citizens' Welfare Forum v. Union of India (SC 1996):**
- Precautionary principle = part of Indian environmental law
- Burden of proof on activity proponent (to prove NO harm)
- Applied when scientific uncertainty exists

### Polluter Pays Principle

**"Polluter must bear cost of pollution control measures and compensation for damage"**

**Indian Council for Enviro-Legal Action v. Union of India (1996):**
- Polluting industries liable to pay restoration cost
- Remediation of contaminated land
- Compensation to affected persons

**Application:**
- Pollution fines/penalties
- Remediation costs
- Compensation to victims

### Sustainable Development

**"Development that meets present needs without compromising future generations' ability to meet their needs"**

**Balancing Test:**
- Economic development vs environmental protection
- NOT absolute preservation (balanced approach)
- Environmental concerns integrated into development projects

## Environmental Protection Act 1986

### Section 3: Power to Take Measures for Environmental Protection

**Central Government can:**
1. Coordinate actions of State Governments, authorities
2. Plan and execute environmental programs
3. Lay down standards for emissions/discharges (air, water, soil)
4. Restrict areas for industries/operations
5. Lay down procedures/safeguards for hazardous substances

### Section 6: Rules for Standards

**Emission/Discharge Standards:**
- Air pollutants (PM10, PM2.5, SO2, NOx)
- Water pollutants (BOD, COD, heavy metals)
- Noise levels (residential, commercial, industrial zones)

**Notified Standards:**
- Environment (Protection) Rules 1986
- Hazardous Waste Management Rules 2016
- E-Waste Management Rules 2016
- Plastic Waste Management Rules 2016

### Section 15: Penalties

**Violation of EPA provisions:**
- Imprisonment: Up to 5 YEARS
- Fine: Up to Rs. 1 LAKH
- Continuing violation: Additional Rs. 5,000 per day

**Subsequent Offense:**
- Imprisonment: Up to 7 YEARS

**Section 17: Offences by Companies:**
- Directors, managers personally liable (if violation with consent/connivance)

## Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act 1974

### Section 24: Prohibition of Discharge Without Consent

**"No person shall discharge pollutants into water sources without consent from State Pollution Control Board"**

**Consent (Section 25-26):**
- **Consent to Establish** (before project setup)
- **Consent to Operate** (renewable periodically - 1-5 years)

**Application:**
- Details of effluent (quantity, quality, composition)
- Treatment methodology
- Discharge point

**Conditions:**
- Meet prescribed standards
- Install effluent treatment plant (ETP)
- Periodically monitor and report
- Zero Liquid Discharge (ZLD) for certain industries

### Section 41-42: Inspection and Samples

**Pollution Control Board Officers Can:**
- Enter premises (with notice)
- Inspect treatment plants
- Collect samples (water, effluent)
- Analyze in approved laboratories

### Section 43-45: Penalties

**Discharge without consent OR exceeding standards:**
- Imprisonment: Up to 6 YEARS
- Fine: Rs. 10,000-1,00,000
- Continuing violation: Rs. 5,000 per day

**Failure to Comply with Board Directions:**
- Imprisonment: Up to 3 months
- Fine: Rs. 10,000

**Closure Order (Section 33A):**
- Board can order closure of polluting industry
- Supreme Court/High Court can direct closure (writ jurisdiction)

## Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act 1981

### Section 21: Prohibition of Air Polluting Industries Without Consent

**Similar Structure to Water Act:**
- Consent to Establish
- Consent to Operate

**Air Pollutants:**
- Suspended Particulate Matter (SPM)
- PM10, PM2.5
- Sulfur dioxide (SO2)
- Nitrogen oxides (NOx)
- Carbon monoxide (CO)

### Section 22A: Power to Give Directions

**Central/State Government can:**
- Direct closure of industry
- Stop electricity/water supply
- Prohibit use of fuel

**Example - Delhi Air Quality:**
- GRAP (Graded Response Action Plan) implemented during winter
- Stage IV: Construction ban, truck entry ban, work-from-home advisories

### Section 37: Penalties

**Operating without consent OR exceeding standards:**
- Imprisonment: Up to 3 months to 5 YEARS (depending on severity)
- Fine: Rs. 10,000 to Rs. 1,00,000

## Noise Pollution (Regulation and Control) Rules 2000

### Ambient Noise Standards

**Decibel Limits (dB):**

| Area Type | Day (6 AM - 10 PM) | Night (10 PM - 6 AM) |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Industrial | 75 dB | 70 dB |
| Commercial | 65 dB | 55 dB |
| Residential | 55 dB | 45 dB |
| Silence Zone | 50 dB | 40 dB |

**Silence Zones:** Within 100 meters of hospitals, educational institutions, courts

### Loudspeakers/Public Address Systems

**Permission Required:**
- From District Magistrate OR authorized officer
- Not permissible in silence zones
- Time restriction: 10 PM - 6 AM (no loudspeakers in public)

**Exceptions:**
- Emergency (police, fire, ambulance)

**Penalty:**
- Fine: Up to Rs. 1 lakh OR imprisonment OR both (under EPA Section 15)

## Environmental Clearance (EIA Notification 2006)

### EIA Requirement

**Environmental Impact Assessment Mandatory for:**
- Mining (>5 hectares)
- Thermal power plants (>25 MW)
- Highways/expressways
- Dams/hydro projects
- Ports/harbors
- Industrial estates (>50 hectares)
- Real estate (>20,000 sq.m. built-up area)
- Chemical/pharmaceutical/textile industries

### Category A vs Category B

**Category A (Central Level - MoEF&CC):**
- Larger projects (e.g., mining >50 hectares, power plants >500 MW)
- EAC (Expert Appraisal Committee) reviews
- Public hearing mandatory

**Category B (State Level - SEIAA):**
- Smaller projects
- Screened by SEAC (State EAC)
- Category B1: Public hearing required
- Category B2: No public hearing

### EIA Process

**Step 1: Screening (Category B only)**
- Determine if project requires EIA
- Categorize as B1 (EIA) or B2 (no EIA)

**Step 2: Scoping**
- Define terms of reference (ToR)
- Identify environmental concerns to be studied

**Step 3: EIA Study**
- Baseline data collection (air, water, soil, biodiversity)
- Impact prediction (construction phase, operation phase)
- Mitigation measures
- Environmental Management Plan (EMP)

**Step 4: Public Hearing**
- Local community consultation
- Concerns recorded
- Project proponent responds

**Step 5: Appraisal**
- EAC/SEAC examines EIA report + public hearing minutes
- Recommends approval/rejection/modifications

**Step 6: Decision**
- MoEF&CC/SEIAA grants/rejects Environmental Clearance
- Validity: Project-specific (usually tied to project completion + 5 years)

**Conditions:**
- Compliance with EMP
- Half-yearly monitoring reports
- Statutory clearances (forest, CRZ)

### Violation of EC Conditions

**Project Proponent:**
- Show-cause notice
- Suspension/revocation of EC
- Bank guarantee forfeiture
- Prosecution under EPA

## Forest (Conservation) Act 1980

### Section 2: Prior Approval for Forest Land Diversion

**"No forest land shall be diverted for non-forest purpose without Central Government approval"**

**Non-Forest Purpose:**
- Mining
- Infrastructure (roads, dams, transmission lines)
- Real estate

**Approval Process:**
1. State Government proposes diversion
2. Central Government (MoEF&CC) examines
3. Clearance granted with conditions:
   - Compensatory afforestation (2× area OR ₹NPV)
   - Net Present Value (NPV) payment
   - Wildlife clearance (if in wildlife area)

**NPV (Net Present Value of Forest):**
- Monetary value of ecosystem services lost
- Varies by state, forest type (Rs. 4-15 lakhs per hectare)

**Compensatory Afforestation:**
- Afforest double the diverted area
- OR pay cost of afforestation

**Penalty (Section 3A - Amendment 2023):**
- Fine: Up to Rs. 1 crore
- Restoration cost: On violator

## Coastal Regulation Zone (CRZ) Notification 2019

### CRZ Classification

**CRZ-I (Ecologically Sensitive):**
- Mangroves, coral reefs, sand dunes, national parks
- NO development (except eco-tourism, renewable energy)

**CRZ-II (Built-Up Areas):**
- Areas already developed up to or close to shoreline
- No-Development Zone (NDZ): 20 meters from HTL (High Tide Line)

**CRZ-III (Rural/Undeveloped):**
- NDZ: 50 meters from HTL (general)
- NDZ: 200 meters for ecologically sensitive areas

**CRZ-IV (Water Area and Tidal Influenced):**
- Water area up to territorial waters
- Tidal influenced water bodies

**Clearance Required:**
- From MoEF&CC for CRZ activities
- Violation: Demolition + penalty

## National Green Tribunal (NGT) Act 2010

### Section 14: Jurisdiction

**NGT has jurisdiction over:**
- All civil cases relating to environmental protection
- Substantial question relating to environment
- Arising under:
  - Water Act 1974
  - Air Act 1981
  - Environment Protection Act 1986
  - Forest Act 1980
  - Biological Diversity Act 2002
  - Public Liability Insurance Act 1991

**Excludes:**
- Wildlife Protection Act 1972 (High Court jurisdiction)

### Section 15: Powers

**NGT Can:**
- Grant relief, compensation
- Order restoration of environment
- Impose costs (polluter pays)
- Issue directions (cease pollution, close factory)

**Not Bound by CPC:**
- Simplified procedure
- Expedited hearings

### Section 16: Applications

**Who Can File:**
- Person affected by pollution
- NGO (not required to be registered)
- Central/State Government
- Local authority

**Against Whom:**
- Polluting industry
- Government authority (for inaction, granting illegal clearances)

**Limitation:** Within 6 MONTHS from knowledge of violation

**Forum:**
- NGT Principal Bench (Delhi)
- Regional Benches (Bhopal, Pune, Kolkata, Chennai)

### Section 17: Procedure

**Filing:**
- Application with facts, relief sought
- Documents, evidence
- Fee: Rs. 1,000

**Admission:**
- NGT admits OR rejects (if frivolous)

**Hearing:**
- Oral arguments, documentary evidence
- Site inspection (if required)

**Order:**
- Within 6 MONTHS (expedited)
- Compensation awarded
- Directions issued

**Execution:**
- Order executed as decree (CPC provisions apply)

### Section 22: Appeal

**Appeal to Supreme Court:**
- Within 90 days from NGT order
- On question of law

## Hazardous Waste Management

### Hazardous Waste Management Rules 2016

**Hazardous Waste Includes:**
- Chemical waste (acids, heavy metals, solvents)
- Pharmaceutical waste
- Waste from healthcare (biomedical waste - separate rules)
- E-waste (electronic waste - separate rules)

**Obligations:**
- **Generator (Occupier):** Store, transport, dispose through authorized agencies only
- **Treatment/Storage/Disposal Facility (TSDF):** Authorized by SPCB
- **Transporter:** Registered with SPCB

**Manifest System:**
- Document tracking waste from generation to disposal

**Penalty:**
- Unauthorized disposal: Imprisonment up to 5 years + fine

### E-Waste Management Rules 2016

**Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR):**
- Manufacturer/importer responsible for collection and recycling
- Targets: 70% collection by weight (7th year onward)

**Channelization:**
- E-waste collected through authorized recyclers only

**Penalty:**
- Violation of EPR: Compensation + prosecution under EPA

## Common Environmental Violations and Remedies

### Scenario 1: Factory Discharging Untreated Effluent into River

**Facts:**
- Textile factory
- No effluent treatment plant (ETP)
- Discharging untreated wastewater into river
- Fish mortality, contaminated drinking water

**Applicable Law:**
- Water Act Section 24-25 (no consent to discharge)
- EPA Section 7 (violation of effluent standards)

**Remedy:**

**Option 1: Complaint to SPCB**
- SPCB inspects, samples effluent
- Show-cause notice
- Closure directions + penalty

**Option 2: NGT Petition**
- File application before NGT
- Relief: Closure of factory, compensation to affected persons, restoration of river

**Option 3: Criminal Complaint**
- Under Water Act Section 43
- Imprisonment + fine

**Typical NGT Order:**
- Immediate closure until ETP installed
- Compensation: Rs. 50 lakhs-10 crores (depending on damage)
- Restoration plan (river rejuvenation)

### Scenario 2: Construction in CRZ Without Clearance

**Facts:**
- Hotel constructed 30 meters from High Tide Line
- CRZ-III area (NDZ 50 meters)
- No CRZ clearance obtained

**Violation:**
- CRZ Notification 2019
- EPA Section 5 (violation of notification)

**Remedy:**

**NGT Petition:**
- Application by local residents/NGO
- NGT orders:
  - Demolition of unauthorized construction
  - Restoration of coastal area
  - Penalty on builder/approving authority

**Example - T.N. Godavarman v. Union of India (ongoing):**
- Continuous monitoring by Supreme Court
- Demolitions ordered for CRZ violations

### Scenario 3: Illegal Mining Without Environmental Clearance

**Facts:**
- Mining lease granted by State Government
- No Environmental Clearance (EC) from MoEF&CC
- Forest land diverted without FC Act approval
- Mining > 5 hectares (requires EC)

**Violations:**
- EIA Notification 2006 (no EC)
- Forest Conservation Act 1980 (no approval)

**Remedy:**

**NGT Petition:**
- File application
- NGT orders:
  - Immediate suspension of mining
  - Penalty on lease holder
  - Restoration of mined area (reafforestation)

**Goa Foundation v. Sesa Sterlite (NGT/SC):**
- Illegal mining in Goa without renewals
- Mining suspended, penalties imposed

### Scenario 4: Air Pollution from Stubble Burning

**Facts:**
- Farmers burning crop residue (paddy stubble)
- Severe air pollution (PM2.5 > 500)
- Delhi air quality crisis

**Applicable Law:**
- Air Act Section 19 (prohibition on air pollution)
- EPA (violation of air quality standards)

**Remedy:**

**NGT Directions:**
- State governments directed to penalize farmers
- In-situ crop residue management incentives
- Enforcement of ban on stubble burning

**Environmental Compensation Charges (ECC):**
- Rs. 2,500 per acre (on violating farmers) - controversial implementation

### Scenario 5: Noise Pollution from Religious Place

**Facts:**
- Temple/mosque using loudspeakers beyond 10 PM
- Residential area (limit 45 dB at night)
- Measured 80 dB

**Violation:**
- Noise Pollution Rules 2000
- EPA Section 15

**Remedy:**

**Option 1: Complaint to Local Police/District Magistrate**
- Seizure of loudspeakers
- Fine

**Option 2: NGT Petition**
- Directions to comply with noise limits
- Penalty

**Church of God (Full Gospel) v. K.K.R. Majestic Colony (SC 2000):**
- Fundamental right to worship does NOT include right to use loudspeakers
- Noise pollution violates Article 21 (right to silence)

## Public Interest Litigation (PIL) for Environment

### Article 32 (SC) and Article 226 (HC): Writ Jurisdiction

**Locus Standi Relaxed:**
- Any concerned citizen can file PIL
- NGOs (environmental groups) frequently file
- Not necessary to be personally affected

**Landmark Environmental PILs:**

**1. M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Oleum Gas Leak - 1987):**
- Absolute liability rule for hazardous industries
- No defenses (not even Act of God)

**2. M.C. Mehta v. Union of India (Taj Trapezium - 1996):**
- 292 polluting industries around Taj Mahal ordered to close/relocate
- Use CNG instead of coal/coke

**3. M.C. Mehta v. Kamal Nath (Span Resorts - 1997):**
- Public Trust Doctrine applied
- Rivers, forests, seashores held in trust for public
- Government cannot alienate to private parties

**4. Vellore Citizens' Welfare Forum v. Union of India (1996):**
- Tanneries in Tamil Nadu polluting river
- Polluter pays principle, precautionary principle applied
- Industries ordered to pay compensation + close

**5. T.N. Godavarman Thirumulpad v. Union of India (1997-ongoing):**
- Forest conservation monitoring
- Nationwide ban on tree felling (until sustainable forest management)
- CEC (Central Empowered Committee) appointed to monitor

## Key Environmental Principles from Case Law

### 1. Absolute Liability (M.C. Mehta v. Union of India - 1987)

**Rule:**
- Enterprise engaged in hazardous/inherently dangerous activity
- Strictly and absolutely liable for harm
- No defenses (not even Act of God, third-party negligence)
- Liability proportionate to magnitude of enterprise

**Application:**
- Chemical plants, nuclear facilities, gas storage

### 2. Public Trust Doctrine (M.C. Mehta v. Kamal Nath - 1997)

**Rule:**
- Natural resources (air, water, forests, seashores) held by State in trust for public
- State as trustee cannot alienate to private parties
- State owes duty to protect for present and future generations

**Application:**
- Government cannot lease forests, rivers to private parties without ensuring public use

### 3. Intergenerational Equity (Vellore Citizens - 1996)

**Rule:**
- Present generation obligated to preserve environment for future generations
- Sustainable use (not depletion)

### 4. Doctrine of Proportionality

**Balancing Test:**
- Environmental protection vs economic development
- Neither absolute (balanced approach)
- Development permitted if:
  - Mitigation measures adequate
  - Compensatory measures (afforestation, payment)
  - Benefits outweigh environmental costs

## Common Mistakes

**1. Starting Project Without Environmental Clearance** → Violation, project halted, penalties

**2. Not Maintaining Consent Validity (Water/Air Act)** → Illegal operation, closure risk

**3. Not Installing Pollution Control Equipment** → Exceeding standards, penalties

**4. Diverting Forest Land Without FC Act Approval** → Illegal diversion, restoration cost

**5. Construction in CRZ/Eco-Sensitive Zone Without Clearance** → Demolition risk

**6. Not Filing NGT Application Within 6 Months** → Barred by limitation

**7. Ignoring SPCB Directions/Show-Cause Notices** → Deemed admission, closure orders

**8. Not Conducting EIA Study Properly** → EC challenge, cancellation

**9. Not Complying with EC Conditions (Post-Approval)** → EC revocation, bank guarantee forfeiture

**10. Assuming Pollution Fines are One-Time Cost** → Continuing daily penalties add up significantly
