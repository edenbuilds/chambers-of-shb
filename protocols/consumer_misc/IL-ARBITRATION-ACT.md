# Indian Consumer & Misc Law: Arbitration & Conciliation

## Purpose
Comprehensive guidance on Arbitration and Conciliation Act 1996 covering arbitration agreements, arbitral tribunal constitution, arbitral procedure, awards, enforcement, setting aside awards, international commercial arbitration, and mediation/conciliation.

## Activation Triggers
Use when: Commercial dispute with arbitration clause | Appointing arbitrator | Challenging arbitrator | Interim measures in arbitration | Arbitral award enforcement | Setting aside award | International arbitration (seat in India) | Conciliation/mediation | Emergency arbitrator | Court intervention in arbitration.

## Core Principles

### Section 7: Arbitration Agreement

**"Agreement to submit present/future disputes to arbitration"**

**Forms:**
1. **Arbitration Clause** - In main contract ("Disputes resolved by arbitration")
2. **Separate Agreement** - Standalone submission agreement

**Requirements:**
- **In Writing** (signed document OR exchange of letters/emails/fax)
- **Clear Intent** to arbitrate
- **Defined Disputes** (present OR future)

**Example Clause:**
```
"Any dispute arising out of or in connection with this contract shall be 
finally resolved by arbitration under Arbitration and Conciliation Act 1996. 
The seat of arbitration shall be New Delhi. The language shall be English. 
The arbitral tribunal shall consist of [one/three] arbitrator(s)."
```

**Validity:** Arbitration agreement survives termination of main contract (Section 16)

### Section 8: Power to Refer to Arbitration

**If arbitration agreement exists:**
- Party can apply to court to refer dispute to arbitration
- Court SHALL refer (mandatory - no discretion) if:
  - Valid arbitration agreement exists
  - Dispute arbitrable

**Timeline:** Apply BEFORE first statement on substance of dispute

**Effect:** Court proceedings stayed, matter referred to arbitration

## Arbitrability (What Can Be Arbitrated)

**Arbitrable Disputes (Section 2(3)):**
- Commercial disputes (contracts, partnerships, joint ventures)
- Civil disputes

**NON-Arbitrable:**
- Criminal offenses
- Matrimonial disputes (divorce, custody)
- Insolvency/winding up
- Disputes affecting third-party rights (tenancy eviction)
- Frauds (if complex - may require court investigation)

## Arbitral Tribunal Constitution

### Number of Arbitrators (Section 10)

**Parties' Choice:**
- 1 arbitrator (sole arbitrator) OR
- 3 arbitrators (usual for large disputes) OR
- Panel (odd number)

**Default:** 1 arbitrator (if parties do not agree)

### Appointment (Section 11)

**By Agreement:**
- Parties agree on appointment procedure

**If No Agreement:**

**Sole Arbitrator:**
- Parties jointly appoint OR
- If fail, application to court (High Court/Supreme Court)

**3 Arbitrators:**
- Each party appoints 1 arbitrator
- 2 arbitrators appoint 3rd (presiding arbitrator)
- If fail, court appoints

**Court Appointment:**
- High Court (for domestic arbitration seated in India)
- Supreme Court (for international commercial arbitration)
- Arbitral institution (if parties agree - e.g., MCIA, Delhi SIAC)

**Timeline:** Court appoints within 60 days

### Qualifications (Section 11(8) - for certain arbitrations)

**For disputes > Rs. 1 crore (if parties opt):**
- Experience as High Court Judge OR
- Senior advocate (15+ years) OR
- Specialized expertise in subject matter

### Challenge to Arbitrator (Section 12-13)

**Grounds:**
- **Bias/Partiality** - Justifiable doubts about independence/impartiality
- **Lack of Qualifications** - Does not meet agreed qualifications

**Disclosure:** Arbitrator must disclose any circumstances likely to give rise to doubts (Section 12)

**Procedure:**
- Challenge within 15 days of becoming aware
- If arbitrator does not withdraw → Arbitral tribunal decides
- If challenge rejected → Party can apply to court (within 30 days)

## Arbitral Procedure

### Seat vs Venue

**Seat:** Legal place of arbitration (determines applicable law, court jurisdiction)

**Venue:** Physical place of hearings (can be different from seat)

**Example:** Seat: New Delhi (Indian law applies); Venue: Mumbai (hearings conducted in Mumbai for convenience)

### Procedure (Section 18-27)

**Party Autonomy (Section 19):**
- Parties free to agree on procedure
- Subject to Part I of Act (mandatory provisions)

**Default Procedure:**
- Statement of Claim (Claimant)
- Statement of Defense (Respondent)
- Documentary evidence
- Oral hearings OR written submissions (tribunal's discretion)
- Witness examination

**Timeline:**
- Award within **12 MONTHS** from tribunal constitution (extendable by 6 months by consent OR court)

**Section 29A - Fast Track Arbitration:**
- If parties agree: 6-month timeline
- Sole arbitrator
- Documents-only (no oral hearings unless parties request)

### Interim Measures (Section 9, 17)

**Section 9 - Court's Power (Before/During Arbitration):**
- Interim injunction
- Appointment of receiver
- Preservation of subject matter
- Securing amount in dispute

**Section 17 - Tribunal's Power (After constitution):**
- Tribunal can order interim measures (same as court under Section 9)
- 2015 Amendment: Tribunal's interim orders enforceable like court orders

## Arbitral Award

### Types of Awards (Section 31)

**1. Final Award:** Deciding all issues

**2. Interim Award:** Partial award on some issues

**3. Award on Agreed Terms (Consent Award):** Parties settle, tribunal records settlement as award

### Contents (Section 31)

**Award Must:**
- Be in writing
- Signed by arbitrators (majority if 3 arbitrators)
- State reasons (unless parties waive)
- State date and place (seat)
- Be delivered to each party

### Costs (Section 31A - 2015 Amendment)

**Costs Follow the Event:**
- Loser pays winner's costs (unless unreasonable to do so)
- Costs = Arbitrator fees + legal fees + administrative costs

**Factors:**
- Conduct of parties
- Whether party's claim/defense was frivolous
- Whether party offered settlement

## Challenge to Award (Section 34)

**Setting Aside Award (Within 3 MONTHS + 30 days condonable):**

**Grounds (Narrow):**

**A. Party Proves (Section 34(2)(a)):**
- **Incapacity** of party
- **Invalid arbitration agreement**
- **Not given proper notice** or unable to present case
- **Award beyond scope** of submission to arbitration
- **Tribunal not constituted** as per agreement/Act

**B. Court Finds (Section 34(2)(b)):**
- **Subject matter not arbitrable** under Indian law
- **Award contrary to public policy** of India

**Public Policy (Section 34(2A) - Narrow):**
- Fraud/corruption
- Violation of fundamental policy of Indian law (not just legal error)
- Conflict with basic notions of morality/justice
- Patently illegal award (post-2015 amendment)

**NOT Grounds for Setting Aside:**
- Errors of fact (arbitrator's findings binding)
- Errors of law (unless patently illegal)
- Different interpretation

**Timeline:**
- Application within 3 months of award (extendable by 30 days)
- Court disposes within 1 year (2015 amendment - expedited)

---

## Enforcement of Award (Section 36)

**Domestic Awards:**

**After 3 Months (from Award):**
- If no setting aside application → Award enforceable like court decree
- Execute under CPC (attachment, sale)

**If Setting Aside Application Pending:**
- Court may stay enforcement (if prima facie case)
- But party must deposit entire award amount OR furnish security (2015 amendment)

**Foreign Awards (Section 44-60):**

**New York Convention (1958):**
- India signatory
- Foreign arbitral awards (from Convention countries) enforceable in India

**Procedure:**
- File application to enforce foreign award (in High Court)
- Court may refuse enforcement ONLY if grounds under Article V of Convention (similar to Section 34)

**Geneva Convention Awards (Section 53-60):**
- Older regime (pre-New York Convention)
- Rarely invoked

---

## International Commercial Arbitration (Section 2(1)(f), Part II)

**Definition:** Arbitration between parties having place of business in different countries

**Seat in India:**
- Part I applies (Indian Arbitration Act governs procedure)
- Indian courts have supervisory jurisdiction

**Seat Outside India:**
- Part II applies (limited court intervention)
- Only enforcement of foreign awards (not supervision)

**UNCITRAL Model Law:**
- Act based on UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration
- Harmonized with international standards

---

## Conciliation (Part III - Section 61-81)

**Alternative to Arbitration:**

**Procedure:**
- Party sends written request to conciliate
- Other party accepts → Conciliation commenced
- Conciliator appointed (1 OR 2-3 conciliators)
- Conciliator assists parties to reach settlement (not binding)

**Conciliator's Role:**
- Neutral facilitator (not arbitrator)
- Hears both sides, suggests settlement terms
- Non-binding recommendations

**Settlement:**
- If settlement reached → Settlement agreement signed
- Enforceable like arbitral award (if parties request)

**Confidentiality:**
- Conciliation proceedings confidential
- Cannot be used as evidence in arbitration/court

**Termination:**
- Settlement reached OR
- Conciliator/party terminates (if settlement not possible) OR
- 180 days elapsed without settlement

---

## Institutional Arbitration

**Ad Hoc vs Institutional:**

**Ad Hoc:**
- Parties appoint arbitrators directly
- No administering institution
- Flexible but may face procedural issues

**Institutional:**
- Arbitration administered by institution (ICC, SIAC, MCIA, DIAC, LCIA)
- Institution appoints arbitrators (if parties don't agree)
- Institutional rules apply (unless modified)
- Administrative support, case management

**Indian Institutions:**
- **Mumbai Centre for International Arbitration (MCIA)**
- **Delhi International Arbitration Centre (DIAC)**
- **Arbitration centres under state laws**

**International Institutions:**
- **ICC (International Chamber of Commerce - Paris)**
- **SIAC (Singapore International Arbitration Centre)**
- **LCIA (London Court of International Arbitration)**
- **HKIAC (Hong Kong International Arbitration Centre)**

## Advantages of Arbitration vs Litigation

| Aspect | Arbitration | Litigation |
|--------|-------------|------------|
| **Confidentiality** | Private (confidential) | Public (open court) |
| **Party Autonomy** | Parties choose arbitrators, procedure, seat | Court, procedure statutory |
| **Expertise** | Technical experts as arbitrators | Generalist judges |
| **Speed** | Usually faster (12 months target) | Slow (3-10 years) |
| **Flexibility** | Flexible procedure | Rigid procedure (CPC) |
| **Finality** | Limited appeal (setting aside - narrow grounds) | Multiple appeals |
| **Enforceability** | International (New York Convention) | Domestic only |
| **Cost** | Arbitrator fees (high for large disputes) | Court fees (lower) |

## Common Scenarios

**Scenario 1: Appointing Arbitrator When Other Party Non-Cooperative**

**Facts:**
- Contract has arbitration clause: "Disputes resolved by 3 arbitrators"
- Dispute arises, Party A appoints arbitrator
- Party B refuses to appoint

**Remedy:**
- Party A applies to High Court under Section 11
- Court appoints arbitrator on behalf of Party B
- Timeline: 60 days

**Scenario 2: Award Challenged on Ground of Legal Error**

**Facts:**
- Arbitrator passes award
- Party claims arbitrator made error of law

**Analysis:**
- Error of law NOT ground for setting aside (unless patently illegal)
- Section 34 grounds narrow
- If interpretation reasonable (even if debatable) → Award upheld

**Likely Outcome:** Challenge dismissed

**Scenario 3: Emergency Arbitrator for Urgent Interim Relief**

**Facts:**
- Dispute arises, arbitration to be commenced
- Party needs urgent interim relief (prevent asset disposal)
- Tribunal not yet constituted

**Remedy:**
- Apply for emergency arbitrator (if institutional rules provide - SIAC, ICC)
- Emergency arbitrator appointed within 24-48 hours
- Passes interim order (valid until tribunal constituted)

**Alternative:** Court under Section 9

## Common Mistakes

**1. Poorly Drafted Arbitration Clause** → Disputes over seat, number of arbitrators, procedure

**2. Not Appointing Arbitrator Timely** → Delay (court appointment takes months)

**3. Not Raising Jurisdictional Objection Early (Section 16)** → Waived (must raise before statement on substance)

**4. Seeking Setting Aside for Legal Errors** → Narrow grounds (patently illegal standard high)

**5. Not Depositing Award Amount When Challenging** → Award enforceable despite challenge

**6. Not Choosing Institutional Arbitration** → Ad hoc difficulties (appointment, administration)

**7. Ignoring Fast Track Option** → Slower timeline (12 months vs 6 months)

**8. Not Agreeing on Costs Consequences** → Winner may not recover full costs

**9. Delay in Filing Enforcement Application** → Limitation may bar

**10. Not Specifying Seat Clearly** → Disputes over applicable law, court jurisdiction
