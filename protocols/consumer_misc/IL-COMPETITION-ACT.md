# Indian Consumer & Misc Law: Competition Law (Anti-Trust)

## Purpose
Comprehensive guidance on Competition Act 2002 covering anti-competitive agreements (cartels, vertical restraints), abuse of dominant position, merger control (combinations), CCI procedures, leniency program, remedies, and competition compliance for businesses.

## Activation Triggers
Use when: Cartel investigation | Price-fixing allegations | Abuse of dominant position | Merger/acquisition (above thresholds) | Exclusive dealing arrangements | Resale price maintenance | Predatory pricing | Bid-rigging | Leniency application | CCI investigation response | Competition compliance audit.

## Core Principles

### Section 3: Anti-Competitive Agreements (PROHIBITED)

**Two Types:**

#### A. Horizontal Agreements (Section 3(3)) - PRESUMED to have AAEC (Adverse Effect on Competition)

**Between competitors at same level:**

**(a) Price-fixing** - Directly/indirectly determining purchase/sale prices

**Example:** 5 cement companies agree to sell cement at Rs. 400/bag (cartel) → Prohibited

**(b) Bid-rigging/Collusive Bidding** - Manipulating tender process

**Example:** 3 contractors agree not to bid against each other in government tenders → Prohibited

**(c) Output Limitation** - Limiting/controlling production/supply

**Example:** Steel manufacturers agree to reduce production to increase prices → Prohibited

**(d) Market Allocation** - Sharing markets by territory/customers

**Example:** 2 FMCG companies agree "Company A sells in North, Company B in South" → Prohibited

**PRESUMPTION:** These agreements presumed to have AAEC (burden on parties to prove otherwise)

#### B. Vertical Agreements (Section 3(4)) - Rule of Reason Analysis

**Between parties at different levels (manufacturer-distributor-retailer):**

**(a) Tie-in Arrangement** - Condition that buyer must purchase other goods

**Example:** Printer manufacturer requires buyers to purchase only its ink cartridges → May be prohibited (if AAEC)

**(b) Exclusive Supply** - Buyer must buy only from one supplier

**Example:** Retailer agrees to stock only Coca-Cola products (not Pepsi) → Analyzed under rule of reason

**(c) Exclusive Distribution** - Supplier sells only to one distributor in territory

**Example:** Car manufacturer appoints single dealer in city → Analyzed under rule of reason

**(d) Refusal to Deal** - Refusing to supply goods/services

**Example:** Dominant telecom company refuses interconnection to new entrant → May be prohibited

**(e) Resale Price Maintenance (RPM)** - Fixing resale price

**Example:** Manufacturer mandates retailers sell product at Rs. 1,000 (no discounting) → Prohibited if AAEC

**Test:** Does agreement cause AAEC? (Analyzed case-by-case, not presumed)

**AAEC Factors (Section 19(3)):**
- Creation of barriers to entry
- Denial of market access
- Foreclosure of competition
- Accrual of benefits to consumers
- Improvements in production/distribution

### Section 4: Abuse of Dominant Position (PROHIBITED)

**Dominant Position (Section 4 + Section 19(4)):**
- Position of strength enabling independent action
- Affecting competitors/consumers
- Market share >50% (rebuttable presumption of dominance)

**Abuse Examples:**

**(a) Unfair/Discriminatory Prices** - Predatory pricing, excessive pricing

**Example:** Telecom company prices below cost to eliminate competitors (predatory) → Prohibited

**(b) Limiting Production/Services** - Reducing output to increase prices

**(c) Denial of Market Access** - Refusing to deal with competitors

**Example:** Dominant OS manufacturer refuses to license to hardware makers → May be abuse

**(d) Leveraging** - Using dominance in one market to enter/protect position in another

**Example:** Dominant search engine ties its browser (leveraging search dominance to browser market) → May be abuse

**(e) Exclusive Supply/Refusal to Deal**

**Test:** Does conduct amount to abuse? (Dominance itself not illegal; abuse is)

**Relevant Market Definition (Section 2(r), (s), (t)):**
- **Product Market** - Substitutable products
- **Geographic Market** - Area where conditions of competition homogeneous
- **Relevant Market** = Product Market × Geographic Market

**Example:**
- Product market: Smartphones (not all mobile phones)
- Geographic market: India
- Relevant market: Smartphone market in India

## Section 5-6: Combinations (Merger Control)

**Combination = Merger, Amalgamation, Acquisition**

**Thresholds (Assets/Turnover - Section 5):**

**Domestic Threshold:**

**Assets:**
- Acquirer + Target combined assets in India > Rs. 2,000 crores OR
- Acquirer + Target combined assets worldwide > USD 1 billion (with India assets > Rs. 550 crores)

**Turnover:**
- Acquirer + Target combined turnover in India > Rs. 6,000 crores OR
- Acquirer + Target combined turnover worldwide > USD 3 billion (with India turnover > Rs. 1,650 crores)

**Group Assets/Turnover Aggregated**

**Exemptions (Section 6):**
- Acquisition of shares <25% (if no control)
- Intra-group transactions
- Banking/insurance sector consolidation (if approved by regulator)

**Notification:**
- **Within 30 days** of trigger event (execution of agreement OR Board approval)
- Form I (short form) OR Form II (detailed)
- Fee: Rs. 10-50 lakhs (depending on deal value)

**CCI Review:**
- Phase I: 30 working days (can approve OR refer to Phase II)
- Phase II: 180 days (detailed investigation)

**Approval:**
- Unconditional approval OR
- Conditional approval (with modifications/commitments) OR
- Disapproval (if AAEC)

**Gun-Jumping:** Implementing combination before CCI approval → Penalty (up to 1% of assets/turnover)

## CCI (Competition Commission of India) - Procedures

### Investigation (Section 26)

**Initiation:**
- **Information** filed by any person/consumer/association OR
- **Suo motu** (CCI's own motion) OR
- **Reference** by statutory authority

**Director General (DG) Investigation:**
- CCI refers matter to DG
- DG investigates (60-90 days, extendable)
- DG Report submitted to CCI

**Search and Seizure (Section 41):**
- DG can search premises
- Seize documents, computers

**Leniency/Settlement (Section 46):**
- Cartel member can apply for leniency (immunity/reduction in penalty)
- **First applicant:** 100% penalty waiver (if full cooperation)
- **Subsequent applicants:** Up to 50% reduction

**CCI Order (Section 27):**
- After DG report + hearing
- **If violation found:**
  - Cease and desist order
  - Penalty: Up to 10% of average turnover for 3 preceding years
  - Structural remedies (divestiture - rare)

**Appeal:**
- To NCLAT (National Company Law Appellate Tribunal) within 60 days
- Further appeal to Supreme Court

---

## Penalties

**Section 27 - Penalty for Anti-Competitive Agreements/Abuse:**
- **Enterprises:** Up to 10% of average turnover for 3 preceding years
- **Individuals (cartels):** Up to Rs. 1 crore OR 3 times profit (whichever higher)

**Section 43A - Penalty for Gun-Jumping (Combinations):**
- Up to 1% of total assets OR turnover (whichever higher)

**Section 42 - Penalty for Non-Compliance:**
- Failure to comply with CCI orders/DG investigation
- Rs. 1 crore OR imprisonment up to 3 years OR both

## Competition Compliance

### Compliance Program Elements:

1. **Policy:** Written competition law policy
2. **Training:** Regular training for employees (sales, procurement, pricing)
3. **Due Diligence:** Pre-M&A competition assessment
4. **Red Flags:** Price discussions with competitors, market allocation
5. **Reporting:** Whistleblower mechanism
6. **Monitoring:** Regular audits

### Do's and Don'ts:

**DON'T:**
- Discuss prices with competitors
- Participate in trade association meetings discussing prices/output
- Engage in bid-rigging
- Impose RPM without justification
- Abuse dominant position (if dominant)

**DO:**
- Compete independently
- Unilateral pricing decisions
- Notify combinations above thresholds
- Seek legal advice for vertical restraints
- Apply for leniency if cartel involvement discovered

## Key Cases

**Shamsher Kataria v. Honda Siel (2014):** RPM by Honda (fixed spare parts prices) → CCI found abuse of dominance

**CCI v. Coordination Committee of Artists (2017):** Film producers' association boycotted multiplex (collective refusal to deal) → Anti-competitive agreement

**Matrimony.com v. Google (2018):** Google alleged to abuse dominance in search market by favoring own services

---

## Common Mistakes

**1. Not Notifying Combination Above Thresholds** → Gun-jumping penalty

**2. Implementing Combination Before CCI Approval** → Violation, penalty

**3. Price Discussions with Competitors** → Cartel evidence (even informal)

**4. RPM Without Economic Justification** → Vertical restraint violation

**5. Not Applying for Leniency (If Cartel Participant)** → Miss immunity opportunity
