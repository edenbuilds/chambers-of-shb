# Protocol: Judgments and Decrees (India)

**Protocol ID**: IL-CPC-JUDGMENTS-DECREES  
**Domain**: Civil Procedure  
**Subdomain**: Trial and Judgment  
**Version**: 1.0.0  
**Last Updated**: 2025-12-06

## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on civil judgments and decrees under the Code of Civil Procedure 1908, including types, requirements, rectification, and enforcement distinctions.

## Activation Triggers

- User asks "What is difference between judgment and decree?"
- Need to draft/understand judgment
- Decree preparation required
- Rectification of clerical errors needed
- Judgment interpretation questions
- Execution vs appeal timeline queries

## Core Principles

### 1. Judgment vs Decree Distinction

```
JUDGMENT (Section 2(9)):
"The statement given by the Judge on the grounds of a decree or order"

= Reasons, findings, analysis, decision-making process
= WHY court decided the way it did
= Narrative form (discussion, reasoning)

DECREE (Section 2(2)):
"The formal expression of an adjudication which, so far as regards the Court 
expressing it, conclusively determines the rights of the parties with regard 
to all or any of the matters in controversy in the suit"

= WHAT court decided
= Operative part (relief granted/denied)
= Executable (can be enforced through execution proceedings)

Relationship:
Judgment → Process → Decree
(Reasoning) → (Court decides) → (Formal order)

Example:
Judgment: 50-page document explaining why plaintiff proved case
Decree: "Defendant shall pay Rs. 10 lakhs to plaintiff"
```

### 2. Decree Characteristics (Section 2(2))

```
For an order to be "DECREE", it must:

1. Be formal expression of adjudication
2. Conclusively determine rights of parties
3. Relate to matters in controversy in the SUIT
4. Be by court

NOT Decrees:
- Orders (interim/interlocutory)
- Orders in execution proceedings
- Orders in appeals (unless finally disposing appeal - then becomes "decree")
```

## JUDGMENT REQUIREMENTS (Order XX)

### Mandatory Contents (Order XX Rule 4)

```
Rule 4: Judgment shall contain:

1. CONCISE STATEMENT OF CASE

Brief summary:
- Parties (plaintiff, defendant)
- Nature of claim (money, property, injunction, etc.)
- Nature of defense (denial, affirmative defense)

2. POINTS FOR DETERMINATION

List of issues framed:
- Issue No. 1: [Question]
- Issue No. 2: [Question]
- ...

3. DECISION ON EACH ISSUE WITH REASONS

For each issue:
- Finding (affirmative/negative)
- Reasons (analysis of evidence, law, precedents)

Example:
"Issue No. 1: Whether plaintiff proves that defendant owes Rs. 10 lakhs?

EVIDENCE: Plaintiff examined 2 witnesses... Defendant examined 1 witness...
ANALYSIS: Contract dated 15/01/2024 (Exhibit P-1) is admitted. Delivery receipts 
(Exhibit P-2) prove supply. Defendant claims payment by cheque, but cheque 
(Exhibit D-1) is for different transaction as per bank statement...
FINDING: Issue No. 1 is answered in the AFFIRMATIVE. Plaintiff has proved 
defendant owes Rs. 10 lakhs."

4. FINAL ORDER

Conclusion:
- Suit decreed/dismissed
- Relief granted/denied
- Costs

5. SIGNATURE AND DATE

- Judge's signature
- Date of pronouncement

OPTIONAL (but recommended):
- Summary of pleadings
- Summary of evidence (witness-wise)
- Discussion of legal provisions and case law
```

### Judgment Types

#### A. Final Judgment

```
Judgment that finally disposes suit
→ Nothing remains except execution (or appeal)

Example:
Judgment in money suit: "Suit decreed. Defendant to pay Rs. 10 lakhs to plaintiff."
→ Final (nothing more to adjudicate)
```

#### B. Judgment Declaring Rights (Preliminary)

```
Judgment that declares rights but does not finally dispose suit
→ Requires further proceedings (final decree application)

Example:
Partition suit judgment: "Plaintiff and defendant each entitled to 1/2 share"
→ Preliminary (actual partition/division still to be done)
→ After partition, final decree passed
```

### Judgment Pronouncement (Order XX Rule 1-3)

```
Rule 1: Judgment pronounced in OPEN COURT

Two Modes:

1. IMMEDIATE PRONOUNCEMENT:
   - After final arguments
   - Judge delivers judgment orally
   - Typed/written transcript prepared later (within few days)

2. RESERVED JUDGMENT:
   - Judge reserves judgment for detailed consideration
   - Date of pronouncement fixed (parties informed)
   - On fixed date, judgment read/handed down in court

Presence of Parties:
- Not mandatory (judgment pronounced whether parties present or not)
- Parties/advocates informed of date
- Copy given to parties on application

Operative Date:
- Judgment takes effect from DATE OF PRONOUNCEMENT
- Limitation for appeal runs from pronouncement date (not date judgment signed/typed)
```

### Judgment Correction (Order XX Rule 6, Section 152 CPC)

```
Section 152: Clerical or Arithmetical Errors Amendable

Court may amend:
- Clerical errors (typing mistakes, name misspellings)
- Arithmetical errors (calculation mistakes in decree amount)
- Errors arising from accidental slip or omission

Procedure:
1. Application by party OR court suo motu
2. Notice to opposite party
3. Hearing (if objection)
4. Order correcting error

Examples of Amendable Errors:

✓ Plaintiff's name typed as "Ramesh Gupta" instead of "Ramesh Kumar" (clerical)
✓ Decree amount calculated as Rs. 9 lakhs instead of Rs. 10 lakhs (interest miscalculation) (arithmetical)
✓ Judgment says "suit decreed" but decree mistakenly drawn as "suit dismissed" (clerical)

NOT Amendable (Substantive Errors):

✗ Changing finding on issue (from affirmative to negative)
✗ Granting different relief than awarded in judgment
✗ Altering reasoning or ratio decidendi

Remedy for Substantive Errors:
- Review (Order 47) - if error apparent on face of record
- Appeal (Section 96-100)
```

## DECREE REQUIREMENTS (Order XX Rule 6-7)

### Decree Contents (Order XX Rule 6)

```
Decree shall contain:

1. NUMBER OF SUIT
2. NAMES OF PARTIES (full description as in plaint)
3. PARTICULARS OF CLAIM (brief)
4. RELIEF GRANTED OR REFUSED (clear and specific)
5. COSTS (amount or direction for assessment)
6. DATE (of pronouncement)
7. SIGNATURE (of judge)
8. SEAL (of court)

Clarity Requirement:
Decree must be CLEAR and SPECIFIC
→ Can be executed without reference to judgment
→ No ambiguity in what defendant must do

Example of CLEAR Decree:
"The Defendant shall pay to the Plaintiff:
(a) Rs. 10,00,000/- (Principal)
(b) Interest @ 9% per annum on Rs. 10,00,000/- from 01/03/2023 till realization
(c) Costs of suit assessed at Rs. 50,000/-"

Example of AMBIGUOUS Decree (DEFECTIVE):
"The Defendant shall pay reasonable amount to Plaintiff"
→ What is "reasonable"? Decree not executable.
```

### Decree Preparation Timeline (Order XX Rule 7)

```
Rule 7: Decree drawn up within 15 DAYS of pronouncement of judgment 
(if practicable)

Procedure:
1. Judgment pronounced
2. Court staff prepares decree draft (based on judgment)
3. Judge signs decree
4. Seal affixed
5. Decree filed in court records

Certified Copy:
- Parties apply for certified copy of decree
- Required for execution or appeal
- Fee payable (as per court fee rules)
```

## TYPES OF DECREES

### 1. Preliminary Decree (Order XX Rule 2(1))

```
Definition (Section 2(2)): Decree passed in suit but does not completely dispose 
the suit, requiring further proceedings to be taken

Characteristics:
- Declares rights
- But does not finally conclude suit
- Further application/proceedings needed

When Passed:

A. Partition Suits:
   Preliminary Decree: Declares shares of co-owners
   Further Proceedings: Actual partition (division of property)
   Final Decree: After partition complete, allocating specific portions

B. Suits for Account:
   Preliminary Decree: Directs defendant to file account
   Further Proceedings: Account filed, objections, Commissioner examines
   Final Decree: After account settled, for balance amount due

C. Redemption of Mortgage:
   Preliminary Decree: Right to redeem established, amount to be paid determined
   Further Proceedings: Payment deadline given
   Final Decree: After payment, property conveyed to plaintiff OR if no payment, 
                 foreclosed

D. Specific Performance with Alternatives:
   Preliminary Decree: Directs defendant to perform within time
   Further Proceedings: If defendant performs → Suit disposed
   Final Decree: If defendant fails to perform → Decree for alternative relief 
                 (compensation)

Effect:
- Preliminary decree ALONE not executable
- Only FINAL decree executable
- Appeal lies from preliminary decree (but court may wait for final decree)
```

### 2. Final Decree (Order XX Rule 2(2))

```
Definition: Decree that finally disposes suit (nothing remains except execution)

Characteristics:
- Suit completely disposed
- All issues decided
- Relief granted/denied
- Executable

Examples:
- Money decree in loan recovery suit
- Injunction decree
- Declaration decree
- Final decree in partition (after preliminary decree and partition complete)

Effect:
- Executable (can be enforced through execution proceedings)
- Appeal lies (within limitation)
```

### 3. Partly Preliminary, Partly Final Decree (Order XX Rule 2(3))

```
Decree that is:
- Final as to some reliefs
- Preliminary as to other reliefs

Example:
Suit for possession + mesne profits (rent for wrongful possession)

Decree:
- Possession granted → FINAL (executable immediately)
- Mesne profits to be ascertained → PRELIMINARY (Commissioner appointed to calculate)
- After calculation, further decree for mesne profits amount → FINAL

Effect:
- Final part executable immediately
- Preliminary part requires further proceedings
```

### 4. Consent Decree (Order XXIII Rule 3)

```
Decree based on compromise/settlement between parties

Procedure:
1. Parties reach settlement
2. Joint application/memo of compromise filed
3. Court examines:
   - Whether parties understand terms
   - Whether compromise lawful (not opposed to public policy, not fraud on statute)
   - Whether parties have capacity to compromise
4. Court records compromise
5. Decree passed as per compromise terms

Characteristics:
- Binding on parties (contract + court order)
- Executable like regular decree
- Appeal limited:
  * Can appeal if compromise vitiated by fraud/coercion/misrepresentation/undue influence
  * CANNOT appeal challenging compromise terms themselves (parties voluntarily agreed)

Advantages:
- Quick disposal
- No lengthy trial
- Parties control outcome
- Costs saved

Disadvantages (for third parties):
- If fraud on creditors/statute → Third parties can challenge
- Example: Debtor and creditor collude in consent decree to defeat other creditors
```

### 5. Ex Parte Decree (Order IX Rule 6)

```
Decree passed when defendant does not appear despite service

Conditions:
1. Summons duly served on defendant
2. Defendant fails to appear on hearing date
3. Plaintiff proves claim (evidence)
4. Court satisfied plaintiff entitled to relief

Procedure:
1. Defendant absent on hearing date
2. Plaintiff produces proof of service (affidavit + acknowledgment)
3. Plaintiff examines witnesses and proves case
4. Court passes ex parte decree

Setting Aside (Order IX Rule 13):
Defendant can apply within 30 days from knowledge of decree:
Grounds:
- Summons not duly served, OR
- Prevented by sufficient cause from appearing

If set aside:
- Suit restored
- Defendant files written statement
- Trial proceeds from that stage
```

### 6. Conditional Decree

```
Decree granting relief subject to condition

Examples:

A. Money Decree with Time to Pay:
"Defendant shall pay Rs. 10 lakhs to plaintiff within 6 months. In default, 
decree executable immediately."

B. Specific Performance with Option:
"Defendant shall execute sale deed within 3 months. If fails, plaintiff 
entitled to Rs. 5 lakhs compensation in lieu."

C. Injunction with Undertaking:
"Defendant restrained from construction, provided plaintiff deposits Rs. 1 lakh 
as security (to compensate defendant if ultimately suit dismissed on appeal)."

Effect:
- Defendant gets opportunity to comply
- If complies → Suit disposed
- If fails → Decree executable for default consequence
```

## JUDGMENT INTERPRETATION

### Ratio Decidendi vs Obiter Dicta

```
RATIO DECIDENDI:
- Reason for decision
- Legal principle on which decision based
- BINDING as precedent (on lower courts)

Example:
Issue: Whether suit barred by limitation?
Finding: Suit filed within 3 years from cause of action (01/01/2020 to 01/01/2023)
Ratio: Suit within limitation under Article 113 Limitation Act
→ This ratio binds lower courts in similar cases

OBITER DICTA:
- Remarks made in passing
- Not necessary for decision
- NOT binding (only persuasive)

Example:
Judge remarks: "Even if suit were barred by limitation, Section 5 condonation 
would apply as delay was only 10 days due to plaintiff's illness"
→ Obiter (hypothetical observation, not necessary for actual decision)
→ Not binding precedent
```

### Operative Part (Decretal Portion)

```
Operative Part of Judgment:
- Conclusion section
- "IT IS ORDERED AND DECREED..."
- What court directs parties to do

This is what gets executed
- Not reasoning or analysis
- Only operative order

Example:
Judgment has 40 pages of reasoning
Operative Part (last paragraph):
"Suit decreed. Defendant to pay Rs. 10 lakhs + interest + costs to plaintiff."

Execution based on: Operative part (decree)
Appeal challenging: Reasoning (if erroneous) leading to operative part
```

## DECREE EXECUTION IMPLICATIONS

### When Decree Executable (Order XXI)

```
Conditions for Execution:

1. Decree must be FINAL (not preliminary)
2. Decree must have become FINAL:
   - No appeal filed within limitation (90 days), OR
   - Appeal filed and dismissed, OR
   - Appeal filed and withdrawn
3. Decree must be for specific relief (money/property/specific act)

NOT Executable:
- Preliminary decree (until final decree passed)
- Decree pending appeal (unless court grants stay)
- Declaratory decree (no specific relief - only declaration of rights)

Example:
Money Decree:
"Defendant to pay Rs. 10 lakhs to plaintiff"
→ Executable (specific relief - money)

Declaratory Decree:
"Plaintiff declared owner of property"
→ NOT executable (only declaration, no specific relief like possession)
→ Plaintiff can file fresh suit for possession based on declaration
```

### Decree Satisfaction

```
Decree Satisfied:
- Judgment debtor performs decree
- Pays money/delivers property/performs act

Certificate of Satisfaction:
- Decree-holder (plaintiff) files application
- States decree fully satisfied
- Court issues certificate
- Decree cannot be executed again

Partial Satisfaction:
- Decree-holder receives part payment
- Files statement of adjustment
- Decree executable for balance amount only
```

## COMMON SCENARIOS

### Scenario 1: Preliminary vs Final Decree (Partition)

```
Facts:
- Plaintiff and defendant co-own ancestral property
- Plaintiff sues for partition

Judgment:
- Court holds plaintiff entitled to 1/2 share, defendant 1/2 share

Preliminary Decree:
"It is declared that plaintiff and defendant are each entitled to 1/2 share 
in the suit property. Matter referred to Commissioner to effect partition."

Further Proceedings:
- Commissioner surveys property
- Prepares partition plan
- Divides into two equal portions (Portion A, Portion B)
- Files report

Final Decree (after partition):
"Portion A (as per Commissioner's report) allotted to plaintiff. Portion B 
allotted to defendant. Plaintiff and defendant shall execute mutual deeds 
conveying respective portions."

Effect:
- Only final decree executable (for possession if required)
- Preliminary decree not executable (only declares shares)
```

### Scenario 2: Consent Decree

```
Facts:
- Plaintiff sues defendant for Rs. 10 lakhs
- During trial, parties reach settlement: Defendant pays Rs. 7 lakhs

Compromise Application:
Joint application filed:
"The parties have amicably settled the dispute. Defendant agrees to pay 
Rs. 7,00,000/- to plaintiff in full and final settlement. Plaintiff waives 
remaining Rs. 3,00,000/-."

Court Examination:
- Parties appear before court
- Court confirms understanding of terms
- Court records compromise

Consent Decree:
"Suit decreed on the basis of compromise between parties. Defendant shall 
pay Rs. 7,00,000/- to plaintiff within 30 days. In default, decree executable. 
No order as to costs."

Effect:
- Binding contract + court order
- Executable if defendant defaults
- Limited grounds for appeal (fraud/coercion, not challenging terms themselves)
```

### Scenario 3: Correction under Section 152

```
Judgment:
"Suit decreed for Rs. 10,00,000/- (principal) + interest @ 9% per annum from 
01/03/2023 till realization."

Decree (Typed by Court Staff):
"Defendant shall pay Rs. 9,00,000/- + interest @ 9% per annum..."

Error: Rs. 9 lakhs instead of Rs. 10 lakhs (arithmetical/clerical error)

Application under Section 152:
- Plaintiff applies to correct error
- Shows judgment clearly states Rs. 10 lakhs
- Decree incorrectly typed as Rs. 9 lakhs

Court Order:
"Error in decree corrected. Rs. 9,00,000/- substituted with Rs. 10,00,000/-."

Effect:
- Decree amended to match judgment
- No appeal needed (mere correction of clerical error)
```

### Scenario 4: Ex Parte Decree Setting Aside

```
Facts:
- Suit filed; summons served on defendant
- Defendant does not appear
- Ex parte decree passed for Rs. 10 lakhs

Defendant's Application (Order IX Rule 13):
Filed within 30 days of knowledge of decree
Grounds:
- Summons not properly served (affixed on door, but defendant was out of station)
- Defendant has valid defense on merits (payment made)

Court Hearing:
- Defendant shows summons not properly served (service defective)
- OR shows prevented by sufficient cause (illness with medical certificate)
- Defendant shows prima facie defense (not frivolous)

Court Order:
"Ex parte decree set aside. Defendant shall file written statement within 
30 days. Suit restored to board. Costs of Rs. 10,000/- imposed on defendant 
(to compensate plaintiff for wasted time)."

Effect:
- Decree vacated
- Suit restored from point where defendant should have appeared
- Trial proceeds with defendant's participation
```

## CASE LAW

### 1. Madan Mohan Pathak v. Chander Bhan Kailashpati (SC 1992)
```
Facts: Distinction between judgment and decree
Held:
- Judgment = Reasons (process of deciding)
- Decree = Formal order (what is decided)
- Decree must follow judgment
- Appeal lies from decree, not judgment alone
```

### 2. Radhey Shyam v. Chhabi Nath (SC 2015)
```
Facts: Correction under Section 152; scope limited to clerical errors
Held:
- Section 152 allows correction of clerical/arithmetical errors only
- NOT for substantive errors (changing findings, reasoning)
- Substantive errors: Review (Order 47) or Appeal
```

### 3. Tek Bahadur Bhujel v. Debi Singh Bhujel (SC 2005)
```
Facts: Consent decree challenged on ground of fraud
Held:
- Consent decree is contract + court order
- Appeal limited: Can challenge if fraud/coercion/misrepresentation/undue influence
- Cannot appeal challenging terms of compromise themselves (parties agreed voluntarily)
- Burden on appellant to prove fraud/coercion
```

### 4. Ram Charan Das v. Girja Nandini Devi (SC 1966)
```
Facts: Preliminary decree in partition; whether executable
Held:
- Preliminary decree NOT executable (Order XX Rule 2)
- Only declares rights, does not finally dispose suit
- Execution only after final decree (after actual partition)
```

### 5. Ajit Singh v. Gopal Singh (SC 1993)
```
Facts: Judgment without reasons for findings on issues
Held:
- Order XX Rule 4: Judgment MUST contain reasons for decision on each issue
- Judgment without reasons violates CPC
- Such judgment liable to be set aside
- Matter remanded for fresh judgment with proper reasons
```

### 6. K.K. Modi v. K.N. Modi (SC 1998)
```
Facts: Decree ambiguous; not clear what defendant must do
Held:
- Decree must be clear and specific (Order XX Rule 6)
- Ambiguous decree not executable
- Decree must be executable without referring to judgment
- Vague decree liable to be struck down
```

## SUMMARY CHECKLIST

### Judgment Checklist:

```
☐ Concise statement of case (parties, claim, defense)
☐ Issues framed listed
☐ Decision on EACH issue with reasons
☐ Evidence summary (plaintiff's, defendant's)
☐ Analysis of law and precedents
☐ Final order (suit decreed/dismissed)
☐ Costs decision
☐ Signature and date of judge
☐ Pronounced in open court
```

### Decree Checklist:

```
☐ Suit number
☐ Parties' names and descriptions
☐ Particulars of claim
☐ Relief granted/refused (CLEAR and SPECIFIC)
☐ Costs (amount or direction for assessment)
☐ Date of pronouncement
☐ Judge's signature
☐ Court seal
☐ Drawn up within 15 days of judgment (if practicable)
☐ No ambiguity (executable without reference to judgment)
```

## COMMON MISTAKES

```
Mistake 1: Judgment Without Reasons
❌ "Issue No. 1 answered in affirmative. Issue No. 2 answered in negative."
✓ Give detailed reasons WHY each issue decided that way (evidence, law, analysis)

Mistake 2: Ambiguous Decree
❌ "Defendant to pay reasonable amount to plaintiff"
✓ "Defendant to pay Rs. 10,00,000/- to plaintiff"

Mistake 3: Decree Not Following Judgment
❌ Judgment decrees suit for Rs. 10 lakhs; decree drawn for Rs. 8 lakhs
✓ Decree MUST exactly reflect judgment's operative part

Mistake 4: Attempting to Execute Preliminary Decree
❌ Plaintiff applies to execute preliminary decree in partition
✓ Wait for final decree (after partition); only final decree executable

Mistake 5: Using Section 152 for Substantive Correction
❌ Apply under Section 152 to change finding on issue from affirmative to negative
✓ Section 152 only for clerical/arithmetical errors; substantive errors → Review/Appeal

Mistake 6: Not Giving Certified Copy of Decree for Execution
❌ Attempt to execute based on judgment or decree photocopy
✓ Obtain CERTIFIED COPY of decree from court for execution

Mistake 7: Confusing Decree and Order
❌ Treat interlocutory order as decree and attempt execution
✓ Only "decree" (as defined in Section 2(2)) executable; orders generally not executable
```
