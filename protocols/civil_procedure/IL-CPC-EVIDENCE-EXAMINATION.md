# Protocol: Evidence and Examination of Witnesses (India)

**Protocol ID**: IL-CPC-EVIDENCE-EXAMINATION  
**Domain**: Civil Procedure  
**Subdomain**: Evidence and Discovery  
**Version**: 1.0.0  
**Last Updated**: 2025-12-06

## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on examination of witnesses, recording of evidence, and evidence procedures in civil suits under the Code of Civil Procedure 1908 and Evidence Act 1872.

## Activation Triggers

- User asks "How to examine witnesses in civil court?"
- Need to prepare for witness examination
- Recording evidence in civil trial
- Cross-examination strategy needed
- Challenge to witness credibility
- Documentary evidence procedures

## Core Principles

### 1. Evidence = Proof of Facts

```
Evidence (Section 3 Evidence Act):
- All statements permitted by court (oral evidence)
- All documents produced for inspection (documentary evidence)

Purpose:
- Prove facts in issue
- Relevant facts
- Establish/disprove claim/defense

Rule: "He who asserts must prove" (Section 101-102 Evidence Act)
→ Burden of proof on party asserting fact
→ Standard: Preponderance of probabilities (civil); Beyond reasonable doubt (criminal)
```

### 2. Examination Stages (Order XVIII Rule 4)

```
Three-Stage Examination:

1. EXAMINATION-IN-CHIEF:
   - By party calling witness
   - Witness states facts in support of case
   - Leading questions NOT allowed (except formal/undisputed)

2. CROSS-EXAMINATION:
   - By opposite party
   - Test credibility, challenge testimony
   - Leading questions ALLOWED

3. RE-EXAMINATION:
   - By party who called witness
   - Clarify/explain matters arising in cross-examination
   - Leading questions NOT allowed
   - Limited to cross-examination scope
```

## WITNESS EXAMINATION PROCEDURE (Order XVIII)

### Summoning Witnesses (Order XVI)

```
Order XVI Rule 1: Summons to Witness

When Issued:
- Party applies for summons
- Court issues summons commanding witness to attend

Summons Contains:
- Name and address of witness
- Date, time, place of appearance
- Whether for examination OR to produce documents
- Consequence of non-attendance (warrant, fine, contempt)

Service (Rule 2):
- By court process server
- Registered post
- Witness must be tendered reasonable expenses (Batta) - Rule 3

Compulsion (Rule 10):
- If witness fails to attend → Warrant of arrest
- Fine up to Rs. 500
- Attachment of property
- Contempt proceedings

Exemptions (Rule 6):
- President, Governor: Not compelled to attend
- Other dignitaries: Commission to record evidence at their place
```

### Examination-in-Chief (Order XVIII Rule 4)

```
Purpose:
- Witness states facts supporting party's case
- Foundation of party's evidence

Procedure:

1. Oath/Affirmation (Section 136 Evidence Act):
   - Every witness sworn before examination
   - Form: "I swear in the name of God that I will state the truth, the whole 
     truth, and nothing but the truth"
   - Affirmation (for non-believers): "I solemnly affirm..."

2. Witness Identifies Himself:
   - Name, age, occupation, address
   - Relationship to parties (if any)

3. Examination by Calling Party:
   - Questions by advocate of party calling witness
   - Witness answers orally
   - Court records testimony

4. Leading Questions Prohibited (Section 141-143):
   - Leading Question = Suggests answer
   - Example (LEADING): "Did defendant pay you Rs. 5 lakhs?" → Suggests "yes"
   - Example (NON-LEADING): "What amount, if any, did defendant pay you?"

   Exceptions (Leading Allowed):
   - Formal matters (name, age, address)
   - Undisputed matters
   - Hostile witness (with court permission)
   - Contradicting witness with prior statement

5. Evidence Recorded (Rule 5):
   - Judge's dictation (verbatim or narrative form)
   - Typed/written
   - Read to witness for confirmation
   - Witness signs each page

Modern Practice:
- Examination-in-chief by AFFIDAVIT (allowed by many courts to save time)
- Witness files affidavit (pre-prepared testimony)
- At trial, witness affirms affidavit
- Straight to cross-examination
```

**Examination-in-Chief Affidavit Format** (Modern Practice):

```
IN THE COURT OF [COURT NAME]

Suit No. ___/20__

AFFIDAVIT OF [WITNESS NAME] (PLAINTIFF'S WITNESS No. __)

I, [Name], aged [__] years, S/o [Father's name], residing at [Address], do 
hereby solemnly affirm and state as follows:

1. I am the [plaintiff/defendant/witness] in the above suit and am well 
   acquainted with the facts and circumstances of the case.

2. [State facts in chronological narrative - para-wise]

3. I have perused the documents annexed to this affidavit marked as Exhibit 
   PW1/1, PW1/2, ... and state that the same are true copies and I am 
   conversant with the contents thereof.

4. I have read and understood the contents of this affidavit and the same are 
   true to my knowledge and belief.

DEPONENT

Verification:
I, the deponent above named, verify that the contents of paragraphs 1 to __ 
are true to my personal knowledge, and no part of it is false and nothing 
material has been concealed.

Verified at [Place] on this __ day of ____, 20__.

DEPONENT
```

### Cross-Examination (Order XVIII Rule 4, Section 137-146 Evidence Act)

```
Purpose:
- Test credibility of witness
- Elicit facts favorable to cross-examining party
- Discredit/impeach witness testimony
- Challenge version given in examination-in-chief

Rights of Cross-Examiner (Section 138):

1. Test Veracity (Credibility):
   - Previous inconsistent statements
   - Character for untruthfulness (with limitations)
   - Bias, interest, motive to lie

2. Shake Credit:
   - Prior convictions (Section 145)
   - Prior inconsistent statements (Section 145)
   - Imputation of bad character (with caution - may allow re-examination)

3. Elicit Facts Favorable to Cross-Examiner's Case:
   - Leading questions ALLOWED (Section 142)
   - Can put propositions (witness may deny)

Leading Questions Allowed:
- "Is it not true that you were not present at the scene?"
- "You received Rs. 10 lakhs from defendant, correct?"
- "Isn't it a fact that you have a dispute with plaintiff?"

Limitations:

1. Cannot Ask Irrelevant Questions:
   - Court may disallow scandalous/vexatious questions (Section 151)

2. Cannot Insult/Annoy Witness:
   - Questions must be pertinent
   - Cannot harass unnecessarily

3. Privilege Protections (Section 121-132):
   - Cannot ask about privileged communications (lawyer-client, state secrets)
   - Self-incrimination (Article 20(3), Section 132)

Techniques:

1. Prior Inconsistent Statements (Section 145):
   - Put: "In your police statement dated DD/MM/YYYY, you said [X]. Today you 
     say [Y]. Which is correct?"
   - If witness denies inconsistency → Prove by producing statement

2. Omissions:
   - "You mentioned [fact] today. But in your affidavit, you did not mention 
     this. Why?"

3. Improbabilities:
   - "You claim you saw accused's face from 100 meters at night without street 
     lights. How is that possible?"

4. Interest/Bias:
   - "You are plaintiff's brother, correct?"
   - "You have a pending lawsuit against defendant yourself, right?"

5. Contradictions with Documents:
   - "This receipt shows payment on 15/01. But you say payment was on 20/01. 
     Explain."
```

### Re-Examination (Order XVIII Rule 4, Section 137 Evidence Act)

```
Purpose:
- Clarify matters raised in cross-examination
- Rehabilitate witness (if credibility shaken)
- Explain apparent contradictions

Scope (Section 137):
"Re-examination may be directed to explanation of matters referred to in 
cross-examination; and if new matter is, by permission of Court, introduced 
in re-examination, opposite party may further cross-examine upon that matter."

Restrictions:

1. Limited to Cross-Examination Scope:
   - Cannot introduce new facts NOT touched in cross-examination
   - Can only clarify/explain cross-examination matters

2. Leading Questions NOT Allowed:
   - Same as examination-in-chief

3. Court Permission for New Matter:
   - If advocate wants to introduce new fact → Seek court permission
   - If allowed → Opposite party gets right to further cross-examine on that new matter

Example:

Cross-Examination:
Q: "You said payment was Rs. 5 lakhs. But receipt shows Rs. 3 lakhs. Explain."
A: "Receipt is for partial payment only."

Re-Examination (Clarifying):
Q: "You mentioned partial payment. Please explain fully."
A: "Rs. 3 lakhs by cheque (receipt given). Rs. 2 lakhs in cash (no receipt as 
    defendant insisted on cash)."

Result: Clarification of apparent contradiction raised in cross-examination.
```

### Recall of Witness (Order XVIII Rule 17)

```
Rule 17: Court may, at any stage, recall witness for further examination

Grounds:
- To clear doubt on any point
- New facts emerged requiring clarification
- Interests of justice

Procedure:
- Party applies OR court suo motu
- Witness re-summoned
- Further examination (may be by court itself)

Effect:
- Testimony continues (not fresh examination)
- Previous testimony stands unless contradicted/explained
```

## TYPES OF EVIDENCE

### A. Oral Evidence (Section 59-60 Evidence Act)

```
Section 59: All facts, except contents of documents, must be proved by oral evidence

Oral Evidence = Statements of witnesses in court

Must Be:
- DIRECT: Witness testifies about facts personally perceived (seen/heard/touched)
- NOT HEARSAY (generally): Witness cannot testify about what someone else told him
  (Exception: Section 32-33 - dying declaration, admissions, etc.)

Example (ADMISSIBLE Direct Evidence):
Witness: "I saw defendant sign the contract on 15/01/2024 in my presence"

Example (INADMISSIBLE Hearsay):
Witness: "My friend told me defendant signed contract" → Hearsay (unless friend is examined)
```

### B. Documentary Evidence (Section 61-90A Evidence Act)

```
Documentary Evidence = Documents produced for court inspection

Types:

1. Public Documents (Section 74-78):
   - Government records, gazettes, registered deeds, court judgments
   - Proved by certified copy (Section 76)

2. Private Documents (Section 67-73):
   - Contracts, letters, receipts, agreements
   - Proved by:
     * Original + attesting witness (Section 68)
     * OR handwriting comparison (Section 73)
     * OR admission of execution (Section 69)

3. Electronic Records (Section 65B IT Act):
   - Emails, digital signatures, computer printouts
   - Proved by Section 65B certificate (MANDATORY - Arjun Panditrao)

Primary Evidence (Section 62):
- Original document itself

Secondary Evidence (Section 63):
- Certified copy, photocopy, oral account
- Allowed ONLY if original:
  * In possession of opposite party (and not produced after notice)
  * Lost/destroyed
  * Cannot be moved (e.g., inscription on building)
  * Public document (certified copy permissible)
```

### C. Expert Evidence (Section 45-51 Evidence Act)

```
Section 45: Opinion of Expert Admissible

When Expert Opinion Relevant:
- Foreign law
- Science, art, handwriting, fingerprints
- Valuations (property, damages)
- Medical opinion

Who is Expert?
Person with specialized knowledge/skill beyond ordinary person

Examples:
- Handwriting expert (for signature verification)
- Medical expert (for injury causation)
- Engineer (for construction defects)
- Chartered Accountant (for financial analysis)

Procedure:
1. Party summons expert witness
2. Expert examines subject matter (document/property/records)
3. Expert prepares report
4. Expert testifies in court (examination-in-chief, cross, re-examination)
5. Court considers expert opinion (not binding, but persuasive)

Limitation (Section 45 proviso):
Expert opinion is OPINION, not fact
Court NOT bound to accept (may reject if finds expert unreliable/biased)
```

### D. Circumstantial Evidence

```
Circumstantial Evidence = Evidence of circumstances from which fact in issue can be inferred

Example:
Fact in Issue: Whether defendant was at scene on 15/01
Circumstantial Evidence:
- Defendant's car seen near scene at that time
- Defendant's fingerprints found at scene
- Witness saw defendant leaving scene

Standard (Sharad Birdhichand Sarda, SC 1984):
"Circumstances must be:
1. Conclusive in nature
2. Exclude every hypothesis except the one to be proved
3. Complete chain (no missing link)"

Civil Suits:
- Preponderance of probabilities sufficient (unlike criminal "beyond reasonable doubt")
```

## HOSTILE WITNESS (Section 154 Evidence Act)

```
Section 154: Court may permit leading questions if witness HOSTILE

Hostile Witness:
- Witness who, though called by party, gives evidence adverse to that party
- OR refuses to answer questions
- OR contradicts earlier statement

Procedure:

Step 1: Party Realizes Witness Hostile
- Witness giving contrary evidence than expected

Step 2: Apply to Court for Permission
- Request to treat witness as hostile
- Court grants permission (discretion)

Step 3: Leading Questions Allowed
- Party who called witness can ask leading questions
- Cross-examine own witness (to discredit)

Step 4: Contradict with Prior Statement (Section 145)
- Show previous statement (e.g., affidavit, police statement)
- Confront witness with inconsistency

Effect:
- Witness's testimony may be rejected/given less weight
- Party may adduce other evidence to prove fact
```

## RECORDING OF EVIDENCE (Order XVIII Rule 5)

### Judge's Dictation

```
Traditional Method (Rule 5(1)):
- Judge dictates evidence
- Stenographer/clerk types
- Read to witness for confirmation
- Witness signs

Modern Practice:
- Electronic recording (audio/video) - permitted by some courts
- Stenography (STENO)
- Transcript prepared and verified

Essential Requirements:
1. Evidence recorded in presence of parties/advocates
2. Witness confirms accuracy
3. Witness signs each page (Rule 5(2))
4. Judge signs record (Rule 5(2))

Language:
- English (in higher courts)
- Vernacular (in lower courts, if witness does not understand English)
- Interpreter used if needed
```

### Evidence by Affidavit (Order XIX Rule 1)

```
Modern Practice (to expedite trials):
Court may order evidence by AFFIDAVIT (instead of oral examination-in-chief)

Procedure:
1. Witness files affidavit (pre-prepared testimony)
2. Affidavit served on opposite party
3. At trial, witness called
4. Witness affirms affidavit contents
5. Straight to cross-examination (no examination-in-chief)

Advantages:
- Saves time (no lengthy examination-in-chief)
- Evidence clear and structured

Disadvantages:
- Witness may not remember all details (if affidavit prepared by advocate)
- Cross-examination more difficult (witness may rely on "as stated in affidavit")

Court's Power (Rule 2):
- Court may require witness to attend for cross-examination
- Court may require further clarification beyond affidavit
```

## COMMISSION EVIDENCE (Order XVI Rule 6-16)

```
When Ordered:
- Witness resides outside court's jurisdiction
- Witness unable to attend (illness, age, other sufficient cause)
- Dignitary (President, Governor, etc.)

Procedure:

Step 1: Application for Commission
- Party applies stating witness details and reason for commission

Step 2: Court Issues Commission
- Letter of request to court in witness's area
- OR appointing commissioner (advocate/officer)

Step 3: Commission Executed
- Commissioner examines witness (examination-in-chief, cross, re-examination)
- Evidence recorded
- Advocates of both parties may attend (if commission in India)

Step 4: Return of Commission
- Commissioner sends report and evidence to court
- Evidence read and treated as if recorded in court

Limitation:
- Less effective than in-person examination (cannot gauge demeanor)
- But necessary if witness genuinely cannot attend
```

## BURDEN OF PROOF (Section 101-106 Evidence Act)

```
Section 101: Burden of Proof on Party Asserting Fact

General Rule: "HE WHO ASSERTS MUST PROVE"

Plaintiff's Burden:
- Prove all facts necessary to establish claim
- If fails → Suit dismissed

Defendant's Burden:
- Prove affirmative defenses (payment, limitation, res judicata, etc.)
- If fails → Defense fails

Exceptions (Statutory Presumptions):

Section 79-85: Presumptions as to Documents
- Section 79: Certified copies of public documents presumed genuine
- Section 81-85: Gazettes, government acts, court proceedings presumed genuine

Section 111-114: Burden of Proof in Certain Cases
- Section 111: Burden on party claiming relationship (if possession with another)
- Section 114(g): Adverse inference if evidence withheld

Illustration:
Suit for money recovery:
- Plaintiff proves: (1) Loan advanced (2) Demand made (3) No payment
- Defendant claims: "I repaid"
  → Burden on DEFENDANT to prove payment (Section 106 - fact especially within knowledge)
```

## ADMISSIONS AND CONFESSIONS (Section 17-23 Evidence Act)

```
Section 17: Admission = Statement suggesting inference as to fact in issue or relevant fact

Types:

1. Judicial Admissions:
   - Made in pleadings, affidavits, court proceedings
   - Binding on party (cannot be contradicted without amendment)
   - Example: Defendant admits in written statement "Contract was signed"
     → No need for plaintiff to prove contract (admitted fact)

2. Extra-Judicial Admissions:
   - Made outside court (letters, conversations, emails)
   - NOT conclusive (can be explained/denied)
   - Admissible as evidence

Section 18: By Whom Admitted
- Party OR person whose position party represents (legal representative, partner, agent)

Section 21: Proof of Admissions
- Oral admissions proved by calling person to whom admission made
- Documentary admissions proved by producing document

Effect:
- Admission shifts burden (but not conclusive in civil suits)
- Can be explained/retracted with valid reason
```

## COMMON OBJECTIONS TO EVIDENCE

```
1. "Leading Question" (Section 141):
   - During examination-in-chief or re-examination
   - Court may disallow

2. "Irrelevant" (Section 5-55):
   - Question not related to facts in issue/relevant facts
   - Court may disallow

3. "Hearsay" (Section 60):
   - Witness testifying about what someone else said (not personally perceived)
   - Generally inadmissible (unless exception applies - Section 32-33)

4. "Privileged Communication" (Section 121-132):
   - Lawyer-client, state secret, self-incrimination
   - Court may sustain objection

5. "Asked and Answered":
   - Question already asked and answered (repetition)
   - Court may disallow

6. "Fishing Expedition":
   - Vague/speculative question to elicit information without basis
   - Court may disallow

7. "Argumentative":
   - Question arguing with witness (not seeking facts)
   - Court may disallow

8. "Compound Question":
   - Multiple questions in one
   - Court may ask to break into separate questions

9. "Assuming Facts Not in Evidence":
   - Question assumes fact not yet proved
   - Court may disallow
```

## Common Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Witness Turns Hostile

```
Facts:
- Plaintiff calls witness (expected to support case)
- Witness gives contrary evidence in examination-in-chief

Procedure:

Step 1: Apply to Court
- Advocate: "My Lord, I seek permission to treat this witness as hostile"

Step 2: Court Grants Permission
- Court: "Permission granted under Section 154 Evidence Act"

Step 3: Cross-Examine Own Witness
- Leading questions allowed
- Confront with prior statement:
  "In your affidavit dated DD/MM/YYYY, you stated [X]. Today you say [Y]. 
  Which is correct?"

Step 4: Prove Prior Inconsistent Statement
- Produce affidavit/police statement
- Mark as exhibit
- Witness may admit or deny prior statement

Effect:
- Witness's testimony may be disregarded
- Plaintiff may call other witnesses to prove fact
```

### Scenario 2: Objection to Leading Question

```
Examination-in-Chief:

Plaintiff's Advocate: "Did defendant pay you Rs. 5 lakhs on 15/01/2024?"

Defendant's Advocate: "Objection, My Lord. Leading question."

Court: "Sustained. Rephrase."

Plaintiff's Advocate: "What amount, if any, did defendant pay you?"

Witness: "Defendant paid me Rs. 5 lakhs."

Plaintiff's Advocate: "When?"

Witness: "On 15/01/2024."

Effect: Non-leading form elicits same facts without suggesting answer
```

### Scenario 3: Impeachment by Prior Inconsistent Statement

```
Cross-Examination:

Defendant's Advocate: "Mr. Witness, you testified that the contract was signed 
in Mumbai, correct?"

Witness: "Yes."

Defendant's Advocate: "I put it to you that in your police statement dated 
05/03/2024, you stated the contract was signed in Delhi. Which is correct?"

Witness: "I don't recall saying Delhi. It was Mumbai."

Defendant's Advocate: "My Lord, I wish to prove prior inconsistent statement 
under Section 145 Evidence Act. I produce the witness's police statement marked 
as Exhibit DW1/1."

Court: "Admitted."

Defendant's Advocate (reading): "In your police statement, paragraph 3, you 
stated: 'The contract was signed at my office in Delhi on 15/01/2024.' Do you 
deny this?"

Witness: "I may have been mistaken in the police statement."

Effect: Witness's credibility shaken; inconsistency noted by court
```

### Scenario 4: Expert Opinion (Handwriting)

```
Facts:
- Defendant denies signature on contract
- Plaintiff calls handwriting expert

Examination-in-Chief (Expert Witness):

Q: "What is your qualification and experience?"
A: "I am a certified handwriting expert with 20 years' experience. I have 
    examined over 5,000 documents for courts."

Q: "Did you examine the disputed signature on the contract?"
A: "Yes, I examined the signature on the contract dated 15/01/2024 marked as 
    Exhibit P-1."

Q: "Did you compare it with admitted signatures of defendant?"
A: "Yes, I compared with defendant's signatures on his passport, PAN card, and 
    bank documents provided to me."

Q: "What is your opinion?"
A: "In my expert opinion, the disputed signature on the contract is GENUINE and 
    was executed by the defendant. The slope, pressure, and unique features 
    match the admitted signatures."

Cross-Examination:

Q: "Mr. Expert, is handwriting analysis 100% accurate?"
A: "No field is 100% accurate. But handwriting analysis, when done properly with 
    adequate samples, has high accuracy."

Q: "Have you ever been wrong?"
A: "In my career, there have been instances where experts differed in opinion."

Q: "So your opinion is not conclusive?"
A: "It is my expert opinion based on my training and experience. Court is free 
    to accept or reject."

Court's Decision:
- Expert opinion considered (persuasive, not binding)
- If corroborated by other evidence → Signature held genuine
- If contradicted → Court may reject expert opinion
```

## Case Law

### 1. Suresh Chandra Bahri v. State of Bihar (SC 1995)
```
Facts: Examination-in-chief by affidavit challenged
Held:
- Court can permit examination-in-chief by affidavit (Order XIX Rule 1)
- Saves time and expedites trials
- Witness must be available for cross-examination
- If cross-examination reveals inconsistencies, affidavit evidence may be discredited
```

### 2. Sharad Birdhichand Sarda v. State of Maharashtra (SC 1984)
```
Facts: Conviction based on circumstantial evidence
Held:
- Circumstantial evidence must form complete chain
- Each circumstance must point to guilt
- No other hypothesis except guilt should be possible
- (In civil suits, lower standard: preponderance of probabilities)
```

### 3. Bhagwati Prasad v. Chandramaul (SC 1966)
```
Facts: Leading questions in examination-in-chief
Held:
- Leading questions prohibited in examination-in-chief (Section 141)
- Exception: Undisputed facts, formal matters, hostile witness
- Court has discretion to allow/disallow
```

### 4. Ramesh Chandra Agrawal v. Regency Hospital (SC 2009)
```
Facts: Burden of proof in medical negligence case
Held:
- Plaintiff must prove: (1) Duty of care (2) Breach (3) Causation (4) Damage
- Burden on plaintiff to establish negligence
- If plaintiff proves prima facie case → Burden shifts to defendant to explain
```


## Common Mistakes

```
Mistake 1: Leading Questions in Examination-in-Chief
❌ "Did defendant sign the contract on 15/01/2024?" (Leading)
✓ "Who signed the contract and when?" (Non-leading)

Mistake 2: Not Objecting to Hearsay
❌ Allow witness to testify "My friend told me defendant said..."
✓ Object: "Hearsay, My Lord. Witness has no personal knowledge."

Mistake 3: Not Marking Documents as Exhibits
❌ Show document to witness but don't mark as exhibit
✓ After witness identifies document, request court to mark as Exhibit P-1/D-1

Mistake 4: Not Confronting Witness with Prior Inconsistent Statement
❌ Accept witness's changed testimony without challenge
✓ Produce prior statement under Section 145 and confront witness

Mistake 5: Failing to Lay Foundation for Expert Opinion
❌ Expert directly gives opinion without stating qualifications/examination
✓ First establish: Qualification → Examination conducted → Opinion

Mistake 6: Re-Examination Introducing New Matter Without Permission
❌ Introduce entirely new fact in re-examination
✓ Seek court permission if new matter; otherwise limit to cross-examination scope

Mistake 7: Not Applying for Hostile Witness Permission
❌ Continue examination-in-chief when witness clearly adverse
✓ Apply to court under Section 154 to treat as hostile → Cross-examine own witness
```
