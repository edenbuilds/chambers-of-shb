## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on special civil procedures under the Code of Civil Procedure 1908, including summary suits (Order XXXVII), interim injunctions (Order XXXIX), receivers (Order XL), and interpleader suits (Order XXXV).

## Activation Triggers

- User asks "What is summary suit procedure?"
- Need urgent interim injunction
- Temporary injunction required
- Appointment of receiver needed
- Multiple claimants to same property (interpleader)
- Speedy recovery for negotiable instruments

## Core Framework

### Special Procedures vs Regular Suits

```
REGULAR SUIT (Ordinary Procedure):
- Full trial procedure (Order XIV-XVIII)
- Evidence examination
- Timeline: 1-3 years (average)

SPECIAL PROCEDURES:
- Expedited procedures
- Limited scope
- Faster resolution
- Specific reliefs/situations

Types:
1. Summary Suit (Order XXXVII) - Speedy money recovery
2. Temporary Injunction (Order XXXIX) - Prevent imminent harm
3. Receiver (Order XL) - Protect disputed property
4. Interpleader (Order XXXV) - Resolve competing claims
```

## SUMMARY SUIT (Order XXXVII)

### Purpose and Scope (Rule 1)

```
Rule 1: Summary Suit for Recovery of Debt

Applicable To:
- Claims based on NEGOTIABLE INSTRUMENTS (promissory notes, bills of exchange, cheques)
- Debt/liquidated demand in writing
- Contracts (if expressly payable at certain time)

Geographical Limit:
- Only in areas where Order XXXVII applies (High Courts with original jurisdiction: Calcutta, Bombay, Madras, Delhi - specific notifications)

NOT Applicable To:
- Unliquidated claims
- Damages for breach of contract (unless liquidated)
- Regular money suits
- Areas where Order XXXVII not in force

Advantage:
- SPEEDY disposal (within 6 months typical)
- Defendant must obtain LEAVE to defend (not automatic right)
```

### Procedure for Summary Suit (Rule 2-6)

```
Step 1: PLAINT Filed (Rule 2)
Similar to regular plaint + special endorsement:
"This suit is filed under Order XXXVII of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908"

Accompanied by:
- Affidavit verifying debt
- Documents (promissory note, bill of exchange, contract)

Step 2: SUMMONS Issued (Rule 3)
Special summons:
- Requires defendant to appear on specified date
- States: "If you wish to defend the suit, you must apply for LEAVE TO DEFEND within 30 days"

Contents:
- Claim details
- Documents on which claim based
- Consequence of non-appearance (summary judgment)

Step 3: Defendant's Options

OPTION A: ADMIT CLAIM (No Response)
- If defendant does not respond within 30 days:
  → Plaintiff entitled to SUMMARY JUDGMENT (immediate decree without trial)
  → Decree passed on affidavit + documents (no evidence needed)

OPTION B: APPLY FOR LEAVE TO DEFEND (Rule 3)
Within 30 days:
- File affidavit showing:
  * Triable defense (not frivolous/vexatious)
  * Grounds to contest claim
- May be UNCONDITIONAL leave OR CONDITIONAL leave (deposit amount in court)

Step 4: Court Decides Leave Application (Rule 3)

Court Examines:
- Is there genuine dispute on merits?
- OR defendant trying to delay?

Options:

(a) REFUSE Leave to Defend:
    - No triable defense shown
    - Defense frivolous/sham
    → Pass SUMMARY DECREE (immediate decree without trial)

(b) GRANT Unconditional Leave to Defend:
    - Triable defense established
    → Convert to REGULAR SUIT (full trial procedure)

(c) GRANT Conditional Leave to Defend (Rule 3 proviso):
    - Defendant shows arguable defense BUT suspicious circumstances
    → Grant leave ON CONDITION: Deposit decree amount/part in court
    → If defendant deposits → Suit proceeds as regular
    → If defendant fails to deposit → Summary decree passed

Step 5: If Leave Granted
- Suit proceeds as REGULAR SUIT (written statement, issues, evidence, trial)
- No longer summary procedure

Step 6: If Leave Refused
- SUMMARY DECREE passed (immediate)
- Defendant can appeal (Order 43 - appealable order)
```

### Grounds for Leave to Defend

```
Defendant Must Show:

✓ TRIABLE DEFENSE (Genuine Dispute):
  - Promissory note forged/fabricated
  - Payment already made (with evidence)
  - Consideration failed
  - Debt time-barred (limitation expired)
  - Debt discharged (release agreement)

✗ NOT TRIABLE DEFENSE:
  - "I need time to verify records" (delaying tactic)
  - "I don't remember signing" (without showing forgery)
  - Vague/general denial without specifics
  - Technical defenses without merit

Standard:
Prima facie defense (not conclusive proof)
→ If defense arguable → Leave granted
→ If defense clearly sham → Leave refused
```

### Summary Judgment (Rule 5)

```
Summary Judgment = Decree without full trial

Passed When:
- Defendant does not apply for leave to defend (within 30 days), OR
- Defendant applies but leave refused

Basis:
- Plaintiff's affidavit + documents
- No oral evidence needed
- No witness examination

Effect:
- Executable decree (like regular decree)
- Defendant can appeal (against refusal of leave + summary decree)

Timeline:
- From plaint to summary decree: 2-4 months (if leave not sought/refused)
- Contrast with regular suit: 1-3 years
```

## TEMPORARY INJUNCTION (Order XXXIX)

### Purpose (Rule 1-2)

```
Temporary Injunction (Interim Injunction):
- Court order restraining party from doing act OR directing party to do act
- PENDING final decision in suit
- Prevents irreparable harm before trial

Types:

1. TEMPORARY INJUNCTION (Rule 1-2):
   - During pendency of suit (before final decree)
   - Preserves status quo
   - Prevents change in situation

2. PERPETUAL INJUNCTION (Sections 36-42 Specific Relief Act):
   - After trial, in final decree
   - Permanent restraint

Distinction:
Temporary = Interim relief (pending suit)
Perpetual = Final relief (in decree after trial)
```

### Conditions for Temporary Injunction (Rule 1-2)

```
Rule 1: Injunction to Restrain Repetition/Continuance of Breach

Rule 2: Injunction to Restrain Waste/Alienation

Court Grants Temporary Injunction If:

1. PRIMA FACIE CASE:
   - Plaintiff shows serious question to be tried
   - NOT conclusive proof; just arguable case
   
   Example: Property title disputed → Prima facie if plaintiff produces sale deed

2. BALANCE OF CONVENIENCE:
   - Greater hardship to plaintiff if injunction NOT granted
   - vs
   - Lesser hardship to defendant if injunction granted
   
   Comparison:
   - If injunction not granted → Plaintiff's property destroyed (irreparable)
   - If injunction granted → Defendant stops construction (can resume if plaintiff loses)
   → Balance favors plaintiff

3. IRREPARABLE INJURY:
   - Injury that cannot be compensated by money (damages inadequate)
   
   Examples:
   ✓ Irreparable: Destruction of ancestral property, trade secret disclosure, goodwill loss
   ✗ NOT Irreparable: Monetary loss (can be compensated)

ALL THREE Must Be Satisfied:
Prima facie + Balance of convenience + Irreparable injury
→ If any one fails → Injunction refused
```

### Procedure for Temporary Injunction (Rule 3)

```
Step 1: File APPLICATION (Interim Application)
With main suit OR during pendency

Contents:
- Grounds (prima facie case, balance, irreparable injury)
- Affidavit supporting application
- Specific relief sought (restraining order)

Step 2: Notice to Defendant (Rule 3)
- Court issues notice to defendant
- Time to file reply (usually 7-15 days)
- OR ex parte application (if urgency - defendant not given prior notice)

Step 3: Hearing
- Arguments by both parties
- Court examines three conditions

Step 4: Order
Options:

(a) GRANT Temporary Injunction:
    - Defendant restrained from [specific act]
    - Conditions: Plaintiff may have to:
      * Furnish security/deposit
      * Give undertaking for damages (if ultimately suit dismissed)
    - Valid till final decree OR earlier vacation

(b) REFUSE Temporary Injunction:
    - Not satisfied with three conditions
    - Plaintiff can seek reconsideration (if circumstances change)

(c) STATUS QUO Order:
    - Both parties maintain existing situation
    - Neither party changes status

Effect of Injunction:
- Defendant must comply (cannot do prohibited act)
- Breach → Contempt of court (Order XXXIX Rule 2A - imprisonment/fine)
```

### Ex Parte Temporary Injunction (Rule 3 proviso)

```
Ex Parte = Without notice to defendant

When Granted:
- EXTREME URGENCY (immediate irreparable harm)
- Delay in notice would defeat purpose

Example:
- Defendant demolishing plaintiff's house tomorrow
- No time to serve notice, hear defendant
→ Court may pass ex parte injunction (immediate restraint)

Procedure:
1. Plaintiff shows extreme urgency
2. Affidavit stating facts + urgency
3. Court may pass ex parte injunction

Duration:
- Very short (7-15 days typically)
- Defendant given notice immediately
- Hearing held within short time
- Court decides: Continue injunction OR vacate

Risk:
- If plaintiff's case weak → Defendant may claim damages for wrongful injunction
- Plaintiff may be required to furnish heavy security
```

### Vacation of Temporary Injunction (Rule 4)

```
Rule 4: Defendant may apply to vacate injunction

Grounds:
- Changed circumstances (facts different from when injunction granted)
- Plaintiff not prosecuting suit diligently
- Plaintiff's case weak (on further examination)

Procedure:
- Defendant files application
- Affidavit showing grounds
- Hearing
- Court decides: Vacate OR continue injunction

Court may also vacate SUO MOTU (on its own) if:
- Plaintiff delays trial unreasonably
- Injunction obtained by misrepresentation/suppression of facts
```

### Damages for Wrongful Injunction (Rule 2A)

```
Rule 2A: Damages if Injunction Wrongfully Obtained

If plaintiff obtains injunction AND ultimately suit dismissed:
→ Defendant may claim damages from plaintiff

Defendant must show:
- Injunction wrongfully obtained (plaintiff's case baseless)
- Defendant suffered loss due to injunction
- Loss quantifiable

Example:
Plaintiff obtained injunction stopping defendant's business
After trial, plaintiff's suit dismissed (no right to injunction)
Defendant lost Rs. 10 lakhs in business revenue
→ Defendant can claim Rs. 10 lakhs damages from plaintiff

Protection:
- Court may require plaintiff to furnish UNDERTAKING for damages before granting injunction
- Ensures defendant compensated if plaintiff fails
```

## RECEIVER (Order XL)

### Purpose (Rule 1)

```
Receiver = Court-appointed person to manage disputed property

When Appointed:
- Property subject to litigation (ownership disputed)
- Risk of waste/destruction/alienation by party in possession
- Preserve property till final decision

Receiver's Role:
- Take possession of property
- Manage property (collect rent, maintain)
- Preserve property value
- Prevent waste
- Act as court's officer (not party's agent)

Property Types:
- Immovable property (land, building)
- Movable property (goods, machinery)
- Business/company (in extreme cases)
- Rent/income from property
```

### Conditions for Appointment (Rule 1)

```
Court Appoints Receiver If:

1. DISPUTED OWNERSHIP:
   - Parties claiming same property
   - Unclear who rightful owner

2. RISK OF WASTE:
   - Party in possession may destroy/damage property
   - Property value diminishing

3. RISK OF ALIENATION:
   - Party may sell/transfer property to third party
   - Defeat other party's claim

4. PRESERVATION NECESSARY:
   - Property requires management (rental property, business)
   - Without receiver, property deteriorates

5. BALANCE OF CONVENIENCE:
   - Appointing receiver beneficial to both parties
   - Neutral management pending decision
```

### Procedure for Receiver (Rule 2-4)

```
Step 1: Application for Receiver
- Party applies (plaintiff or defendant)
- Affidavit showing:
  * Disputed ownership
  * Risk of waste/alienation
  * Necessity of receiver

Step 2: Notice to Opposite Party
- Court issues notice
- Opposite party may:
  * Consent
  * Object (no necessity, willing to furnish security)

Step 3: Hearing
- Court examines necessity
- Balances hardship

Step 4: Appointment Order
If satisfied:
- Court appoints receiver (advocate, chartered accountant, or court officer)
- Defines receiver's powers:
  * Take possession
  * Collect rent/income
  * Maintain property
  * File periodic reports to court

Step 5: Receiver's Oath (Rule 3)
- Receiver takes oath to discharge duties faithfully
- Acts as court's officer (impartial)

Step 6: Remuneration (Rule 4)
- Receiver entitled to remuneration (paid from property income/court-fixed)
- Expenses reimbursed
```

### Powers and Duties of Receiver (Rule 1)

```
Powers:
- Take possession of property
- Collect rent/income
- Initiate/defend suits related to property (with court permission)
- Sell property (only with court permission)
- Engage staff for property management

Duties:
- Preserve property
- File periodic accounts (income, expenses)
- Seek court direction for major decisions
- Act impartially (not favor any party)
- Hand over property to rightful owner after decree

Accountability:
- Receiver accountable to court
- Any misconduct → Removal + damages
```

### Discharge of Receiver (Rule 4)

```
Receiver Discharged When:
- Final decree passed (court decides rightful owner)
- Property handed over to decree-holder
- Parties reach settlement
- Court finds receiver no longer necessary

Final Account:
- Receiver files final account (income, expenses, balance)
- Court approves account
- Surplus paid to decree-holder
- Receiver discharged
```

## INTERPLEADER SUIT (Order XXXV)

### Purpose (Rule 1)

```
Interpleader = When two or more persons claim same property/debt from third party

Scenario:
- A holds property/money
- B claims: "That property is mine"
- C also claims: "No, it's mine"
→ A is STAKEHOLDER (holder of property)
→ B and C are CLAIMANTS

A files INTERPLEADER SUIT:
- Court decides between B and C
- A hands over property to rightful claimant
- A relieved from liability

Example:
- Tenant holds Rs. 1 lakh rent
- Landlord claims: "Pay me rent"
- Mortgagee claims: "Landlord mortgaged property to me; pay me rent"
→ Tenant files interpleader suit
→ Court decides: Landlord vs Mortgagee
→ Tenant pays rent to winner
```

### Conditions for Interpleader (Rule 1)

```
Interpleader Suit Lies If:

1. TWO OR MORE PERSONS claim same property/debt from stakeholder
2. Stakeholder has NO INTEREST in property (mere holder)
3. Stakeholder READY TO HAND OVER to rightful claimant
4. Stakeholder does NOT KNOW who rightful claimant is
5. Claimants' claims INDEPENDENT (not from same right)

NOT Interpleader If:
- Stakeholder has own interest in property
- Stakeholder knows rightful claimant (collusion)
- Only one claimant (regular suit)
```

### Procedure for Interpleader (Rule 1-5)

```
Step 1: Stakeholder Files PLAINT
- Names both/all claimants as defendants
- States: "I hold property; B and C both claim; I have no interest; I am ready to hand over to rightful claimant"
- Deposits property in court OR gives security

Step 2: Court Summons Claimants
- Both claimants summoned
- Required to state their claims

Step 3: Claimants File Written Statements
- Each claimant states basis of claim
- May dispute other claimant's claim

Step 4: Trial Between Claimants
- Stakeholder generally OUT of suit (after depositing property)
- Trial proceeds between claimants (like regular suit)
- Evidence examination

Step 5: Decree
- Court decides: Which claimant entitled to property
- Property/money paid to successful claimant
- Costs: Usually borne by claimants (stakeholder protected)

Stakeholder's Protection:
- If acted bona fide → Not liable for costs
- Protected from suit by either claimant (court's decision final)
```

## COMMON SCENARIOS

### Scenario 1: Summary Suit for Cheque Dishonor

```
Facts:
- Defendant issued cheque for Rs. 10 lakhs (promissory note)
- Cheque bounced
- Plaintiff sues under Order XXXVII (summary suit)

Procedure:
1. Plaint filed with affidavit + cheque copy
2. Summons issued: "Apply for leave to defend within 30 days"
3. Defendant's options:
   
   Option A: No response → Summary decree for Rs. 10 lakhs (within 2-3 months)
   
   Option B: Apply for leave to defend:
   Defendant shows: "Cheque was security for loan; loan never availed"
   Court decides: Triable defense → Grant unconditional leave → Convert to regular suit

   Option C: Apply for leave but weak defense:
   Defendant shows: "Need time to verify accounts"
   Court decides: Not triable defense → Refuse leave → Summary decree

Result: If leave refused → Decree in 3-4 months (vs 2-3 years for regular suit)
```

### Scenario 2: Temporary Injunction to Stop Construction

```
Facts:
- Plaintiff claims ownership of land
- Defendant constructing on same land
- Plaintiff files suit for possession + applies for temporary injunction

Three-Fold Test:

1. Prima Facie Case:
   - Plaintiff produces sale deed (1980)
   - Defendant has no documents
   → Prima facie case established

2. Balance of Convenience:
   - If injunction NOT granted: Defendant builds house (difficult to demolish later)
   - If injunction granted: Defendant stops construction (can resume if wins)
   → Balance favors plaintiff

3. Irreparable Injury:
   - If construction completed, demolition costly/impractical
   - Damages inadequate (plaintiff wants land, not money)
   → Irreparable injury shown

Court Order:
"Defendant restrained from constructing on suit property till final decree. 
Plaintiff to furnish security of Rs. 5 lakhs (for damages if ultimately suit dismissed)."

Effect:
- Defendant must stop construction
- If continues → Contempt (imprisonment/fine)
- Status quo maintained till trial complete
```

### Scenario 3: Receiver for Disputed Rental Property

```
Facts:
- Plaintiff and defendant both claim ownership of building (generating Rs. 2 lakhs/month rent)
- Defendant in possession, collecting rent
- Plaintiff fears defendant will dissipate rent, not maintain building

Application for Receiver:
- Plaintiff applies: "Appoint receiver to collect rent, maintain building"

Court Decision:
- Ownership disputed (trial needed - 1-2 years)
- Risk: Defendant may collect rent, not account for it
- Building needs maintenance (roof leaking)
→ Appoint receiver

Receiver Appointed:
- Advocate Mr. X appointed receiver
- Powers: Collect rent (Rs. 2 lakhs/month), pay for repairs, file monthly accounts

During Suit (2 years):
- Receiver collects Rs. 48 lakhs rent
- Spends Rs. 8 lakhs on repairs
- Balance: Rs. 40 lakhs (deposited in court)

After Decree (Plaintiff Wins):
- Court orders: Pay Rs. 40 lakhs + accrued rent to plaintiff
- Receiver discharged

If Defendant Had Won:
- Rs. 40 lakhs paid to defendant
- Receiver ensured fair management (regardless of winner)
```

### Scenario 4: Interpleader Suit

```
Facts:
- Bank holds fixed deposit (FD) of Rs. 20 lakhs in name of Mr. A (deceased)
- Mr. A's son (B) claims: "I am heir; pay me FD"
- Mr. A's brother (C) claims: "Mr. A executed will leaving FD to me; pay me"
→ Bank does not know who rightful claimant

Interpleader Suit:
1. Bank files interpleader suit naming B and C as defendants
2. Bank deposits Rs. 20 lakhs in court (or gives security)
3. Bank states: "I have no interest; ready to pay rightful claimant"

Trial Between B and C:
- B proves: He is son (succession certificate)
- C proves: Will executed by Mr. A leaving FD to C (will + attesting witnesses)
- Court examines will validity

Decree:
- If will valid → C gets Rs. 20 lakhs
- If will invalid/forged → B gets Rs. 20 lakhs
- Bank protected (paid to winner as per court order)
- Costs: B or C (whoever loses)

Bank's Protection:
- Cannot be sued later by loser (court decided)
- Acted bona fide (no costs on bank)
```

## CASE LAW

### 1. Shiv Kumar Chadha v. Municipal Corporation of Delhi (SC 1993)
```
Facts: Temporary injunction; three-fold test application
Held:
- Prima facie case + Balance of convenience + Irreparable injury → ALL three must be satisfied
- If any one fails → Injunction refused
- Courts must balance competing interests (plaintiff vs defendant hardship)
```

### 2. Mechelec Engineers v. Basic Equipment Corporation (SC 1976)
```
Facts: Summary suit; leave to defend
Held:
- Summary suit procedure for speedy disposal (Order XXXVII)
- Defendant must show TRIABLE defense to get leave to defend
- If defense sham/frivolous → Refuse leave → Summary decree
- Burden on defendant to show defense has substance
```

### 3. Dalpat Kumar v. Prahlad Singh (SC 1992)
```
Facts: Receiver appointed; powers and duties
Held:
- Receiver is court's officer (acts impartially)
- Preserves property pending final decision
- Accountable to court (files periodic reports)
- Any misconduct → Removal + liability for damages
```

### 4. Ramesh B. Desai v. Bipin Vadilal Mehta (SC 2006)
```
Facts: Interpleader suit; stakeholder's protection
Held:
- Interpleader suit protects bona fide stakeholder from competing claims
- Stakeholder must have no interest in property (mere holder)
- Trial between claimants (stakeholder generally out after depositing property)
- Costs usually borne by claimants (stakeholder protected if acted bona fide)
```

## COMMON MISTAKES

```
Mistake 1: Filing Summary Suit for Unliquidated Claim
❌ File summary suit for damages (unliquidated)
✓ Summary suit ONLY for liquidated debts/negotiable instruments

Mistake 2: Not Satisfying Three-Fold Test for Injunction
❌ Apply for injunction showing prima facie case but no irreparable injury
✓ MUST show ALL three: Prima facie + Balance + Irreparable injury

Mistake 3: Not Depositing Property in Interpleader Suit
❌ File interpleader suit but retain property/money
✓ Deposit property in court OR give security (essential for stakeholder protection)

Mistake 4: Treating Receiver as Party's Agent
❌ Assume receiver works for plaintiff/defendant
✓ Receiver is COURT'S OFFICER (acts impartially for both parties)

Mistake 5: Not Applying for Leave to Defend in Time (Summary Suit)
❌ Apply for leave to defend after 30 days
✓ Apply WITHIN 30 days from summons (strict timeline)

Mistake 6: Breaching Temporary Injunction
❌ Defendant ignores injunction order (does prohibited act)
✓ Comply strictly; breach → Contempt (imprisonment/fine)

Mistake 7: Not Furnishing Security for Injunction
❌ Court grants injunction conditional on security; plaintiff does not furnish
✓ Furnish security immediately (otherwise injunction vacated)
```
