# Protocol: Trial Procedure (India)

**Domain**: Civil Procedure

## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on civil trial procedure in Indian courts, from framing of issues through judgment, covering the complete trial workflow under the Code of Civil Procedure 1908.

## Activation Triggers

- User asks "What is the procedure for civil trial?"
- Need to understand trial stages
- Framing of issues required
- Order of evidence questions
- Arguments and final hearing procedures
- Trial timeline and stages

## Core Principles

### 1. Trial Objective

```
Objective: Adjudicate dispute based on:
- Pleadings (plaint + written statement)
- Evidence (oral + documentary)
- Arguments (legal submissions)
- Law applicable

Result: Judgment and decree
```

### 2. Trial Stages Overview

```
STAGE 1: PLEADINGS (Order VI-VIII)
→ Plaint filed
→ Summons issued and served
→ Written statement filed
→ Replication (if needed)

STAGE 2: FRAMING OF ISSUES (Order XIV)
→ Court identifies disputed questions of fact and law
→ Issues framed
→ Parties' agreement/objections

STAGE 3: EVIDENCE (Order XVIII)
→ Plaintiff's evidence (examination-in-chief, cross, re-examination)
→ Defendant's evidence (examination-in-chief, cross, re-examination)
→ Rebuttal evidence (if needed)

STAGE 4: ARGUMENTS (Order XVIII Rule 2)
→ Final arguments by both parties
→ Written submissions (if court directs)

STAGE 5: JUDGMENT (Order XX)
→ Court decides issues
→ Reasons given
→ Judgment pronounced

STAGE 6: DECREE (Order XX Rule 6)
→ Formal adjudication
→ Decree drawn up and signed
```

## FRAMING OF ISSUES (Order XIV)

### Purpose of Issues (Order XIV Rule 1)

```
Rule 1: "Issues arise when material proposition of fact or law is affirmed by 
one party and denied by other"

Issue = Disputed question to be decided by court

Types:

1. Issues of Fact:
   - Disputed factual questions
   - Example: "Did defendant pay Rs. 5 lakhs to plaintiff on 15/01/2024?"

2. Issues of Law:
   - Disputed legal questions
   - Example: "Whether suit is barred by limitation?"

3. Mixed Issues (Fact + Law):
   - Example: "Whether defendant executed contract under coercion?"
     (Fact: Was there threat? Law: Does it amount to coercion under Section 15?)
```

### Procedure for Framing Issues (Order XIV Rule 1-5)

```
Step 1: Court Examines Pleadings
- Reads plaint + written statement + replication (if any)
- Identifies material propositions affirmed by one party and denied by other

Step 2: Court Frames Issues
- Formulates questions (disputed points)
- Arranges in logical order

Step 3: Parties' Input (Rule 2)
- Court may hear parties on issues to be framed
- Parties may suggest additional issues
- Court decides final issues

Step 4: Issues Recorded (Rule 1)
- Framed issues entered in case record
- Numbered sequentially (Issue No. 1, 2, 3...)

Step 5: Amendment (Rule 5)
- Court may amend/add issues at any stage if necessary for determining real controversy
```

### Formulation of Issues

```
Issues should be:
- SPECIFIC (not vague or general)
- CONCISE (not lengthy or argumentative)
- DISPUTED (not admitted facts)
- MATERIAL (relevant to decision)

Format:

Issue No. 1: "Whether the plaintiff proves that defendant borrowed Rs. 10 lakhs 
from plaintiff on 15/01/2024?"

Issue No. 2: "Whether defendant proves payment of Rs. 10 lakhs to plaintiff?"

Issue No. 3: "Whether suit is barred by limitation?"

Issue No. 4: "Whether plaintiff is entitled to interest and if so at what rate?"

Issue No. 5: "To what relief, if any, is plaintiff entitled?"

Final Issue (Standard):
"To what relief?" → Determines remedy based on findings on earlier issues
```

### Striking Out Issues (Order XIV Rule 3)

```
Rule 3: Court may strike out issues which:
- Appear not to require determination (admitted facts)
- Not material to decision
- Cannot be tried for legal reasons

Example:
Plaintiff claims: "Defendant owes Rs. 10 lakhs + Rs. 5 lakhs interest"
Defendant admits: "Rs. 10 lakhs owed, but denies interest"

Issues Framed:
1. Whether defendant liable to pay Rs. 10 lakhs? → STRUCK OUT (admitted)
2. Whether defendant liable to pay interest? → RETAINED (disputed)
3. To what relief?
```

## ORDER OF EVIDENCE (Order XVIII)

### General Principle (Order XVIII Rule 1)

```
Rule 1: Right to begin

Party bearing burden of proof has right to begin evidence

Usual Order:
1. Plaintiff begins (proves claim)
2. Defendant follows (proves defense)
3. Plaintiff's rebuttal (if defendant raised new matter)

Exception (Rule 1 proviso):
If defendant admits plaintiff's case substantially but sets up affirmative defense:
→ Defendant may begin (prove defense)
→ Then plaintiff rebuts

Example:
Plaint: "Defendant owes Rs. 10 lakhs"
Written Statement: "Defendant admits owing Rs. 10 lakhs BUT has already paid it"

Here: Defendant begins (proves payment), plaintiff rebuts (shows no payment received)
```

### Plaintiff's Evidence (Order XVIII Rule 4)

```
Procedure:

1. Plaintiff Calls Witnesses:
   - Plaintiff witness (PW) 1, PW2, PW3...
   - Each examined-in-chief, cross-examined, re-examined

2. Plaintiff Produces Documents:
   - Mark documents as Exhibit P-1, P-2, P-3...
   - Prove through witnesses

3. Plaintiff Closes Evidence:
   - After all witnesses examined
   - "Plaintiff's evidence closed"

Content to Cover:
- Prove all material facts alleged in plaint
- Prove cause of action
- Prove documents relied upon
- Anticipate and disprove defendant's defenses (if known)
```

### Defendant's Evidence (Order XVIII Rule 4)

```
Procedure (same as plaintiff):

1. Defendant Calls Witnesses:
   - Defendant witness (DW) 1, DW2, DW3...
   - Each examined-in-chief, cross-examined, re-examined

2. Defendant Produces Documents:
   - Mark as Exhibit D-1, D-2, D-3...
   - Prove through witnesses

3. Defendant Closes Evidence:
   - After all witnesses examined
   - "Defendant's evidence closed"

Content to Cover:
- Disprove plaintiff's case (if denying)
- Prove affirmative defenses (payment, limitation, res judicata, etc.)
- Prove counterclaim (if raised)
```

### Rebuttal Evidence (Order XVIII)

```
Plaintiff's Rebuttal (Sur-Rejoinder):
If defendant raises NEW MATTER in evidence (not in written statement):
→ Plaintiff may lead rebuttal evidence to disprove new matter

Court's Permission Required:
- Not automatic right
- Court decides if new matter arose
- Court allows rebuttal if in interest of justice

Example:
Defendant's Defense (in written statement): "Contract void for uncertainty"
Defendant's Evidence: "Contract also procured by fraud" (NEW MATTER not pleaded)
Plaintiff's Rebuttal: Evidence to show no fraud

Defendant's Rebuttal (Rare):
If plaintiff leads rebuttal on new matter beyond scope
→ Court may allow defendant's sur-rejoinder
```

### Court's Power to Examine Witnesses (Order XVIII Rule 17, Section 165 Evidence Act)

```
Section 165: Court may examine witness at any stage

Court's Examination (Suo Motu):
- To clear doubt on any point
- To elicit facts not brought out by parties
- In interest of justice

When Exercised:
- During witness examination (to clarify ambiguous answer)
- After parties' examination complete (if gap in evidence)
- To recall witness

Effect:
- Court's questions may help one party or other
- Impartiality maintained (court seeking truth, not favoring party)
```

## FINAL ARGUMENTS (Order XVIII Rule 2)

```
Rule 2: After evidence closed, parties address court

Order of Arguments:

1. Plaintiff's Arguments (First):
   - Summarizes evidence
   - Refers to law and precedents
   - Submits plaintiff proved case on preponderance of probabilities
   - Prays for relief

2. Defendant's Arguments (Response):
   - Counters plaintiff's submissions
   - Summarizes defendant's evidence
   - Refers to law and precedents
   - Submits plaintiff failed to prove case OR defendant proved defenses
   - Prays for dismissal (or decree on counterclaim if applicable)

3. Plaintiff's Reply (Brief):
   - Responds to defendant's submissions (limited to reply, not new arguments)

Written Submissions:
- Court may direct parties to file written arguments
- Time limit given (usually 7-15 days)
- Oral arguments limited to highlighting written submissions

Mode:
- Oral arguments (traditional)
- Written submissions (modern practice to save time)
- Hybrid: Written + brief oral highlighting
```

### Content of Arguments

```
Effective Final Argument Covers:

1. Facts:
   - Summary of undisputed facts
   - Disputed facts and evidence led
   - Which version should be believed (credibility assessment)

2. Law:
   - Applicable statutory provisions
   - Case law (precedents) supporting party's position
   - Distinguish opposite party's precedents

3. Issues:
   - Address each framed issue
   - Show how evidence proves/disproves issue
   - Burden of proof discharged/not discharged

4. Relief:
   - Specific relief claimed
   - Why entitled based on facts + law

Structure:
I. Facts (undisputed + disputed with evidence)
II. Law (statutes + case law)
III. Application (facts to law)
IV. Issues (findings on each issue)
V. Relief (prayer)

Length:
- Concise (court time limited)
- Focused on material points (not every detail)
- Respectful to court and opposite party
```

## JUDGMENT (Order XX)

### Judgment Preparation (Order XX Rule 1-5)

```
Rule 1: Court shall pronounce judgment in open court

Timeline:
- Within 30 days from final hearing (if possible)
- If reserved (not immediate), pronounced on date fixed (parties informed)

Judgment Contents (Rule 4):

MANDATORY:

1. Concise Statement of Case:
   - Parties
   - Plaintiff's claim
   - Defendant's defense

2. Points for Determination (Issues Framed):
   - List of issues

3. Decision on Each Issue with Reasons:
   - Issue No. 1: [Decision] - Reasons: [...]
   - Issue No. 2: [Decision] - Reasons: [...]
   - ...

4. Final Order (Conclusion):
   - Suit decreed/dismissed
   - Relief granted (or denied)
   - Costs

Optional but Recommended:
- Summary of evidence (of each witness)
- Analysis of documents
- Reasoning (detailed discussion of law and facts)

Signature and Date:
- Judge signs judgment
- Date of pronouncement
```

### Judgment Format (Order XX Rule 4)

```
Typical Structure:

1. HEADING:
   Court name, suit number, parties

2. INTRODUCTION:
   Brief background and claim

3. PLEADINGS SUMMARY:
   Plaintiff's case (from plaint)
   Defendant's case (from written statement)

4. ISSUES FRAMED:
   List of issues for determination

5. EVIDENCE SUMMARY:
   Plaintiff's evidence (witness-wise + documents)
   Defendant's evidence (witness-wise + documents)

6. FINDINGS ON ISSUES:
   Issue No. 1: [Findings with reasons]
   Issue No. 2: [Findings with reasons]
   ...

7. CONCLUSION:
   Final decision (suit decreed/dismissed)

8. DECREE:
   Specific relief granted/denied
   Costs

9. SIGNATURE AND DATE:
   Judge's signature
   Date of pronouncement
```

**Sample Judgment Excerpt**:

```
IN THE COURT OF CIVIL JUDGE (SENIOR DIVISION), DELHI

Suit No. 123/2023

Ramesh Kumar ............. Plaintiff
Versus
Suresh Enterprises Pvt. Ltd. ............. Defendant

JUDGMENT

This is a suit for recovery of Rs. 10,00,000/- (Rupees Ten Lakhs only) being 
the amount allegedly owed by the defendant to the plaintiff pursuant to a 
contract dated 15/01/2024.

PLAINTIFF'S CASE:
The plaintiff claims that he entered into a contract with the defendant for 
supply of 1000 widgets at Rs. 10,000/- each, total Rs. 1,00,00,000/-. Plaintiff 
supplied the widgets but defendant failed to pay...

DEFENDANT'S CASE:
The defendant admits the contract but contends that payment of Rs. 10,00,000/- 
was made vide cheque no. 123456 dated 20/03/2024, which was encashed by the 
plaintiff...

ISSUES:
1. Whether plaintiff proves that defendant owes Rs. 10,00,000/-?
2. Whether defendant proves payment?
3. To what relief is plaintiff entitled?

EVIDENCE:
Plaintiff examined 2 witnesses (PW1 - plaintiff himself, PW2 - delivery person).
Plaintiff produced 3 documents (Exhibit P-1: Contract, P-2: Delivery receipts, 
P-3: Demand notice).

Defendant examined 1 witness (DW1 - defendant's accountant).
Defendant produced 2 documents (Exhibit D-1: Cheque copy, D-2: Bank statement).

FINDINGS:

Issue No. 1: Whether plaintiff proves that defendant owes Rs. 10,00,000/-?

[Analysis of plaintiff's evidence, contract, delivery receipts...]
FINDING: Answered in the AFFIRMATIVE. Plaintiff has proved delivery of goods 
worth Rs. 10,00,000/-.

Issue No. 2: Whether defendant proves payment?

[Analysis of defendant's evidence, cheque, bank statement...]
[Finding that cheque was for different transaction, not this contract]
FINDING: Answered in the NEGATIVE. Defendant has failed to prove payment of 
Rs. 10,00,000/-.

Issue No. 3: To what relief is plaintiff entitled?

In view of my findings on Issue No. 1 and 2, plaintiff is entitled to recovery 
of Rs. 10,00,000/- with interest @ 9% per annum from date of suit till realization.

CONCLUSION:
The suit is DECREED in favor of the plaintiff and against the defendant.

DECREE:
The defendant is directed to pay Rs. 10,00,000/- (Rupees Ten Lakhs) to the 
plaintiff with interest @ 9% per annum from 01/03/2023 (date of suit) till 
realization.

The defendant shall pay costs of the suit to the plaintiff.

Announced in open court.

Date: 15/11/2024                                           [Signature]
                                                    Civil Judge (Sr. Div.)
```

### Judgment Pronouncement (Order XX Rule 1-3)

```
Rule 1: Judgment Pronounced in Open Court

Modes:

1. Immediate Pronouncement:
   - After final arguments complete
   - Judge delivers judgment orally
   - Judgment typed/written same day or within few days

2. Reserved Judgment:
   - Judge reserves judgment for detailed consideration
   - Date of pronouncement fixed (usually within 30 days)
   - Parties informed of date
   - On pronouncement date, judgment read in court

Presence of Parties:
- Parties/advocates informed of pronouncement date
- Presence not mandatory (judgment pronounced whether parties present or not)
- Copy of judgment given to parties (on application)

Operative Date:
- Judgment takes effect from date of pronouncement
- Limitation for appeal runs from pronouncement date
```

## DECREE (Order XX Rule 6-7)

```
Rule 6: Decree to Follow Judgment

Decree = Formal expression of adjudication

Difference: Judgment vs Decree:
- Judgment: Reasons, findings, decision (narrative)
- Decree: Formal order, executable (operational)

Decree Contents (Rule 6):

1. Number of suit
2. Names of parties (with full description)
3. Particulars of claim
4. Clear statement of relief granted/refused
5. Costs
6. Date of pronouncement
7. Signature of judge

Decree must be:
- Drawn up within 15 days of judgment (Rule 7 - if practicable)
- Signed by judge
- Sealed
```

### Types of Decrees

```
1. Preliminary Decree (Rule 2):
   - Declares rights but does not finally dispose suit
   - Example: Partition suit - preliminary decree declares shares; final decree 
     after actual partition

2. Final Decree (Rule 2):
   - Finally disposes suit (nothing remains except execution)

3. Partly Preliminary, Partly Final (Rule 2):
   - Example: Suit for account - decree for account (preliminary); after account, 
     decree for balance (final)

4. Consent Decree (Order XXIII Rule 3):
   - Based on compromise between parties
   - Court records compromise and passes decree accordingly

5. Ex Parte Decree (Order IX Rule 6):
   - When defendant does not appear despite service
   - Plaintiff proves claim
   - Decree passed in defendant's absence

6. Decree on Admission (Order XII):
   - When defendant admits plaintiff's claim
   - Court passes decree without trial
```

### Decree Format (Order XX Rule 6, Appendix A)

```
IN THE COURT OF [COURT NAME]

DECREE

Suit No. ____ of 20__

[Date of Institution: DD/MM/YYYY]
[Date of Decision: DD/MM/YYYY]

Plaintiff: [Name, description, address]
Versus
Defendant: [Name, description, address]

This suit coming on for final hearing on [date] in the presence of [advocates], 
upon hearing the evidence and upon reading the pleadings filed, the Court 
delivered the following Judgment:

[Brief recital of judgment - findings on issues]

IT IS HEREBY ORDERED AND DECREED as follows:

1. The suit is DECREED in favor of the Plaintiff and against the Defendant.

2. The Defendant shall pay to the Plaintiff:
   (a) Rs. 10,00,000/- (Rupees Ten Lakhs only)
   (b) Interest @ 9% per annum on Rs. 10,00,000/- from 01/03/2023 till realization
   (c) Costs of the suit assessed at Rs. 50,000/-

3. In default of payment within [time], the decree-holder shall be entitled to 
   execute the decree.

Given under my hand and the seal of the Court this [date].

                                                           [Signature]
                                                           Judge
Seal of Court
```

## COSTS (Section 35, Order XX Rule 2)

```
Section 35: Court's Discretion on Costs

General Rule:
"Costs follow the event" → Winning party entitled to costs from losing party

Court's Discretion:
- Award costs to successful party
- Deny costs (even to successful party)
- Apportion costs (if partial success)
- Order one party to pay costs (regardless of result)

Types of Costs:

1. Court Fees Paid:
   - Amount plaintiff paid as court fees

2. Advocate Fees:
   - Reasonable fee for advocate services
   - NOT actual fee (court assesses reasonable amount)

3. Witness Expenses:
   - Batta paid to witnesses

4. Other Expenses:
   - Process fees, typing, photocopying, etc.

Assessment:
- Summary assessment (court estimates reasonable amount)
- OR detailed assessment (advocate files bill of costs, court scrutinizes)

Denial of Costs:
- Vexatious litigation
- Unreasonable conduct by successful party
- Trivial/technical success (no real relief)

Costs in Defendant's Favor (if suit dismissed):
- Defendant entitled to costs for defending frivolous suit
- Compensation for time and expense
```

## CASE MANAGEMENT AND TIMELINE

### Timeline Targets (CPC Amendment 1999, 2002 - Commercial Courts Act 2015)

```
Commercial Courts Act 2015 (for commercial suits):
- Pleadings: 120 days from summons service
- Trial: Commence within 6 months
- Judgment: Within 18 months from plaint filing

General Civil Suits (Non-Commercial):
- No strict statutory timeline (but court aims for disposal within 1-3 years)

Order VIII Rule 1: Written statement within 90 days (MANDATORY)
Order XVII Rule 1: Day-to-day trial (in practice, dates given with adjournments)
Order XX Rule 1: Judgment within 30 days of final hearing (if practicable)
```

### Adjournments (Order XVII)

```
Rule 1: Court may adjourn hearing

Grounds for Adjournment:
- Illness of party/advocate
- Non-service of summons/documents
- Non-availability of witness (valid reason)
- Settlement negotiations

Restrictions:
- Not granted as matter of course
- Must show sufficient cause
- Costs may be imposed for adjournment
- Repeated adjournments discouraged

Commercial Courts:
- Maximum 3 adjournments to each party (Rule 1 proviso - Commercial Courts Act)
- Strict adherence to timelines
```

## Common Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Framing Issues - Contract Dispute

```
Plaint Claims:
- Contract dated 15/01/2024 for supply of goods
- Plaintiff supplied goods worth Rs. 10 lakhs
- Defendant failed to pay

Written Statement:
- Admits contract and supply
- Claims payment made vide cheque on 20/03/2024
- Alternatively, suit barred by limitation

Issues Framed:

Issue No. 1: Whether plaintiff proves that defendant owes Rs. 10 lakhs?
(Fact: Did defendant pay or not?)

Issue No. 2: Whether suit is barred by limitation?
(Law: Limitation period applicable and whether expired)

Issue No. 3: To what relief is plaintiff entitled?
(Final issue - depends on findings on Issues 1 and 2)
```

### Scenario 2: Order of Evidence - Affirmative Defense

```
Plaint: Plaintiff sues for Rs. 10 lakhs (contract breach)
Written Statement: Defendant admits contract BUT claims suit barred by limitation

Order of Evidence (Order XVIII Rule 1 proviso):
1. Defendant begins (proves limitation defense)
   - Limitation is affirmative defense
   - Defendant bears burden (Section 106 Evidence Act)
2. Plaintiff rebuts (shows suit within limitation OR Section 5 condonation)

Contrast with Normal:
If defendant simply denied liability (no affirmative defense):
→ Plaintiff would begin (prove contract + breach + damages)
→ Defendant would rebut (deny contract/breach)
```

### Scenario 3: Rebuttal Evidence

```
Facts:
- Plaintiff sues for breach of contract
- Defendant's Written Statement: "No breach; plaintiff failed to perform"
- Defendant's Evidence: "Plaintiff also committed fraud" (NEW - not in written statement)

Plaintiff's Application:
- Request permission for rebuttal evidence
- Show fraud allegation is new matter

Court's Decision:
- Allows rebuttal (in interest of justice)
- Plaintiff leads evidence to disprove fraud

Effect:
- If new matter not allowed to be rebutted → Prejudice to plaintiff
- Rebuttal ensures fair trial
```

### Scenario 4: Judgment on Admitted Facts

```
Plaint:
- Plaintiff lent Rs. 10 lakhs to defendant
- Demand made; no payment

Written Statement:
- Admits Rs. 10 lakhs borrowed
- Admits no payment yet
- Requests time to pay (financial hardship)

Issues Framed:
Issue No. 1: Whether defendant admits owing Rs. 10 lakhs? → YES (admitted)
Issue No. 2: To what relief?

Evidence:
- No evidence needed on Issue 1 (admitted)
- Plaintiff may still lead minimal evidence for formality

Judgment:
- Suit decreed for Rs. 10 lakhs
- Court may give time to pay (installments) based on defendant's financial status
```

## Case Law

### 1. Sangram Singh v. Election Tribunal (SC 1955)
```
Facts: Issues framed; whether issues binding
Held:
- Issues framed are not conclusive or binding
- Court may add/amend issues at any stage if necessary for determining real controversy
- Issues are guide for trial, not straitjacket
```

### 2. Roop Kumar v. Mohan Thedani (SC 2003)
```
Facts: Suit dismissed for non-prosecution (plaintiff failed to lead evidence on dates)
Held:
- Plaintiff must prosecute suit diligently
- Repeated adjournments without valid cause → Dismissal for default (Order XVII Rule 2)
- Costs imposed on plaintiff for wasting court time
```

### 3. Kaushalya Devi Massand v. Roopkishore Khore (SC 1990)
```
Facts: Decree passed without hearing defendant's evidence
Held:
- Both parties must be given opportunity to lead evidence (natural justice)
- Decree passed without hearing defendant's evidence → Violation of Order XVIII
- Decree set aside, matter remanded for fresh trial
```

### 4. Ajit Singh v. Gopal Singh (SC 1993)
```
Facts: Judgment did not give reasons for findings on issues
Held:
- Order XX Rule 4: Judgment MUST contain reasons for decision on each issue
- Judgment without reasons → Invalid
- Remanded for fresh judgment with reasons
```

## Common Mistakes

```
Mistake 1: Not Addressing All Framed Issues in Judgment
❌ Judgment addresses Issue 1 and 3, but skips Issue 2
✓ Judgment must contain finding on EACH framed issue (Order XX Rule 4)

Mistake 2: Leading Evidence on Facts Not Pleaded
❌ Plaint does not allege fraud; plaintiff leads evidence on fraud at trial
✓ Only facts pleaded can be proved (unless amendment allowed)

Mistake 3: Not Closing Evidence Formally
❌ After last witness, move to arguments without formal closure
✓ "Plaintiff's/Defendant's evidence closed" → Prevents later addition of witnesses

Mistake 4: Final Arguments Introducing New Facts/Documents
❌ In arguments, refer to document not marked as exhibit or fact not in evidence
✓ Arguments based ONLY on pleadings + evidence on record

Mistake 5: Decree Not Matching Judgment
❌ Judgment decrees suit for Rs. 10 lakhs; decree drawn for Rs. 8 lakhs
✓ Decree must exactly reflect judgment's conclusion

Mistake 6: Not Recording Reasons for Findings
❌ Judgment says "Issue 1 answered in affirmative" without reasons
✓ MUST give reasons for each finding (Order XX Rule 4)

Mistake 7: Not Giving Opportunity to Both Parties
❌ Court allows plaintiff's evidence but refuses to hear defendant's evidence
✓ Both parties must get fair opportunity (natural justice)
```
