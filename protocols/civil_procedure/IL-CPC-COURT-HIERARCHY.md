## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on the hierarchy of civil courts in India, their powers, appellate structure, and the flow of civil litigation from trial to Supreme Court.

## Activation Triggers

- User asks "What is the court hierarchy in India?"
- Need to understand appellate options after judgment
- Determining which court to appeal to
- Understanding powers of different courts
- Concurrent jurisdiction questions
- Original vs appellate jurisdiction queries

## Core Framework

### Constitutional Courts vs Subordinate Courts

```
CONSTITUTIONAL COURTS (Created by Constitution):
1. Supreme Court of India (Article 124-147)
2. High Courts (Article 214-231) - One or more per state

SUBORDINATE COURTS (Created by State Acts):
1. District Courts (District Judge/Additional District Judge)
2. Civil Courts (Civil Judge Senior Division / Junior Division)
3. Special Courts (Small Causes, Family, Consumer, etc.)

Key Distinction:
- Constitutional courts: Cannot be abolished (constitutional protection)
- Subordinate courts: Can be reorganized by state legislation
```

## Detailed Court Structure

### 1. Supreme Court of India

**Constitutional Provisions**: Articles 124-147

#### A. Original Jurisdiction (Article 131)

```
Limited to:
1. Disputes between Union and States
2. Disputes between States
3. Disputes involving enforcement of Fundamental Rights (Article 32)

Examples:
- State A vs State B over river water sharing
- Union of India vs State over constitutional validity
- Fundamental Rights violation (writ jurisdiction)

NOT available for:
- Private disputes between citizens
- Ordinary civil/criminal matters
```

#### B. Appellate Jurisdiction (Article 132-136)

**Civil Appeals**:

**1. Appeal as of Right (Article 133 - REPEALED 1973)**
```
Post-1973: No automatic right of appeal in civil matters
Exception: Must obtain certificate from High Court OR SLP under Article 136
```

**2. Certificate from High Court (Order XLV Rule 1-2)**:
```
High Court may grant certificate if:
- Case involves substantial question of law of general importance, AND
- Question needs to be decided by Supreme Court

Procedure:
- Party applies for certificate within 60 days of HC judgment
- HC decides if question of general importance exists
- If granted → Direct appeal to SC (no leave required)
```

**3. Special Leave Petition (Article 136)**:
```
Article 136: "Supreme Court may, in its discretion, grant special leave to 
appeal from any judgment, decree, determination, sentence or order in any 
cause or matter passed by any court or tribunal."

Discretionary power:
- SC NOT bound to grant leave
- Generally granted if:
  * Substantial question of law
  * Perversity in HC findings
  * Gross injustice
  * Public importance

Limitation: 90 days from HC order/judgment (can be condoned)

Procedure:
- File SLP petition with leave application
- SC may:
  * Grant leave → Becomes regular appeal
  * Dismiss leave → No appeal
  * Issue notice → Hearing on merits
```

#### C. Advisory Jurisdiction (Article 143)

```
President may refer questions of law/fact to SC for advisory opinion

SC's opinion:
- Advisory (not binding on President)
- BUT carries weight as SC's view

Examples:
- Constitutional interpretation questions
- International treaty obligations
- Public importance matters
```

#### D. Powers

```
1. Transfer Cases (Article 139A):
   - Transfer pending cases from one HC to another HC
   - Transfer subordinate court cases to itself

2. Writ Jurisdiction (Article 32):
   - Enforcement of Fundamental Rights
   - Can issue writs: Habeas Corpus, Mandamus, Prohibition, Quo Warranto, Certiorari

3. Review (Article 137):
   - Review own judgments
   - Limited grounds (error apparent on face of record)
   - No appeal against review dismissal

4. Curative Petition (Rupa Ashok Hurra 2002):
   - Beyond review (if review dismissed)
   - Extremely narrow grounds (abuse of process + miscarriage of justice)
```

### 2. High Courts

**Constitutional Provisions**: Articles 214-231

#### A. Original Jurisdiction (Article 225)

```
Only certain High Courts have original civil jurisdiction:
- Calcutta High Court (Ordinary Original Civil Jurisdiction - OOCJ)
- Bombay High Court (OOCJ)
- Madras High Court (OOCJ)
- Delhi High Court (limited)

Pecuniary limits:
Set by state notification (varies by HC)
Example (Bombay): Rs. 2 lakhs and above

Other High Courts:
NO original civil jurisdiction (only appellate + writ)
```

#### B. Appellate Jurisdiction (Article 227 + CPC)

**Appeals from District Courts** (Section 96 CPC):

```
Section 96: "Appeal from original decree"

Right of appeal from District Court to High Court:
- ALL decrees appealable UNLESS:
  * Expressly barred by CPC or other law
  * Suit value below prescribed limit (state notification)
  * Decree by consent (unless fraud/misrepresentation)

Examples barred:
- Section 104: Certain interlocutory orders not appealable
- Order 43 Rule 1: Limited list of appealable orders
- Provincial Small Cause Courts Act: No appeal on facts from Small Causes
```

**Appeals from Orders** (Section 104 + Order 43):

```
Section 104: "Appeals from orders"
General rule: Orders NOT appealable (only decrees appealable)

Exception - Order 43 Rule 1: Following orders appealable:
(a) Rejection of plaint (Order VII Rule 11)
(b) Rejection of set-off/counter-claim
(c) Refusal to grant temporary injunction (Order XXXIX)
(d) Receiver appointment/removal
(e) Issue of commission
(f) Rejection of application under Order 9 Rule 13 (set aside ex parte decree)
...and 22 other specified orders

Appeal time: 30 days from order
```

**Second Appeal** (Section 100 CPC):

```
Section 100: "Second appeal to High Court from subordinate courts"

From subordinate courts → High Court (if decree by District Court was appellate)

Grounds (RESTRICTED - amended 1976):
ONLY if case involves SUBSTANTIAL QUESTION OF LAW

NOT available for:
- Questions of fact
- Mixed questions of law and fact (unless substantial legal question extracted)
- Re-appreciation of evidence

Procedure:
1. Appellant must FORMULATE substantial question of law
2. HC decides if question is substantial
3. If NO substantial question → Dismiss at admission
4. If YES → Frame substantial question → Hear appeal ONLY on that question

Example:
First appeal: Civil Judge → District Court (decided)
Second appeal: District Court → High Court (only if substantial question of law)
```

#### C. Supervisory Jurisdiction (Article 227)

```
Article 227: "Power of superintendence over all courts and tribunals"

High Court can:
- Call for records of subordinate courts
- Satisfy itself as to correctness/legality/propriety of proceedings
- Issue directions if irregularity

NOT an appeal:
- Cannot re-appreciate evidence
- Cannot substitute own findings on facts
- Only corrects jurisdictional errors/legal errors

Grounds:
- Excess of jurisdiction
- Jurisdictional error
- Violation of natural justice
- Perversity

Limitation: Not specified (but generally within reasonable time)
```

#### D. Writ Jurisdiction (Article 226)

```
Article 226: High Court can issue writs for enforcement of Fundamental Rights 
AND other rights

Wider than Article 32 (SC):
- SC: ONLY Fundamental Rights
- HC: Fundamental Rights + legal rights + statutory rights

Territorial jurisdiction:
- Where cause of action arose, OR
- Where respondent resides/carries business, OR
- Where government office situated

Writs available:
1. Habeas Corpus (illegal detention)
2. Mandamus (compel public duty)
3. Prohibition (prevent excess of jurisdiction)
4. Certiorari (quash unlawful order)
5. Quo Warranto (challenge illegal assumption of office)

Discretionary:
- HC may refuse writ if:
  * Alternative remedy available
  * Unclean hands
  * Laches (delay)
  * Not in public interest
```

#### E. Powers

```
1. Transfer Cases (Section 24-25 CPC):
   - Transfer from subordinate court to another subordinate court
   - Transfer from subordinate to HC (if substantial question of law)

2. Revision (Section 115 CPC - ABOLISHED for most HCs 2002):
   - Revisional jurisdiction limited post-2002 amendment
   - Only if subordinate court:
     * Exercised jurisdiction not vested in it, OR
     * Failed to exercise jurisdiction vested in it, OR
     * Acted illegally/with material irregularity

3. Reference (Section 113):
   - Subordinate court can refer question of law to HC
   - HC's decision binding on subordinate court

4. Review (Order 47 CPC):
   - Review own judgments
   - Grounds: Error apparent, new evidence, other sufficient reason
```

### 3. District Courts

**Statutory Basis**: State Civil Courts Acts (varies by state)

#### A. Original Jurisdiction

```
Pecuniary:
- Set by state notification
- Typical: Suits above Rs. 50 lakhs up to Rs. 3 crores (varies)
- Some states: Unlimited

Territorial:
- District where cause of action arose/defendant resides (Section 20 CPC)

Types of suits:
- Ordinary civil suits (money, property, specific performance, injunction, declaration)
- NOT exclusively assigned to tribunals
```

#### B. Appellate Jurisdiction (Section 96 CPC)

```
From subordinate courts in the district:
- Civil Judge (Senior Division) → District Court (first appeal)
- Civil Judge (Junior Division) → District Court (first appeal)

Decree appealable:
- ALL decrees UNLESS barred

Appeal time: 90 days from decree (can be condoned up to 90 more days)

Powers on appeal (Section 107 Order 41 Rule 33):
(a) Determine case finally (on merits)
(b) Remand case (for fresh decision on specific issues)
(c) Frame issues and refer to lower court (for finding)
(d) Take additional evidence (Order 41 Rule 27)
(e) Reverse, affirm, or vary decree
```

#### C. Revisional Jurisdiction

```
Varies by state legislation:
Some states: District Court can revise orders of subordinate courts
Others: No revisional power

If available:
- Similar to Section 115 CPC (pre-2002)
- Excess/lack of jurisdiction, illegality, material irregularity
```

#### D. Designation

```
District Judge = Principal Civil Judge of the district
Additional District Judges = Same powers as District Judge (for case load)

Administrative:
- District Judge is head of civil judiciary in district
- Supervises subordinate courts
- Transfers, inspections, administrative orders
```

### 4. Subordinate Civil Courts

#### A. Civil Judge (Senior Division)

```
Pecuniary:
- Typically: Rs. 5 lakhs to Rs. 50 lakhs (varies by state)
- Suits below District Court limit but above Junior Division

Powers:
- Try all civil suits within pecuniary limit
- Grant injunctions, specific performance, declarations
- Issue commissions, order discovery
```

#### B. Civil Judge (Junior Division)

```
Pecuniary:
- Typically: Up to Rs. 5 lakhs (varies by state)

Powers:
- Limited to pecuniary jurisdiction
- All civil remedies available (if within jurisdiction)
```

#### C. Small Causes Court (Provincial Small Cause Courts Act 1887)

```
Jurisdiction:
- Urban areas only (not rural)
- Small value disputes (typically up to Rs. 20,000-50,000)
- Specific categories: Rent, debt, account, partnership, contract

Procedure:
- Summary (speedy disposal)
- Informal pleadings
- Limited adjournments
- No decree, only "order" (BUT treated as decree for execution - Section 25)

Appeal:
- No appeal on FACTS (Section 25)
- Appeal to High Court on QUESTIONS OF LAW only
- Limitation: 30 days
```

### 5. Tribunals (Specialized)

```
Family Courts (Family Courts Act 1984):
- Matrimonial disputes (divorce, maintenance, custody)
- Guardianship under Guardians and Wards Act
- Appeal: High Court (within 90 days)

Debt Recovery Tribunals (SARFAESI Act 2002):
- Bank debt recovery (above Rs. 20 lakhs)
- Secured creditor enforcement
- Appeal: Debt Recovery Appellate Tribunal → High Court (Section 17)

National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT) (Companies Act 2013):
- Company law disputes (oppression, winding up, mergers)
- Appeal: NCLAT → Supreme Court

Consumer Forums (Consumer Protection Act 2019):
- District: Up to Rs. 1 crore
- State: Rs. 1 crore to Rs. 10 crores
- National: Above Rs. 10 crores
- Appeal: State → National → Supreme Court

Motor Accidents Claims Tribunal (MACT) (Motor Vehicles Act 1988):
- Accident compensation claims
- Appeal: High Court (within 90 days)
```

## Appellate Flow Chart

### Scenario 1: Suit in Civil Judge (Junior Division)

```
Civil Judge (JD) → DECREE
          ↓
District Court (First Appeal under Section 96)
          ↓
High Court (Second Appeal under Section 100 - IF substantial question of law)
          ↓
Supreme Court (SLP under Article 136 OR certificate)
```

### Scenario 2: Suit in District Court (Original)

```
District Court → DECREE
          ↓
High Court (First Appeal under Section 96)
          ↓
Supreme Court (SLP under Article 136 OR certificate)

No second appeal (as District Court was original court, HC appeal is first appeal)
```

### Scenario 3: Suit in High Court (Original Side)

```
High Court (Original) → DECREE
          ↓
High Court (Appellate Side) - Division Bench (Letters Patent Appeal if available)
          ↓
Supreme Court (SLP under Article 136 OR certificate)

Note: Letters Patent Appeal exists only in Calcutta, Bombay, Madras, some other HCs
```

### Scenario 4: Small Causes Court

```
Small Causes Court → ORDER (treated as decree)
          ↓
High Court (Appeal ONLY on questions of law - Section 25 PSCCA)
          ↓
Supreme Court (SLP - rarely granted for small value disputes)
```

### Scenario 5: Family Court

```
Family Court → DECREE
          ↓
High Court (Appeal under Family Courts Act 1984)
          ↓
Supreme Court (SLP under Article 136)
```

### Scenario 6: Tribunal (NCLT/NCLAT)

```
NCLT → ORDER
          ↓
NCLAT (National Company Law Appellate Tribunal)
          ↓
Supreme Court (Direct appeal under Companies Act 2013 Section 423)
```

## Concurrent Jurisdiction

### When Multiple Courts Have Jurisdiction

```
Scenario:
Suit value: Rs. 30 lakhs
Cause of action: Delhi + Mumbai
Defendant: Resides in Delhi, business in Mumbai

Courts with jurisdiction:
1. Civil Judge (Senior Division) Delhi (pecuniary + territorial)
2. Civil Judge (Senior Division) Mumbai (pecuniary + territorial)
3. District Court Delhi (pecuniary + territorial)
4. District Court Mumbai (pecuniary + territorial)

Plaintiff's choice:
Can file in ANY of the above (concurrent jurisdiction)

Section 15 mandate:
SHOULD file in LOWEST competent court (Civil Judge SD)

Practical:
- Plaintiff often prefers higher court (District) for efficiency/expertise
- BUT if challenged, court may return plaint for filing in lower court (Order VII Rule 10)
```

## Transfer of Cases

### Section 24: Transfer from Lower Court to Higher Court

```
Grounds (Court may transfer on application OR suo motu):
1. Withdrawal of suit from court NOT competent → Transfer to competent court
2. Substantial question of law of general importance → Transfer to HC
3. Numerous persons interested → Transfer to prevent multiplicity
4. Ends of justice require

Procedure:
- Application by party OR court's own motion
- Notice to opposite party
- Hearing
- Order of transfer
- Case deemed filed in transferee court from original institution date

Effect on limitation:
Institution date in original court = Institution date in transferee court
(Benefit of original filing date preserved)
```

### Section 25: Transfer by Supreme Court/High Court

```
Supreme Court:
Can transfer any case pending in ANY court in India to:
- Any High Court, OR
- Any subordinate court

High Court:
Can transfer cases pending in subordinate courts within its jurisdiction to:
- Another subordinate court, OR
- Itself (if substantial question of law)

Grounds:
- Ends of justice
- Convenience of parties
- Important question of law
- Multiplicity of proceedings
```

## Powers of Courts on Appeal

### Section 107 Order 41 Rule 33 (Appellate Court Powers)

```
Appellate court may:

(a) Determine case finally:
    - Decide on merits
    - Pass decree affirming, varying, or reversing lower court decree

(b) Remand case:
    - Send back to lower court for fresh decision
    - Specific issues for reconsideration
    - Additional evidence if needed

(c) Frame issues and refer:
    - Frame specific issues
    - Send to lower court for finding on those issues
    - Return with findings for appellate court to decide

(d) Take additional evidence (Order 41 Rule 27):
    - If necessary for pronouncing judgment, OR
    - If lower court improperly refused/received evidence

(e) Reverse, affirm, vary:
    - Reverse: Set aside lower court decree
    - Affirm: Uphold lower court decree
    - Vary: Modify decree (increase/decrease relief)

(f) Set aside decree and remand:
    - If decree suffers from jurisdictional defect
    - If lower court decided on wrong grounds
    - Fresh decision needed

(g) Pass any other order:
    - As justice requires
```

## Review Jurisdiction

### Order 47 CPC (Review of Judgments and Orders)

```
Grounds for review (Rule 1):
1. Discovery of new and important matter/evidence which, after due diligence, 
   was not within knowledge or could not be produced at trial
2. Mistake or error apparent on face of record
3. Any other sufficient reason

Limitation: 30 days from date of decree/order (can be extended up to 30 more days)

Procedure:
- File review petition with grounds
- Court may dismiss at admission if no ground made out
- If admitted, hear parties and decide

Restrictions:
- Review NOT a re-hearing (cannot re-argue entire case)
- "Error apparent" = Obvious error visible on record (not debatable point)
- "Sufficient reason" = Narrow interpretation (fraud, natural justice violation)

Orders not reviewable:
- Order 47 Rule 1 proviso: Decree/order from which appeal preferred → Not reviewable
```

## Limitation Periods for Appeals

```
First Appeal from decree (Section 96):
- 90 days from date of decree
- Extension: Up to 90 more days (if sufficient cause - Section 5 Limitation Act)

Appeal from order (Section 104 + Order 43):
- 30 days from order
- Extension: Up to 30 more days

Second Appeal (Section 100):
- 90 days from decree of first appellate court
- Extension: Up to 90 more days

Appeal to Supreme Court (SLP - Article 136):
- 90 days from HC order/judgment
- Extension: Discretionary (SC may condone any delay)

Review (Order 47):
- 30 days from decree/order
- Extension: Up to 30 more days

Execution of decree:
- 12 years from date decree becomes final (Article 136 Limitation Act 1963)
```

## Common Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Which Court for Rs. 25 Lakh Suit?

```
Facts: Plaintiff wants to file Rs. 25 lakh money recovery suit

Pecuniary jurisdiction (assuming typical state limits):
- Civil Judge (Junior Division): Up to Rs. 5 lakhs → NO jurisdiction
- Civil Judge (Senior Division): Rs. 5-50 lakhs → HAS jurisdiction
- District Court: Above Rs. 50 lakhs OR unlimited → HAS jurisdiction

Section 15 mandate:
File in LOWEST competent court

Answer:
Civil Judge (Senior Division) is appropriate court
BUT District Court also has jurisdiction (if state notification permits)

Practical:
If plaintiff files in District Court, court MAY:
- Retain (if jurisdiction unlimited)
- Return plaint to file in Civil Judge SD (Order VII Rule 10)
```

### Scenario 2: Appeal Options After Decree

```
Facts:
- Suit filed in Civil Judge (Junior Division)
- Decree passed on DD/MM/YYYY
- Plaintiff/Defendant wants to appeal

First Appeal:
TO: District Court (Section 96)
TIME: 90 days from decree (extendable up to 90 more days)

If First Appeal dismissed/decreed in District Court:
Second Appeal:
TO: High Court (Section 100)
GROUND: ONLY if substantial question of law
TIME: 90 days from District Court decree (extendable up to 90 more days)

If Second Appeal dismissed/decreed in High Court:
Final Appeal:
TO: Supreme Court (Article 136 SLP)
TIME: 90 days from HC order (condonable)
DISCRETIONARY: SC not bound to grant leave
```

### Scenario 3: Concurrent Jurisdiction - Multiple Courts

```
Facts:
- Suit value: Rs. 40 lakhs
- Cause of action: Partly Mumbai, partly Delhi
- Defendant: Resides Mumbai, business in Delhi

Courts with jurisdiction:
1. Civil Judge (SD) Mumbai
2. Civil Judge (SD) Delhi
3. District Court Mumbai (if pecuniary jurisdiction permits)
4. District Court Delhi (if pecuniary jurisdiction permits)

Plaintiff files in: District Court Delhi

Can defendant object?
YES, on ground:
- Section 15: Should file in lowest competent court (Civil Judge SD)
- Court may return plaint (Order VII Rule 10)

Alternatively:
If defendant does not object → Submits to jurisdiction
Plaintiff can proceed in District Court
```

### Scenario 4: Transfer Due to Lack of Jurisdiction

```
Facts:
- Suit filed in Civil Judge (JD) Delhi
- Suit value later found to be Rs. 8 lakhs (above CJ JD limit)

Section 24 application:
- Plaintiff/Defendant applies for transfer
- Court finds pecuniary jurisdiction lacking
- Transfers to Civil Judge (SD) Delhi

Effect:
- Suit deemed filed in Civil Judge SD from original institution date
- Limitation preserved
- Evidence recorded in CJ JD → Valid in CJ SD
```

### Scenario 5: Revisional Jurisdiction Under Section 115

```
Facts:
- District Court passed order refusing to set aside ex parte decree
- Order appealable under Order 43? NO (not in Order 43 Rule 1 list)
- Can challenge in HC?

YES - Under Section 115 CPC (Revision):
Grounds:
- Subordinate court exercised jurisdiction not vested
- Failed to exercise jurisdiction vested
- Acted illegally or with material irregularity

HC can call for records and satisfy itself
BUT Section 115 limited post-2002 (not a regular appeal)
```

## Case Law

### 1. State of AP v. National Thermal Power Corporation (SC 2002)
```
Facts: Jurisdiction of Civil Court vs Arbitral Tribunal
Held:
- Court hierarchy must be respected
- Cannot bypass intermediate appellate forums
- Direct access to SC restricted (Article 136 discretionary)
```

### 2. Surya Dev Rai v. Ram Chander Rai (SC 2003)
```
Facts: Second appeal on questions of fact
Held:
- Section 100 (post-1976): ONLY substantial questions of law
- Questions of fact/mixed fact and law NOT appealable
- HC must formulate substantial question before admitting second appeal
```

### 3. Santosh Hazari v. Purushottam Tiwari (SC 2001)
```
Facts: Delay in filing appeal, condonation of delay
Held:
- Section 5 Limitation Act: Sufficient cause required
- Liberal approach in civil appeals (unlike criminal)
- BUT gross negligence/inaction → No condonation
- Maximum condonation: 90 days beyond 90 days for appeal from decree
```

### 4. Radhey Shyam v. Chhabi Nath (SC 2015)
```
Facts: Review petition, scope of "error apparent on face of record"
Held:
- Error apparent = Self-evident error; no elaborate argument needed
- Not debatable point of law
- Review NOT re-hearing
- Cannot re-argue case on merits
```

### 5. Shalini Shyam Shetty v. Rajendra Shankar Patil (SC 2010)
```
Facts: Letters Patent Appeal from Single Judge to Division Bench of HC
Held:
- LPA available only where Letters Patent exists (Calcutta, Bombay, Madras, etc.)
- Not available in HCs without Letters Patent
- Intra-court appeal (within HC) limited to specific judgments
```

### 6. Laxminarayan v. Mst. Kamlabai (SC 1987)
```
Facts: Transfer of suit from one district to another for convenience
Held:
- Section 25: HC/SC can transfer for ends of justice
- Convenience of parties is relevant factor
- BUT not if amounts to forum shopping
- Balancing test: Plaintiff's choice vs defendant's convenience
```

## Summary Chart

```
COURT HIERARCHY (Civil):

Supreme Court (Article 124-147):
- SLP (Article 136): Discretionary
- Certificate from HC: Substantial question of law
- Original: Union-State disputes, Fundamental Rights (Article 32)

High Court (Article 214-231):
- First Appeal from District Court (Section 96)
- Second Appeal from Subordinate Court (Section 100 - only substantial question)
- Writ (Article 226): Fundamental + legal rights
- Supervisory (Article 227): Over all subordinate courts
- Original (only some HCs): Civil suits within pecuniary limit

District Court:
- Original: Suits within pecuniary limit
- First Appeal from Subordinate Courts (Section 96)

Subordinate Courts:
- Civil Judge (SD): Rs. 5-50 lakhs (typical)
- Civil Judge (JD): Up to Rs. 5 lakhs (typical)
- Small Causes: Summary procedure, limited appeal

APPEAL LIMITATION:
First Appeal: 90 days + 90 days extension
Second Appeal: 90 days + 90 days extension
SLP: 90 days (condonable)
Review: 30 days + 30 days extension

TRANSFER:
Section 24: To competent court if lacking jurisdiction
Section 25: HC/SC for ends of justice
```

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Filing Directly in High Court Original Side
```
❌ Wrong: Suit worth Rs. 50 lakhs filed in HC (where HC has original jurisdiction from Rs. 2 lakhs)
✓ Correct: File in District Court (Section 15 - lowest competent court)

Exception: HC retains if it accepts (discretionary)
```

### Mistake 2: Second Appeal on Questions of Fact
```
❌ Wrong: Appeal to HC from District Court decree challenging findings of fact
✓ Correct: Second appeal ONLY on substantial questions of LAW (Section 100 post-1976)

HC will dismiss at admission if no substantial legal question
```

### Mistake 3: Missing Appeal Limitation
```
❌ Wrong: File appeal after 90 days without condonation application
✓ Correct: File appeal within 90 days OR file with condonation application (up to 90 more days)

After 180 days → Decree becomes final (executable)
```

### Mistake 4: Review as Re-Hearing
```
❌ Wrong: File review petition re-arguing entire case on merits
✓ Correct: Review only for error apparent, new evidence, or sufficient reason (Order 47)

Review NOT a second appeal
```

### Mistake 5: Appealing Non-Appealable Order
```
❌ Wrong: Appeal from order dismissing application (not in Order 43 Rule 1 list)
✓ Correct: Only orders in Order 43 Rule 1 appealable; others challenge via revision (Section 115)

Check Order 43 list before filing appeal from order
```
