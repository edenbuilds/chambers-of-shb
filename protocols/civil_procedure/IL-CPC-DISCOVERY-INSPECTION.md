# Protocol: Discovery and Inspection of Documents (India)

**Protocol ID**: IL-CPC-DISCOVERY-INSPECTION  
**Domain**: Civil Procedure  
**Subdomain**: Evidence and Discovery  
**Version**: 1.0.0  
**Last Updated**: 2025-12-06

## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on discovery of documents, inspection, admission/denial of documents, and production of documents under the Code of Civil Procedure 1908, ensuring fair disclosure and preventing surprise evidence.

## Activation Triggers

- User asks "How to get documents from opposite party?"
- Need to discover what documents other party possesses
- Want to inspect documents mentioned in pleadings
- Opposing party not disclosing relevant documents
- Need to produce documents as evidence
- Challenge document's authenticity

## Core Principles

### 1. Discovery = Disclosure of Documents

```
Discovery (Order XI):
- Each party discloses documents in possession/power
- Related to matters in question
- Prevents "trial by ambush"
- Ensures fair trial (both parties know evidence)

Rule: "No document, no evidence"
→ Document not disclosed/admitted → Generally cannot be used at trial
→ Exception: Court's discretion (Order XIII Rule 2)
```

### 2. Purpose of Discovery and Inspection

```
1. Narrow Issues:
   - Know what documents exist
   - Focus trial on disputed documents

2. Prevent Surprise:
   - Opposite party not caught unaware
   - Time to prepare response

3. Fair Trial:
   - Both parties on equal footing
   - Access to relevant evidence

4. Judicial Efficiency:
   - Documents admitted → No need to prove
   - Documents denied → Only those need formal proof
```

## DISCOVERY OF DOCUMENTS (Order XI)

### Scope of Discovery (Order XI Rule 1)

```
Rule 1: "Court may, at any time, order any party to make discovery on oath of 
documents which are or have been in his possession or power relating to any 
matter in question in the suit"

Key Elements:

1. "Documents":
   - Deeds, contracts, letters, emails, SMS, WhatsApp chats
   - Invoices, receipts, vouchers
   - Photographs, videos, audio recordings (if reduced to tangible form)
   - Electronic records (under IT Act 2000)
   - Maps, plans, drawings

2. "In possession or power":
   - Currently in physical possession, OR
   - Legal right to obtain possession, OR
   - Practical ability to procure

3. "Relating to matter in question":
   - Relevant to issues in suit
   - Not privileged
   - Not oppressive to produce

NOT Discoverable:
- Privileged communications (lawyer-client, doctor-patient, priest-penitent)
- Self-incriminating documents (Article 20(3) Constitution)
- State secrets (Section 123 Evidence Act)
```

### Types of Discovery

#### A. Discovery by List (Order XI Rule 12)

```
Rule 12: Court may order party to file AFFIDAVIT OF DOCUMENTS

Affidavit shall contain:
1. Schedule 1: Documents in party's possession/power which he does not object to produce
2. Schedule 2: Documents in party's possession/power which he objects to produce (state grounds)
3. Schedule 3: Documents not in party's possession/power, but were at some time (state when parted with and current whereabouts)

Format of Affidavit of Documents:

"AFFIDAVIT OF DOCUMENTS

I, [Name], the [Plaintiff/Defendant] above named, make oath and say as follows:

1. I have in my possession or power the documents relating to the matters in 
   question in this suit set forth in Schedule 1 hereto, and I do not object 
   to produce the same.

2. I have in my possession or power the documents relating to the matters in 
   question in this suit set forth in Schedule 2 hereto, and I object to 
   produce the same on the following grounds: [State grounds - privilege, 
   self-incrimination, etc.]

3. I have had but have not now in my possession or power the documents relating 
   to the matters in question in this suit set forth in Schedule 3 hereto. 
   The said documents were last in my possession on [date] and are now in the 
   possession/power of [name/whereabouts unknown].

[Schedules attached]

Sworn before me this __ day of ____, 20__.

                                                          [Signature of Deponent]
```

**Schedule 1: Documents Party Will Produce**:
```
S.No. | Description of Document | Date | Remarks
------|-------------------------|------|--------
1. | Sale Agreement | 15/01/2024 | Between plaintiff and defendant
2. | Receipt for advance payment | 20/01/2024 | Rs. 1 lakh paid
3. | Legal Notice dated 05/03/2024 | 05/03/2024 | Calling upon defendant to perform
...
```

**Schedule 2: Documents Objected to Produce**:
```
S.No. | Description | Ground for Objection
------|-------------|--------------------
1. | Correspondence with lawyer dated 10/02/2024 | Legal professional privilege (Section 126 Evidence Act)
2. | Internal audit report | Not relevant to matters in question
...
```

**Schedule 3: Documents Not in Possession**:
```
S.No. | Description | When Parted With | Current Whereabouts
------|-------------|------------------|-------------------
1. | Original purchase deed | 01/06/2023 | Deposited with bank for loan
2. | Email correspondence with third party | Deleted | Unknown/not recoverable
...
```

#### B. Discovery by Interrogatories (Order XI Rule 1-10) - RARELY USED

```
Interrogatories = Written questions requiring written answers on oath

Rare in India (unlike common law jurisdictions):
- Not frequently ordered by courts
- Oral examination preferred (witness examination)

If Ordered:
1. Party files interrogatories (written questions)
2. Court allows specific interrogatories (not all)
3. Opposite party answers on oath
4. Answers admissible as evidence

Grounds for Interrogatories:
- Necessary for disposing fairly of suit
- Saving expense
- Opposite party has exclusive knowledge of facts
```

#### C. Discovery by Notice to Produce (Order XI Rule 15)

```
Rule 15: Either party may call on other to produce document referred to in 
pleadings or affidavits

Notice to Produce:
- Informal notice by advocate
- Requires opposite party to produce specific document(s) at trial/hearing
- If party fails to produce → Adverse inference (document would have been against him)

Format:
"NOTICE TO PRODUCE

TAKE NOTICE that you are hereby called upon to produce at the hearing of this 
suit on [date] the following documents referred to in your written statement/
affidavit:

1. [Description of document - e.g., \"Sale Agreement dated 15/01/2024 referred 
   to in para 3 of written statement\"]
2. [Description of document]

Dated: [Date]

                                                              [Advocate for Plaintiff]"

Effect:
- If document produced → Can be used as evidence
- If not produced → Court may presume document would have been unfavorable to party
```

### Privilege from Discovery (Order XI Rule 13)

```
Rule 13: No one shall be compelled to produce documents which:
(a) He could not be compelled to produce at trial (Section 123-129 Evidence Act)

Privileged Documents (Section 126-129 Evidence Act):

1. Legal Professional Privilege (Section 126):
   - Communications between lawyer and client
   - Purpose: Obtaining legal advice or for litigation
   - Client's privilege (not lawyer's)

2. State Secrets (Section 123):
   - Unpublished official records
   - Disclosure injurious to public interest
   - Court decides after examining document

3. Official Communications (Section 124):
   - Between public officers (if public interest harmed by disclosure)

4. Information about Commission of Offenses (Section 125):
   - Magistrate can forbid disclosure if public interest

5. Professional Communications (Section 126-129):
   - Lawyer-client (Section 126)
   - Interpreters, clerks (Section 127)
   - Production of title deeds (Section 128)
   - Suits for partition (Section 129 - title deeds need not be produced)

Self-Incrimination Privilege:
Article 20(3) Constitution + Section 132 Evidence Act:
- Witness cannot be compelled to answer if tends to expose to criminal charge
- Extends to production of documents (Sharma v. Satish Chandra)

Grounds for Objection to Production:
- Document privileged (state which privilege)
- Not relevant to matters in question
- Oppressive/vexatious (fishing expedition)
- Trade secret/confidential commercial information (court may allow inspection under confidentiality)
```

## INSPECTION OF DOCUMENTS (Order XI Rule 14)

```
Rule 14: After discovery, party entitled to INSPECT documents disclosed (Schedule 1)

Purpose:
- Verify existence of documents
- Examine contents
- Decide which to use as evidence
- Prepare case

Procedure:

Step 1: After Affidavit of Documents Filed
- Party files application for inspection of documents in Schedule 1

Step 2: Time and Place Fixed
- Court fixes time for inspection (usually at opposite party's advocate's office)
- Opposite party produces documents

Step 3: Inspection
- Applicant (or advocate) inspects documents
- Can take notes, photographs (with court permission)
- Can request certified copies

Step 4: Selection
- Party selects which documents to rely on
- Includes in own list of documents (if needs to use at trial)

Effect:
- Inspected document → Can be used at trial (if relevant)
- If party refuses inspection → Court may order production OR dismiss party's case
```

## PRODUCTION OF DOCUMENTS AT TRIAL (Order XIII)

### Documents to Be Produced (Order XIII Rule 1)

```
Rule 1: Party intending to rely on document must:
1. Annex document to plaint/written statement (if in possession)
   OR
2. Enter document in list of documents filed
   OR
3. Give notice to opposite party to produce (Order XI Rule 15)

Document NOT in Above Categories:
- Generally CANNOT be used at trial
- Exception: Court's discretion (Rule 2 - if sufficient cause shown)
```

### Admission and Denial of Documents (Order XIII Rule 2, 4)

```
Rule 2: Court may summon parties before trial to:
- Admit/deny documents
- Narrow issues for trial

Procedure:

Step 1: Notice to Admit Documents
- Party serves notice calling on opposite party to admit documents
- Opposite party must admit/deny within prescribed time (usually 7-10 days)

Step 2: Opposite Party's Response
Options:
(a) Admit: Document proved (no need for formal proof at trial)
(b) Deny: Document disputed (must be proved at trial by calling witness/certifying officer)
(c) Refuse to admit/deny: Deemed not admitted (must be proved)

Consequence of Denial:
- If document later proved at trial → Denying party may pay costs of proof (Section 35 Evidence Act)
- If genuine denial (document later not proved) → No costs penalty
```

### Proof of Documents If Not Admitted (Order XIII Rule 4)

```
If document denied → Must be proved at trial

Modes of Proof (Evidence Act):

1. Public Documents (Section 74-78):
   - Certified copy sufficient (Section 76)
   - Examples: Government gazette, registered deed, court judgment

2. Private Documents (Section 67-73):
   - Original produced + proved by:
     * Attesting witness (if attested) - Section 68
     * Execution admitted (Section 69)
     * Handwriting proved by expert/familiar witness (Section 47, 67)
     * Comparison of handwriting (Section 73)

3. Electronic Records (Section 65B IT Act 2000):
   - Certificate under Section 65B(4) required
   - Certifies: Computer used, data source, etc.
   - Without certificate → Inadmissible (Arjun Panditrao v. Kailash Kushanrao, SC 2020)

Adverse Inference (Section 114(g) Evidence Act):
"Court may presume that evidence which could be produced and is not produced 
would, if produced, be unfavorable to the person who withholds it"
```

### Impounding Documents (Order XIII Rule 9)

```
Rule 9: Court may impound document if:
- Appears to be forged, OR
- Materially altered, OR
- Inadmissible in evidence

Procedure:
1. Mark document "Impounded"
2. Send to police for investigation (if forgery suspected)
3. Await police report/chargesheet
4. Trial continues (impounded document not used)
```

## PRODUCTION BY THIRD PARTY (Order XVI Rule 6)

```
Rule 6: If document in possession of third party (not party to suit):

Procedure:

Step 1: Application for Summons to Produce
- Party applies to court
- Requests summons to third party

Step 2: Court Issues Summons
- Summons to third party to produce document on specified date
- OR deposit with court

Step 3: Third Party's Options
(a) Produce document
(b) Object to production (if privileged/not relevant)
(c) Seek costs/protection of confidentiality

Step 4: Court Orders Production or Rejects
- If third party fails to produce without valid excuse → Contempt of court
```

## ELECTRONIC DOCUMENTS (IT Act 2000 + Evidence Act)

### Discoverability

```
Section 65A Evidence Act:
"Electronic records admissible subject to Section 65B"

Section 65B: Certificate Required
For electronic record to be admissible:
- Certificate under Section 65B(4) stating:
  * Computer from which record produced
  * Computer regularly used for storing/processing information
  * Information fed into computer in ordinary course
  * Computer was operating properly (or defect did not affect record)

Arjun Panditrao v. Kailash Kushanrao (SC 2020):
"Section 65B certificate is MANDATORY; no exception unless original electronic 
record produced and proved under Section 67"

Discovery of Electronic Documents:
- Emails: Discoverable (party must produce printouts + Section 65B certificate)
- WhatsApp chats: Discoverable (screenshots + Section 65B certificate)
- SMS: Discoverable (call detail record + Section 65B certificate)
- Social media posts: Discoverable (printout/screenshot + Section 65B certificate)
```

### Procedure for Electronic Evidence

```
Step 1: Disclose in Affidavit of Documents
- "Email dated 15/01/2024 from defendant to plaintiff"

Step 2: Produce at Inspection
- Printout of email with headers (showing sender, recipient, date, time)

Step 3: At Trial
(a) Produce original electronic device (phone/laptop), OR
(b) Produce printout + Section 65B certificate

Section 65B Certificate Format:
"I, [Name], [Designation], certify that:
1. The electronic record containing [description] was produced by computer 
   [details].
2. The said computer was regularly used for storing and processing information 
   in the ordinary course of [business/activities].
3. During the material period, information was regularly fed into the computer.
4. The computer was operating properly at the material time, or if not, the 
   defect did not affect the electronic record or its accuracy.
5. The information in the electronic record reproduces or is derived from 
   information fed into the computer in the ordinary course.

[Signature]
[Date]"

Effect:
- With certificate → Electronic record admissible
- Without certificate → Inadmissible (unless original device produced + proved under Section 67)
```

## Common Scenarios

### Scenario 1: Discovering Contract in Defendant's Possession

```
Facts:
- Plaintiff claims contract exists
- Plaintiff lost his copy
- Defendant has original

Solution:

Step 1: In Plaint
- State: "Original contract in defendant's possession" (Order VII Rule 14)

Step 2: Apply for Discovery
- Application under Order XI Rule 12 for defendant to file affidavit of documents

Step 3: Defendant Files Affidavit
- Schedule 1: Includes contract (if willing to produce)
- OR Schedule 2: Objects to produce (state ground)

Step 4: Apply for Inspection
- Plaintiff applies to inspect documents in Schedule 1
- Inspect contract, verify existence

Step 5: Notice to Produce (Order XI Rule 15)
- Serve notice on defendant to produce contract at trial

Step 6: At Trial
- If defendant produces → Use as evidence
- If defendant refuses → Adverse inference (Section 114(g)) + Court may order production
```

### Scenario 2: Opponent Not Disclosing Relevant Documents

```
Facts:
- Defendant claims payment made by cheque
- Defendant not annexing cheque copy to written statement
- Plaintiff wants to see cheque

Solution:

Step 1: Notice to Admit Documents
- Plaintiff serves notice asking defendant to admit/deny existence of cheque

Step 2: Defendant's Response
- If admits → Cheque proved (no need to see)
- If denies → Plaintiff challenges at trial (defendant must prove payment)
- If ignores → Deemed not admitted

Step 3: Apply for Discovery
- Application under Order XI Rule 12 for defendant to file affidavit listing all payment documents

Step 4: Defendant Files Affidavit
- Schedule 1: Includes cheque copy (if exists and willing to produce)

Step 5: Apply for Inspection
- Inspect cheque, verify details (amount, date, payee)

Step 6: Notice to Produce
- Require defendant to produce original cheque at trial (for bank verification if needed)
```

### Scenario 3: Privileged Document (Lawyer-Client Communication)

```
Facts:
- Plaintiff applies for discovery
- Defendant has email to lawyer seeking legal advice

Defendant's Response:

Affidavit of Documents:
- Schedule 2: "Email dated 10/02/2024 to lawyer [Name] seeking legal advice"
- Ground for objection: Legal professional privilege (Section 126 Evidence Act)

Plaintiff's Options:
(a) Accept privilege claim (cannot access document)
(b) Challenge privilege (apply to court to inspect)

Court Decides:
- May inspect document in camera (private)
- Decide if truly privileged
- If privileged → No production
- If not privileged (e.g., email discussing facts, not seeking advice) → Order production
```

### Scenario 4: Electronic Evidence (WhatsApp Chat)

```
Facts:
- Defendant sent WhatsApp message admitting breach
- Plaintiff wants to use as evidence

Procedure:

Step 1: Disclose in Plaint
- State: "Defendant sent WhatsApp message dated 15/01/2024 admitting breach"

Step 2: Discovery
- Include in list of documents: "Screenshot of WhatsApp chat dated 15/01/2024"

Step 3: At Trial
- Produce screenshot (printout)
- Produce Section 65B certificate:
  * Computer/phone from which screenshot taken
  * WhatsApp regularly used for communication
  * Screenshot taken in ordinary course
  * Phone operating properly

Step 4: Proof
- With certificate → Admissible
- Without certificate → Inadmissible (Arjun Panditrao)

Alternative (if defendant admits):
- Serve notice to admit WhatsApp message
- If admitted → No need for Section 65B certificate
```

### Scenario 5: Document in Bank's Possession

```
Facts:
- Plaintiff needs bank statement to prove defendant's account transactions
- Bank not party to suit

Solution:

Step 1: Application for Summons (Order XVI Rule 6)
- Request court to summon bank to produce defendant's bank statement

Step 2: Bank's Response
- Objects: Banking secrecy (customer confidentiality)
- OR Produces: If defendant consents OR court orders

Step 3: Court Decision
- May order bank to produce (if relevant to suit and court overrides confidentiality in interest of justice)
- May reject (if privilege/confidentiality stronger than relevance)

Alternative:
- Defendant voluntarily produces bank statement (avoids third-party summons)
```

## Case Law

### 1. Arjun Panditrao Londhe v. Kailash Kushanrao Gorantyal (SC 2020)
```
Facts: Electronic evidence (call detail record) produced without Section 65B certificate
Held:
- Section 65B certificate MANDATORY for electronic evidence
- No exception to certificate requirement
- Without certificate → Electronic evidence inadmissible
- Overrules previous judgments allowing electronic evidence without certificate
```

### 2. Sharma v. Satish Chandra (SC 1970)
```
Facts: Compulsory production of documents tending to incriminate
Held:
- Article 20(3) protection against self-incrimination extends to documents
- Cannot compel production if document tends to expose to criminal charge
- Privilege to be claimed by party (not automatic)
```

### 3. Duncan v. Cammell Laird (UK 1942, persuasive in India)
```
Facts: State claiming privilege for official documents
Held:
- State can claim privilege if disclosure injurious to public interest
- BUT court examines document (in camera if needed) to decide privilege validity
- Blanket claim insufficient; must show specific injury to public interest
```

### 4. Ramesh Chandra Agrawal v. Regency Hospital (SC 2009)
```
Facts: Defendant not producing documents disclosed in affidavit
Held:
- After disclosure in affidavit (Schedule 1), party MUST produce on inspection
- Refusal → Court may draw adverse inference OR strike out defense
- Discovery imposes obligation to produce (not optional)
```

### 5. Anvar P.V. v. P.K. Basheer (SC 2014) [OVERRULED by Arjun Panditrao]
```
Facts: Secondary electronic evidence admissibility
Held (at that time, now overruled):
- Section 65B certificate not mandatory if original electronic device produced
- Computer printout could be proved under Section 63 (secondary evidence)

Arjun Panditrao (2020) overruled this:
- Section 65B certificate ALWAYS required (no exception)
```

## Summary Checklist

### Discovery Application Checklist:

```
☐ File application under Order XI Rule 12 for discovery
☐ Specify documents sought (describe with particularity)
☐ Show relevance to matters in question
☐ Serve notice on opposite party
☐ Attend hearing
☐ If ordered, opposite party files affidavit of documents (Schedules 1, 2, 3)
☐ Apply for inspection of documents in Schedule 1
☐ Inspect documents at fixed time/place
☐ Select documents to rely on
☐ Serve notice to produce at trial (Order XI Rule 15)
```

### Document Proof Checklist (Trial):

```
☐ Ensure document disclosed in pleadings/list (Order XIII Rule 1)
☐ Serve notice to admit document on opposite party
☐ If admitted → No further proof needed
☐ If denied → Prepare proof:
   ☐ Public document: Certified copy (Section 76)
   ☐ Private document: Original + witness to prove execution
   ☐ Electronic document: Original/printout + Section 65B certificate
☐ Produce document at trial
☐ Examine witness to prove (if not admitted)
☐ Mark document as exhibit
```

## Common Mistakes

```
Mistake 1: Not Disclosing Document in Pleadings
❌ Rely on document at trial without annexing to plaint or listing
✓ Annex to plaint (Order VII Rule 14) OR list in affidavit of documents

Mistake 2: Electronic Evidence Without Section 65B Certificate
❌ Produce WhatsApp chat screenshot without certificate (inadmissible after Arjun Panditrao)
✓ Always obtain Section 65B certificate for electronic evidence

Mistake 3: Refusing Inspection After Disclosure
❌ Disclose document in Schedule 1 of affidavit but refuse inspection
✓ If disclosed in Schedule 1, MUST allow inspection (or face adverse consequences)

Mistake 4: Not Challenging False Privilege Claim
❌ Accept opponent's privilege claim without question
✓ Apply to court to examine document (in camera) to verify privilege validity

Mistake 5: Not Serving Notice to Admit
❌ Assume opponent will admit documents; go straight to trial proof
✓ Serve notice to admit; if admitted, saves time and proof effort

Mistake 6: Blanket Discovery Request (Fishing Expedition)
❌ Request "all documents in defendant's possession"
✓ Specify documents with particularity (relate to matters in question)

Mistake 7: Not Taking Adverse Inference
❌ Opponent withholds document; plaintiff doesn't request adverse inference
✓ Apply for adverse inference under Section 114(g) Evidence Act
```
