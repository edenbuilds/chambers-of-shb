# Protocol: Appeals and Revision (India)

**Protocol ID**: IL-CPC-APPEALS-REVISION  
**Domain**: Civil Procedure  
**Subdomain**: Post-Judgment  
**Version**: 1.0.0  
**Last Updated**: 2025-12-06

## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on appeals, second appeals, and revision petitions in civil matters under the Code of Civil Procedure 1908, including grounds, procedures, and strategic considerations.

## Activation Triggers

- User asks "Can I appeal this judgment?"
- Need to file appeal from decree/order
- Second appeal to High Court questions
- Revision petition under Section 115
- Appeal limitation queries
- Stay of decree pending appeal

## Core Principles

### 1. Appeal = Right (Subject to Law)

```
Section 96: "Save where otherwise expressly provided, an appeal shall lie from 
every decree passed by any Court exercising original jurisdiction"

General Rule: APPEAL IS A RIGHT
→ From every decree (unless expressly barred)
→ Not from every order (only specified orders - Order 43)

Exceptions (No Appeal):
- Expressly barred by CPC or other statute
- Decree by consent (unless fraud/misrepresentation - limited grounds)
- Certain interlocutory orders (not in Order 43 Rule 1 list)
- Small value suits (if state notification bars appeal)
```

### 2. Hierarchy of Appeals

```
First Appeal (Section 96-99):
FROM: Original decree of any court
TO: Immediately higher court (District Court → High Court; Civil Judge → District Court)
SCOPE: Full rehearing on facts + law

Second Appeal (Section 100):
FROM: Appellate decree (first appeal decided)
TO: High Court
SCOPE: ONLY substantial questions of LAW (NOT facts)

Special Leave Petition (Article 136 Constitution):
FROM: High Court judgment/order
TO: Supreme Court
SCOPE: Discretionary (SC may refuse)
```

## FIRST APPEAL (Section 96-99, Order 41)

### Right of Appeal (Section 96)

```
Section 96: Appeal lies from every decree passed by court exercising original 
jurisdiction to court authorized to hear appeals

Appealable:
✓ Decrees (preliminary or final)
✓ Decrees in suits (not in execution or other proceedings)

NOT Appealable:
✗ Orders (unless specified in Order 43 Rule 1)
✗ Decrees expressly barred
✗ Decrees by consent (limited exceptions)

Who Can Appeal:
- Person aggrieved by decree
- Legal representative of deceased person aggrieved
- Assignee (if assignment before decree)
```

### Procedure for First Appeal (Order 41)

```
Step 1: File MEMORANDUM OF APPEAL
Within: 90 days from date of decree
Extension: Up to 90 MORE days if sufficient cause shown (Section 5 Limitation Act)

Contents (Order 41 Rule 1):
1. Name and description of appellant
2. Name and description of respondent
3. Decree appealed from (date, court, suit number)
4. Grounds of appeal (specific, not vague)
5. Relief sought
6. Valuation for court fees
7. Verification (by appellant)

Step 2: Pay COURT FEES
Ad valorem (on decree value) or fixed (as per state court fees rules)

Step 3: Serve NOTICE to Respondent
Memorandum of appeal + grounds

Step 4: Respondent Files VAKALATNAMA
(If contesting appeal)

Step 5: Hearing
Arguments on merits
Reference to evidence/documents from trial court record

Step 6: Judgment
Appellate court decides
Affirms, varies, or reverses decree
```

### Grounds of Appeal (Order 41 Rule 1)

```
Grounds must be SPECIFIC, stating:

1. Errors of Fact:
   "The learned trial court erred in holding that defendant paid Rs. 5 lakhs, 
   when evidence (PW1 testimony + Exhibit P-2) clearly shows no payment."

2. Errors of Law:
   "The learned trial court erred in applying Section 73 Indian Contract Act 
   when Section 74 (liquidated damages) was applicable."

3. Errors in Appreciation of Evidence:
   "The learned trial court erred in disbelieving plaintiff's witness PW1 
   without valid reasons, when defendant's witness DW1 was contradictory."

4. Procedural Errors:
   "The learned trial court erred in passing decree without giving defendant 
   opportunity to cross-examine plaintiff's witness PW2."

NOT Valid Grounds:
- Mere disagreement with findings (without showing error)
- Re-arguing facts without pointing out specific errors
- New facts not pleaded/proved at trial
```

### Powers of Appellate Court (Section 107, Order 41 Rule 33)

```
Section 107: Appellate Court Powers

Appellate court may:

(a) DETERMINE CASE FINALLY:
    - Decide on merits
    - Affirm, vary, or reverse decree

(b) REMAND CASE:
    - Send back to trial court for fresh decision
    - Reasons: Trial court decided on wrong issue, failed to frame/decide issue, 
      jurisdictional defect

(c) FRAME ISSUES AND REFER:
    - Frame specific issues
    - Send to trial court for findings
    - Return with findings for appellate court to decide

(d) TAKE ADDITIONAL EVIDENCE (Order 41 Rule 27):
    - If necessary for pronouncing judgment
    - If trial court improperly refused/admitted evidence
    - Conditions:
      * Court records reasons why additional evidence needed
      * Opportunity to opposite party

(e) PASS ANY OTHER ORDER:
    - As justice requires
    - Modify decree
    - Grant different relief (if within scope of pleadings)
```

### Scope of First Appeal

```
First Appeal = FULL REHEARING

Appellate court:
- Re-appreciates evidence
- Reconsiders findings of fact
- Reviews application of law
- Can reverse trial court on facts + law

Standard:
- Appellate court NOT bound by trial court's findings
- BUT trial court's findings entitled to weight (saw witnesses, judged credibility)
- Appellate court interferes only if:
  * Findings perverse (against evidence)
  * Material evidence ignored
  * Inadmissible evidence relied upon
  * Legal error in approach
```

### Remand (Order 41 Rule 23-25)

```
Rule 23: Appellate Court may remand if:
(a) Trial court decided suit on preliminary point without deciding all issues
(b) Trial court decided wrong issue
(c) Trial court failed to try suit on merits due to jurisdictional or procedural error
(d) Additional evidence necessary + cannot be taken by appellate court

Procedure on Remand:
1. Appellate court passes remand order (specifying issues for fresh decision)
2. Trial court re-hears on remanded issues
3. Records evidence, makes findings
4. Sends findings + evidence to appellate court
5. Appellate court decides appeal based on fresh findings

Effect of Remand:
- Original decree set aside
- Fresh trial on remanded issues
- Parties may adduce fresh evidence on remanded issues
- After fresh findings, appellate court decides

Limitation:
- Frequent remands discouraged (delays disposal)
- Remand only if absolutely necessary
```

## APPEAL FROM ORDERS (Section 104, Order 43)

```
Section 104: Appeals from orders

General Rule: ORDERS NOT APPEALABLE (only decrees appealable)

Exception: Order 43 Rule 1 lists SPECIFIC ORDERS from which appeal lies:

(a) Rejection of plaint (Order VII Rule 11)
(b) Rejection of set-off/counter-claim (Order VIII Rule 6C)
(c) Refusal to grant temporary injunction (Order XXXIX Rule 1-2)
(d) Granting/refusing to grant injunction (interim)
(e) Receiver appointment/removal
(f) Commission refusal/grant
(g) Setting aside ex parte decree (Order IX Rule 13) - refusal
(h) Rejection of application to set aside decree (Order IX Rule 13)
...and 14 other specified orders

Appeal Timeline: 30 DAYS from order
TO: Same court as first appeal from decree would lie

NOT Appealable Orders (Examples):
- Adjournment orders
- Cost orders (unless part of decree)
- Discovery orders
- Orders allowing/refusing amendment (generally)
- Most interlocutory/procedural orders
```

## SECOND APPEAL (Section 100, Order 42)

### Restricted Scope (Section 100 - Amended 1976)

```
Section 100: Second appeal to High Court on SUBSTANTIAL QUESTION OF LAW only

BEFORE 1976: Second appeal on facts + law (full rehearing)
AFTER 1976: ONLY on substantial questions of LAW

Second Appeal Conditions:

1. FROM: Appellate decree (first appeal decided)
2. TO: High Court
3. GROUND: Case involves SUBSTANTIAL QUESTION OF LAW

NOT Available for:
- Questions of fact
- Mixed questions of fact and law (unless substantial legal question can be extracted)
- Re-appreciation of evidence
- Perverse findings (remedy: First appeal, not second appeal)

What is "Substantial Question of Law"?

1. Substantial:
   - Important, material (not trivial/technical)
   - Affects rights of parties significantly
   - Not settled by precedent (or conflicting precedents)

2. Question of Law:
   - Legal principle/interpretation
   - Construction of statute/document
   - Applicability of legal provision
   
   NOT Question of Law:
   - Findings of fact (credibility of witnesses, weight of evidence)
   - Whether evidence sufficient (fact)
   - Appreciation of evidence (fact)
```

### Formulation of Substantial Question (Section 100)

```
Procedure:

Step 1: Appellant Formulates Substantial Question of Law
In memorandum of second appeal:
"This appeal raises the following substantial question of law:
Whether, on the facts and circumstances of this case, the first appellate court 
erred in law in holding that Section 74 Indian Contract Act applies when the 
contract clause is a genuine pre-estimate of damages and not a penalty?"

Step 2: High Court Decides Whether Substantial Question Exists
- Admission stage (preliminary hearing)
- HC examines: Is question substantial? Is it question of law?
- Options:
  (a) Admit appeal (if substantial question of law present)
  (b) Dismiss appeal (if no substantial question)

Step 3: If Admitted, HC Frames Substantial Question
HC may reframe or add questions

Step 4: Hearing Limited to Substantial Question
Arguments confined to framed question(s)
HC does NOT re-appreciate evidence or findings of fact

Step 5: HC Decides
Answers substantial question
Affirms, varies, or reverses appellate decree based on answer
```

### Examples of Substantial Questions of Law

```
✓ Substantial Questions (Second Appeal Admitted):

1. "Whether suit for specific performance is barred when contract does not 
   specify time as of the essence?" (Legal interpretation)

2. "Whether Section 27 Indian Contract Act bars a non-compete clause during 
   employment or only post-employment?" (Statutory construction)

3. "Whether partition can be ordered when one co-owner is minor?" 
   (Legal capacity issue)

✗ NOT Substantial Questions (Second Appeal Dismissed):

1. "Whether plaintiff proved payment by preponderance of evidence?" 
   (Question of fact - appreciation of evidence)

2. "Whether defendant's witness was credible?" 
   (Question of fact - credibility assessment)

3. "Whether first appellate court correctly weighed evidence?" 
   (Question of fact - re-appreciation)
```

### Powers in Second Appeal (Order 42)

```
High Court in second appeal may:

1. Reverse/Affirm/Modify first appellate decree (Rule 3)
2. Remand (if necessary to determine substantial question) (Rule 3)
3. Frame issues and refer to lower court (rare)
4. NOT re-appreciate evidence (limited to legal question)

Remand in Second Appeal:
Very rare (defeats purpose of restricting second appeal to law)
Allowed only if:
- Lower court decided on wrong legal principle
- Substantial question cannot be answered without fresh findings on specific issue
```

## REVISION (Section 115 CPC)

### Revisional Jurisdiction (Section 115 - AMENDED 2002)

```
Section 115 (Before 2002):
High Court could revise ANY order of subordinate court
Scope: Broad revisional power

Section 115 (After 2002):
HIGH COURT SHALL NOT revise unless:
(a) Subordinate court EXERCISED JURISDICTION NOT VESTED IN IT, OR
(b) Subordinate court FAILED TO EXERCISE JURISDICTION VESTED IN IT, OR
(c) Subordinate court ACTED ILLEGALLY OR WITH MATERIAL IRREGULARITY in 
    exercise of jurisdiction

Effect of Amendment:
- Revisional power severely curtailed
- NOT a regular appellate remedy (like pre-2002)
- Limited to jurisdictional errors, illegality, material irregularity

Note: Many High Courts' revisional jurisdiction under Section 115 restricted 
to orders NOT appealable (no revision if appeal lies)
```

### Grounds for Revision (Post-2002)

```
1. Excess of Jurisdiction:
   - Court exercised power it did not have
   - Example: Civil Judge (Junior Division) trying suit worth Rs. 10 lakhs 
     (beyond pecuniary jurisdiction)

2. Lack of Jurisdiction:
   - Court failed to exercise power it had
   - Example: Court refused to hear suit on ground it lacks jurisdiction, 
     when it actually had jurisdiction

3. Illegality:
   - Court acted contrary to law
   - Example: Passed decree without giving opportunity to defendant (violation 
     of natural justice)

4. Material Irregularity:
   - Procedural error materially affecting rights
   - Example: Framed wrong issues, leading to erroneous decision

NOT Grounds for Revision (Post-2002):
- Erroneous findings of fact (appeal remedy)
- Incorrect appreciation of evidence (appeal remedy)
- Wrong decision on merits (appeal remedy)
```

### Limitation for Revision

```
Section 115: 90 days from order (NOT expressly stated, but applied)

In Practice:
- No specific limitation in CPC for revision
- Courts apply 90 days (analogous to appeal limitation)
- Can be extended if sufficient cause
```

## APPEAL/REVISION - STRATEGIC CHOICE

```
Scenario: Subordinate court passed order refusing to set aside ex parte decree

Options:

1. APPEAL (Order 43 Rule 1(h)):
   - Order refusing to set aside ex parte decree is appealable
   - Timeline: 30 days
   - Full hearing on merits

2. REVISION (Section 115):
   - Argue jurisdictional error/illegality/material irregularity
   - Timeline: 90 days
   - Limited grounds

Recommendation: APPEAL (preferred - broader scope)
Revision only if appeal not available OR appeal time expired (within 90 days revision time)
```

## STAY OF DECREE PENDING APPEAL (Order 41 Rule 5)

```
Rule 5: Appellate court may stay execution of decree pending appeal

Conditions:
1. Application by appellant
2. Appellate court satisfied:
   - Prima facie case (substantial question involved)
   - Balance of convenience (appellant suffers greater hardship if no stay)
   - Irreparable injury (if decree executed, cannot be undone)

Procedure:

Step 1: File Application for Stay
With appeal OR separately after appeal admitted

Step 2: Affidavit
Stating:
- Decree executable
- If executed, appellant suffers irreparable loss
- Appellant willing to furnish security (deposit/bank guarantee)

Step 3: Hearing
Court examines:
- Merits of appeal (prima facie)
- Hardship to appellant vs respondent
- Public interest (if any)

Step 4: Order
(a) Grant stay (unconditional OR conditional - deposit/security)
(b) Refuse stay (decree executable despite appeal)

Conditions for Stay:
- Deposit decree amount in court, OR
- Furnish bank guarantee, OR
- Other security (as court directs)

Effect of Stay:
- Decree cannot be executed till appeal decided
- Respondent cannot recover/enforce decree
- Status quo maintained

Vacation of Stay:
- Respondent may apply to vacate stay (if circumstances change)
- Court may vacate if:
  * Appeal delayed without valid reason
  * Appellant abusing stay (delaying deliberately)
  * Security insufficient
```

## LIMITATION FOR APPEALS (Limitation Act 1963)

```
First Appeal from Decree (Section 96):
- 90 days from decree date
- Extension: Up to 90 MORE days (Section 5 - if sufficient cause)

Appeal from Order (Order 43):
- 30 days from order date
- Extension: Up to 30 MORE days (Section 5)

Second Appeal (Section 100):
- 90 days from first appellate decree
- Extension: Up to 90 MORE days (Section 5)

Revision (Section 115):
- 90 days from order (practice, not expressly stated)

Special Leave Petition (Article 136):
- 90 days from High Court order/judgment
- Extension: Discretionary (Supreme Court may condone any delay)

Counting of Days (Section 12 Limitation Act):
- Exclude date of decree/order
- Include last day
- If last day is holiday → Limit extends to next working day

Sufficient Cause (Section 5):
- Illness, accident, bona fide mistake
- Continuing cause (litigation in wrong forum)
- NOT mere negligence/laches
```

## CONDONATION OF DELAY (Section 5 Limitation Act)

```
Section 5: "Any appeal or application may be admitted after prescribed period 
if appellant/applicant satisfies court that there was sufficient cause for not 
preferring appeal/application within such period"

Burden: On appellant to show sufficient cause

What is "Sufficient Cause"?
- Illness preventing filing (medical certificate)
- Accident, natural calamity
- Bona fide mistake (filed in wrong court, promptly corrected)
- Continuing litigation in wrong forum (Section 14 exclusion)
- Fraud by opposite party preventing filing

NOT Sufficient Cause:
- Mere negligence/carelessness
- Advocate's mistake (unless extraordinary circumstances)
- Financial hardship (not filing fees)
- Busy schedule

Liberal Approach:
- Courts generally liberal in condoning delay (substantial justice over technicality)
- BUT gross negligence/deliberate inaction → No condonation

Maximum Condonation:
- 90 additional days for appeal from decree (total 180 days)
- 30 additional days for appeal from order (total 60 days)
- NO LIMIT for SLP (Supreme Court may condone any delay)
```

## CASE LAW

### 1. Surya Dev Rai v. Ram Chander Rai (SC 2003)
```
Facts: Second appeal on questions of fact
Held:
- Section 100 (post-1976): Second appeal ONLY on substantial questions of law
- Questions of fact NOT appealable in second appeal
- High Court must formulate substantial question before admitting second appeal
- If no substantial question → Dismiss appeal at admission stage
```

### 2. Santosh Hazari v. Purushottam Tiwari (SC 2001)
```
Facts: Condonation of delay in filing appeal
Held:
- Section 5 Limitation Act: Sufficient cause required
- Courts should adopt liberal approach in condoning delay (substantial justice)
- BUT gross negligence/deliberate inaction → No condonation
- Each case on its own facts
```

### 3. Ramesh Chandra Sankla v. Vikram Cement (SC 2008)
```
Facts: Scope of first appeal; whether appellate court can re-appreciate evidence
Held:
- First appeal is full rehearing on facts + law
- Appellate court can re-appreciate evidence
- BUT trial court's findings entitled to weight (saw witnesses, judged demeanor)
- Appellate court interferes only if findings perverse/against evidence
```

### 4. Keshavlal Khemchand v. State of Gujarat (SC 2015)
```
Facts: Revision under Section 115 CPC post-2002 amendment
Held:
- Section 115 (post-2002): Limited to jurisdictional errors, illegality, material irregularity
- NOT a regular appellate remedy
- Cannot revise on grounds of erroneous findings of fact/law (appeal remedy)
- Revisional power severely curtailed compared to pre-2002
```

### 5. Anil Kumar Singh v. Shivnath Mishra (SC 2011)
```
Facts: Stay of decree pending appeal
Held:
- Order 41 Rule 5: Stay discretionary
- Factors: Prima facie case, balance of convenience, irreparable injury
- Generally stay granted if appellant furnishes security (deposit/bank guarantee)
- Prevents miscarriage of justice if decree executed and later reversed
```

## SUMMARY CHECKLIST

### First Appeal Checklist:

```
☐ Memorandum of appeal drafted (grounds specific)
☐ Filed within 90 days from decree (or with condonation application)
☐ Court fees paid (ad valorem on decree value)
☐ Certified copy of decree attached
☐ Notice served on respondent
☐ Application for stay of decree (if needed) filed
☐ Security/deposit (if stay granted) furnished
☐ Attend hearing
☐ Judgment received
```

### Second Appeal Checklist:

```
☐ Formulate substantial question of law
☐ File within 90 days from first appellate decree
☐ Court fees paid
☐ Certified copy of first appellate decree attached
☐ Show how substantial question of law arises
☐ Admission hearing (HC decides if question substantial)
☐ If admitted, attend hearing (arguments limited to legal question)
☐ Judgment received
```

### Revision Checklist:

```
☐ Identify jurisdictional error/illegality/material irregularity
☐ File within 90 days from order
☐ Show no appeal lies OR appeal time expired (in some HCs)
☐ State specific ground (excess/lack of jurisdiction, illegality, irregularity)
☐ Court fees paid
☐ Attend hearing
☐ Order received
```

## COMMON MISTAKES

```
Mistake 1: Second Appeal on Questions of Fact
❌ File second appeal challenging findings of fact/appreciation of evidence
✓ Second appeal ONLY on substantial questions of LAW (Section 100 post-1976)

Mistake 2: Not Formulating Substantial Question in Second Appeal
❌ Memorandum of second appeal does not formulate specific legal question
✓ MUST formulate substantial question clearly (HC decides if substantial)

Mistake 3: Not Seeking Stay of Decree
❌ File appeal but don't apply for stay; respondent executes decree
✓ Apply for stay of decree pending appeal (Order 41 Rule 5) to prevent execution

Mistake 4: General/Vague Grounds of Appeal
❌ "Trial court erred in passing decree" (no specificity)
✓ "Trial court erred in [specific finding] when evidence (PW1 + Exhibit P-2) shows [contrary fact]"

Mistake 5: Missing Limitation
❌ File appeal after 90 days without condonation application
✓ File within 90 days OR with condonation application (Section 5) showing sufficient cause

Mistake 6: Attempting Revision When Appeal Lies
❌ File revision under Section 115 when order is appealable (Order 43)
✓ File appeal (if lies); revision only if no appeal OR appeal time expired

Mistake 7: Not Paying Security for Stay
❌ Court grants stay conditional on deposit; appellant doesn't deposit; stay vacated
✓ Comply with stay conditions immediately (deposit decree amount/furnish bank guarantee)
```
