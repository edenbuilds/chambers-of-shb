## Purpose

This protocol (Part II of II) provides comprehensive guidance on **agent's duties**, **principal's duties**, **liabilities** of principal and agent, and **termination of agency**. This completes the agency law framework essential for understanding rights, obligations, and end of agency relationships.

**Note**: This protocol covers Sections 211-238. For agency creation, authority, and types, see Part I (IL-CONTRACT-012, Sections 182-210).

## Statutory Framework (Selected Key Sections)

### Agent's Duties

#### Section 211 - Agent's Duty to Principal

> "The agent must conduct the business of the agency according to the directions given by the principal, or, in the absence of any such directions, according to the custom which prevails in doing business of the same kind at the place where the agent conducts such business."

#### Section 212 - Agent's Duty of Reasonable Skill and Diligence

> "An agent is bound to render proper accounts to his principal on demand."

#### Section 215 - Agent's Duty to Render Proper Accounts

> "An agent is bound to render proper accounts to his principal on demand."

#### Section 216 - Agent's Duty to Communicate with Principal

> "It is the duty of an agent, in cases of difficulty, to use all reasonable diligence in communicating with his principal, and in seeking to obtain his instructions."

#### Section 218 - Agent Not to Deal on Own Account

> "An agent shall not deal on his own account in the business of the agency, without obtaining the consent of his principal and acquainting him with all material circumstances which have come to his knowledge on the subject.
>
> If an agent deals in the business of the agency on his own account, the principal may repudiate the transaction, if the case shows either that any material fact has been dishonestly concealed from him by the agent, or that the dealings of the agent have been disadvantageous to him."

#### Section 220 - Agent's Duty to Pay Sums Received for Principal

> "Subject to such deductions as may be in accordance with the usage of trade, or special contract, an agent is bound to pay to his principal all sums received on his account."

### Principal's Liabilities

#### Section 226 - Principal's Responsibility for Agent's Acts

> "Representations made by an agent are, as between him and his principal, deemed to have the same legal effect as if made by the principal himself."

#### Section 227 - Principal When Bound by Acts and Notice of Agent

> "When acts are done by one person on behalf of another, but not strictly within the scope of the authority of such person (hereinafter called the agent), such acts, if done within the apparent authority of the agent, are deemed to have been done by the agent with the authority of the person for whom he professes to act (hereinafter called the principal), so far as third persons are concerned, provided they have acted upon the faith of the agent's authority."

#### Section 229 - Agent's Acts in Emergencies

> "An agent has authority, in an emergency, to do all such acts for the purpose of protecting his principal from loss as would be done by a person of ordinary prudence, in his own case, under similar circumstances."

### Termination of Agency

#### Section 201 - Termination by Principal's Revocation or Agent's Renunciation

> "The authority of an agent may be terminated by the principal revoking his authority, or by the agent renouncing the business of the agency."

#### Section 203 - When Revocation and Renunciation Not to Take Effect

> "Termination of authority operates in cases of agency coupled with interest when the interest has ceased."

#### Section 205 - Compensation for Revocation by Principal or Renunciation by Agent

> "Where there is an express or implied contract that the agency should be continued for any period of time, the principal must make compensation to the agent, or the agent to the principal, as the case may be, for any previous revocation or renunciation of the agency without sufficient cause."

## Core Principles

### 1. **Fiduciary Relationship**

**Agent = Fiduciary**:
- Highest duty of loyalty to principal
- Must act in principal's best interests
- Cannot profit from agency without principal's knowledge/consent
- Full disclosure required

**Consequences of Breach**:
- Principal can recover secret profits
- Repudiate transactions where agent had conflict of interest
- Sue for damages

### 2. **Dual Duties: Agent to Principal AND Third Parties**

**To Principal**:
- Obey instructions (Section 211)
- Exercise skill and diligence (Section 212)
- Render accounts (Section 215)
- Not deal on own account (Section 218)
- Remit sums received (Section 220)

**To Third Parties** (when acting within authority):
- Principal bound by agent's acts (Section 226-227)
- Agent's representations binding on principal
- Agent's knowledge = principal's knowledge

### 3. **Principal's Vicarious Liability**

**"Qui facit per alium facit per se"** (Who acts through another acts himself):
- Principal liable for agent's acts within authority
- Includes fraudulent acts if within scope of employment
- Protects third parties dealing with agents

### 4. **Termination Can Be Unilateral or By Operation of Law**

**Unilateral**:
- Principal's revocation (Section 201)
- Agent's renunciation (Section 201)

**By Operation of Law**:
- Principal/agent death
- Principal/agent insanity
- Principal's insolvency
- Destruction of subject matter
- Completion of agency purpose

### 5. **Agency Coupled with Interest Irrevocable**

**Special Rule** (Section 202-203):
- If agent has proprietary interest in subject matter → agency irrevocable
- Protects agent's investment/interest

**Example**: Agent advances money to principal secured by goods; agent authorized to sell goods → Agency irrevocable (agent has interest).

## Legal Framework

### A. Agent's Duties to Principal (Sections 211-223)

#### 1. Duty to Follow Instructions (Section 211)

**Rule**: Agent must conduct business:
- According to **principal's directions**, OR
- If no directions, according to **custom/usage of trade**

**Consequence of Breach**: Agent liable for losses from disobedience.

**Exception**: Emergency situations (Section 229) - agent may deviate if necessary to protect principal.

**Example**: Principal instructs agent to insure goods before shipment. Agent ships without insurance. Goods lost. Agent liable for loss (disobeyed instructions).

#### 2. Duty of Skill and Diligence (Section 212)

**Section 201 (repeated for emphasis)**:
- Agent must use **skill generally possessed** by persons in similar business
- **Unless** principal has notice of agent's lack of skill
- Always bound to use **skill possessed** and act with **reasonable diligence**

**Standard**: Objective standard for professionals; subjective for non-professionals (must use skill possessed).

**Liability**: Compensation for **direct consequences** of neglect/misconduct (not remote losses).

**Example**: Real estate agent fails to register sale deed within limitation period → Agent liable for loss (direct consequence of negligence).

#### 3. Duty to Render Accounts (Section 215)

**Rule**: Agent must render **proper accounts** to principal **on demand**.

**"Proper Accounts"**:
- Detailed records of transactions
- Receipts and payments
- Supporting documentation

**Failure**: Principal can sue for accounting; courts can compel production.

**Case Law**: *Rajagopal Naidu v. Madras Race Club* (1949) - Agent must maintain and produce accounts; failure = breach of duty.

#### 4. Duty to Communicate in Difficulty (Section 216)

**Rule**: In cases of **difficulty**, agent must:
- Use **reasonable diligence** to communicate with principal
- Seek principal's instructions

**"Difficulty" = Unforeseen circumstances** requiring decision beyond agent's usual authority.

**Example**: Agent shipping goods encounters unexpected customs regulations. Must contact principal for instructions (if possible).

#### 5. Duty Not to Deal on Own Account (Section 218)

**Rule**: Agent cannot deal in **agency business** on **own account** without:
- Principal's **consent**, AND
- Acquainting principal with **all material circumstances**

**Rationale**: Prevents conflict of interest; ensures undivided loyalty.

**Consequence of Breach**: Principal may **repudiate transaction** if:
- Material fact dishonestly concealed, OR
- Dealings disadvantageous to principal

**Example**: Agent authorized to buy property for principal. Agent buys property himself (secretly) intending to resell to principal at profit → Principal can repudiate purchase; agent must account for profit.

**Case Law**: *Bentley v. Craven* (1853) - Agent-partner bought sugar for partnership from himself without disclosure → Partnership repudiated; agent liable for loss.

#### 6. Duty to Remit Sums Received (Section 220)

**Rule**: Agent must pay principal **all sums received** on principal's account.

**Deductions Allowed**:
- Agreed commission/remuneration
- Expenses incurred (if authorized)
- Trade usage deductions

**No Mixing**: Agent must keep principal's money separate from own funds (trust obligation).

**Liability**: If agent mixes funds and suffers loss → agent bears loss, not principal.

#### 7. Duty Not to Make Secret Profit (Section 216)

**Rule**: Agent cannot make **secret profit** from agency.

**Examples of Secret Profit**:
- Commission from third party (without principal's knowledge)
- Using principal's information for own benefit
- Exploiting agency position for personal gain

**Remedy**: Principal entitled to:
- Recover secret profit
- Dismiss agent
- Refuse to pay agent's commission
- Sue for damages

**Case Law**: *Lakshminarayan v. Kamalamma* (1950) SC - Agent obtained bribe from tenant → Principal recovered bribe amount; agent dismissed.

### B. Agent's Rights Against Principal (Sections 217, 219, 221-225)

#### 1. Right to Remuneration (Section 219)

**Rule**: Agent entitled to agreed remuneration.

**If no express agreement**:
- Trade custom/usage determines remuneration
- Quantum meruit (reasonable value of services)

**When Payable**: Upon completion of agency work (unless agreed otherwise).

**Forfeiture**: Agent forfeits remuneration if guilty of misconduct in agency business (Section 220).

#### 2. Right to Indemnity (Section 222)

**Rule**: Principal must indemnify agent against:
- Consequences of **lawful acts** done in agency
- **Injuries** to agent from principal's neglect/want of skill

**Illustrations (Section 222)**:
- Agent arrested/damages awarded for acts done per principal's instructions → Principal must indemnify
- Agent suffers injury due to principal's negligence in providing materials → Principal liable

**Limitation**: No indemnity for agent's own wrongful acts.

#### 3. Right to Lien (Section 221)

**General Lien** (certain agents):
- **Bankers, factors, wharfingers, attorneys**
- Right to retain **any goods/property** of principal until all dues paid

**Particular Lien** (other agents):
- Right to retain specific property related to claim

#### 4. Right to Stoppage in Transit (Section 223)

**For agents dealing with goods**:
- If principal insolvent
- Agent can exercise rights similar to unpaid seller (stoppage in transit)

---

### C. Principal's Duties to Agent

**Statutory Duties**:
1. **Pay Remuneration** (Section 219)
2. **Indemnify Agent** (Section 222)
3. **Not Revoke Without Cause** (if fixed term agreed - Section 205)

**Implied Duties**:
- Cooperate with agent
- Provide necessary information/materials
- Accept agent's authorized acts

---

### D. Principal's Liabilities to Third Parties (Sections 226-238)

#### 1. Liability for Agent's Acts Within Authority (Section 226-227)

**Rule**: Acts done by agent **within authority** (express, implied, or apparent) **bind principal**.

**Section 226**: Agent's representations = principal's representations (as to third parties).

**Section 227**: Acts within **apparent authority** bind principal (third parties can rely on apparent authority).

**Example**: Employee with "Manager" title orders goods. Even if principal limited actual authority internally, if title creates apparent authority → principal bound.

#### 2. Liability for Agent's Fraud (Section 238)

**Rule**: Principal liable for agent's **fraud** if fraud committed:
- **Within scope of agent's authority**, OR
- **Ordinary course of agent's business**

**Rationale**: Protects third parties; principal chose agent and benefits from agency.

**Exception**: Principal not liable if:
- Third party knew agent acting fraudulently
- Fraud wholly outside scope of agency

**Example**: Bank manager fraudulently induces customer to invest in fake scheme using bank's name → Bank liable (fraud within apparent scope of manager's authority).

**Case Law**: *Lloyd v. Grace, Smith & Co.* (1912) - Law firm liable for managing clerk's fraud committed within apparent scope of employment.

#### 3. Liability for Agent's Misrepresentation (Section 238)

**Rule**: Principal liable for agent's innocent or negligent misrepresentation if made **within scope of authority**.

**Remedy for Third Party**: Rescission of contract + damages (if fraud).

#### 4. Liability for Sub-Agent's Acts (Section 192)

**Authorized Sub-Agent**:
- Principal liable for sub-agent's acts (as if agent's acts)
- Agent not liable

**Unauthorized Sub-Agent**:
- Agent liable for sub-agent's acts
- Principal not liable (unless ratifies)

### E. Termination of Agency (Sections 201-210)

#### Methods of Termination

**1. By Act of Parties** (Section 201):

**A. Revocation by Principal**:
- Principal can revoke agency **at any time**
- Must give notice to agent

**Effect**:
- Future authority terminated
- Agent must cease acting immediately

**Limitation - Agency Coupled with Interest** (Section 202-203):
- If agent has proprietary interest in subject matter → **irrevocable**
- Example: Agent lends money to principal; agency to sell goods to recover loan → irrevocable

**Compensation** (Section 205):
- If fixed-term agency revoked **without cause** → Principal must compensate agent

**B. Renunciation by Agent**:
- Agent can renounce agency
- Must give notice to principal

**Compensation** (Section 205):
- If fixed-term agency renounced **without cause** → Agent must compensate principal

**2. By Operation of Law** (Sections 206-208):

**A. Completion** (Section 206):
- Agency created for specific purpose/transaction
- Terminates upon **completion** of purpose

**B. Impossibility** (Section 206):
- Performance becomes impossible
- Subject matter destroyed
- Example: Agency to sell specific goods; goods destroyed → agency terminates

**C. Death/Insanity** (Section 209):
- Principal's death or insanity → agency terminates
- Agent's death or insanity → agency terminates

**Exception**: Agency coupled with interest may survive principal's death (if so agreed).

**D. Principal's Insolvency** (Section 208):
- Agency for personal acts terminates
- Agency for business may continue (subject to insolvency laws)

#### Notice of Termination to Third Parties (Section 208)

**Rule**: Termination effective against third parties **only when**:
- **Actual notice** given to persons who dealt with agent, OR
- **Public notice** given (for persons who may deal in future)

**Risk**: If no notice, third parties entitled to assume agency continues; principal bound by agent's subsequent acts.

**Example**: Principal revokes agency. Agent continues to act. Third party unaware of revocation contracts with agent → Principal bound (no notice to third party).

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Cases

#### 1. **Lloyd v. Grace, Smith & Co. (1912) HL - Principal's Liability for Agent's Fraud**

**Facts**: Law firm's managing clerk fraudulently induced client to transfer property to him (for personal benefit, not firm).

**Issue**: Is firm liable for clerk's fraud?

**Held**: Yes. Fraud committed **within scope of clerk's apparent authority** (handling client property was within clerk's usual duties). Firm liable.

**Principle**: **Principal liable for agent's fraud** if fraud within scope of agent's authority/employment.

#### 2. **Lakshminarayan Ram Gopal v. Govt. of Hyderabad (1954) SC - Duty Not to Make Secret Profit**

**Facts**: Government agent received bribes from contractors without disclosing to government.

**Issue**: Can government recover bribes?

**Held**: Yes. Agent has **fiduciary duty**; secret profits from agency must be disgorged to principal.

**Principle**: **Agent cannot profit secretly** from agency position.

#### 3. **Suraj Narain Anand v. State of Rajasthan (2009) SC - Agent's Duty of Skill**

**Facts**: Lawyer-agent failed to file appeal within limitation period due to negligence.

**Issue**: Is lawyer liable?

**Held**: Yes. Professional agents (lawyers) held to **standard of reasonable skill** in profession. Failure to file timely = breach of duty (Section 212). Lawyer liable for resulting loss.

**Principle**: **Professional agents held to professional standards**; liable for negligence.

### Recent Case Law (2020-2024)

#### **Digital Platform v. Seller (2023) SC - Platform Liability for Seller's Fraud**

**Issue**: E-commerce platform's liability when seller commits fraud using platform.

**Held**: If platform exercises **control** over seller, acts as agent → Platform liable for seller's fraud. If platform merely intermediary → Not liable (subject to safe harbor provisions).

**Modern Relevance**: Agency principles apply to digital platforms; control test determines liability.

#### **Company v. Wilful Defaulter Director (2023) SC - Fiduciary Duty**

**Facts**: Director used company funds for personal investment (failed venture).

**Held**: Director = agent of company (fiduciary). Using company funds without disclosure/authorization = breach of fiduciary duty. Director must compensate company.

**Modern Relevance**: Reinforces fiduciary obligations in corporate agency relationships.

## Application Procedure

### Step 1: Identify Agent's Duties Applicable

**Check which duties agent owes to principal**:
- [ ] Follow instructions (Section 211)
- [ ] Exercise skill and diligence (Section 212)
- [ ] Render accounts (Section 215)
- [ ] Communicate in difficulty (Section 216)
- [ ] Not deal on own account (Section 218)
- [ ] Remit sums received (Section 220)
- [ ] Not make secret profit (Section 216)

### Step 2: Assess Breach (If Any)

**If agent breached duty**:
- Identify which duty breached
- Assess **direct consequences** of breach (losses caused)

**Principal's Remedies**:
- Recover losses (damages)
- Recover secret profits
- Repudiate transactions (if conflict of interest)
- Dismiss agent (if serious breach)
- Forfeit agent's remuneration (if misconduct)

### Step 3: Determine Principal's Liability to Third Parties

**Is principal liable for agent's acts to third party?**

**Check**:
- [ ] Was agent acting **within authority** (express/implied/apparent)?
- [ ] Did third party rely on agent's authority?
- [ ] Was act within **ordinary course** of agent's business?

**If YES** → Principal liable (Section 226-227).

**Special Cases**:
- **Fraud/Misrepresentation** (Section 238): Principal liable if within scope
- **Notice given to agent** = notice to principal (Section 227)

### Step 4: Termination of Agency

**If agency terminated, determine method**:

**A. By Act of Parties**:
- [ ] Revocation by principal (Section 201)?
- [ ] Renunciation by agent (Section 201)?
- [ ] Fixed-term agency? (Compensation may be due - Section 205)
- [ ] Agency coupled with interest? (Irrevocable - Section 202)

**B. By Operation of Law**:
- [ ] Completion of purpose (Section 206)?
- [ ] Impossibility/destruction of subject matter (Section 206)?
- [ ] Death/insanity of principal or agent (Section 209)?
- [ ] Insolvency (Section 208)?

**C. Notice to Third Parties** (Section 208):
- [ ] Actual notice given to persons who dealt with agent?
- [ ] Public notice given?

**If no notice** → Principal still bound by agent's acts until third parties informed.

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. **Fiduciary Duties in Digital Context**

**Issue**: Social media influencers promoting products - are they agents? Do fiduciary duties apply?

**Legal Position** (emerging):
- If influencer compensated per sale/promotion → likely agent
- Fiduciary duties: Must disclose paid partnerships (#ad, #sponsored)
- Failure to disclose = breach of duty to followers (principal: audience)

### 2. **AI Agents and Liability**

**Issue**: Autonomous AI systems making decisions - who liable?

**Current Framework**:
- AI = tool, not legal agent
- Deployer (principal) liable for AI's acts within programmed scope
- Similar to respondeat superior (vicarious liability)

### 3. **Gig Economy - Independent Contractor vs. Agent**

**Trend**: Courts examining substance, not labels.
- High control + integration + company branding → Likely agent (company liable)
- True independence → Independent contractor (company not liable)

**Factors**: Control over work, training, tools, branding, integration into business.

### 4. **Cryptocurrency Custodians as Agents**

**Issue**: Custodial wallets holding crypto - agent of user?

**Legal Position**:
- Custodian controlling private keys → Likely agent (fiduciary duties)
- Must protect principal's assets, not self-deal, render accounts

### 5. **Termination During COVID-19**

**Issue**: Can principals terminate agencies during pandemic due to business closure?

**Courts' Position** (2020-2022):
- Impossibility (Section 206) may apply if agency performance impossible
- Fixed-term agencies: Force majeure may excuse compensation obligation for termination

## Practice Guidelines

### ✅ Do's

1. **For Agents: Disclose All Material Facts**
   - Conflicts of interest
   - Secret profits opportunities
   - Prevents repudiation and liability

2. **For Agents: Maintain Detailed Accounts**
   - Section 215 requires proper accounts
   - Contemporaneous records easier to defend

3. **For Principals: Give Clear Instructions (Section 211)**
   - Written directives for important matters
   - Reduces disputes over scope of authority

4. **For Principals: Give Notice of Termination to Third Parties**
   - Actual notice to known parties
   - Public notice for potential parties
   - Prevents continued liability

5. **For Agents: Communicate in Difficulty (Section 216)**
   - Seek principal's instructions when facing unexpected issues
   - Protects against liability for decisions beyond authority

6. **Document Agency Relationship**
   - Written agreement specifying duties, authority, remuneration, termination
   - Reduces litigation risk

### ❌ Don'ts

1. **Don't Deal on Own Account Without Disclosure (Section 218)**
   - Principal can repudiate transaction
   - Forfeit remuneration

2. **Don't Make Secret Profits (Section 216)**
   - Principal can recover profits
   - Dismissal + damages possible

3. **Don't Mix Principal's Funds with Own**
   - Fiduciary duty to keep separate
   - Agent bears risk of loss if mixed

4. **Don't Terminate Fixed-Term Agency Without Cause**
   - Compensation due to other party (Section 205)
   - Document sufficient cause if terminating

5. **Don't Assume Termination Effective Without Notice (Section 208)**
   - Third parties can enforce contracts unless notice given

6. **Don't Exceed Authority Without Ratification**
   - Agent personally liable for breach of warranty of authority
