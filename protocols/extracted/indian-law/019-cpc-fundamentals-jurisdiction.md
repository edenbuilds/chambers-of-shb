# Protocol: Code of Civil Procedure - Fundamentals and Jurisdiction

**Protocol ID**: IL-CPC-001
**Domain**: Indian Civil Law - Civil Procedure
**Subdomain**: Procedural Law, Court Jurisdiction, Forum Selection
**Classification**: Foundational Procedural Framework
**Version**: 1.0
**Last Updated**: 2024-12-04
**Governs**: Fundamental principles of civil litigation procedure and jurisdictional rules under Code of Civil Procedure, 1908

---

## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive coverage of the foundational concepts of the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (CPC), focusing on the structure of civil litigation, types of jurisdiction, forum selection, and procedural fundamentals. It serves as the essential starting point for understanding how civil disputes are litigated in Indian courts.

---

## Scope of Application

### When This Protocol Applies

This protocol applies to ALL civil disputes in India involving:
- **Monetary claims** (breach of contract, damages, debt recovery)
- **Property disputes** (ownership, possession, partition)
- **Specific performance** and injunctions
- **Declarations** of rights and status
- **Family law** civil matters (divorce, maintenance, custody)
- **Commercial disputes** (subject to Commercial Courts Act overlay)
- **Consumer disputes** (subject to Consumer Protection Act overlay)

### Jurisdictional Boundaries

**Primary Statute**: Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (as amended 2024)
- **Territorial Application**: Whole of India except State of Jammu & Kashmir (has separate CPC)
- **Hierarchical Courts**: District Courts, High Courts, Supreme Court

**Supplementary/Special Legislation**:
- **Commercial Courts Act, 2015**: Modified CPC for commercial disputes
- **Consumer Protection Act, 2019**: Separate forum for consumer disputes
- **Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996**: Alternative dispute resolution
- **Specific Relief Act, 1963**: Substantive law for specific performance, injunctions

### Exclusions

CPC does NOT apply to:
- **Criminal proceedings** (governed by CrPC)
- **Revenue matters** (Board of Revenue jurisdiction)
- **Tribunals** (NCLT, NCLAT, ITAT, etc. - have own procedural rules)
- **Arbitration proceedings** (Arbitration Act governs, though CPC applies suppletorily)

## Statutory Framework

### Structure of Code of Civil Procedure, 1908

**Total Provisions**: 158 Sections + 52 Orders + 5 Schedules

**Sections 1-158**: Substantive provisions
**Orders I-LI**: Detailed procedural rules (under Section 122)
**Schedules**: Forms, court fees, provincial insolvency

### Division into Parts

**Part I (Sections 1-3)**: Preliminary
- Extent, commencement, definitions

**Part II (Sections 4-13)**: Summons to Defendants
- Service, territorial limits

**Part III (Sections 14-35A)**: Institution of Suits
- **Sections 15-20**: Jurisdiction (key provisions)
- **Sections 21-35A**: Stay, transfer, consolidation

**Part IV (Sections 36-58)**: Trial and Disposal of Suits
- Pleadings, evidence, judgment, decree

**Part V (Sections 59-74)**: Appeals from Original Decrees
- First appeals, second appeals, appeals from orders

**Part VI (Sections 75-101)**: Execution of Decrees and Orders
- Execution procedures, attachment, sale

**Part VII (Sections 102-112)**: Incidental Proceedings
- Arrest, attachment before judgment, interim orders

**Part VIII (Sections 113-114A)**: Special Proceedings
- Public nuisances, suits by paupers

**Part IX (Sections 115-120)**: Miscellaneous
- Revision, reference, review, inherent powers

**Part X (Sections 121-158)**: Supplemental Provisions
- Rules by High Courts, amendment of decree, appeals to Supreme Court

### Key Definitions (Section 2)

**Section 2(2) - "Decree"**:
- Formal expression of **adjudication**
- Conclusively determines **rights of parties**
- Regarding all or any **matters in controversy** in suit
- Example: "Defendant shall pay plaintiff ₹10 lakh with interest"

**Distinction - Decree vs. Order**:
- **Decree**: Determines rights, ends suit
- **Order**: Procedural direction, suit continues

**Section 2(14) - "Order"**:
- Formal expression of **any decision** of court
- Which is NOT a decree
- Example: "Application for adjournment allowed"

**Section 2(11) - "Legal Representative"**:
- Person who represents estate of deceased
- Heir, executor, administrator

**Section 2(17) - "Pleader"**:
- Advocate enrolled under Advocates Act, 1961

**Section 2(9) - "Judge"**:
- Presiding officer of civil court

## Core Legal Principles

### Principle 1: Jurisdiction - The Foundation of Valid Adjudication

**"Jurisdiction" = Power of court to hear and decide a case**

**Three Types of Jurisdiction** (ALL must be satisfied):

#### 1. Subject-Matter Jurisdiction (Pecuniary + Nature of Suit)

**Pecuniary Jurisdiction**: Based on monetary value of suit

**Court Hierarchy by Pecuniary Value**:

| Court | Pecuniary Jurisdiction | Notes |
|-------|------------------------|-------|
| **Supreme Court** | No limit | Appellate/advisory, not original civil jurisdiction |
| **High Court** | No limit | Original (if within state), appellate, supervisory |
| **District Court / Principal Civil Court** | Above ₹3 lakh (typical state law) | Varies by state |
| **Civil Judge (Senior Division)** | ₹1 lakh to ₹3 lakh | Varies by state |
| **Civil Judge (Junior Division)** | Up to ₹1 lakh | Varies by state |
| **Small Causes Court** | Up to ₹25,000 (typical) | Summary procedure |

**Note**: Pecuniary limits set by **State Government** (varies across states)

**Nature of Suit Jurisdiction**:
- Family Courts: Matrimonial, custody, maintenance (regardless of value)
- Commercial Courts: Specified commercial disputes above ₹3 lakh (up to ₹1 crore at District level, above ₹1 crore at High Court)
- IP Division of High Courts: Intellectual property disputes

**No Subject-Matter Jurisdiction → Decree VOID** (not merely voidable)

**Kiran Singh v. Chaman Paswan (1954) SC**:
- Suit for more than court's pecuniary limit
- Decree passed
- Held: Decree **void ab initio** (no jurisdiction, decree nullity)

#### 2. Territorial Jurisdiction (Section 16-20)

**Section 16 - General Rule - Defendant's Residence/Business Place**:

Suit filed where:
- **Defendant resides**, OR
- **Defendant carries on business**, OR
- **Defendant personally works for gain**

**Rationale**: Convenience of defendant (defendant should not be compelled to defend far from home)

**Section 17 - Immovable Property Suits**:

Suit regarding immovable property filed where:
- **Property is situated**

**Exceptions**:
- Mortgage suits for recovery of money: Section 16 or Section 17 (plaintiff's choice)
- Partition suits: Where property situated (Section 17)

**Section 18 - Suits for Compensation (Wrongs)**:

Suit for compensation for wrongful act:
- Where **act done**, OR
- Where **defendant resides** (Section 16)

**Section 19 - Other Suits (Residuary)**:

Suits not covered by Sections 15-18:
- Where **cause of action wholly or partly arises**, OR
- Where **defendant resides or business** (Section 16)

**"Cause of Action"** (Section 19 key concept):
- Bundle of facts which give rise to right to sue
- Must allege facts showing cause of action arose in that jurisdiction

**Example**: Contract signed in Mumbai, performance in Delhi, breach in Bangalore
- Cause of action arises: Mumbai (contract), Delhi (performance), Bangalore (breach)
- Plaintiff can sue in ANY of these places (Section 19 - partly arises)

**Section 20 - Multiple Defendants**:

If defendants reside in different jurisdictions:
- Sue where ANY defendant resides (if permission from court - "leave of court")
- OR sue all in one court if cause of action common

#### 3. Hierarchy Jurisdiction (Original vs. Appellate)

**Original Jurisdiction**: Court of first instance (trial court)
- District Court, Civil Judge

**Appellate Jurisdiction**: Hears appeals from lower courts
- High Court (appeals from District Courts)
- Supreme Court (appeals from High Courts)

**Supervisory Jurisdiction**: High Court's constitutional power (Article 227)
- Superintendence over subordinate courts

**Cannot confer jurisdiction by consent**: Parties cannot agree to give court jurisdiction it doesn't have

**A.B.C. Laminart v. A.P. Agencies (1989) SC**:
- Parties consented to High Court jurisdiction
- But High Court had no territorial jurisdiction
- Held: Consent CANNOT confer jurisdiction; Lack of jurisdiction goes to root

### Principle 2: Res Judicata - No Relitigation of Decided Matters (Section 11)

**Section 11 - Res Judicata**:

No court shall try suit/issue in which **matter in issue** has been:
- Directly and substantially in issue in former suit
- Between same parties (or parties under whom they claim)
- In court **competent to try** subsequent suit
- Heard and finally decided

**Essentials**:
1. Former suit decided by competent court
2. Matter **directly and substantially** in issue (not incidental/collateral)
3. **Same parties** or privies
4. **Heard and finally decided** (not dismissed for default)

**Explanation I - Constructive Res Judicata**:
- Matter which **ought to have been** made ground of defense/attack in former suit
- Treated as if heard and decided (cannot raise in subsequent suit)

**Rationale**:
- Finality of litigation (parties cannot relitigate indefinitely)
- Prevent harassment

**Sulochana Amma v. Narayanan Nair (1994) SC**:
- Plaintiff sued for possession of property (Suit 1) - Lost
- Plaintiff sued again for partition of same property (Suit 2)
- Held: Barred by res judicata (if partition issue could have been raised in Suit 1, cannot now sue separately)

**Exception - Criminal Acquittal vs. Civil Suit**:
- Criminal acquittal does NOT bar civil suit (different standard of proof)
- But criminal conviction can be used in civil suit (as evidence)

### Principle 3: Forum Non Conveniens - Discretionary Transfer (Section 24)

**Section 24 - General Power to Transfer**:

High Court may transfer suit:
- From subordinate court to itself, OR
- From one subordinate court to another

**Grounds**:
- Ends of justice require transfer
- Prevent abuse of process

**Supreme Court** may transfer (Article 139A):
- From one High Court to another
- From subordinate court in one state to another state

**"Forum Non Conveniens"**:
- Court has jurisdiction BUT declines to exercise (inconvenient forum)
- Discretionary (not automatic)

**Kusum Ingots v. Union of India (2004) SC**:
- Multiple suits in different states on same cause of action
- Supreme Court transferred all to one High Court
- Principle: Prevent multiplicity, forum shopping

### Principle 4: Written Pleadings Mandatory (Order VI)

**Order VI - Pleadings**:

**Plaint** (Plaintiff's pleading): Must contain:
1. Court name
2. Parties' names, descriptions, addresses
3. **Facts constituting cause of action** (when arose)
4. Facts showing court has jurisdiction
5. Relief claimed
6. Valuation (for court fees and jurisdiction)

**Written Statement** (Defendant's pleading):
- Must file within **30 days** (extendable max 90 days) - 2002 Amendment
- Admit or deny each allegation
- State own case

**"Material Facts" vs. "Evidence" vs. "Law"**:
- Plead **material facts** (not evidence or law)
- Example: "Defendant breached contract by not delivering goods" (fact) ✓
- NOT: "Witness X will testify that defendant didn't deliver" (evidence) ✗

### Principle 5: Summary Judgment (Order XIII-A, added 2002)

**Order XIII-A - Summary Judgment**:

Court may give **judgment without full trial** if:
- Plaintiff/Defendant has **no real prospect of success**
- No **other compelling reason** for trial

**Procedure**:
- Party applies for summary judgment
- Opposite party shows cause why shouldn't be granted
- Court decides based on pleadings + limited evidence

**Objective**: Reduce frivolous litigation, expedite clear-cut cases

**Applicable to**: Money suits, specific performance (practical effect limited; rarely used)

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Supreme Court Cases on Jurisdiction

**1. A.B.C. Laminart Pvt Ltd v. A.P. Agencies (1989) 2 SCC 163 - Jurisdiction Cannot Be Conferred by Consent**

**Facts**:
- Parties had arbitration agreement (venue: Salem)
- Arbitration held in Bombay (parties consented)
- Award challenged in Bombay High Court

**Issue**: Can parties confer territorial jurisdiction by consent?

**Held**:
- **NO**, territorial jurisdiction cannot be conferred by consent
- Jurisdiction determined by statute (CPC/Arbitration Act)
- Even if parties agree, court without jurisdiction cannot entertain

**Principle**: Jurisdiction is creature of statute; Parties' consent irrelevant

**2. Kusum Ingots & Alloys Ltd v. Union of India (2004) 6 SCC 254 - Forum Shopping and Multiplicity**

**Facts**:
- Same cause of action
- Multiple suits filed in different High Courts simultaneously
- Classic forum shopping

**Issue**: Can Supreme Court transfer/consolidate to prevent forum shopping?

**Held**:
- YES, Supreme Court has power under Article 139A
- Forum shopping abuse of process
- Transferred all suits to one High Court

**Principle**: Courts will prevent multiplicity and forum shopping; Transfer powers exercised to serve justice

**3. Kiran Singh v. Chaman Paswan (1954) SCR 117 - Pecuniary Jurisdiction Void Decree**

**Facts**:
- Suit valuation above court's pecuniary limit
- Court passed decree despite lack of jurisdiction

**Issue**: Effect of decree passed by court without pecuniary jurisdiction?

**Held**:
- Decree **void ab initio** (not voidable)
- No jurisdiction → No power to decide → Decree nullity
- Can be challenged at any stage (even in execution)

**Principle**: Lack of subject-matter jurisdiction renders decree void; Not curable by limitation or acquiescence

**4. Sulochana Amma v. Narayanan Nair (1994) 2 SCC 14 - Res Judicata Constructive**

**Facts**:
- First suit for possession - Dismissed
- Second suit for partition of same property (issue could have been raised in first suit)

**Issue**: Does res judicata bar second suit?

**Held**:
- YES, barred by **constructive res judicata** (Explanation I to Section 11)
- Matter which ought to have been raised treated as decided
- Prevents splitting causes of action

**Principle**: Parties must raise all related claims in one suit; Cannot split and relitigate

**5. Dhodha House v. S.K. Maingi (2006) 9 SCC 41 - Cause of Action Jurisdiction**

**Facts**:
- Contract executed in one state
- Performance in another
- Breach in third

**Issue**: Where can suit be filed under Section 20 (cause of action)?

**Held**:
- Cause of action = **Entire bundle** of facts giving right to sue
- Arises where **any material fact** occurred
- Plaintiff can sue where **any part** of cause of action arose

**Principle**: Section 20 gives plaintiff choice of multiple forums if cause of action has elements in different places

### Cases on Forum Non Conveniens and Transfer

**6. Nawal Kishore Sharma v. Union of India (2014) 9 SCC 1 - Balance of Convenience**

**Facts**:
- Plaintiff (elderly, poor) filed suit in home state
- Defendant (Union of India) sought transfer to another state

**Issue**: On what grounds should court transfer suit?

**Held**:
- Transfer discretionary (Section 24)
- Consider: Convenience of parties, witnesses, location of documents
- Balance tilted in favor of plaintiff's forum (defendant can afford travel)

**Principle**: Transfer not automatic; Court balances convenience; Weak party favored

**7. Swastik Gases v. Indian Oil Corporation (2013) 9 SCC 32 - Exclusive Jurisdiction Clause**

**Facts**:
- Contract had exclusive jurisdiction clause (courts of Delhi)
- Plaintiff filed suit in Rajasthan

**Issue**: Is exclusive jurisdiction clause binding?

**Held**:
- Exclusive jurisdiction clause **prima facie valid**
- BUT not absolute; Court can decline if:
  - Obtained by fraud/coercion
  - Manifestly unjust
- Here: Clause valid, suit transferred to Delhi

**Principle**: Exclusive jurisdiction clauses generally enforced; Exception if unfair/fraudulent

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. CPC Amendment Act, 2024 (Proposed - Under Parliamentary Review)

**Key Proposed Changes**:

**A. Video Conferencing as Default** (New Section 4A proposed):
- Summons service and appearance via video conference default (unless physical presence required)
- Rationale: COVID-19 demonstrated feasibility; Reduces travel, costs

**B. Mandatory Pre-Litigation Mediation** (Amendment to Section 12A):
- Before filing suit, parties must attempt mediation (except urgent interim relief)
- Mediation Centre to be established in each district
- Timeline: 3 months for mediation (extendable 2 months)

**Effect if Enacted**:
- Plaints filed without mediation certificate may be rejected
- Reduces court backlog (encourage settlement)

**C. Stricter Timeline for Written Statement** (Order V Rule 1 amendment):
- 30 days from service of summons (current law)
- Extension: Only once, up to 60 days total (currently 90 days total)
- If not filed in 60 days: **Defendant forfeits right** (no further extension)

**D. Electronic Service of Summons** (Section 27 amendment):
- Email, WhatsApp, SMS deemed valid service (if acknowledged)
- Physical service only if electronic fails

**Status**: Bill introduced in Lok Sabha (Monsoon Session 2024); Referred to Standing Committee; NOT yet enacted

**Portal**: eCourts Services (https://services.ecourts.gov.in/)

**Impact on CPC Practice**:

### 3. Commercial Courts Act, 2015 - Amendments 2018 & 2024 Trends

**Commercial Courts Act, 2015**:
- Special courts for commercial disputes (above specified value)
- **Modified CPC** (stricter timelines, limited adjournments)

**2018 Amendment**:
- Pecuniary threshold lowered from ₹1 crore to **₹3 lakh**
- Massive increase in Commercial Court jurisdiction

**Key CPC Modifications for Commercial Disputes**:

| CPC Provision | Regular Suit | Commercial Suit |
|---------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Written Statement filing | 30+60 days | 30+90 days (no further extension) |
| Case Management Hearing | No specific provision | Mandatory within 4 weeks |
| Adjournments | Liberal (practice) | **Prohibited** except exceptional |
| Summary Judgment | Rarely used | Encouraged (Order XIII-A) |
| Appeals | Liberal admission | Strict: Prima facie case + deposit |

**2024 Practice Trends**:
- Commercial Courts disposing cases 2x faster than regular courts (2023 NITI Aayog report)
- Success in metros (Delhi, Mumbai, Bangalore)
- Challenges in Tier-2 cities (lack of trained judges)

### 4. Supreme Court Decisions on Jurisdiction (2020-2024)

**A. Amazon.com NV Investment Holdings LLC v. Future Retail Ltd (2021) - Emergency Arbitration Jurisdiction**

**Facts**:
- Singapore-seated arbitration between Amazon and Future
- Emergency arbitrator passed order (injunction)
- Future challenged in Delhi High Court (jurisdictional objection)

**Issue**: Do Indian courts have jurisdiction to interfere with foreign-seated arbitration?

**Held**:
- **Limited jurisdiction** under Section 9 Arbitration Act (before arbitration commences)
- Once arbitration underway: Foreign-seated arbitration = Indian courts cannot interfere (except enforcement)
- Delhi HC lacked jurisdiction

**Impact**: Clarified that choice of foreign seat = Exclusion of Indian courts' supervisory jurisdiction during arbitration

**B. BGS SGS Soma JV v. NHPC Ltd (2020) 4 SCC 234 - Section 9 vs. Section 11 Jurisdiction**

**Facts**:
- Arbitration agreement
- Party approached court under Section 9 (interim relief) AND Section 11 (appointment of arbitrator)

**Issue**: Can parties approach multiple courts for different reliefs?

**Held**:
- Section 9: Court where **part of cause of action arises**
- Section 11: Court where **principal civil jurisdiction** over subject matter
- Different jurisdiction rules; Parties must approach correct court for each relief

**Principle**: Jurisdiction for arbitration-related matters governed by Arbitration Act (CPC applies suppletorily)

### 5. Pandemic Impact on CPC Procedure (2020-2024)

**Supreme Court Directives (COVID-19)**:

**A. Limitation Act Suspension** (March 2020 - February 2022):
- SC exercised Article 142 powers
- Limitation period **excluded** (all time-barred matters during pandemic saved)
- Impacted CPC: Suits filed even if limitation expired during lockdown

**Post-Pandemic**: Normal limitation resumed March 1, 2022

**B. Virtual Hearings Mandatory** (2020-2021):
- All courts to conduct hearings via video conference
- SC, HCs created VC platforms
- Initially mandatory; Now optional (hybrid model)

**2024 Position**:
- SC: Mix of physical and virtual (case-dependent)
- HCs: Virtual hearings for interim applications, physical for final hearings
- District Courts: Mostly physical (infrastructure limitations)

**C. Paperless Courts Initiative**:
- Courts encouraged e-filing (reduce physical contact)
- Document bundles: Electronic (PDF bookmarks)
- Judgments: All courts now publish online (real-time)

**Permanent Changes Post-Pandemic**:
- Virtual hearings normalized (earlier exceptional)
- E-filing standard (earlier optional in many courts)
- Electronic service recognized (earlier only physical)

### 6. Mandatory Pre-Institution Mediation Pilots (2023-2024)

**Background**:
- Section 12A CPC (added 2002): Court may refer suit to mediation after filing
- 2023: Some High Courts piloted **pre-filing mandatory mediation**

**Delhi High Court Pre-Litigation Mediation Centre (2023)**:
- Parties must attempt mediation before filing (civil suits)
- Mediation Centre at Delhi HC: 3-month process
- If settlement: Mediated settlement executed as decree (Mediation Act 2023)
- If failure: Mediation failure certificate issued (required to file suit)

**Mumbai High Court (2024 Pilot)**:
- Similar scheme for commercial disputes above ₹50 lakh

**Results (Preliminary - 2024 Data)**:
- 40% settlement rate at pre-litigation stage
- Reduced burden on courts
- Faster resolution (90 days mediation vs. 3-5 years litigation)

**If CPC Amendment 2024 Enacted**: This becomes **nationwide mandatory**

## Application Procedures

### Procedure 1: Determining Correct Court Jurisdiction

**Step 1: Identify Type of Jurisdiction Needed**

Answer these questions:
- [ ] What is the **monetary value** of suit? (Pecuniary jurisdiction)
- [ ] What is the **nature** of dispute? (Subject-matter jurisdiction)
- [ ] Where do parties **reside/business**? (Territorial jurisdiction)
- [ ] Where did **cause of action arise**? (Territorial jurisdiction)

**Step 2: Pecuniary Jurisdiction**

Check state-specific pecuniary limits:

| Value | Typical Court (varies by state) |
|-------|----------------------------------|
| Up to ₹25,000 | Small Causes Court |
| Up to ₹1 lakh | Civil Judge (Junior Division) |
| ₹1-3 lakh | Civil Judge (Senior Division) |
| Above ₹3 lakh | District Court |

**Commercial Disputes**: Commercial Court/Division (if above ₹3 lakh)

**Step 3: Subject-Matter Jurisdiction**

Check nature of suit:
- **Immovable property**: Civil Court (where property situated)
- **Family matter**: Family Court (if established in district)
- **Commercial dispute**: Commercial Court (above ₹3 lakh)
- **Consumer dispute**: Consumer Commission (if consumer transaction)

**Step 4: Territorial Jurisdiction (Apply Sections 16-20)**

**Flowchart**:

```
START
│
├─ Suit relates to IMMOVABLE PROPERTY? (Section 17)
│  YES → File where PROPERTY SITUATED
│  NO → Next question
│
├─ Suit for COMPENSATION for wrong? (Section 18)
│  YES → File where WRONG DONE or DEFENDANT RESIDES
│  NO → Next question
│
├─ Defendant RESIDES/CARRIES ON BUSINESS? (Section 16 - General Rule)
│  YES → File where DEFENDANT RESIDES/BUSINESS
│
├─ CAUSE OF ACTION ARISES (Section 19 - Residuary)
│  → File where ANY PART of cause of action arose
```

**Step 5: Check Exclusive Jurisdiction Clauses**

If contract has exclusive jurisdiction clause:
> "Any dispute arising out of this agreement shall be subject to exclusive jurisdiction of courts at [City]."

- **Prima facie binding** (Swastik Gases case)
- File suit in specified city (unless clause unfair/fraudulent)

**Step 6: Verify No Res Judicata Bar**

Check if matter already decided:
- Same parties?
- Same subject matter?
- Former suit decided by competent court?

If YES to all → New suit **barred by res judicata** (Section 11)

**Step 7: Draft Plaint with Jurisdiction Averments**

In plaint (Order VI Rule 2), state:
> "This Hon'ble Court has jurisdiction to entertain this suit because:
> (a) The Defendant resides at [Address] within the jurisdiction of this Court.
> (b) The cause of action arose [partly/wholly] within the jurisdiction of this Court on [Date] when [Fact]."

### Procedure 2: Filing a Civil Suit (Step-by-Step)

**Step 1: Pre-Filing Checklist**

- [ ] Cause of action accrued? (Can sue only after right to sue arises)
- [ ] Within limitation period? (Limitation Act - typically 3 years for contracts, property)
- [ ] Attempted mandatory pre-litigation mediation? (If applicable - Delhi HC, or if CPC 2024 Amendment enacted)
- [ ] Correct court identified? (Jurisdiction)
- [ ] Court fees calculated? (Court Fees Act + valuation)

**Step 2: Draft Plaint (Order VI)**

**Essential Components**:

**A. Title**:
```
IN THE COURT OF [Court Name]
AT [Place]

Civil Suit No. ______ of 2024

[Plaintiff Name] ...Plaintiff
Versus
[Defendant Name] ...Defendant
```

**B. Plaint Body** (Order VI Rule 2):

1. **Court Name and Jurisdiction**
2. **Parties**: Names, descriptions (age, occupation), addresses
3. **Valuation**: For court fees and jurisdiction
4. **Facts**: Chronologically, material facts (not evidence)
   - Plaintiff and Defendant relationship
   - Contract/transaction
   - Breach/wrong
   - Damage suffered
5. **Cause of Action**: State when and where arose
6. **Jurisdiction Averments**: Why this court has jurisdiction
7. **Limitation**: State suit within limitation (if close to expiry, explain)
8. **Relief**: Specific prayer (e.g., "Decree for ₹10 lakh with interest @ 9% p.a.")
9. **Verification**: Affirm contents true to knowledge/belief

**Step 3: Calculate and Pay Court Fees**

**Court fees** based on valuation (State Court Fees Act):
- Typically: ₹X per ₹1,000 of valuation (varies by state)
- Ad valorem: Percentage of claim amount
- Fixed fee: For certain reliefs (injunction, declaration)

**Example** (Illustrative - Karnataka):
- Suit for ₹10 lakh: 2% = ₹20,000 court fees

**Payment**: Via challan, court fee stamps, or online (e-filing)

**Step 4: File Plaint**

**Physical Filing**:
- Submit plaint + copies (defendants + court record)
- Court fees paid
- Vakalatnama (if advocate filing)

**E-Filing** (Mandatory in HCs, Commercial Courts):
- Upload PDF of plaint on eCourts portal
- Pay court fees online
- Digital signature of advocate

**Step 5: Scrutiny by Court Office**

Court office checks:
- Court fees correct?
- Jurisdiction clear?
- Plaint format compliant?

If defect: Court may return plaint for rectification (within 30 days to cure)

**Step 6: Registration and Numbering**

Once accepted:
- Suit registered
- Unique case number assigned (e.g., CS 123/2024)

**Step 7: Summons Issuance (Order V)**

Court issues **summons** to defendant:
- Notice of suit filed
- Attach copy of plaint
- Instruct defendant to appear/file written statement within 30 days

**Service of Summons**:
- Physical: Court process server delivers to defendant's address
- Registered post: AD (Acknowledgement Due)
- Email/WhatsApp: If CPC 2024 Amendment enacted

**Step 8: Defendant's Written Statement**

Defendant must file written statement within:
- **30 days** from service of summons
- Extendable **once** to 60/90 days total (varies by court rules)

If defendant fails to file written statement:
- Defendant forfeits right to contest (ex parte proceedings)

**Step 9: Case Proceeds to Trial**

(Covered in subsequent protocols: IL-CPC-002 Pleadings, IL-CPC-004 Trial)

### Procedure 3: Challenging Jurisdiction of Court

**When**: Defendant served with summons, believes court lacks jurisdiction

**Step 1: Raise Preliminary Objection (Order VII Rule 11)**

**Order VII Rule 11**: Court may reject plaint if:
- (a) Doesn't disclose cause of action
- (d) Suit barred by law (including jurisdiction)

**Procedure**:
- File application under Order VII Rule 11 BEFORE written statement
- Raise jurisdictional objection (pecuniary, territorial, subject-matter)

**Step 2: Written Statement with Jurisdictional Objection**

Even if Order VII Rule 11 not filed, raise in written statement:
> "PRELIMINARY OBJECTION: This Hon'ble Court has no jurisdiction to try this suit because [Defendant resides outside jurisdiction / Pecuniary value exceeds court's limit / Subject matter falls under tribunal jurisdiction]."

**Step 3: Court Decides Preliminary Issue**

Court may:
- **Decide jurisdiction immediately** (preliminary issue) - Preferred
- **Defer to final judgment** (decide with main suit)

**Step 4: If Jurisdiction Upheld**

Defendant can:
- File written statement on merits
- OR appeal against jurisdictional finding (if appealable order)

**Step 5: If Jurisdiction Declined**

Court rejects plaint/returns for presentation to correct court:
- Plaintiff can file in correct court (limitation saved under Section 14 - Time in good faith litigation excluded)

**Step 6: Challenge in Higher Court (If Needed)**

If court wrongly assumes jurisdiction:
- **Revision** to High Court (Section 115 CPC)
- **Writ** (Article 227 Constitution - High Court supervisory powers)

### Procedure 4: Transferring Suit from One Court to Another (Section 24)

**Grounds for Transfer**:
- Ends of justice
- Prevent abuse of process (forum shopping)
- Convenience of parties/witnesses
- Related suits pending elsewhere (consolidation)

**Who Can Transfer**:
- **High Court**: Transfer within state (subordinate courts or to HC itself)
- **Supreme Court**: Transfer across states (one HC to another, or subordinate courts across states)

**Procedure**:

**Step 1: File Transfer Petition**

**In High Court** (Section 24):
- Original Side or writ jurisdiction (Article 227)
- State: Current court, proposed court, grounds

**In Supreme Court** (Article 139A):
- Transfer petition with SLP memo
- State: Courts involved, why transfer necessary

**Step 2: Serve Notice to Opposite Party**

Court issues notice to parties in original suit

**Step 3: Hearing**

Court considers:
- Convenience (parties, witnesses)
- Related litigation elsewhere?
- Forum shopping alleged?
- Balance of justice

**Step 4: Transfer Order**

If granted:
- Order transferring suit to specified court
- Records transmitted to receiving court
- Receiving court continues from stage where left off

**Step 5: Continuation in Transferred Court**

No need to refile plaint:
- Original plaint stands
- Parties continue from stage reached (if written statement filed, proceed to trial)

## Practice Guidelines

### For Plaintiffs - Choosing the Right Forum

**1. Identify Multiple Permissible Forums**:
- Contract signed in City A, performed in City B, breached in City C
- Under Section 19: Can sue in A, B, or C (where cause of action arose)

**2. Strategic Considerations**:
- **Convenience**: Where are witnesses, documents?
- **Speed**: Which court has lower pendency? (Check NJDG data)
- **Judge's reputation**: Some courts known for efficiency
- **Exclusive jurisdiction clause**: Check contract (binding unless unfair)

**3. Calculate Court Fees Accurately**:
- Undervaluation: Plaint returned, suit refiled → Delay
- Overvaluation: Pay excess fees unnecessarily

**4. Draft Jurisdiction Averments Clearly**:
- Not enough to say "Court has jurisdiction"
- State **facts** showing jurisdiction (defendant's address, cause of action arose)

**5. Prepare for Jurisdictional Challenge**:
- Defendant will challenge if weak jurisdiction
- Have supporting documents (defendant's address proof, contract documents showing local connection)

### For Defendants - Challenging Jurisdiction

**1. Act Promptly**:
- Raise jurisdictional objection BEFORE written statement (Order VII Rule 11)
- Delay may be construed as waiver (though jurisdiction non-waivable, delay weakens case)

**2. Distinguish Objections**:
- **Pecuniary/Subject-matter**: Goes to court's power (strong objection - decree void if lacking)
- **Territorial**: Convenience-based (court has discretion; Transfer possible under Section 24)

**3. Provide Alternative Forum**:
- Don't just say "No jurisdiction here"
- State: "Correct forum is [Court] because [Defendant resides there / Property located there]"

**4. Exclusive Jurisdiction Clause**:
- If contract has exclusive jurisdiction clause (favoring defendant's location): Strong ground for transfer

**5. Avoid Dilatory Tactics**:
- Frivolous jurisdictional challenges → Costs imposed
- Raise only if bona fide ground exists

### For Drafting Exclusive Jurisdiction Clauses in Contracts

**Model Clause** (Enforceable):
> "Subject to the provisions of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996, any dispute arising out of or in connection with this Agreement, including any question regarding its existence, validity, or termination, shall be subject to the **exclusive jurisdiction** of the courts at [City, State].
>
> The parties irrevocably waive any objection to the jurisdiction of such courts on grounds of inconvenient forum or otherwise."

**Drafting Tips**:
- Use "**exclusive**" (not "sole" or "concurrent")
- Specify: City AND State (avoid ambiguity)
- Include: "Irrevocably waive objection" (strengthens enforceability)
- Exception for urgent interim relief: May add "Except that parties may seek urgent interim relief from any court of competent jurisdiction"

**Common Pitfalls**:
- Vague clause: "Courts in India" (which court? - Unenforceable)
- Contradictory clause: "Exclusive jurisdiction of Delhi courts, subject to arbitration" (Confusion - harmonize)

### For Judges and Court Staff - Managing Jurisdiction Objections

**1. Decide Preliminary Issues First**:
- Jurisdiction: Preliminary issue (decide early)
- Avoid: Conducting full trial if jurisdiction lacking (waste)

**2. Encourage Transfer Over Dismissal**:
- If wrong forum but suit otherwise valid: Transfer (Section 24)
- Don't dismiss for peripheral jurisdictional defect (prejudices plaintiff)

**3. Consolidate Related Suits**:
- Multiple suits on same transaction: Consolidate (prevent conflicting judgments)

**4. Proactive Case Management**:
- Commercial Courts: Use Case Management Hearings (identify issues, narrow disputes)

- **Arbitration**: Arbitration Act governs, but CPC applies suppletorily (Sections 9, 11, 34, 37)
- **Constitutional Law**: Article 227 (HC supervisory jurisdiction), Article 136 (SC appellate jurisdiction)

### Framework Integration

**CPC as Procedural Backbone**:
- Substantive law (Contract Act, Sale of Goods Act) defines rights
- CPC provides procedure to enforce those rights
- Special legislation (Commercial Courts Act, Consumer Protection Act) modifies CPC for specific disputes
