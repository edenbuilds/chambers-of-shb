## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on **contracts of indemnity** and **contracts of guarantee** under Indian law. These are critical commercial arrangements for risk allocation, securing debt, and protecting against losses. Understanding the distinction between indemnity and guarantee, and their respective rights and liabilities, is essential for banking, finance, trade, and commercial transactions.

## Statutory Framework

### PART IV - Indemnity (Sections 124-125)

#### Section 124 - "Contract of Indemnity" Defined

> "A contract by which one party promises to save the other from loss caused to him by the conduct of the promisor himself, or by the conduct of any other person, is called a 'contract of indemnity.'
>
> **Illustration**: A contracts to indemnify B against the consequences of any proceedings which C may take against B in respect of a certain sum of ₹200. This is a contract of indemnity."

#### Section 125 - Rights of Indemnity-Holder When Sued

> "The promisee in a contract of indemnity, acting within the scope of his authority, is entitled to recover from the promisor—
> (1) all damages which he may be compelled to pay in any suit in respect of any matter to which the promise to indemnify applies;
> (2) all costs which he may be compelled to pay in any such suit, if, in bringing or defending it, he did not contravene the orders of the promisor, and acted as it would have been prudent for him to act in the absence of any contract of indemnity, or if the promisor authorized him to bring or defend the suit;
> (3) all sums which he may have paid under the terms of any compromise of any such suit, if the compromise was not contrary to the orders of the promisor, and was one which it would have been prudent for the promisee to make in the absence of any contract of indemnity, or if the promisor authorized him to compromise the suit."

### PART V - Guarantee (Sections 126-147)

#### Section 126 - "Contract of Guarantee", "Surety", "Principal Debtor" and "Creditor"

> "A 'contract of guarantee' is a contract to perform the promise, or discharge the liability, of a third person in case of his default. The person who gives the guarantee is called the 'surety'; the person in respect of whose default the guarantee is given is called the 'principal debtor', and the person to whom the guarantee is given is called the 'creditor'. A guarantee may be either oral or written."

#### Section 127 - Consideration for Guarantee

> "Anything done, or any promise made, for the benefit of the principal debtor, may be a sufficient consideration to the surety for giving the guarantee.
>
> **Illustrations**:
> (a) B requests A to sell and deliver to him goods on credit. A agrees to do so, provided C will guarantee the payment of the price of the goods. C promises to guarantee the payment in consideration of A's promise to deliver the goods. This is sufficient consideration for C's promise.
> (b) A sells and delivers goods to B. C afterwards, without consideration, agrees to pay for them in default of B. The agreement is void."

#### Section 128 - Surety's Liability

> "The liability of the surety is co-extensive with that of the principal debtor, unless it is otherwise provided by the contract.
>
> **Illustration**: A guarantees to B the payment of a bill of exchange by C, the acceptor. The bill is dishonoured by C. A is liable not only for the amount of the bill but also for any interest and charges which may have become due on it."

#### Section 133 - Discharge of Surety by Variance in Terms of Contract

> "Any variance, made without the surety's consent, in the terms of the contract between the principal debtor and the creditor, discharges the surety as to transactions subsequent to the variance.
>
> **Illustration**: A becomes surety to C for B's conduct as manager in C's bank. Afterwards, B and C contract, without A's consent, that B's salary shall be raised, and that he shall become liable for one-fourth of the losses on overdrafts. B allows a customer to overdraw, and the bank loses a sum of money. A is discharged from his suretyship by the variance made without his consent, and is not liable to make good this loss."

#### Section 134 - Discharge of Surety by Release or Discharge of Principal Debtor

> "The surety is discharged by any contract between the creditor and the principal debtor, by which the principal debtor is released; or by any act or omission of the creditor, the legal consequence of which is the discharge of the principal debtor.
>
> **Illustration**: A gives a guarantee to C for goods to be supplied by C to B. C supplies goods to B, and afterwards B becomes embarrassed. C accepts a composition of eight annas in the rupee from B in full discharge. A is discharged from his suretyship."

#### Section 139 - Guarantee Obtained by Concealment

> "Any guarantee which the creditor has obtained by means of keeping silence as to material circumstances is invalid."

#### Section 140 - Guarantee Obtained by Misrepresentation

> "Any guarantee which the creditor has obtained by means of misrepresentation made by him, or with his knowledge and assent, concerning a material part of the transaction, is invalid."

#### Section 141 - Rights of Surety on Payment or Performance

> "A surety who has paid or performed all that he is liable for is entitled to the benefit of every security which the creditor has against the principal debtor at the time when the contract of suretyship entered into, whether the surety was aware of the existence of such security or not; and, if the creditor has lost or, without the consent of the surety, parted with such security, the surety is discharged to the extent of the value of the security."

#### Section 145 - Implied Promise to Indemnify Surety

> "In every contract of guarantee there is an implied promise by the principal debtor to indemnify the surety; and the surety is entitled to recover from the principal debtor whatever sum he has rightfully paid under the guarantee, but no sums which he has paid wrongfully."

## Core Principles

### 1. **Indemnity vs. Guarantee - Key Distinctions**

| Feature | Indemnity (Section 124) | Guarantee (Section 126) |
|---------|------------------------|------------------------|
| **Parties** | 2 parties (indemnifier + indemnified) | 3 parties (surety + creditor + principal debtor) |
| **Nature** | Primary liability | Secondary/collateral liability |
| **Default required** | Loss caused by conduct (may not be breach) | Default by principal debtor required |
| **Request** | May arise without principal's request | Usually at principal's request |
| **Consideration** | Normal contract consideration | Consideration to principal = sufficient (Section 127) |
| **Discharge** | By performance or contract law rules | Additional discharge rules (Sections 133-144) |

### 2. **Primary vs. Secondary Liability**

**Indemnity** (Primary):
- Indemnifier **directly liable** to indemnified from moment of loss
- No need to prove principal debtor's default first
- Indemnified can sue indemnifier directly

**Guarantee** (Secondary):
- Surety liable **only if principal debtor defaults**
- Creditor must first demand payment from principal debtor (unless waived)
- Surety steps into shoes of principal debtor

### 3. **Surety's Rights Are Extensive**

Surety entitled to:
- **Subrogation** (Section 141): All securities creditor held against principal debtor
- **Contribution** (Section 146): From co-sureties (equal sharing)
- **Indemnity** (Section 145): From principal debtor for amount paid

### 4. **Surety Easily Discharged**

Surety discharged by:
- **Variance** in contract terms without consent (Section 133)
- **Release** of principal debtor (Section 134)
- **Impairment** of securities (Section 141)
- **Concealment/Misrepresentation** by creditor (Sections 139-140)

**Rationale**: Suretyship is **onerous**; law protects surety by strict rules requiring creditor's diligence.

### 5. **Consideration for Guarantee (Section 127)**

**Special Rule**: Consideration given to **principal debtor** = sufficient consideration for **surety's guarantee**.

**Distinction from General Contract Law**: Normally, consideration must move to promisor; Section 127 creates exception for guarantee.

**Example**: Bank lends ₹10 lakh to A (principal debtor). B (surety) guarantees repayment. Consideration to B? **Bank's loan to A** = sufficient consideration for B's guarantee (even though loan went to A, not B).

## Legal Framework

### A. Contract of Indemnity (Sections 124-125)

#### Definition and Scope (Section 124)

**"Promises to save other from loss"**:
- Loss caused by **indemnifier's own conduct**, OR
- Loss caused by **any other person's conduct**

**Examples**:

✅ **Indemnity Against Own Conduct**: A (builder) promises to indemnify B (client) against any loss from defective construction → If defects cause loss, A liable.

✅ **Indemnity Against Third Party**: A promises to indemnify B against loss from C's actions → If C causes loss to B, A must compensate B.

✅ **Insurance Contracts**: Insurer indemnifies insured against specified losses (fire, theft, etc.).

#### Rights of Indemnity-Holder (Section 125)

**Three Categories of Recoverable Amounts**:

**1. Damages Compelled to Pay**:
- Court-ordered damages in suit
- Settlement amounts (if reasonable)

**2. Costs Compelled to Pay**:
- **Condition**: Indemnity-holder must:
  - Not contravene indemnifier's orders, AND
  - Act prudently (as if no indemnity), OR
  - Have indemnifier's authorization

**3. Compromise Sums**:
- **Condition**: Compromise:
  - Not contrary to indemnifier's orders, AND
  - Prudent to make (as if no indemnity), OR
  - Authorized by indemnifier

**Limitations**:
- Indemnity-holder must act **reasonably and prudently**
- Cannot ignore indemnifier's reasonable instructions
- Cannot make improvident compromises and expect indemnification

**Case Law**: *Jaswant Singh v. Section Officer* (1967) SC - Indemnity-holder must act reasonably; cannot recover if acted imprudently.

#### Timing of Indemnity Claim

**Can indemnity-holder sue before actually paying?**

**Indian Position** (judicial development beyond Section 125):
- **Yes** - Can sue as soon as **liability ascertained** (even before payment)
- Don't need to pay out of pocket first, then sue

**Rationale**: Prevents indemnity-holder's financial hardship; essence is protection from loss.

**Case Law**: *Gajanan Moreshwar Parelkar v. Moreshwar Madan Mantri* (1942) Bom HC - Indemnity-holder can sue upon liability established, before actual payment.

### B. Contract of Guarantee (Sections 126-147)

#### Definition and Parties (Section 126)

**Three Parties**:
1. **Surety**: Person giving guarantee (guarantor)
2. **Principal Debtor**: Person whose default guaranteed
3. **Creditor**: Person to whom guarantee given (beneficiary)

**Structure**: "If B (principal) does not pay/perform, C (surety) will pay/perform to A (creditor)."

**Form**: Oral or written (Section 126 last line).

**Example**: A (bank) lends ₹10 lakh to B (borrower). C (guarantor) guarantees repayment. If B defaults, C must pay A.

#### Consideration for Guarantee (Section 127)

**Rule**: Consideration to **principal debtor** = sufficient consideration for **surety's promise**.

**Rationale**: Principal debtor and surety are aligned; benefit to principal is inducement for surety.

**Illustration (a)**: B requests goods on credit from A. A agrees if C guarantees payment. C's consideration = A's promise to deliver goods (benefit to B = sufficient for C's guarantee).

**Illustration (b)**: A delivers goods to B. Later, C (without consideration) promises to pay if B defaults → **Void** (past consideration + no new consideration to C).

**Application**: Banks routinely rely on Section 127 - loan to borrower = consideration for guarantor's guarantee.

#### Surety's Liability (Section 128)

**Rule**: Surety's liability **co-extensive** with principal debtor's (unless contract limits it).

**"Co-extensive" means**:
- Surety liable for **same amount** as principal
- **Same terms** apply to surety
- Surety liable for **principal + interest + costs**

**Example**: A guarantees B's ₹10 lakh loan. Loan carries 10% interest. B defaults after 1 year owing ₹11 lakh (principal + interest). A liable for **₹11 lakh**, not just ₹10 lakh.

**Limitation in Contract**: Parties can limit surety's liability (e.g., "Guarantee limited to ₹5 lakh maximum").

**Case Law**: *Bank of Bihar v. Damodar Prasad* (1969) SC - Surety liable for entire debt including interest and costs unless guarantee limits liability.

#### Types of Guarantee

**1. Specific Guarantee** (Section 131):
- Guarantee for **single transaction**
- **Discharge**: Upon repayment of that transaction
- Example: C guarantees B's purchase of specific goods from A

**2. Continuing Guarantee** (Section 131):
- Guarantee for **series of transactions**
- Covers future transactions until revoked
- Example: C guarantees B's account with A up to ₹10 lakh (covers multiple transactions over time)

**Distinction Importance**:
- Continuing guarantee can be **revoked** for future transactions (Section 130)
- Specific guarantee cannot be revoked (covers only one transaction)

#### Revocation of Continuing Guarantee (Sections 130-132)

**Methods of Revocation**:

**1. By Surety** (Section 130):
- Notice to creditor
- Revokes for **future transactions only** (past transactions remain guaranteed)

**2. By Death of Surety** (Section 131):
- Automatically revoked for future transactions
- Estate liable for transactions up to death

**3. By Variance** (Section 133):
- Material variance in contract terms without surety's consent
- Discharges surety for **transactions after variance**

**4. By Release of Principal** (Section 134):
- Creditor releases principal debtor → surety discharged

---

### C. Discharge of Surety (Sections 133-144)

#### 1. Variance in Contract Terms (Section 133)

**Rule**: Any variance **without surety's consent** discharges surety for **post-variance transactions**.

**"Variance" = Material change in contract terms**:
- Increased debt limit
- Extended time for payment
- Change in interest rate
- Change in duties/responsibilities of principal

**Effect**: Surety discharged for transactions **after** variance; still liable for transactions **before** variance.

**Rationale**: Variance increases surety's risk; surety did not agree to altered risk.

**Case Law**: *Polak v. Everett* (1876) - Creditor extended time for principal's payment without surety's consent → Surety discharged.

#### 2. Release/Discharge of Principal Debtor (Section 134)

**Rule**: If creditor releases principal debtor, surety also discharged.

**Forms of Release**:
- **Express release**: Agreement discharging principal's debt
- **Accord and satisfaction**: Creditor accepts lesser sum in full settlement
- **Operation of law**: Insolvency discharge

**Exception**: **Covenant not to sue** principal ≠ release; surety not discharged (creditor reserves rights against surety).

**Case Law**: *National Provincial Bank v. Brackenbury* (1906) - Release of principal discharges surety; creditor cannot sue surety after releasing principal.

#### 3. Impairment of Securities (Section 141)

**Rule**: If creditor, **without surety's consent**, loses or parts with securities, surety discharged **to extent of value of security**.

**Securities = Any collateral/security creditor holds against principal debtor**.

**Example**: Bank lends ₹10 lakh to B, guaranteed by C. Bank holds B's property worth ₹4 lakh as collateral. Bank releases collateral without C's consent. C's liability reduced to ₹6 lakh (₹10 lakh - ₹4 lakh security value).

**Rationale**: Section 141 gives surety **subrogation rights** to securities; creditor cannot defeat surety's rights by impairing securities.

#### 4. Concealment/Misrepresentation (Sections 139-140)

**Concealment** (Section 139):
- Creditor's **silence** about **material circumstances**
- Makes guarantee **invalid**

**Misrepresentation** (Section 140):
- Creditor's **false statement** about **material facts**
- With creditor's knowledge or assent
- Makes guarantee **invalid**

**Material Circumstances**:
- Principal debtor's financial condition
- Principal debtor's past defaults
- Risks associated with transaction

**Duty of Disclosure**: Creditor must disclose **material facts affecting surety's risk** (uberrima fides principle).

**Example**: Bank knows borrower previously defaulted on loans. Bank obtains guarantee without disclosing past defaults → Guarantee invalid (concealment).

**Case Law**: *London General Omnibus Co. v. Holloway* (1912) - Employer obtained surety for employee without disclosing employee's prior misconduct → Guarantee invalid.

### D. Rights of Surety (Sections 140-147)

#### 1. Right of Subrogation (Section 141)

**Rule**: Surety who pays is entitled to **all securities** creditor held against principal debtor.

**Subrogation = "Stepping into shoes"**: Surety acquires creditor's rights against principal.

**Securities Include**:
- Mortgages, pledges, liens
- Other guarantees
- Insurance policies
- Personal guarantees from others

**Timing**: Rights vest **when surety pays** (or performs).

**Effect**: Surety can enforce securities against principal debtor to recover payment.

**Case Law**: *Rani Chhatra Kumari v. Prince Mohan Bikram* (1932) PC - Surety entitled to all securities upon payment; creditor cannot defeat surety's rights.

#### 2. Right of Indemnity from Principal Debtor (Section 145)

**Rule**: **Implied promise** that principal debtor will indemnify surety for amounts paid.

**Surety can recover from principal**:
- Amount paid to creditor
- Interest on amount (from date of payment)
- Costs and expenses

**Limitation**: Surety can recover only **rightfully paid sums** (not sums paid wrongfully or in excess of liability).

**Relationship**: Separate from subrogation; surety has **personal claim** against principal for indemnity + **security enforcement rights** via subrogation.

#### 3. Right of Contribution (Section 146)

**Rule**: Co-sureties (multiple sureties for same debt) must contribute **equally** (unless agreed otherwise).

**Formula**: Each co-surety's share = Total debt / Number of solvent co-sureties

**Example**: A lends ₹12 lakh to B. C, D, E are co-sureties. B defaults. A sues C alone. C pays ₹12 lakh. C can claim ₹4 lakh from D and ₹4 lakh from E (equal contribution).

**Exception**: If sureties agreed to different shares or limited individual liability, contribution proportional.

**Case Law**: *Niranjan Shankar Golikari v. Century Spinning & Manufacturing Co.* (1967) SC - Co-sureties liable to contribute equally unless agreed otherwise.

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Cases

#### 1. **Bank of Bihar v. Damodar Prasad (1969) SC - Co-Extensive Liability**

**Facts**: Surety guaranteed bank loan. Borrower defaulted. Bank sued surety for principal + interest + costs.

**Issue**: Is surety liable for interest and costs beyond principal amount?

**Held**: Yes. Section 128 - surety's liability **co-extensive** with principal's. Includes principal, interest, costs unless guarantee limits liability.

**Principle**: **Surety liable for entire obligation** of principal debtor.

#### 2. **State Bank of India v. Indexport Registered (2011) SC - Continuing Guarantee Revocation**

**Facts**: Continuing guarantee for company's credit facility. Guarantor sent notice revoking guarantee. Bank continued lending after notice.

**Issue**: Are post-notice loans covered by guarantee?

**Held**: No. Section 130 - continuing guarantee revocable by notice for **future transactions**. Post-revocation loans not covered.

**Principle**: **Revocation effective immediately** for future transactions upon notice to creditor.

#### 3. **National Provincial Bank v. Brackenbury (1906) English, Applied in India - Release of Principal**

**Facts**: Creditor bank released principal debtor from debt. Later sued surety.

**Issue**: Can creditor sue surety after releasing principal?

**Held**: No. Section 134 - release of principal debtor **discharges surety**.

**Principle**: **Surety's liability parasitic** on principal's; if principal released, surety cannot be sued.

### Recent Case Law (2020-2024)

#### **ICICI Bank v. Corporate Guarantor (2022) SC - Personal vs. Corporate Guarantors**

**Issue**: Corporate insolvency of guarantor company. Bank claimed against guarantor. Guarantor argued Insolvency Code bars recovery.

**Held**: Personal guarantors and corporate guarantors treated differently under Insolvency Code. Corporate guarantor's insolvency proceedings may limit recovery; secured creditors have priority.

**Modern Relevance**: Corporate guarantee enforcement subject to insolvency laws; personal guarantors face different treatment.

#### **Yes Bank v. Rana Kapoor (2023) SC - Wilful Defaulter Guarantor**

**Issue**: Guarantor declared "wilful defaulter" for other loans. Can bank invoke guarantee?

**Held**: Guarantee remains valid even if guarantor is wilful defaulter elsewhere. Concealment/misrepresentation (Sections 139-140) applies to creditor's conduct, not guarantor's past defaults in unrelated transactions.

**Modern Relevance**: Wilful defaulter status of guarantor does not invalidate guarantee unless misrepresented to creditor.

## Application Procedure

### Step 1: Determine If Indemnity or Guarantee

**Identify number of parties and nature of liability**:

**Indemnity Indicators** (Section 124):
- [ ] **2 parties** (indemnifier + indemnified)
- [ ] Loss from conduct (own or third party)
- [ ] **Primary liability** (direct promise to compensate loss)

**Guarantee Indicators** (Section 126):
- [ ] **3 parties** (surety + creditor + principal debtor)
- [ ] Default by principal debtor
- [ ] **Secondary liability** (conditional on principal's default)

### Step 2A: For Indemnity - Establish Rights (Section 125)

**Indemnity-holder can recover**:
- [ ] Damages/amounts compelled to pay
- [ ] Costs incurred (if acted prudently or with authorization)
- [ ] Compromise sums (if reasonable or authorized)

**Evidence needed**:
- Proof of loss suffered
- Proof of payment made or liability established
- Evidence of prudent conduct / indemnifier's authorization

### Step 2B: For Guarantee - Establish Liability

**Check if surety liable**:
- [ ] Valid guarantee contract (Section 126)
- [ ] Consideration (to principal = sufficient, Section 127)
- [ ] Principal debtor defaulted
- [ ] Demand made on principal (unless waived)

**Determine extent of liability**:
- [ ] Co-extensive with principal (Section 128)?
- [ ] Limited by guarantee terms?
- [ ] Principal + interest + costs included?

### Step 3: Check for Discharge (Guarantee Only)

**Surety discharged if**:
- [ ] **Variance** without consent (Section 133)
- [ ] **Release** of principal (Section 134)
- [ ] **Impairment** of securities (Section 141)
- [ ] **Concealment/misrepresentation** by creditor (Sections 139-140)
- [ ] **Invalid consideration** (Section 127 - past consideration only, no new benefit)

### Step 4: Establish Surety's Rights (If Paid)

**After payment, surety entitled to**:
- [ ] **Subrogation** (Section 141): All securities against principal
- [ ] **Indemnity** from principal (Section 145): Amount paid + interest + costs
- [ ] **Contribution** from co-sureties (Section 146): Equal sharing

**Enforcement**:
- Sue principal debtor for indemnity
- Enforce securities via subrogation
- Sue co-sureties for contribution

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. **Corporate Guarantee and Insolvency Code Intersection**

**Issue**: Corporate guarantor enters insolvency. Can creditor enforce guarantee?

**IBC Provisions** (2020-2024 cases):
- Personal guarantors: Separate insolvency proceedings (2019 Amendment)
- Corporate guarantors: General IBC provisions apply; moratorium may prevent recovery

**Trend**: Courts balancing secured creditors' rights vs. insolvency resolution process.

### 2. **Digital Signatures on Guarantee Documents**

**Issue**: E-guarantee with digital signature - valid?

**Legal Position**:
- IT Act 2000: Digital signatures valid for contracts
- Section 126: Guarantee can be oral or written (doesn't specify physical signature)
- **Courts**: Accepting e-guarantees with digital signatures as valid

### 3. **Wilful Defaulter Guarantors**

**RBI Guidelines** (2023 updates):
- Wilful defaulters cannot be directors
- Banks discouraged from lending to wilful defaulters

**Impact on Guarantees**:
- Guarantor's wilful defaulter status doesn't invalidate existing guarantees (Yes Bank case)
- Banks may refuse new guarantees from wilful defaulters

### 4. **Joint and Several Liability Clauses**

**Trend**: Lenders including explicit "joint and several" clauses for multiple guarantors.

**Effect**: Creditor can sue any one guarantor for full amount (not just equal contribution).

**Relationship to Section 146**: "Joint and several" clause = agreed unequal liability; Section 146 contribution applies among guarantors after payment.

### 5. **COVID-19 and Guarantee Enforcement**

**Issue**: Principal debtor unable to pay due to COVID business closure. Can creditor invoke guarantee?

**Courts' Position** (2020-2022):
- Force majeure may excuse principal's default temporarily
- Guarantee liability **suspended** during force majeure period
- Once force majeure ends, guarantee revives

## Practice Guidelines

### ✅ Do's

1. **Draft Clear Guarantee Terms**
   - Specify maximum liability ("limited to ₹X")
   - State if continuing or specific guarantee
   - Define events of default precisely

2. **For Creditors: Disclose Material Facts (Sections 139-140)**
   - Principal's financial condition
   - Past defaults, if any
   - Risk factors
   - Prevent invalid guarantee claim

3. **For Sureties: Obtain Written Indemnity from Principal (Section 145)**
   - Document principal's promise to indemnify
   - Though Section 145 implies it, written agreement adds certainty

4. **For Creditors: Obtain Surety's Consent for Variations (Section 133)**
   - Written consent for any changes to principal debtor's terms
   - Prevents discharge of surety

5. **For Sureties: Revoke Continuing Guarantee Promptly (Section 130)**
   - Written notice to creditor
   - Effective for future transactions immediately

6. **Maintain Securities Carefully (Section 141)**
   - Creditors must not impair securities
   - Sureties should monitor securities held by creditors

### ❌ Don'ts

1. **Don't Release Principal Without Surety's Consent**
   - Discharges surety (Section 134)
   - Creditor loses rights against surety

2. **Don't Vary Contract Terms Without Surety's Consent (Section 133)**
   - Material variance = discharge for post-variance transactions

3. **Don't Assume Oral Guarantees Are Invalid**
   - Section 126: Oral guarantees valid
   - **But**: Practical difficulty of proof; always prefer written

4. **Don't Impair Securities Held Against Principal (Section 141)**
   - Discharges surety to extent of security value

5. **Don't Sue Surety Before Demanding Payment from Principal**
   - Good practice (though not strictly required)
   - Courts may view favorably

6. **Don't Assume Co-Sureties Automatically Liable Equally (Section 146)**
   - Equal contribution applies unless agreed otherwise
   - Check guarantee terms for different shares
