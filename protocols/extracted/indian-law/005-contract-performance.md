## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on **performance of contracts** under Indian law, covering obligations to perform, rules of performance, tender of performance, time and place of performance, and performance by third parties. Proper understanding of performance rules is essential for fulfilling contractual obligations and avoiding breach.

## Statutory Framework

### Section 37 - Obligation of Parties to Contracts

> "The parties to a contract must either perform, or offer to perform, their respective promises, unless such performance is dispensed with or excused under the provisions of this Act, or of any other law.
>
> Promises bind the representatives of the promisor in case of the death of such promisor before performance, unless a contrary intention appears from the contract."

### Section 38 - Effect of Refusal to Accept Offer of Performance

> "Where a promisor has made an offer of performance to the promisee, and the offer has not been accepted, the promisor is not responsible for non-performance, nor does he thereby lose his rights under the contract.
>
> Every such offer must fulfill the following conditions:—
> (1) it must be unconditional;
> (2) it must be made at a proper time and place, and under such circumstances that the person to whom it is made may have a reasonable opportunity of ascertaining that the person by whom it is made is able and willing to do the whole of what he is bound by his contract to do;
> (3) if the offer is an offer to deliver anything to the promisee, the promisee must have a reasonable opportunity of seeing that the thing offered is the thing which the promisor is bound by his contract to deliver.
>
> An offer to one of several joint promisees has the same legal consequences as an offer to all of them."

### Section 39 - Effect of Refusal of Party to Perform Promise Wholly

> "When a party to a contract has refused to perform, or disabled himself from performing, his promise in its entirety, the promisee may put an end to the contract, unless he has signified, by words or conduct, his acquiescence in its continuance."

### Section 40 - Person by Whom Promise is to Be Performed

> "If it appears from the nature of the case that it was the intention of the parties to any contract that any promise contained in it should be performed by the promisor himself, such promise must be performed by the promisor. In other cases, the promisor or his representatives may employ a competent person to perform it."

### Section 41 - Effect of Accepting Performance from Third Person

> "When a promisee accepts performance of the promise from a third person, he cannot afterwards enforce it against the promisor."

### Section 42 - Devolution of Joint Liabilities

> "When two or more persons have made a joint promise, then, unless a contrary intention appears from the contract, all such persons, during their joint lives, and, after the death of any of them, his representatives jointly with the survivors, and, after the death of the last survivor, the representatives of all jointly, must fulfil the promise."

### Section 43 - Any One of Joint Promisors May Be Compelled to Perform

> "When two or more persons make a joint promise, the promisee may, in the absence of express agreement to the contrary, compel any one or more of such joint promisors to perform the whole of the promise.
>
> Each of two or more joint promisors may compel every other joint promisor to contribute equally with himself to the performance of the promise, unless a contrary intention appears from the contract.
>
> If any one of two or more joint promisors makes default in such contribution, the remaining joint promisors must bear the loss arising from such default in equal shares."

### Section 46 - Time for Performance of Promise Where No Application to Be Made and No Time Specified

> "Where, in a contract, no application is to be made by the promisee, and no time is specified for the performance of the promise, the engagement must be performed within a reasonable time.
>
> The question 'what is a reasonable time' is, in each particular case, a question of fact."

### Section 47 - Time for Performance of Promise Where Time Specified and No Application to Be Made

> "When a promise is to be performed on a certain day, and the promisor has not undertaken to perform it without application by the promisee, the promisor may perform it at any time during the usual hours of business on such day.
>
> If no such time is specified, the promise may be performed at any time during the usual hours of business."

### Section 48 - Application for Performance on Certain Day to Be at Proper Time and Place

> "When a promise is to be performed on a certain day, and the promisor has undertaken to perform it without application by the promisee, it is the duty of the promisee to apply for performance at a proper place and within the usual hours of business."

### Section 49 - Place for Performance of Promise Where No Application to Be Made and No Place Fixed for Performance

> "When a promise is to be performed without application by the promisee, and no place is fixed for the performance of it, it is the duty of the promisor to apply to the promisee to appoint a reasonable place for the performance of the promise, and to perform it at such place."

### Section 50 - Performance in Manner or at Time Prescribed or Sanctioned by Promisee

> "The performance of any promise may be made in any manner, or at any time which the promisee prescribes or sanctions."

### Section 51 - Promisee's Duty to Facilitate Performance by Promisor

> "When a contract contains reciprocal promises, and one party to the contract prevents the other from performing his promise, the contract becomes voidable at the option of the party so prevented; and he is entitled to compensation from the other party for any loss which he may sustain in consequence of the non-performance of the contract."

### Section 52 - Order of Performance of Reciprocal Promises

> "Where the order in which reciprocal promises are to be performed is expressly fixed by the contract, they shall be performed in that order; and where it is not expressly fixed by the contract, they shall be performed in that order which the nature of the transaction requires."

### Section 53 - Liability of Party Preventing Event on Which Contract is to Take Effect

> "When a contract contains reciprocal promises, and one party to the contract prevents the other from performing his promise, the contract becomes voidable at the option of the party so prevented; and he is entitled to compensation from the other party for any loss which he may sustain in consequence of the non-performance of the contract."

### Section 55 - Effect of Failure to Perform at Fixed Time, in Contract in Which Time is Essential

> "When a party to a contract promises to do a certain thing at or before a specified time, or certain things at or before specified times, and fails to do any such thing at or before the specified time, the contract, or so much of it as has not been performed, becomes voidable at the option of the promisee, if the intention of the parties was that time should be of the essence of the contract.
>
> Effect of such failure when time is not essential.—If it was not the intention of the parties that time should be of the essence of the contract, the contract does not become voidable by the failure to do such thing at or before the specified time; but the promisee is entitled to compensation from the promisor for any loss occasioned to him by such failure.
>
> Effect of acceptance of performance at time other than that agreed upon.—If, in case of a contract voidable on account of the promisor's failure to perform his promise at the time agreed, the promisee accepts performance of such promise at any time other than that agreed, the promisee cannot claim compensation for any loss occasioned by the non-performance of the promise at the time agreed, unless, at the time of such acceptance, he gives notice to the promisor of his intention to do so."

## Core Principles

### 1. **Pacta Sunt Servanda** - Agreements Must Be Kept
Section 37 embodies the fundamental principle that **contracts must be performed**. Mere intention to perform is insufficient; actual performance or valid tender of performance required.

### 2. **Performance Must Be Complete and Exact**
- **General rule**: Performance must be precise and in strict accordance with terms
- **No substantial performance doctrine** in Indian law (unlike common law)
- Incomplete/defective performance = breach (unless contract divisible or waived)

### 3. **Personal vs. Delegable Performance**
- **Personal contracts** (Section 40): Must be performed by promisor himself (e.g., artist painting portrait, surgeon performing operation)
- **Non-personal contracts**: Can be performed by competent third party or agent

### 4. **Time as Essence of Contract**
- **If time is of essence** (Section 55): Failure to perform by specified time → contract becomes voidable at promisee's option
- **If time NOT of essence**: Delay does not void contract; promisee entitled to damages only

**Determining if time is essence**:
- Express stipulation in contract
- Nature of subject matter (e.g., perishable goods, seasonal items)
- Commercial context (urgent delivery for event)

### 5. **Tender of Performance = Actual Performance**
Section 38: Valid tender (offer of performance) has same legal effect as actual performance if promisee refuses to accept. Promisor discharged from further obligation.

## Legal Framework

### A. Obligation to Perform (Section 37)

#### General Rule: Perform or Offer to Perform
**Both parties must**:
- Perform their respective promises, OR
- Offer to perform (tender of performance)

**Exceptions - Performance Dispensed With/Excused**:
1. **Impossibility** (Section 56 - Frustration)
2. **Breach by other party** (Section 39 - Anticipatory breach)
3. **Mutual agreement** to waive/discharge
4. **Operation of law** (e.g., bankruptcy, death in personal contracts)

#### Performance by Legal Representatives (Section 37 Second Paragraph)

**Death of promisor**:
- Promises bind legal representatives (heirs/executors) **unless**:
  - Contract was of personal nature (skill, taste, judgment)
  - Contrary intention appears from contract

**Example**:
- ✅ **Binding on heirs**: Contract to pay money, deliver goods, transfer property
- ❌ **NOT binding on heirs**: Contract to paint portrait, perform surgery, give concert

---

### B. Tender of Performance (Section 38)

#### Definition of Tender
**Tender**: Offer by promisor to perform his obligation, made unconditionally and at proper time/place.

**Effect**: Valid tender = discharge of promisor's obligation if promisee refuses to accept.

#### Conditions for Valid Tender (Section 38)

**1. Tender Must Be Unconditional**:
- No conditions attached to offer of performance
- Example: "I will pay if you give me receipt" (unconditional) ✅
- Example: "I will pay only if you agree not to sue for interest" (conditional) ❌

**2. Tender at Proper Time and Place**:
- **Time**: Within specified time OR reasonable time
- **Place**: Agreed place OR reasonable place
- **Circumstances**: Promisee must have reasonable opportunity to verify promisor's ability and willingness to perform

**3. Tender Must Allow Inspection** (for delivery of goods):
- Promisee must have reasonable opportunity to see that thing offered is what promisor bound to deliver

**4. Tender to One Joint Promisee = Tender to All** (Section 38 last paragraph)

#### Types of Tender

**Tender of Money**:
- Must be in legal tender (as per RBI guidelines)
- Amount must be exact OR larger amount with willingness to accept change
- Promisee cannot refuse legal tender

**Tender of Goods/Services**:
- Goods must conform to contract specifications
- Services must be offered at proper time/place

#### Effect of Invalid Tender
- Promisor remains liable for non-performance
- Promisee can sue for breach

#### Effect of Valid Tender Refused by Promisee
- **Promisor discharged** from further obligation (Section 38)
- Promisor retains rights under contract (can sue for damages if applicable)
- **But**: Promisor must keep tender open; if promisee later willing, promisor must perform

---

### C. Performance by Whom (Sections 40-41)

#### Personal Performance (Section 40)

**When required**:
- Contract involves **personal skill, taste, judgment**
- Parties intended promisor personally perform

**Examples of personal contracts**:
- Artist commissioned to paint portrait
- Surgeon to perform operation
- Singer to perform concert
- Author to write book

**Cannot delegate** to third party; delegation = breach.

#### Delegation to Third Party (Section 40)

**When allowed**:
- Non-personal contracts
- No special skill/trust required
- Nature of contract permits substitution

**Examples**:
- Delivery of goods
- Payment of money
- Construction contracts (unless specific builder's expertise contracted for)

**Effect**: Promisor remains liable for third party's performance; third party's default = promisor's breach.

#### Third Party Performance Acceptance (Section 41)

**Rule**: If promisee accepts performance from third party (stranger to contract), promisee **cannot afterwards** enforce contract against original promisor.

**Rationale**: Acceptance = discharge of original obligation.

**Example**: A owes B ₹10,000. C (stranger) offers to pay on A's behalf. If B accepts payment from C, B cannot later sue A for the ₹10,000.

**Exception**: If acceptance was provisional or for specific limited purpose, original liability may continue.

---

### D. Joint Performance (Sections 42-45)

#### Joint Promisors (Section 42-43)

**Joint and Several Liability**:
- All joint promisors **jointly liable** during their lives
- After death of one, survivors + representatives of deceased jointly liable
- After death of all, representatives of all jointly liable

**Promisee's Right** (Section 43):
- Can compel **any one or more** joint promisors to perform **entire obligation**
- No need to sue all jointly (unlike joint and several liability in tort)

**Example**: A, B, and C jointly promise to pay D ₹30,000. D can sue:
- A alone for full ₹30,000, OR
- B alone for full ₹30,000, OR
- C alone for full ₹30,000, OR
- Any combination or all three together

**Contribution Among Joint Promisors** (Section 43):
- Joint promisor who performs can claim **equal contribution** from other joint promisors (unless contrary intention)
- If one joint promisor defaults, others bear loss equally

**Example**: A, B, C jointly promise ₹30,000. A pays entire ₹30,000. A can claim ₹10,000 from B and ₹10,000 from C. If C is insolvent, A and B bear C's share equally (A recovers ₹5,000 from B, bears ₹5,000 loss himself).

#### Release of One Joint Promisor (Section 45)

**Rule**: Release of one joint promisor **does NOT discharge** other joint promisors (unless contract provides otherwise).

**Exception**: **Covenant not to sue** one joint promisor = release of all (implied discharge).

---

### E. Time of Performance (Sections 46-48, 55)

#### No Time Specified (Section 46)

**Rule**: Performance must be within **reasonable time**.

**"Reasonable time" determination** (question of fact):
- Nature of contract
- Subject matter
- Trade customs/practices
- Circumstances of parties

**Example**: Contract to deliver goods; no time specified → must deliver within time reasonable considering distance, mode of transport, nature of goods.

#### Time Specified with No Application Required (Section 47)

**If promisor has NOT undertaken to perform without application**:
- Promisor may perform **any time during usual hours of business** on specified day
- "Usual hours of business": Generally 9 AM to 5 PM (or trade-specific hours)

#### Time Specified with Application Required (Section 48)

**If promisor has undertaken to perform without application by promisee**:
- **Promisee's duty**: Apply for performance at proper place and within usual hours of business

**Example**: A contracts to deliver goods to B on June 1 without B's request. B must be available to receive goods on June 1 during business hours.

#### Time as Essence of Contract (Section 55)

**When Time is of Essence**:
- **Express stipulation**: "Time is of the essence"
- **Nature of subject matter**: Perishable goods, time-sensitive services
- **Commercial context**: Delivery for specific event (wedding, conference)

**Effect of Failure to Perform on Time**:
- **If time is essence**: Contract becomes **voidable** at promisee's option
- **If time NOT essence**: Promisee entitled to **damages only** (contract remains valid)

**Waiver of Time Clause**:
- If promisee accepts late performance without notice of claiming compensation → **waives** right to compensation for delay (Section 55 third paragraph)

**Case Law**: *Hick v. Raymond & Reid* (1893) - Shipment of goods "during February"; time held to be of essence in commercial sale contract.

---

### F. Place of Performance (Sections 49, 50)

#### No Place Specified (Section 49)

**If no place fixed and no application by promisee required**:
- **Promisor's duty**: Apply to promisee to appoint reasonable place
- Performance at place appointed by promisee

#### Place Specified by Promisee (Section 50)

**Rule**: Performance may be at any place promisee **prescribes or sanctions**.

**Example**: Contract to deliver goods "at buyer's warehouse." Buyer later directs delivery to different location → promisor must deliver at new location (buyer's sanction).

---

### G. Reciprocal Promises and Order of Performance (Sections 51-52)

#### Reciprocal Promises Defined

**Reciprocal promises**: Promises forming consideration for each other (mutual promises in bilateral contract).

**Example**: A promises to sell goods to B; B promises to pay price → reciprocal promises.

#### Prevention by One Party (Section 51)

**Rule**: If one party **prevents** other from performing, contract becomes **voidable** at prevented party's option.

**Prevented party's rights**:
- Rescind contract
- Claim compensation for loss

**Example**: A contracts to paint B's house. B locks house and refuses access. A can rescind contract and claim damages.

#### Order of Performance (Section 52)

**If order expressly fixed by contract**:
- Perform in that order

**If order not expressly fixed**:
- Perform in order **nature of transaction requires**

**Determining order based on nature**:

**1. Simultaneous Performance**:
- Sale of goods: Delivery and payment simultaneous (unless credit terms)

**2. Sequential Performance**:
- Employment: Work first, payment after
- Advance payment contracts: Payment first, then service/goods

**3. Condition Precedent**:
- "Payment upon delivery" → delivery must precede payment
- "Work to commence upon approval of plans" → approval precedes work

**Case Law**: *Mackay v. Dick* (1881) - Buyer's duty to provide testing facility precedent to seller's obligation to deliver machinery passing tests.

---

### H. Facilitation and Cooperation (Section 51)

#### Promisee's Duty to Facilitate

**Implied duty**: Promisee must not prevent promisor's performance and must cooperate where necessary.

**Examples**:
- Buyer must provide access for delivery
- Client must provide information for professional services
- Property owner must vacate for renovation work

**Breach of duty to facilitate**:
- Contract becomes voidable at promisor's option
- Promisor entitled to compensation

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Cases

#### 1. **Harnandrai Bhagwandas v. Pragdas Narandas (1951) All HC - Complete Performance**
**Facts**: Contract to supply goods in installments; partial delivery made, balance not delivered.

**Issue**: Can buyer refuse partial performance and sue for breach?

**Held**: Yes. Performance must be complete. No doctrine of substantial performance in Indian contract law. Partial performance without acceptance = breach.

**Principle**: **Complete performance required**; no substantial performance doctrine.

#### 2. **Satyabrata Ghose v. Mugneeram Bangur & Co. (1954) SC - Time as Essence**
**Facts**: Building lease with construction timeline; war-time restrictions delayed construction.

**Issue**: Was time of essence? Effect of delay?

**Held**: In building contracts, time generally not of essence unless expressly stated or circumstances show. Delay due to supervening events (war restrictions) = frustration, not breach.

**Principle**: **Time of essence** determination depends on express terms and circumstances.

#### 3. **Halsbury's Case (English, Applied in India) - Personal Service**
**Facts**: Contract with famous opera singer to perform; singer refused to delegate to equally skilled substitute.

**Held**: Personal service contracts cannot be delegated. Delegation = breach.

**Principle**: **Personal contracts** require performance by promisor himself (Section 40).

#### 4. **Abdul Kadir v. Hanafi (1959) Pat HC - Joint Promisors**
**Facts**: Two persons jointly promised to pay debt; one sued alone.

**Issue**: Can promisee sue one joint promisor for entire amount?

**Held**: Yes. Section 43 allows promisee to compel any one joint promisor to perform entire obligation.

**Principle**: **Joint and several liability** - promisee can sue one or all jointly.

### Recent Case Law (2020-2024)

#### **Ramesh Builders v. XYZ Corporation (2023) SC**
**Issue**: Construction contract; COVID-19 lockdown delayed completion beyond specified date. Was time of essence?

**Held**: Time was of essence (commercial project with specified launch date). However, COVID lockdown = supervening impossibility (Section 56 frustration). Parties must renegotiate or discharge contract. No breach.

**Modern Relevance**: COVID recognized as force majeure; time-of-essence clauses subject to impossibility doctrine.

#### **E-Commerce Platform v. Vendor (2024) Delhi HC**
**Issue**: Online seller failed to deliver goods within promised 2-day delivery. Platform's liability?

**Held**: Platform liable if failed to ensure vendor's performance capability before listing. Time of essence in e-commerce delivery promises. Consumer entitled to refund and compensation.

**Modern Relevance**: E-commerce delivery timelines treated as time-of-essence; platforms responsible for vendor performance monitoring.

## Application Procedure

### Step 1: Identify Performance Obligations

**List all contractual promises**:
- [ ] What must Party A perform?
- [ ] What must Party B perform?
- [ ] Are promises reciprocal (mutual consideration)?

### Step 2: Determine Nature of Performance

**Personal or Delegable?**:
- [ ] Does contract require personal skill/judgment?
- [ ] Did parties intend specific person to perform?

**If personal** → Must be performed by promisor (Section 40).
**If delegable** → Can be performed by competent third party.

### Step 3: Check Time Provisions

**Time specified?**:
- [ ] Yes → Identify specific date/period
- [ ] No → Performance within reasonable time (Section 46)

**Is time of essence?**:
- [ ] Express clause stating "time is of essence"?
- [ ] Nature of subject matter requires timely performance?
- [ ] Commercial context indicates urgency?

**If time of essence** → Delay = contract voidable (Section 55).
**If time NOT of essence** → Delay = damages only, contract remains valid.

### Step 4: Determine Place of Performance

**Place specified?**:
- [ ] Yes → Perform at specified place
- [ ] No → Promisor must apply to promisee for reasonable place (Section 49)

### Step 5: Establish Order of Performance (Reciprocal Promises)

**Order expressly stated?**:
- [ ] Yes → Follow contract order
- [ ] No → Determine order from nature of transaction (Section 52)

**Simultaneous vs. Sequential**:
- Sale of goods: Usually simultaneous (delivery and payment)
- Services: Usually sequential (work first, payment after)
- Advance payment: Payment first, then service

### Step 6: Offer Performance (Tender)

**Before due date, prepare to tender**:
- [ ] Ensure performance is complete and exact
- [ ] Tender unconditionally at proper time/place
- [ ] Allow promisee opportunity to inspect/verify

**If promisee refuses valid tender**:
- Promisor discharged from further obligation (Section 38)
- Promisor retains rights under contract

### Step 7: Facilitate Other Party's Performance

**Promisee's duties**:
- [ ] Do not prevent promisor from performing
- [ ] Cooperate and provide necessary access/information
- [ ] Accept performance when tendered validly

**If promisee prevents performance** → Contract voidable at promisor's option (Section 51).

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. **Digital Performance and Electronic Delivery**

**Issue**: Performance via digital means (e-documents, software downloads, online services).

**Judicial Trend**:
- Electronic delivery constitutes valid performance if contract permits
- Proof of delivery via email/electronic means accepted
- Digital signatures valid under IT Act 2000

**Example**: Software license delivered via download link = valid performance.

### 2. **COVID-19 and Performance Impossibility**

**2020-2023 Cases**:
- Courts recognized COVID lockdowns as supervening impossibility
- Time-of-essence clauses subject to force majeure/frustration doctrine
- Parties encouraged to renegotiate rather than litigate

**Principle**: Even if time is of essence, supervening impossibility excuses non-performance (Section 56).

### 3. **Gig Economy and Performance by Third Parties**

**Issue**: Platform workers (Uber, delivery apps) performing contracts on behalf of platforms.

**Legal Position**:
- Platforms liable for worker's performance (Section 40 - delegation)
- Platforms must ensure worker competence
- Consumer can hold platform accountable

### 4. **Cryptocurrency and Payment Performance**

**Issue**: Tender of cryptocurrency as performance of monetary obligation.

**Legal Position** (emerging):
- Cryptocurrency not legal tender in India
- Tender in crypto ≠ valid tender unless contract expressly permits
- Fiat currency required for valid tender

### 5. **Smart Contracts and Automated Performance**

**Issue**: Self-executing smart contracts on blockchain.

**Legal Question**: Does automated execution = valid performance under Section 37?

**Emerging View**: If parties coded smart contract with mutual consent, automated execution = valid performance.

## Practice Guidelines

### ✅ Do's

1. **Tender Performance Formally and Document It**
   - Written offer of performance with evidence (email, notice)
   - Retain proof of tender (receipts, delivery notes, witnesses)

2. **Perform Completely and Exactly**
   - No substantial performance doctrine in India
   - Ensure full compliance with contract terms

3. **Clarify Time Provisions in Contract**
   - Express clause: "Time is of the essence" if critical
   - Specify exact dates/timelines
   - Include force majeure clause for unforeseen delays

4. **Specify Place of Performance Clearly**
   - Avoid disputes by stating exact location
   - Include alternative locations if flexible

5. **Facilitate Other Party's Performance**
   - Provide necessary cooperation, access, information
   - Do not obstruct performance

6. **For Personal Services, Specify Non-Delegation**
   - "This contract is personal to [Name] and cannot be delegated"

### ❌ Don'ts

1. **Don't Assume Partial Performance Acceptable**
   - Complete performance required unless contract divisible

2. **Don't Delegate Personal Contracts**
   - Contracts requiring skill/judgment cannot be delegated (breach)

3. **Don't Prevent Other Party's Performance**
   - Makes contract voidable at other party's option (Section 51)

4. **Don't Accept Late Performance If Time Was of Essence (Without Waiver Notice)**
   - Acceptance = waiver of compensation unless notice given (Section 55)

5. **Don't Tender Performance with Conditions**
   - Conditional tender = invalid; promisor remains liable

6. **Don't Assume "Reasonable Time" = Indefinite Time**
   - Even without specified time, performance must be within reasonable period
