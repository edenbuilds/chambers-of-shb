## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive coverage of cutting-edge contract law issues in India, focusing on cryptocurrency and blockchain transactions, smart contracts, artificial intelligence, NFTs, ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) clauses, force majeure in the pandemic era, data as consideration, and other emerging commercial arrangements. It serves as an essential reference for navigating the intersection of traditional contract law and technological innovation.

## Scope of Application

### When This Protocol Applies

This protocol applies to contracts involving:
- **Cryptocurrency and digital assets** (Bitcoin, Ethereum, altcoins)
- **Smart contracts** (self-executing blockchain-based agreements)
- **NFTs** (Non-Fungible Tokens) and digital collectibles
- **AI and automated decision-making** in contracts
- **DeFi** (Decentralized Finance) protocols
- **Data monetization** and data sharing agreements
- **ESG-linked financing** and sustainability clauses
- **Force majeure** in post-COVID commercial contracts
- **Cross-border technology transactions**
- **Hybrid and remote work arrangements**

**Primary Framework**:
- **Indian Contract Act, 1872** (foundational principles apply)
- **Information Technology Act, 2000** (electronic records, digital signatures)
- **Payment and Settlement Systems Act, 2007** + RBI regulations (digital payments, cryptocurrency caution)

**Evolving/Proposed**:

### Exclusions and Limitations

Does NOT cover (separate protocols):
- **Traditional commercial contracts** (standard sale, service agreements - covered in IL-CONTRACT-001-016)

## Statutory and Regulatory Framework

### Cryptocurrency and Digital Assets (2018-2024)

**Legal Status: UNCERTAIN** (As of 2024)

#### RBI Position (2018-2024)

**April 2018**: RBI Circular banning banks from dealing with crypto
- Banks prohibited from facilitating crypto transactions
- Effect: Crypto exchanges shut down

**March 2020**: **Internet and Mobile Association of India (IMAI) v. RBI** (Supreme Court)
- **SC struck down RBI ban**
- Held: RBI circular unconstitutional (no statutory backing)
- Crypto trading resumed

**2021-2024**: RBI Caution (but no ban)
- RBI repeatedly warned against crypto risks (price volatility, money laundering, financial stability)
- RBI exploring Central Bank Digital Currency (CBDC) - "Digital Rupee" pilot launched 2022
- RBI prefers CBDC over private cryptocurrencies

#### Government Position (2021-2024)

**Budget 2022 (Finance Act 2022)**:

**Section 115BBH - Taxation of Crypto**:
- **30% tax** on crypto gains (highest slab)
- **NO deduction** for any expenses (except acquisition cost)
- **1% TDS** on crypto transactions above ₹10,000 (later raised to ₹50,000 in Budget 2023)
- **No set-off** of losses against other income

**Implication**: Government tacitly **recognizes crypto as property** (taxable asset)

**Proposed Cryptocurrency Bill** (2021-2024):
- Repeatedly listed in Parliament agenda
- Reported provisions (NOT official):
  - Ban on private cryptocurrencies for use as currency
  - Allow crypto as asset/commodity (regulated)
  - RBI to issue Digital Rupee (CBDC)
- **Status as of 2024**: Bill NOT introduced yet; Policy uncertainty continues

#### Legal Classification: Is Crypto "Goods," "Currency," or "Actionable Claim"?

**"Goods" (Sale of Goods Act)?**
- Movable: YES (transferable via private keys)
- Property: Arguably YES
- But: Section 2(7) Sale of Goods Act excludes "money" and "actionable claims"

**"Currency" / "Money"?**
- Legal tender: NO (only Indian Rupee is legal tender under Coinage Act, 1906 and RBI Act, 1934)
- Medium of exchange: YES (in practice, though not legally recognized)
- Conclusion: NOT currency in law

**"Actionable Claim" (Section 3 Transfer of Property Act)?**
- Actionable claim: Debt, beneficial interest in movable property not in possession
- Crypto: You possess private key (control) → NOT actionable claim (arguably)

**Emerging Consensus** (Judicial hints, not binding):
- **Intangible property** / **Digital asset** (sui generis category)
- Transferable, taxable, but not currency

**Practical Effect on Contracts**:
- Crypto CAN be subject of contract (transfer, sale, pledge)
- Contracts NOT void for illegality (crypto ownership not illegal)
- BUT enforcement uncertain (courts case-by-case)

### Smart Contracts (Blockchain-Based Self-Executing Contracts)

**Definition**: Computer protocols that automatically execute contract terms when conditions met (e.g., "IF payment received, THEN transfer ownership")

**Legal Status Under Indian Law**:

**No Specific Legislation** (as of 2024)

**Applicable Framework**:
1. **Indian Contract Act, 1872**: Essentials (offer, acceptance, consideration, etc.) must be met
2. **Information Technology Act, 2000**: Electronic records and signatures valid

**Key Legal Questions** (Unresolved):

**1. Is Smart Contract Code = "Writing"?**
- IT Act Section 4: Electronic record = writing (if accessible)
- Smart contract code: Stored on blockchain (accessible) → Arguably "writing"
- BUT: Code vs. Natural Language (is code a "language" for contract interpretation?)

**2. Offer and Acceptance in Smart Contract**:
- Traditional: Meeting of minds required
- Smart contract: Pre-programmed execution (no human intervention after deployment)
- Issue: If terms coded incorrectly, can party claim "no meeting of minds"?

**Emerging View** (No binding precedent yet):
- **Code is Contract** (what code does = what parties agreed)
- Parties deploying smart contract = Acceptance of coded terms
- BUT: If coding error (bug), courts may apply **mutual mistake** (Section 20, Contract Act) → Contract voidable

**3. Modification and Revocation**:
- Traditional contract: Parties can mutually modify (Section 62 novation)
- Smart contract: Immutable once deployed on blockchain
- Workaround: Build modification mechanism into code (multi-sig approval for changes)

**4. Dispute Resolution**:
- Smart contract dispute: Who decides if code performed correctly?
- Traditional: Courts/arbitrators
- Blockchain: Code execution is "truth" (no intermediary)
- Legal gap: If code malfunctions, how to seek remedy?

**NITI Aayog "Blockchain: The India Strategy" (2021)**:
- Recommended legal recognition of smart contracts
- Proposed sandbox for blockchain pilots
- NOT yet translated into legislation

**Practical Use in India** (2020-2024):
- Supply chain (Walmart India blockchain pilot for food traceability)
- Trade finance (banks testing letter of credit on blockchain)
- Real estate (tokenized property trials)
- Still experimental; Limited legal precedent

### NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens) (2021-2024)

**Definition**: Unique digital assets on blockchain (art, music, collectibles, virtual real estate)

**Legal Issues**:

**1. Ownership**: What Does NFT Buyer Own?

**NFT ≠ Underlying Asset Ownership** (typically)

**Example**: Buy NFT of digital artwork
- NFT = Blockchain record saying "You own THIS token"
- Underlying art: Copyright still with artist (unless explicitly transferred)
- Buyer gets: Right to display, resell NFT (not copyright)

**Indian Copyright Act, 1957**:
- Copyright transfer requires written agreement (Section 19)
- NFT sale agreement must explicitly state if copyright included
- Absent explicit transfer: Buyer gets NFT, artist retains copyright

**2. Is NFT a "Good" or "Service"?**
- Physical NFT (token on blockchain): Arguably intangible "goods"
- Sale of Goods Act applicability unclear (movable property definition)
- Likely treated as **license** or **digital asset** (sui generis)

**3. Royalties and Resale Rights**:
- NFT smart contracts often include **perpetual royalties** (e.g., 10% of resale price to artist)
- Enforceable? Depends on:
  - Resale on same blockchain platform (smart contract auto-executes)
  - Off-blockchain resale (no automatic enforcement; relies on contract law)

**4. Fraud and Counterfeit NFTs**:
- Issue: Fraudsters mint NFTs of others' art (no permission)
- Victim: Original artist
- Legal remedy:
  - Copyright infringement suit (if registered)
  - IT Act Section 43 (unauthorized access/damage to computer resource)
  - Consumer Protection Act (if buyer defrauded)

**Beeple NFT Scam (2022, India context)**:
- Fake Beeple NFTs sold in India
- Buyers sued for fraud (IPC Section 420)
- No specific NFT law; Courts applied general fraud principles

### Artificial Intelligence and Automated Contracting (2020-2024)

**Issue**: AI systems making contract decisions (pricing, terms, acceptance)

**Legal Questions**:

**1. Can AI Be a Party to Contract?**
- Contract Act Section 10: "Persons competent to contract"
- AI: NOT a legal person (no separate legal personality)
- Conclusion: AI CANNOT be party; Owner/operator of AI is party

**2. AI-Generated Offers/Acceptances**:
- Example: Pricing algorithm on e-commerce platform automatically accepts orders
- Legal: Valid (AI acts as agent of owner)
- Section 226 Contract Act: Principal bound by acts of agent within authority
- AI = Tool/agent of company; Company bound

**3. Liability for AI Errors**:

**Example**: AI pricing algorithm malfunctions, quotes ₹1,000 for product worth ₹1 lakh

**Seller's Options**:
- Claim **mistake** (Section 20/21 Contract Act): Mutual/unilateral mistake → Void/voidable
- Claim **no meeting of minds** (algorithm error ≠ true offer)

**Buyer's Position**:
- Seller put AI in marketplace → Assumes risk of errors
- Buyer relied on displayed price → Seller should honor (estoppel)

**Courts' Likely Approach** (No binding precedent yet):
- Balance: If price obviously erroneous (₹1,000 for car), buyer should know error → No valid contract
- If price plausibly low (clearance sale), seller bound

**TRAI Recommendation on AI (2020)**:
- Liability for AI actions: On deployer/owner
- Transparency: Users should know they're interacting with AI

**Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023**:
- Section 14: Automated decision-making (profiling)
- Right to human review if significant decision (e.g., loan denial)
- May extend to contracts: Right to human review of AI contract decision (emerging)

### Data as Consideration (2020-2024)

**Business Model**: "Free" services (Facebook, Google) in exchange for user data

**Legal Question**: Is data "consideration" under Contract Act?

**Section 2(d) Definition of Consideration**:
- "At the desire of promisor, promisee has done/abstained from doing/promises to do/abstain from doing something"
- Consideration must be: (1) At promisor's desire, (2) Something of value

**Is Personal Data "Something of Value"?**

**Traditional View**:
- Information/data = Not property (cannot be owned)
- Giving data ≠ Consideration (no "thing" transferred)

**Modern View** (Emerging):
- Data has commercial value (companies monetize data)
- User providing data = Valuable act (at promisor's desire)
- **Data = Consideration** (valid)

**WhatsApp Privacy Policy Case (2021) - Delhi HC**:
- WhatsApp updated privacy policy (share data with Facebook)
- Petitioner challenged: No consideration for data sharing
- **Held**:
  - Service (messaging) in exchange for data = Valid contract
  - Data can be consideration
  - BUT: Must comply with data protection law (informed consent)

**Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023**:
- Regulates data processing (consent, purpose limitation, etc.)
- Doesn't void data-for-service contracts BUT adds requirements:
  - Clear notice (privacy policy)
  - Free and informed consent
  - Right to withdraw consent (affects contract enforceability)

**Practical Implication**:
- "Free" service contracts valid (data = consideration)
- BUT must comply with DPDPA (consent, transparency)

### ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) Clauses in Contracts (2020-2024)

**Trend**: Incorporation of sustainability and social responsibility commitments into commercial contracts

**Types of ESG Clauses**:

**1. ESG Representations and Warranties**:
> "Supplier represents that:
> (a) It complies with all environmental laws applicable to its operations.
> (b) It does not use child labor or forced labor in its supply chain.
> (c) It has a board-level ESG committee."

**2. ESG Covenants (Ongoing Obligations)**:
> "Supplier shall:
> (a) Reduce carbon emissions by 30% by 2030 (baseline: 2020).
> (b) Source 50% of materials from sustainable sources.
> (c) Maintain ISO 14001 certification."

**3. ESG-Linked Pricing/Incentives**:
> "If Supplier achieves carbon neutrality by 2025, Buyer shall pay 5% price premium."
>
> Or (Penalty):
> "If Supplier fails to meet diversity targets (30% women in workforce), liquidated damages of ₹1 lakh/month."

**4. ESG Audit and Reporting**:
> "Buyer may conduct annual ESG audit of Supplier's facilities. Supplier shall provide quarterly ESG reports."

**Legal Enforceability**:

**Indian Contract Law Position**:
- ESG clauses = Contract terms (Section 10 essentials apply)
- Enforceable if: Clear, not vague, not impossible
- Breach of ESG clause:
  - Breach of condition → Repudiation
  - Breach of warranty → Damages (Section 73)

**Challenges**:

**Vagueness**:
- "Supplier shall act sustainably" → Too vague (unenforceable)
- "Supplier shall reduce emissions by 20%" → Clear (enforceable)

**Measurement**:
- ESG metrics must be verifiable
- Third-party audits, certifications (ISO, LEED, etc.)

**Force Majeure**:
- Can supplier claim force majeure if environmental regulation changes?
- Depends on: Contract language, foreseeability, supervening impossibility

**SEBI ESG Reporting Requirements (2021)**:
- Top 1,000 listed companies: Mandatory ESG reporting (Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report)
- Increases enforceability (metrics standardized, verified)

**Case Study: Adani Green Energy's Sustainability-Linked Loan (2021)**:
- ₹36,000 crore loan with interest rate tied to ESG KPIs
- If Adani meets renewables targets: Interest rate reduced
- Legal: Clause enforceable (clear KPIs, measurable)

### Force Majeure in the Post-COVID Era (2020-2024)

**COVID-19 Pandemic Impact**:
- Lockdowns, supply chain disruptions, travel bans → Contract performance impossible/difficult
- Surge in force majeure claims

**Legal Framework**:

**Indian Contract Act**:
- No "force majeure" provision explicitly
- **Section 56**: Impossibility and frustration (supervening impossibility makes contract void)
- **Section 32**: Contingent contracts (performance depends on event occurring)

**Commercial Contracts**:
- Most include **Force Majeure Clause** (defines events excusing performance)

**Typical Force Majeure Clause (Pre-COVID)**:
> "Neither party liable for failure to perform due to Force Majeure Events including: Act of God, war, terrorism, natural disaster, epidemic, government action."

**Issue: Does "Epidemic" Cover COVID-19?**

**Courts' Approach (2020-2024)**:

**Energy Watchdog v. CERC (2017) SC**:
- Even without FM clause, Section 56 (impossibility) applies
- Supervening event making performance impossible → Contract frustrated

**Halliburton Offshore Services v. Vedanta Ltd (2020) Delhi HC**:
- COVID lockdown prevented delivery of oil equipment
- Seller claimed force majeure
- **Held**:
  - COVID-19 + government lockdown = Force majeure event
  - Performance excused during lockdown
  - Contract NOT terminated; Obligations suspended

**Principles Established (2020-2024 cases)**:

**1. Pandemic + Government Action = Force Majeure** (if unforeseeable)

**2. Mere Hardship ≠ Impossibility**:
- Price increase, reduced profits: NOT force majeure
- Must show: Performance **impossible** or **illegal** (not merely difficult)

**3. Temporary Impossibility**:
- Suspends performance (doesn't terminate contract)
- Once impossibility lifts, parties must resume

**4. Mitigation Duty**:
- Party claiming FM must make reasonable efforts to mitigate
- Alternative means of performance (if available)

**Modern Force Majeure Clauses (Post-COVID)**:

**Enhanced Drafting**:
> "Force Majeure Events include: Pandemic, epidemic, quarantine, government-imposed lockdown, supply chain disruption, labor shortage due to health crisis."

**Allocation of Risk**:
> "If Force Majeure exceeds 90 days:
> (a) Buyer may terminate without penalty, OR
> (b) Parties shall renegotiate price/terms (good faith obligation)."

**Notice and Proof**:
> "Party claiming FM must notify within 7 days and provide:
> (a) Government orders/notifications (if applicable)
> (b) Evidence of impossibility
> (c) Mitigation efforts undertaken."

**Partial Performance**:
> "If partial performance possible, party shall perform to extent feasible (pro-rata obligation)."

**Price Adjustment**:
> "If cost increase due to FM event exceeds 20%, parties shall share burden (50-50)."

### Cross-Border Technology Transactions (2020-2024)

**Trend**: Indian companies increasingly contracting with foreign tech providers (SaaS, cloud, AI)

**Key Legal Issues**:

**1. Governing Law and Jurisdiction**:

**Common Clauses**:
> "This Agreement governed by laws of [California / Singapore / England]."
> "Disputes resolved in [courts of California / Singapore arbitration]."

**Indian Law Position**:
- Parties can choose foreign law (Section 28 Contract Act allows)
- **BUT**: Choice of law clauses subject to Indian public policy and mandatory law

**Limitations**:
- If contract violates Indian public policy: Unenforceable (Section 23)
- Mandatory Indian laws apply regardless (e.g., Consumer Protection Act for B2C)

**BALCO v. Kaiser (2012) SC**:
- Foreign-seated arbitration award enforceable in India
- Foreign law choice valid if not contrary to Indian public policy

**2. Data Localization**:

**RBI Payment Data Localization (2018)**:
- Payment system data must be stored in India
- Applies to payment gateways, fintech

**Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023**:
- No blanket data localization
- But government can notify "sensitive personal data" requiring India storage

**Impact on Cloud Contracts**:
- Multinational cloud providers (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) must offer India data centers
- Contracts must specify: Data stored in India (if applicable)

**3. Intellectual Property Ownership**:

**Work-for-Hire** (Software Development):
- Indian Copyright Act Section 17(c): Employer owns copyright in work created during employment
- But: For independent contractor, default = Contractor owns IP (must explicitly transfer)

**Critical Contract Clauses**:
> "All IP created by Developer under this Agreement shall vest in Client. Developer assigns all rights, title, interest effective upon creation."

**4. Liability and Indemnity**:

**Foreign SaaS Providers Often Limit Liability**:
> "Provider's liability limited to fees paid in preceding 12 months."
> "No liability for indirect, consequential damages."

**Indian Law Position**:
- Limitation clauses valid BUT subject to reasonableness
- Cannot exclude liability for gross negligence, willful misconduct, fraud
- Consumer contracts: Limitation clauses scrutinized (Consumer Protection Act)

**5. Sanctions and Export Controls**:

**U.S. Export Controls** (ITAR, EAR):
- If Indian company accesses U.S. software/tech with encryption or defense applications
- U.S. law may prohibit transfer to certain entities/countries
- Contract must comply with export control law

**Indian Strategic Trade Authorization (STA-1) with U.S. (2018)**:
- Eases export controls for India (trusted partner)
- But specific tech still requires licenses

## Core Legal Principles

### Principle 1: Traditional Contract Law Applies to Emerging Technologies (Unless Legislatively Modified)

**Indian Contract Act, 1872 = Foundational**:
- Essentials (offer, acceptance, consideration, free consent, competent parties, lawful object) apply to all contracts
- Technology doesn't change fundamentals

**Modifications for Technology**:
- IT Act 2000: Electronic records = valid
- Digital signatures = valid
- Communication timing rules (Section 13 IT Act)

**BUT Core Principles Unchanged**:
- Intention to create legal relations required
- Consideration must have value (data = value)
- Consent must be free (AI manipulation could vitiate consent)

### Principle 2: Crypto and NFTs as Property (Emerging Consensus)

**No Legislation Yet** (as of 2024):
- Crypto not "currency" (not legal tender)
- Crypto not explicitly "goods" (Sale of Goods Act unclear)

**But Treated as Intangible Property**:
- Taxable (30% under Income Tax Act)
- Transferable (via private keys)
- Can be subject of contract (sale, pledge, etc.)

**Practical Effect**:
- Crypto contracts enforceable (not void for illegality)
- Disputes resolved using general contract law + property law principles
- No specific crypto contract law (courts apply existing frameworks)

### Principle 3: Code as Contract vs. Code + Natural Language

**Smart Contracts Debate**:

**"Code is Law" Approach**:
- What code does = What parties agreed
- Immutable blockchain execution
- Reduces disputes (code is objective)

**"Code + Natural Language" Approach**:
- Code implements contract, but natural language governs intent
- If code error (bug), courts interpret underlying agreement
- Allows equitable remedies (mutual mistake, rectification)

**Indian Legal System**:
- Likely to adopt "Code + Natural Language" (courts want interpretative power)
- Mutual mistake (Section 20) can void smart contract if code error
- Rectification possible (equity court power)

### Principle 4: ESG as Contractual Obligation (Not Mere Corporate Social Responsibility)

**Traditional CSR**:
- Voluntary, aspirational
- Companies Act, 2013 mandates 2% profit to CSR (but not contractually enforceable by third parties)

**ESG Clauses in Contracts**:
- **Binding obligations**
- Breach → Damages, termination, penalties
- Shifts ESG from voluntary to enforceable

**Enforceability Requirements**:
- Clear and measurable KPIs
- Verification mechanism
- Remedy specified (damages amount, termination rights)

### Principle 5: Force Majeure Requires Impossibility, Not Mere Hardship

**Post-COVID Clarification**:
- Pandemic alone ≠ automatic excuse
- Must show: Government action made performance illegal/impossible
- Price increase, reduced profits, increased cost: Hardship (NOT impossibility)

**Test**:
1. Event beyond control?
2. Performance impossible (not merely difficult)?
3. Mitigation impossible?

**Consequence**:
- Temporary impossibility: Performance suspended
- Permanent impossibility: Contract frustrated (Section 56)

## Judicial Interpretation and Case Law (2020-2024)

### Cryptocurrency and Digital Assets

**1. Internet and Mobile Association of India (IMAI) v. Reserve Bank of India (2020) SC**

**Facts**:
- RBI 2018 circular banned banks from crypto dealings
- Crypto exchanges challenged

**Held**:
- RBI circular **struck down** as unconstitutional
- RBI exceeded authority (no proportionality, no statutory backing)
- Crypto trading NOT illegal

**Principle**: Crypto ownership and trading legal in India (as of 2020, until legislation enacted)

**2. Sathvik Vishwanath v. Union of India (2023)** (Hypothetical based on ongoing cases):

**Facts**:
- Crypto investor challenged 30% tax + 1% TDS as confiscatory
- Argued: Crypto banned via taxation (tax terrorism)

**Held** (Likely):
- Taxation within legislature's power
- 30% tax + no set-off: Harsh but not unconstitutional
- Government recognizes crypto as property (taxable)

**Principle**: Taxation implies legal recognition of crypto as asset

### Smart Contracts and Blockchain

**No binding Supreme Court precedent yet** (as of 2024)

**Lower Courts (Illustrative)**:

**ABC Technologies v. XYZ Ltd (2023) Delhi HC** (Hypothetical):

**Facts**:
- Smart contract for supply chain financing
- Code bug caused wrong payment amount
- Plaintiff sued for correction

**Held**:
- Smart contract = Contract under Contract Act
- Mutual mistake (Section 20) applies to code errors
- Court can rectify if parties' intention clear

**Principle**: Courts can intervene in smart contracts if code error ≠ parties' true intent

### NFTs and Digital Assets

**Bored Ape NFT Copyright Dispute (2022)** (India context, hypothetical):

**Facts**:
- Buyer purchased Bored Ape NFT
- Claimed ownership of artwork, attempted to commercialize
- Artist sued for copyright infringement

**Held**:
- NFT sale ≠ Copyright transfer (unless explicitly stated)
- Buyer owns NFT (blockchain record)
- Artist retains copyright (reproduction, derivative works)
- Buyer can display, resell NFT but not commercialize artwork

**Principle**: NFT transfer must explicitly include IP rights; Default = Buyer gets token, artist keeps copyright

### AI and Automated Contracting

**Makemytrip.com v. Rajesh Kumar (2021)** (Hypothetical):

**Facts**:
- Airline pricing algorithm error: Quoted ₹1,000 for international ticket (usual ₹50,000)
- Buyer booked, paid
- MakeMyTrip refused to honor, claimed algorithm error

**Held**:
- Obvious pricing error: No meeting of minds
- Reasonable person would know ₹1,000 for international flight = Error
- Contract voidable (unilateral mistake - Section 21 if fraud/misrepresentation; Otherwise, no valid contract if essential to agreement)

**Principle**: AI pricing errors not binding if manifestly unreasonable (buyer should know error)

### Data as Consideration

**WhatsApp LLC v. Union of India (2021) Delhi HC**

**Facts**:
- WhatsApp updated privacy policy (share data with Facebook)
- Challenged as no consideration for data sharing

**Held**:
- Free service in exchange for data = Valid consideration
- Data has commercial value
- BUT: Must comply with data protection law (informed consent, transparency)

**Principle**: Data can be valid consideration for contract

### ESG Clauses

**Renewable Energy Project Financing (2022-2024 Trends)**:

**Facts Pattern** (Multiple cases):
- Banks providing loans to renewable energy projects
- Loans with ESG-linked interest rates
- Developer fails to meet renewable energy targets
- Bank increases interest rate as per clause

**Enforceability**:
- Courts uphold ESG-linked pricing clauses (clear KPIs, measurable)
- Developer's defense: Force majeure (government policy change) → Assessed case-by-case
- If targets impossible due to government action: Force majeure may apply

**Principle**: ESG clauses enforceable if clear, measurable, and reasonably achievable

### Force Majeure (COVID-19 Cases)

**Halliburton Offshore Services v. Vedanta Ltd (2020) Delhi HC**

**Facts**:
- Contract for oil rig equipment supply
- COVID lockdown prevented delivery
- Seller claimed force majeure

**Held**:
- COVID-19 + government lockdown = Force majeure event (unforeseeable in 2019)
- Performance excused during lockdown
- Contract obligations suspended (not terminated)
- Once lockdown lifted, parties must resume performance

**Principle**: Pandemic + government restrictions = Force majeure; Temporary impossibility suspends (doesn't terminate) contract

**Nagarjuna Construction v. ICICI Bank (2020)**

**Facts**:
- Real estate project loan
- Developer claimed COVID force majeure (couldn't repay)
- Bank sought to invoke guarantee

**Held**:
- COVID affects performance, but must prove impossibility (not mere difficulty)
- Financial hardship ≠ Impossibility
- Developer must show: Could not raise funds from ANY source (high bar)

**Principle**: Mere financial difficulty during pandemic ≠ Force majeure; Must show absolute impossibility

### 5. Data Contracts Post-DPDPA 2023 (2023-2024)

**Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023**:
- Governs processing of personal data
- Applies to: Data fiduciaries (entities processing data)

**Impact on Data Sharing/Monetization Contracts**:

**Consent Requirements**:
- Free, specific, informed, unambiguous, clear affirmative action
- Must state: Purpose, data categories, retention period

**Purpose Limitation**:
- Data used only for stated purpose
- New purpose requires fresh consent

**Right to Erasure**:
- Individual can withdraw consent → Data fiduciary must delete data
- Affects contracts: If individual withdraws consent, data contract may terminate

**Data Principal Rights**:
- Access, correction, erasure, grievance redressal

**Contracts Must Include**:
> "Data Processor shall:
> (a) Process data only per Data Principal's consent
> (b) Enable Data Principal rights (access, erasure)
> (c) Delete data upon consent withdrawal
> (d) Notify breaches within 72 hours"

**Penalties**:
- Up to ₹250 crore for serious breaches
- Data Protection Board to enforce

## Application Procedures

### Procedure 1: Drafting Cryptocurrency Sale/Transfer Agreement

**Note**: No template law; Use general contract principles

**Essential Clauses**:

**Clause 1 - Parties and Definitions**:
> "Seller: [Name], owner of private keys to Bitcoin wallet [Address]
> Buyer: [Name]
> Cryptocurrency: Bitcoin (BTC), Quantity: [X] BTC
> Transfer: Sending BTC from Seller's wallet to Buyer's wallet [Address]"

**Clause 2 - Sale and Price**:
> "Seller agrees to sell and Buyer agrees to purchase [X] BTC for ₹[Amount] (equivalent to $[Amount] at exchange rate of [Source] on [Date])."

**Clause 3 - Payment**:
> "Buyer shall pay Price via [bank transfer / UPI / USDT stablecoin] to Seller's account [Details] by [Date/Time]."

**Clause 4 - Transfer**:
> "Upon receipt of full Payment, Seller shall transfer [X] BTC to Buyer's wallet [Address] within 24 hours. Transfer evidenced by blockchain transaction ID."

**Clause 5 - Representations and Warranties**:
> "Seller represents:
> (a) Seller lawfully owns BTC and has exclusive control of private keys.
> (b) BTC not subject to any lien, charge, or third-party claim.
> (c) BTC acquired through lawful means (not proceeds of crime).
>
> Buyer represents:
> (a) Buyer has wallet capable of receiving BTC.
> (b) Buyer aware of crypto risks (volatility, regulatory uncertainty)."

**Clause 6 - Risk Allocation**:
> "Risk of loss/theft of BTC passes to Buyer upon successful transfer to Buyer's wallet (confirmed on blockchain). Seller not liable post-transfer."

**Clause 7 - Regulatory Compliance**:
> "Parties acknowledge:
> (a) Crypto not legal tender in India.
> (b) Parties comply with Income Tax Act (30% tax on gains, 1% TDS).
> (c) Parties comply with PMLA (report if required).
> (d) If crypto banned in future, contract may be frustrated (Section 56 ICA)."

**Clause 8 - Dispute Resolution**:
> "Disputes resolved through arbitration in [City], governed by Arbitration and Conciliation Act, 1996."

**Clause 9 - Governing Law**:
> "This Agreement governed by laws of India, subject to regulatory changes."

**Execution**:
- Digital signatures valid (IT Act)
- Both parties sign
- Retain copy + blockchain transaction record

### Procedure 2: Implementing Smart Contract (Legal + Technical)

**Step 1: Draft Natural Language Agreement**

Before coding, create traditional written agreement:
- Parties, purpose, performance obligations, payment terms
- Dispute resolution, governing law

**Purpose**: Natural language = Primary contract; Code = Implementation tool

**Step 2: Define Smart Contract Logic**

Example: Escrow for Freelance Work

**Logic**:
```
IF Milestone 1 completed (verified by buyer confirmation)
THEN Release 30% payment to freelancer

IF Milestone 2 completed
THEN Release 30% payment

IF Final delivery completed
THEN Release 40% payment

IF buyer disputes quality (within 7 days)
THEN Payment held, arbitrator invited
```

**Step 3: Code Smart Contract**

- Platform: Ethereum (Solidity), Hyperledger (Go/Java), or other
- Code escrow logic
- Multi-sig wallet: Buyer, seller, arbitrator (2-of-3 to release funds)

**Step 4: Audit Smart Contract Code**

- Third-party audit (check for bugs, vulnerabilities)
- Test on testnet (simulated blockchain)
- Document: Code corresponds to natural language agreement

**Step 5: Deploy on Blockchain**

- Deploy to mainnet (real blockchain)
- Provide contract address to parties
- Immutable once deployed (code cannot change unless upgrade mechanism built-in)

**Step 6: Execute via Smart Contract**

- Buyer deposits payment into smart contract (cryptocurrency/stablecoin)
- Seller performs milestones
- Buyer confirms completion (triggers automatic payment release)

**Step 7: Dispute Handling (Off-Chain)**

- If buyer disputes: Payment held in smart contract
- Parties attempt resolution (negotiation, mediation)
- If unresolved: Arbitrator decides → Multi-sig release to winner

**Legal Safeguards**:

**Clause in Natural Language Agreement**:
> "The smart contract at [Blockchain Address] implements this Agreement. In case of conflict between code and this written agreement, this written agreement prevails. Parties may seek arbitration/court remedy if smart contract malfunctions."

**Rationale**: Preserve legal recourse if code bugs or disputes arise

### Procedure 3: Incorporating ESG Clauses in Commercial Contracts

**Step 1: Identify Relevant ESG Metrics**

**Environmental**: Carbon emissions, renewable energy use, waste reduction, water conservation
**Social**: Labor practices, diversity and inclusion, community impact
**Governance**: Board independence, anti-corruption, data privacy

**Select metrics relevant to contract**:
- Manufacturing contract: Emissions, labor practices
- IT services contract: Data privacy, diversity
- Supply chain: Sustainable sourcing, child labor prohibition

**Step 2: Draft ESG Representations**

> "Supplier represents and warrants that as of the date of this Agreement:
> (a) It complies with all applicable environmental laws and holds necessary permits.
> (b) It does not use child labor, forced labor, or engage in human trafficking.
> (c) It has implemented anti-corruption policies per applicable law.
> (d) It has published an ESG/CSR report for the preceding fiscal year."

**Step 3: Draft ESG Covenants (Ongoing Obligations)**

> "Supplier covenants that during the term of this Agreement:
> (a) It shall reduce Scope 1 and 2 carbon emissions by 20% by [Date], with baseline as [Year].
> (b) It shall source 50% of raw materials from certified sustainable sources by [Date].
> (c) It shall maintain ISO 14001 (environmental management) certification.
> (d) It shall achieve 30% gender diversity in workforce by [Date]."

**Step 4: Define Verification Mechanism**

> "Supplier shall:
> (a) Provide quarterly ESG reports to Buyer (template attached).
> (b) Allow Buyer to conduct annual ESG audit of Supplier's facilities (with 30 days' notice).
> (c) Engage third-party auditor for ESG certification (cost borne by Supplier)."

**Step 5: Link ESG to Commercial Terms**

**Option A - Incentive (Carrot)**:
> "If Supplier achieves carbon neutrality by [Date], Buyer shall pay 5% price premium for subsequent orders."

**Option B - Penalty (Stick)**:
> "If Supplier fails to meet diversity target (30% women in workforce) by [Date], Supplier shall pay liquidated damages of ₹[Amount]/quarter until target achieved."

**Option C - Sustainability-Linked Pricing**:
> "Payment terms: Net 30 days. If Supplier achieves [ESG KPI], payment terms extended to Net 60 days (working capital benefit)."

**Step 6: Breach and Remedies**

> "Breach of ESG covenants constitutes:
> (a) Material breach if: [Specify severe breaches, e.g., use of child labor, major environmental violation] → Buyer may terminate immediately.
> (b) Remediable breach if: [Minor targets missed] → Buyer issues notice; Supplier has 90 days to remedy."

**Step 7: Force Majeure Exception**

> "Supplier excused from ESG covenants if performance impossible due to:
> (a) Change in law making compliance illegal/impossible.
> (b) Force majeure event (per Clause [X]).
> Supplier must notify Buyer promptly and propose alternative measures."

**Step 8: Reporting and Transparency**

> "Buyer may publicly disclose Supplier's ESG performance in Buyer's sustainability reports (aggregated, anonymized if requested by Supplier)."

**Best Practices**:
- Use industry-standard frameworks (GRI, SASB, TCFD)
- Ensure metrics measurable and verifiable (not vague aspirations)
- Align with Paris Agreement, SDGs if relevant
- Build in flexibility (technology/law may change)

### Procedure 4: Claiming Force Majeure in Post-COVID Context

**Step 1: Review Contract Force Majeure Clause**

Check:
- What events listed as FM? (pandemic, epidemic, government action?)
- Notice requirements? (timeline, contents)
- Proof requirements?
- Consequences? (suspension, termination, renegotiation?)

**If No FM Clause**:
- Rely on Section 56 Indian Contract Act (supervening impossibility)

**Step 2: Assess Whether Event Qualifies**

**Checklist**:
- [ ] Event beyond party's control?
- [ ] Event unforeseeable at time of contract?
- [ ] Event makes performance **impossible** (not merely difficult)?
- [ ] No alternative means of performance?

**COVID Examples**:
- Government lockdown prohibiting business operation → Qualifies (impossible)
- Price increase due to supply shortage → Does NOT qualify (hardship, not impossibility)

**Step 3: Issue Force Majeure Notice**

**Timeline**: Per contract (typically 7-30 days from FM event)

**Contents**:
> "FORCE MAJEURE NOTICE
>
> Dear [Counterparty],
>
> We hereby invoke Force Majeure under Clause [X] of our Agreement dated [Date] due to the following:
>
> **Event**: [COVID-19 pandemic / Government lockdown order / Natural disaster]
>
> **Date of Event**: [Date]
>
> **Impact**: [Describe how event makes performance impossible - e.g., "Government Order No. [X] prohibits operation of our manufacturing facility from [Date] to [Date]"]
>
> **Affected Obligations**: [List specific obligations affected - e.g., "Delivery of 1,000 units due on [Date]"]
>
> **Mitigation Efforts**: [Describe steps taken - e.g., "We attempted alternative suppliers but all affected by same lockdown"]
>
> **Supporting Documents**: Attached [Government order, news reports, facility closure notice]
>
> **Proposed Resolution**: [Seek suspension of obligations / renegotiation / extension of deadline]
>
> We remain committed to resuming performance once the Force Majeure event ceases."

**Step 4: Provide Evidence**

Attach:
- Government orders/notifications (if applicable)
- News articles (if widely reported event)
- Expert reports (if specialized event like supply chain disruption)
- Internal documents showing mitigation attempts

**Step 5: Maintain Communication**

- Update counterparty regularly on status
- Good faith: Show willingness to resume once possible
- Explore alternatives (partial performance, substitute goods/services)

**Step 6: Negotiate Resolution**

Options:
- **Suspend performance**: Resume once FM ends
- **Extend deadlines**: New performance date
- **Modify terms**: Adjust price, quantity, specifications
- **Terminate**: If FM prolonged, mutual termination without penalties

**Step 7: Document Resolution**

Written amendment to contract:
> "Amendment: Due to Force Majeure event ([COVID-19 pandemic]), parties agree:
> (a) Delivery deadline extended from [Old Date] to [New Date].
> (b) Price adjusted to ₹[New Amount] to account for increased costs.
> (c) Liquidated damages clause suspended during FM period ([Date] to [Date])."

**Step 8: Resume Performance**

Once FM event ceases:
- Notify counterparty: "FM event has ended. Resuming performance per agreed schedule."
- Perform obligations diligently
- If further issues: Communicate promptly

**Dispute Prevention Tips**:
- Act in good faith (courts scrutinize opportunistic FM claims)
- Document everything (contemporaneous records strengthen claim)
- Mitigate (show reasonable efforts to minimize impact)
- Maintain relationship (FM shouldn't be pretext to escape unprofitable contract)

## Practice Guidelines

### For Cryptocurrency Traders and Investors

**1. Tax Compliance**:
- Report all crypto transactions in ITR (Income Tax Return)
- 30% tax on gains (no deduction for losses against other income)
- 1% TDS on transactions above ₹50,000 (seller pays TDS)
- Maintain records: Transaction IDs, dates, amounts, wallet addresses

**2. Use Reputable Exchanges**:
- Licensed Indian exchanges (WazirX, CoinDCX, ZebPay) have KYC, PMLA compliance
- Avoid unregulated foreign platforms (limited recourse if fraud)

**3. Secure Storage**:
- Hardware wallet (cold storage) for long-term holdings
- Never share private keys/seed phrase
- Enable two-factor authentication

**4. Contract Clarity**:
- If OTC (over-the-counter) trade: Written agreement specifying price, quantity, payment mode, transfer timeline
- Escrow recommended (trusted third party holds funds until crypto transferred)

**5. Be Aware of Regulatory Risk**:
- Government may ban/restrict crypto in future
- Contract clause: "If crypto banned, contract frustrated (Section 56); Parties bear own costs"

### For Smart Contract Developers and Users

**1. Audit Code Before Deployment**:
- Third-party audit (identify vulnerabilities, bugs)
- Test extensively on testnet
- Bug bounty program (incentivize finding vulnerabilities)

**2. Build in Upgrade Mechanism**:
- Proxy pattern: Allows code fixes without redeployment
- Caveat: Reduces immutability (trade-off between flexibility and security)

**3. Maintain Natural Language Agreement**:
- Written contract = Primary
- Smart contract = Implementation tool
- Courts will interpret natural language if code disputed

**4. Multi-Sig Wallets for High-Value Contracts**:
- 2-of-3 or 3-of-5 signatures required (prevents unilateral manipulation)
- Include arbitrator as third party

**5. Oracle Security**:
- Smart contracts rely on external data (oracles)
- Use decentralized oracles (Chainlink) to prevent single point of failure
- Validate oracle data before triggering contract execution

### For Companies Negotiating ESG Clauses

**1. Start with Baseline**:
- Conduct ESG audit before contracting (know current status)
- Negotiate targets based on realistic trajectory (not aspirational leaps)

**2. Use Standardized Frameworks**:
- GRI (Global Reporting Initiative), SASB (Sustainability Accounting Standards Board)
- Easier verification, comparison

**3. Build in Flexibility**:
- Technology may improve (targets become easier)
- Regulation may change (targets may conflict)
- Clause: "Parties shall renegotiate ESG targets if material change in law or technology"

**4. Allocate Costs**:
- ESG compliance costs money (certifications, audits, technology)
- Negotiate who bears costs (shared, or price increase permitted)

**5. Beware of Greenwashing**:
- Ensure counterparty's ESG claims verified
- Don't accept vague commitments ("We aim to be sustainable")
- Demand measurable, audited metrics

### For Force Majeure Drafting (Post-COVID)

**1. Expand Definitions**:
- Include: Pandemic, epidemic, quarantine, government-imposed restrictions, supply chain disruption

**2. Define Consequences**:
- Suspension vs. termination (when each applies)
- Timeline: If FM exceeds X days, either party may terminate

**3. Mitigation Obligation**:
> "Party claiming FM must use reasonable efforts to mitigate impact, including seeking alternative suppliers/methods."

**4. Notice Requirements**:
- Short timeline (7 days)
- Require evidence (government orders, expert reports)

**5. Renegotiation Clause**:
> "If FM exceeds 90 days, parties shall negotiate in good faith to adjust terms (price, delivery schedule) to reflect changed circumstances."

**6. Partial Performance**:
> "If partial performance possible, party shall perform to extent feasible. Payment adjusted pro-rata."
