# IL-CPC-007: Interim Relief and Injunctions

## Protocol Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-CPC-007
- **Domain**: Indian Civil Law - Civil Procedure
- **Category**: Provisional Remedies
- **Complexity**: High
- **Last Updated**: 2024-12-04
- **Prerequisites**: IL-CPC-001 (CPC Fundamentals), IL-CONTRACT-007 (Breach and Remedies)

## Purpose and Scope

This protocol covers the comprehensive framework for obtaining interim relief and injunctions under the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, focusing on Orders XXXVIII and XXXIX, Section 94, and related provisions. These provisional remedies are critical tools for preserving rights and property during the pendency of litigation.

**Primary Coverage:**
- Temporary injunctions (Order XXXIX Rules 1-2)
- Attachment before judgment (Order XXXVIII)
- Supplemental proceedings (Section 94)
- Three-tier test for injunctive relief
- Modern developments in interim relief practice (2020-2024)

**Key Statutory References:**
- Code of Civil Procedure, 1908: Order XXXVIII, Order XXXIX, Section 94, Section 151
- Specific Relief Act, 1963: Sections 36-42 (permanent injunctions - for context)
- Commercial Courts Act, 2015: Sections 12-13 (time-bound interim orders)

**Practice Context:**
Interim relief is among the most frequently invoked procedures in civil litigation, serving to maintain the status quo, prevent irreparable harm, and ensure the efficacy of final relief. The COVID-19 pandemic (2020-2024) has significantly impacted interim relief practice through virtual hearings and modified urgency standards.

## I. Statutory Framework

### A. Order XXXIX: Temporary Injunctions

**Order XXXIX Rule 1: Cases in which temporary injunctions may be granted**

The court may grant a temporary injunction to restrain the defendant from:
- Committing a breach of contract or other injury (Rule 1(a))
- Removing or disposing of property in dispute (Rule 1(b))
- Removing from local limits of court's jurisdiction any property (Rule 1(c))
- Doing any act in relation to subject matter which would tend to render judgment ineffectual (Rule 1(d))

**Order XXXIX Rule 2: Injunction to restrain repetition or continuance of breach**

Where the defendant threatens to commit a breach of contract or other injury, or is repeatedly committing breach, the plaintiff may be granted an injunction to restrain such action.

**Order XXXIX Rule 3: Before granting injunction, Court to direct notice**

Unless circumstances justify ex parte relief, the court must give notice to the opposite party before granting an injunction. Ex parte injunctions are exceptional and require compelling justification.

**Order XXXIX Rule 4: Order for injunction may be discharged, varied or set aside**

Any order for injunction may be:
- Discharged
- Varied
- Set aside by the court on application of any party dissatisfied

The court may also discharge injunction if the plaintiff fails to institute the suit within the time fixed.

**Key Procedural Rules:**
- Rule 2-A: Security for damages - Court may order plaintiff to furnish security for damages defendant may sustain
- Rule 5: Injunction to stay legal proceedings - Court may grant injunction to stay proceedings before another court in certain circumstances
- Rule 7: Attachment in case of disobedience - Property of defendant may be attached if disobedience to injunction

### B. Order XXXVIII: Attachment Before Judgment

**Order XXXVIII Rule 5: Attachment of property in certain cases**

The court may order attachment before judgment if:
1. The defendant is about to dispose of whole or any part of property, OR
2. With intent to:
   - Obstruct or delay execution of any decree
   - That may be passed against him
3. The defendant has dishonestly disposed of property, OR
4. The defendant is about to remove property from local limits

**Conditions Precedent:**
- Plaintiff must show prima facie case
- Defendant's conduct must demonstrate intent to frustrate decree
- Attachment must be necessary to prevent frustration of decree

**Order XXXVIII Rule 6: Application for attachment and procedure**

Application must be supported by affidavit showing:
- Cause of action and amount claimed
- Property to be attached
- Grounds for believing defendant is about to dispose of/remove property
- That applicant has no other sufficient remedy

Court may:
- Grant attachment immediately if satisfied
- Fix date for hearing the defendant
- Require security from plaintiff for any loss defendant may sustain

### C. Section 94: Supplemental Proceedings

**Section 94(a): Interim Sale**
Court may order interim sale of perishable goods or goods deteriorating in value.

**Section 94(b): Detention, Preservation, Inspection**
Court may order detention, preservation or inspection of property which is subject matter of suit.

**Section 94(c): Appointment of Receiver**
Court may appoint receiver of property:
- In question between parties
- To prevent ends of justice being defeated
- Where receiver already appointed by sovereign
- Where property outside India

**Section 94(d): Interim Custody and Detention**
Court may order interim custody and detention of any property which forms subject matter of suit.

**Section 94(e): Directing Samples**
Court may direct that samples be taken for purposes of evidence or comparison.

### D. Section 151: Inherent Powers

Courts possess inherent powers to:
- Make such orders as necessary for ends of justice
- Prevent abuse of process of court
- Give effect to orders already made

Section 151 supplements specific provisions but cannot override express statutory restrictions.

**2020-2024 Development:**
Supreme Court in *Mohd. Irshad v. State of U.P.* (2020) emphasized Section 151 cannot be used to circumvent specific provisions of CPC or other statutes. Inherent powers are supplementary, not superseding.

## II. Core Principles of Interim Relief

### A. The Three-Tier Test

Indian courts apply a rigorous three-part test for granting temporary injunctions, established in *Dalpat Kumar v. Prahlad Singh* (1992) and consistently applied:

**1. Prima Facie Case**
- Plaintiff must establish a serious question to be tried
- Not required to prove case conclusively at interim stage
- Must show reasonable probability of success
- Balance: Not too high (would make interim relief meaningless), not too low (would trivialize it)

**Standard:** "Arguable case" or "serious question raised" - plaintiff must show they have a real prospect of succeeding at trial.

**Example:** In contract breach cases, plaintiff must show:
- Existence of valid contract
- Breach or threatened breach by defendant
- Specific obligation sought to be enforced

**2. Balance of Convenience**

The court must weigh:
- **Inconvenience to plaintiff** if injunction refused vs.
- **Inconvenience to defendant** if injunction granted

Courts consider:
- Comparative hardship to parties
- Whether damages would be adequate remedy for plaintiff
- Whether plaintiff can compensate defendant if injunction wrongly granted
- Public interest considerations
- Status quo position

**Key Principle:** Injunction should not be granted if monetary compensation would adequately compensate plaintiff for loss.

**Illustration:** *Gujarat Bottling Co. v. Coca Cola Co.* (1995) - Balance tilted in favor of trademark owner (Coca Cola) as damage to goodwill and brand irreparable; monetary compensation inadequate.

**3. Irreparable Injury**

Plaintiff must demonstrate:
- Injury that cannot be adequately compensated in money
- Injury that cannot be measured in monetary terms
- Injury that would render final decree ineffective

**Nature of Irreparable Injury:**
- Loss of goodwill and reputation
- Dispossession from property
- Destruction or disposal of subject matter
- Multiplicity of proceedings
- Injury to unique property (heritage buildings, artworks)

**Not Irreparable:**
- Mere pecuniary loss (unless exceptional)
- Ordinary commercial loss
- Delay in receiving payment

**2020-2024 Trend:** Courts have become more stringent in applying "irreparable injury" test. In *Ramrameshwari Devi v. Nirmala Devi* (2011) reaffirmed in 2021 cases, Supreme Court held irreparable injury must be specific, particularized, and demonstrable - not general or speculative.

### B. Balance of Convenience - Detailed Analysis

**Factors Considered:**

1. **Status Quo:** Which position maintains existing state of affairs?
2. **Conduct of Parties:** Has either party created fait accompli?
3. **Delay:** Has plaintiff delayed unduly before seeking relief?
4. **Clean Hands:** Has plaintiff approached court with clean hands?
5. **Public Interest:** Does grant/refusal affect public at large?
6. **Third Party Rights:** Will injunction affect rights of third parties?
7. **Enforceability:** Can order be effectively enforced?
8. **Comparative Mischief:** Which course causes greater harm?

**Special Situations:**

**Construction/Development Cases:**
- Courts reluctant to stop construction at advanced stage
- But will intervene if illegal construction on public land
- Balance tilts against developer if environmental norms violated

**Labor/Employment Cases:**
- Status quo usually favors employee in service
- But courts consider operational requirements of employer

**Intellectual Property Cases:**
- Strong prima facie case may tilt balance despite other factors
- Public confusion and market dilution given weight

### C. Exceptions to Normal Rules

**1. Prohibitory vs. Mandatory Injunctions**

**Prohibitory:** Restrains defendant from doing something - easier to obtain
**Mandatory:** Compels defendant to perform positive act - higher threshold

**Standard for Mandatory Injunction:**
- Strong prima facie case required
- Balance of convenience must strongly favor plaintiff
- Defendant must have created situation requiring urgent positive action

*Dorab Cawasji Warden v. Coomi Sorab Warden* (1990): Mandatory injunctions are exceptions, granted only in urgent necessity where keeping things in status quo would cause greater hardship.

**2. Ex Parte Injunctions**

Granted without notice to defendant in exceptional circumstances:
- Extreme urgency
- Giving notice would defeat purpose
- Defendant likely to create irreversible situation if alerted

**Procedural Safeguards:**
- Party obtaining ex parte order must make full and frank disclosure
- Must disclose facts favorable to opposite side
- Any suppression/misrepresentation leads to vacation of order
- Ex parte order typically valid for limited period (7-14 days)
- Early hearing to be given to defendant

**Leading Case:** *Kiran Singh v. Chaman Paswan* (1954) - Ex parte orders are extraordinary; court must be satisfied normal procedure would frustrate object of order.

**3. Injunctions Against Government**

**General Rule:** Courts reluctant to grant interim relief against government in policy/administrative matters.

**Exceptions:**
- Clear violation of statutory provisions
- Arbitrary or mala fide action
- Action contrary to constitutional provisions
- Protection of fundamental rights

**Commonwealth of Australia v. John Fairfax & Sons Ltd.** principles adopted in India:
- Public interest in both directions must be considered
- Government's pleading of public interest not automatically decisive
- Court must balance competing public interests

**COVID-19 Context (2020-2022):**
Courts showed extreme reluctance to interfere with executive decisions on lockdowns, vaccine distribution, resource allocation, citing separation of powers and administrative expertise. However, intervened where arbitrary or discriminatory implementation alleged.

### D. Modern Developments (2020-2024)

**1. Virtual Hearings for Urgent Interim Relief**

E-Courts Phase III (2023-2024) has integrated:
- Online filing of interim applications
- Virtual urgent mentioning before duty judges
- Video conferencing for ex parte hearings
- Digital service of injunction orders

**Procedure:** Most High Courts now have dedicated email IDs for urgent mentioning. Applications can be filed electronically with supporting documents and mentioned for same-day/next-day hearing.

**2. Commercial Courts Act 2015 - Time-Bound Orders**

**Section 12-13 Requirements:**
- Commercial Courts must dispose interim applications within 30 days from date of service of notice
- Extension by reasoned order, not exceeding 60 days
- Fast-track procedure for commercial disputes

**Impact:** Significantly reduced time for obtaining/opposing interim relief in commercial matters. Parties cannot indefinitely delay through adjournments.

**3. Stricter Standards for Injunctions Restraining Business/Trade**

Post-2020 trend: Courts applying higher thresholds for orders that:
- Restrain legitimate business activity
- Prevent use of property/assets
- Freeze bank accounts
- Impact employment/livelihoods

**Rationale:** COVID-19 economic impact has made courts more cautious about orders with severe business consequences. Greater emphasis on alternative mechanisms like escrow accounts, conditional permissions.

**4. Environmental and Public Interest Cases**

Courts more willing to grant interim relief in:
- Environmental degradation cases (precautionary principle)
- Heritage conservation (irreplaceable nature of structures)
- Public health emergencies
- Consumer protection matters affecting large populations

**Leading Case:** *M.C. Mehta v. Union of India* (various orders 2020-2024) - Courts continue to exercise proactive jurisdiction in environmental matters, granting interim relief to prevent irreversible harm.

## III. Attachment Before Judgment - Detailed Framework

### A. Statutory Requirements - Order XXXVIII Rule 5

**Four Circumstances for Attachment:**

1. **Defendant about to dispose of property:**
   - Disposal includes sale, gift, mortgage, lease
   - "About to dispose" requires credible evidence of imminent action
   - Mere apprehension insufficient; specific facts needed

2. **Defendant about to remove property from jurisdiction:**
   - Physical removal of movable property
   - Transfer of title to person outside jurisdiction
   - Creating encumbrances to frustrate execution

3. **Intent to obstruct/delay execution:**
   - Subjective element - must prove fraudulent intent
   - Inferred from conduct, surrounding circumstances
   - Pattern of disposing assets, creating sham transactions

4. **Dishonest disposal already made:**
   - Past conduct showing dishonesty
   - Transfers to family members below market value
   - Creation of fictitious debts/liabilities

**Evidence Required:**
- Affidavit with specific allegations and supporting documents
- Cannot be based on "information and belief" alone
- Must disclose details of property to be attached
- Should show prima facie case on merits of suit

### B. Procedural Requirements

**Application Must Contain:**

1. **Cause of action** - Brief statement of grounds of suit
2. **Amount claimed** - Quantification of plaintiff's claim
3. **Description of property** - Specific identification of property sought to be attached
4. **Grounds for attachment** - Detailed facts showing defendant's conduct falls within Rule 5
5. **No other remedy** - Explanation why attachment necessary

**Affidavit Requirements:**
- Deponent must have personal knowledge of facts
- Cannot be vague or based on hearsay
- Must disclose source of information for facts not personally known
- Concealment of material facts can lead to rejection

**Court's Powers:**

1. **Immediate attachment:** If satisfied on ex parte reading
2. **Notice to defendant:** Fix date for hearing defendant's objections
3. **Conditional attachment:** Require plaintiff to furnish security for defendant's loss if attachment wrongly granted
4. **Rejection:** If application prima facie not maintainable

**Time-Bound Disposal:** Commercial Courts Act requires disposal within 30 days (extendable to 60 days maximum) in commercial disputes.

### C. Defendant's Remedies

**Objections to Attachment:**

Defendant may apply under Order XXXVIII Rule 5 to set aside attachment on grounds:
- Plaintiff failed to establish prima facie case
- No evidence of conduct justifying attachment
- Property wrongly described or attached
- Plaintiff made material misrepresentation/suppression

**Furnishing Security:**

Under Order XXXVIII Rule 5 proviso, defendant may obtain release of attached property by:
- Furnishing security to satisfaction of court
- Security amount = estimated value of property or plaintiff's claim, whichever lower
- Security can be cash deposit, bank guarantee, surety bond

**Damages for Wrongful Attachment:**

If attachment set aside as wrongly obtained, defendant entitled to:
- Compensation for loss from wrongful attachment
- Costs of application for setting aside
- Damages if plaintiff acted mala fide

**Leading Case:** *Raman Tech. & Process Engg. Co. v. Solanki Traders* (2008) - Wrongful attachment causing business disruption entitles defendant to compensation including loss of reputation, business opportunities.

### D. Modern Practice Issues (2020-2024)

**1. Attachment of Intangible Property**

Courts have expanded attachment to include:
- Bank accounts (by garnishee order to bank)
- Intellectual property rights (trademarks, copyrights, patents)
- Shares and securities (through depositories)
- Digital assets (cryptocurrencies - evolving area)

**Cryptocurrency Attachment:**
Courts have in some cases ordered:
- Disclosure of crypto wallet addresses
- Restraint on transfer of cryptocurrencies
- Cooperation from exchanges to freeze accounts

**Challenge:** Decentralized nature of crypto makes enforcement difficult. Courts increasingly requiring plaintiffs to show ability to trace and identify assets.

**2. Pre-Institution Attachment**

**Question:** Can attachment be ordered before suit is filed?

**Answer:** Generally no. Order XXXVIII applies to pending suits. However:

- **Exception:** If plaintiff shows extreme urgency and undertakes to file suit within stipulated time (typically 7-15 days), some courts have exercised inherent powers to grant temporary protection
- **Commercial Courts:** More liberal in exceptional cases to prevent dissipation
- **Condition:** Plaintiff must file suit within time, otherwise order automatically vacates

**3. Attachment in Arbitration Matters**

**Arbitration and Conciliation Act 1996, Section 9:** Parties can approach court for interim measures including attachment before/during arbitration.

**Section 17:** Arbitral tribunal itself can order interim measures, including directing parties not to dispose of property.

**Enforcement:** Court attachment orders binding. Arbitral tribunal orders enforceable as court orders under Section 17(2) (as amended 2015).

**Recent Trend (2020-2024):** Courts granting Section 9 attachment applications liberally where:
- Arbitration seated in India
- Clear prima facie case of intent to dissipate assets
- Subject matter located within jurisdiction

## IV. Appointment of Receiver - Section 94(e) and Order XL

### A. When Court Appoints Receiver

**Circumstances (Order XL Rule 1):**

1. **Property in dispute between parties** - Ownership/title contested
2. **Prevent ends of justice being defeated** - Risk of destruction, waste, mismanagement
3. **Receiver already appointed** - By sovereign authority or other competent court
4. **Property outside India** - Where property situated outside territorial jurisdiction

**Discretionary Power:** Appointment of receiver is discretionary equitable relief. Court considers:
- Protection of property interests
- Preservation of status quo
- Inability of parties to manage property cooperatively
- Risk of deterioration, dissipation or misappropriation

**Leading Case:** *Kerr on Receivers* principle adopted in India: "Receiver is hand of court, not agent or servant of any party. Appointed to preserve property for benefit of whoever ultimately entitled."

### B. Receiver's Powers and Duties

**Powers (Order XL Rule 1-2):**
- Take possession of property
- Collect rents, profits, income
- Realize debts due to property
- Carry on business (if authorized)
- Incur necessary expenses
- File accounts with court

**Duties:**
- Act impartially between parties
- Preserve property in good condition
- Not deal with property except under court orders
- Maintain proper accounts
- File periodic reports to court
- Obtain court sanction for major decisions

**Remuneration:** Order XL Rule 1 - Receiver entitled to remuneration fixed by court, payable from property under receivership.

### C. Types of Receivers

**1. Receiver Pendente Lite**
- Appointed during pendency of suit
- Temporary arrangement until final decree
- Most common form

**2. Receiver Ad Litem**
- Appointed to represent person under disability (minor, unsound mind)
- Prosecutes/defends suit on their behalf

**3. Receiver Post-Decree**
- Appointed after judgment for purpose of executing decree
- Example: Receiver appointed to manage and sell business of judgment debtor

### D. Modern Applications (2020-2024)

**Corporate Disputes:**
- Receiver appointed for deadlocked companies
- Management of business during shareholder disputes
- Protection of assets during insolvency proceedings (though now IBC moratorium limits this)

**IBC Impact:**
Once insolvency resolution process initiated under Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code 2016:
- Moratorium prevents appointment of receiver (Section 14)
- Interim Resolution Professional/Resolution Professional manages property
- Civil court receiver applications typically rejected in IBC cases

**Intellectual Property:**
- Receiver appointed to manage trademark/brand during pendency
- Accountant appointed to monitor sales and maintain accounts
- Particularly in pharmaceutical patent disputes

**Real Estate Projects:**
- Courts increasingly appointing retired judges as receivers for stalled projects
- Receiver completes construction using deposited funds
- Protects homebuyer interests

**Example:** *Lucknow Development Authority v. M.K. Gupta* (1994) affirmed practice of appointing receiver for public interest in stalled development projects.

## V. Application Procedures

### A. Filing Application for Temporary Injunction

**Step 1: Drafting Application**

**Format:** Application under Order XXXIX Rules 1 & 2 read with Section 151 CPC

**Contents:**
1. **Title** - "Application for grant of temporary injunction"
2. **Parties** - Same as main suit
3. **Grounds** - Detailed facts showing:
   - Breach or threatened breach
   - Prima facie case
   - Balance of convenience
   - Irreparable injury
4. **Prayer** - Specific relief sought (ex parte or on notice)
5. **Annexures** - Supporting documents (contract, correspondence, photographs, etc.)

**Affidavit:** Must accompany application verifying facts stated

**Step 2: Filing and Court Fee**

- File application along with main suit or during its pendency
- Court fee as per state rules (usually ad valorem on amount claimed or subject matter value)
- Commercial disputes: Follow Commercial Courts Act procedures

**Step 3: Mentioning for Urgent Hearing**

- If ex parte relief sought, mention before duty judge
- In Commercial Courts, mention for hearing within 7 days
- Regular suits: Listed according to ordinary course

**Step 4: Hearing**

**Ex Parte Hearing:**
- Counsel presents case
- Court may ask questions
- Order passed same day if satisfied

**Hearing on Notice:**
- Opposite party served with notice
- Respondent files reply/objection
- Both sides heard
- Order passed with reasons

**Step 5: Complying with Conditions**

If court grants injunction subject to:
- Furnishing security - deposit amount or bank guarantee
- Undertaking as to damages - furnish undertaking to compensate if injunction wrongly granted
- Filing suit within time - if pre-institution relief, file suit within stipulated days

**Step 6: Service and Compliance**

- Obtain certified copy of order
- Serve on opposite party (through process server or registered post)
- Ensure compliance by parties
- If disobeyed, file application under Order XXXIX Rule 7 for attachment

### B. Opposing Injunction Application

**Step 1: Receiving Notice**

- Note date of hearing
- Obtain copy of application and affidavit
- Analyze grounds stated by plaintiff

**Step 2: Drafting Reply/Objection**

**Contents:**
1. **Preliminary objections** - Procedural defects, suppression of facts
2. **On merits:**
   - No prima facie case
   - Balance of convenience favors defendant
   - No irreparable injury to plaintiff
   - Delay and acquiescence by plaintiff
3. **Counter-affidavit** - Contradicting factual allegations

**Step 3: Filing Reply**

- File before hearing date
- Serve copy on plaintiff
- Commercial Courts: Reply within 7 days of notice

**Step 4: Arguments at Hearing**

**Key Points:**
- Challenge plaintiff's prima facie case
- Highlight adequacy of damages as remedy
- Show greater hardship to defendant if injunction granted
- Point out delay, acquiescence, unclean hands
- Offer undertaking if that balances equities

**Step 5: If Injunction Granted**

**Options:**
1. **Comply** with order
2. **Apply for vacation/modification** under Order XXXIX Rule 4
3. **Furnish security** if permitted
4. **Appeal** if order manifestly unjust (rare at interim stage)

### C. Attachment Before Judgment - Procedure

**Step 1: Drafting Application**

**Format:** Application under Order XXXVIII Rule 5

**Contents:**
1. **Cause of action** - Brief statement
2. **Amount claimed**
3. **Property details** - Specific description with location, boundaries
4. **Grounds** - Detailed facts showing defendant about to dispose/remove property or has dishonestly disposed
5. **No other remedy** - Why attachment necessary
6. **Prayer** - Order for attachment

**Affidavit Requirements:** Strict - must have specific facts, not conclusions

**Step 2: Filing and Mentioning**

- File with main suit or during pendency
- If urgent, mention for immediate orders
- Court may grant ex parte attachment or fix date for hearing

**Step 3: Execution of Attachment Order**

**Immovable Property:**
- Court issues proclamation of attachment
- Proclamation affixed on property and court notice board
- Entry made in Collector's office (if registered property)
- Effect: Property cannot be transferred; any transfer void

**Movable Property:**
- Attached by actual seizure by Court officer
- Or by appointment of receiver
- Or by order on person in possession not to deliver

**Bank Accounts:**
- Garnishee notice sent to bank
- Bank freezes account to extent of attachment amount

**Step 4: Defendant's Options**

- File objections under Rule 5
- Furnish security for release
- File suit application to set aside
- Move for damages if wrongful attachment

**Step 5: Continuation During Suit**

- Attachment continues until:
  - Final decree passed and satisfied
  - Order set aside
  - Defendant furnishes security
  - Suit dismissed

### D. Appointment of Receiver - Procedure

**Step 1: Application**

**Format:** Application under Section 94(e) CPC read with Order XL

**Contents:**
1. **Nature of property** - Detailed description
2. **Dispute between parties**
3. **Grounds** - Why receiver necessary (risk of waste, mismanagement, inability to cooperate)
4. **Suggested receiver** - Name and qualifications (often retired judicial officer, chartered accountant)
5. **Terms of appointment** - Powers sought, remuneration

**Step 2: Court's Consideration**

Court examines:
- Prima facie case on merits
- Risk to property if receiver not appointed
- Ability of parties to cooperate
- Suitability of proposed receiver

**Step 3: Order of Appointment**

Order specifies:
- Receiver's name
- Property under receivership
- Powers and duties
- Remuneration
- Period of appointment
- Security (if any) to be furnished by receiver

**Step 4: Receiver's Assumption of Charge**

- Receiver files undertaking in court
- Takes physical possession with police assistance if needed
- Opens separate bank account for receivership
- Maintains detailed records

**Step 5: Periodic Reporting**

- File accounts every 6 months (or as directed)
- Obtain court approval for major decisions
- Parties may inspect accounts
- Court reviews receiver's functioning

**Step 6: Discharge**

- After final decree or settlement
- Receiver files final accounts
- Court passes discharge order
- Remuneration and expenses paid from property

## VI. Judicial Interpretation and Leading Cases

### A. Supreme Court Landmark Cases

**1. Dalpat Kumar v. Prahlad Singh (1992) 1 SCC 719**

**Facts:** Land dispute; plaintiff sought injunction to restrain construction by defendant.

**Held:** Court laid down definitive three-tier test:
1. Prima facie case
2. Balance of convenience
3. Irreparable injury

All three must be cumulatively satisfied. No mathematical formula; court exercises discretion judiciously.

**Key Quote:** "Grant of temporary injunction is discretionary and equitable remedy. Court weighs comparative mischief and decides which course would cause greater harm."

**Impact:** This case is cited in virtually every injunction application across India. Establishes foundational framework.

**2. Gujarat Bottling Co. Ltd. v. Coca Cola Co. (1995) 5 SCC 545**

**Facts:** Coca Cola terminated bottling agreement. Gujarat Bottling sought injunction against trademark enforcement.

**Held:** Balance of convenience tilts in favor of trademark owner. Damage to brand and goodwill irreparable. Monetary compensation inadequate for loss of intangible property rights.

**Principle:** Intellectual property cases require careful balance. Strong prima facie case on IP rights often decisive.

**3. Dorab Cawasji Warden v. Coomi Sorab Warden (1990) 2 SCC 117**

**Facts:** Dispute over tenancy; mandatory injunction sought to restore possession.

**Held:** Mandatory injunction requires stronger case than prohibitory. Court must be satisfied:
- Prima facie case is very strong
- Defendant created situation requiring positive action
- Balance of convenience clearly favors plaintiff
- Consequences of refusal more serious than granting

**Principle:** Mandatory injunctions are exceptions; higher threshold justified by intrusive nature.

**4. Seema Arshad Zaheer v. Municipal Corporation of Greater Mumbai (2006) 5 SCC 282**

**Facts:** Illegal construction; municipal corporation sought to demolish. Builder sought injunction.

**Held:** No injunction against public authority exercising statutory duty in public interest. Court cannot facilitate continuance of illegality.

**Principle:** Public interest trumps private interest. Courts will not grant injunctions enabling violation of law.

**5. Kiran Singh v. Chaman Paswan (1954) SCR 117**

**Facts:** Ex parte injunction granted; defendant moved to set aside.

**Held:** Ex parte orders require extraordinary circumstances. Party obtaining must:
- Make full and frank disclosure
- Disclose facts favorable to opposite side
- Show giving notice would defeat purpose

Any suppression = automatic vacation of order.

**Principle:** Uberrimae fidei (utmost good faith) required for ex parte relief.

### B. High Court Developments (2020-2024)

**1. Digital Evidence in Injunction Applications**

**Delhi High Court - Christian Louboutin v. Nakul Bajaj (2018) continued application in 2020-2024:**

Courts accepting digital evidence for injunctions:
- Screenshots of websites selling counterfeit goods
- Social media posts evidencing breach/infringement
- WhatsApp/email exchanges
- Online marketplace listings

**Requirement:** Section 65B certificate not mandated at interim stage, but advisable for credibility.

**2. Injunctions in Cryptocurrency Disputes**

**Karnataka High Court - Various unreported orders 2021-2023:**

Courts granted injunctions:
- Restraining unauthorized trading in investor's crypto account
- Freezing exchange wallets pending fraud investigation
- Requiring disclosure of wallet addresses

**Challenge:** Enforcement across borders and on decentralized platforms remains difficult.

**3. Virtual Hearing Protocols**

**Supreme Court E-Committee Guidelines 2020-2024:**

Interim applications heard through video conferencing:
- Advance uploading of documents on portal
- Oral arguments via VC platform
- Orders uploaded digitally on same day
- Digital service of orders to parties

**Impact:** Significantly reduced time for urgent interim relief. Parties across India can appear without travel.

**4. Stricter Standards Post-COVID**

**Bombay High Court - Multiple 2021-2023 decisions:**

Higher threshold for injunctions affecting business/employment:
- Courts requiring stronger prima facie case
- More emphasis on adequacy of damages as remedy
- Reluctance to freeze accounts or stop business operations
- Preference for conditional orders (escrow, limited permissions)

**Rationale:** Economic hardship from pandemic necessitates balancing business survival with rights protection.

## VII. Practice Guidelines

### A. For Plaintiffs/Applicants

**1. Preparation Phase**

- **Document Everything:** Collect all evidence of breach/threatened breach
  - Contracts, agreements, correspondence
  - Photographs, videos (especially for property disputes)
  - Financial records (for disposal of assets cases)
  - Digital evidence with proper authentication

- **Act Swiftly:** Delay weakens case. Acquiescence may defeat injunction.
  - File within reasonable time of knowledge of breach
  - Explain any delay convincingly

- **Quantify Loss:** Calculate and articulate irreparable injury
  - Cannot be vague ("will suffer loss")
  - Specify nature and extent of harm

**2. Drafting Application**

- **Be Specific:** Vague allegations rejected
  - Name exact property/asset
  - State precise nature of breach/threatened breach
  - Identify specific acts to be restrained

- **Full Disclosure:** Concealment fatal to ex parte applications
  - Disclose adverse facts
  - Inform court of defendant's likely defenses
  - Reveal any delay or acquiescence

- **Legal Research:** Cite relevant precedents
  - Binding judgments from Supreme Court/High Court
  - Similar fact situations
  - Recent developments

**3. At Hearing**

- **Clarity:** Present case succinctly
  - Highlight three-tier test satisfaction
  - Emphasize urgency if ex parte
  - Offer undertakings if needed

- **Flexibility:** Be open to conditional orders
  - Security for damages
  - Limited restraint instead of absolute
  - Undertaking as to damages

**4. Post-Grant**

- **Prompt Service:** Serve order immediately on defendant
  - Maintain proof of service
  - If defendant abroad, follow international service rules

- **Compliance Monitoring:** Ensure defendant complies
  - Document violations
  - File contempt/attachment application if breach

- **Progress Main Suit:** Injunction pendente lite only
  - Actively prosecute main suit
  - Delayed suits may lead to discharge of injunction

### B. For Defendants/Respondents

**1. Upon Receiving Notice/Order**

- **Read Carefully:** Understand exact scope of restraint
  - What actions prohibited
  - What actions permitted
  - Duration of order

- **Comply Immediately:** Non-compliance = contempt
  - Even if order perceived as wrong
  - Challenge through legal means, but comply meanwhile

- **Seek Legal Advice:** Consult counsel promptly
  - Assess prospects of setting aside
  - Evaluate furnishing security option

**2. Opposing at Hearing**

- **Challenge on Facts:** Contest plaintiff's allegations
  - File counter-affidavit with contradictory evidence
  - Show suppression/misrepresentation by plaintiff

- **Challenge on Law:** Attack legal grounds
  - No prima facie case
  - Balance favors defendant
  - Damages adequate remedy

- **Offer Alternatives:** Propose middle ground
  - Undertaking not to alienate property
  - Maintaining accounts of transactions
  - Limited operations with safeguards

**3. Moving for Vacation/Modification**

**Grounds for Vacation (Order XXXIX Rule 4):**
- Change in circumstances
- Plaintiff not prosecuting suit diligently
- Interim order based on misrepresentation
- Balance of convenience shifted
- Order causing disproportionate hardship

**Procedure:**
- File application with affidavit
- Serve on plaintiff
- Hearing on notice
- Court exercises discretion

**4. Claiming Damages**

If injunction wrongly granted and later set aside:
- File application for damages under Order XXXIX Rule 2-A or plaintiff's undertaking
- Prove actual loss suffered
- Quantify damages with evidence

### C. For Courts (Best Practices from Judicial Training Programs 2020-2024)

**1. Initial Scrutiny**

- Check compliance with procedural requirements
- Verify court fee payment
- Ensure proper affidavit filed

**2. Applying Three-Tier Test**

- Analyze prima facie case objectively (not conclusive adjudication)
- Weigh balance realistically (not mechanically)
- Scrutinize irreparable injury claim (specific vs. general)

**3. Ex Parte Orders**

- Grant only in exceptional urgency
- Record reasons for dispensing with notice
- Fix early date (within 7-15 days) for hearing defendant
- Question applicant on full disclosure

**4. Conditioning Relief**

- Consider security for damages
- Require undertaking as to damages
- Grant limited restraint if absolute unnecessary
- Build in review mechanism

**5. Time-Bound Disposal**

- Commercial Courts: 30-day mandate
- Other courts: Aim for 2-3 months
- Avoid repeated adjournments
- List for final arguments promptly

**6. Reasoned Orders**

- Briefly state facts
- Apply three-tier test explicitly
- Record reasons for grant/refusal
- Specify exact relief granted

**7. Monitoring Compliance**

- Ensure orders served
- Check compliance at next hearing
- Take action on breach promptly

## VIII. Modern Developments and Future Trends

### A. Technology Integration (2020-2024)

**1. E-Filing and Digital Service**

- **Current State:** All High Courts, most District Courts have e-filing for interim applications
- **Advantages:**
  - Instant filing 24/7
  - Reduced pendency at filing counter
  - Digital record maintenance
  - Easier access to orders/judgments
- **Challenges:**
  - Digital divide for litigants in rural areas
  - Technical glitches during filing
  - Server downtime

**2. Virtual Urgent Mentioning**

- **Practice:** Email urgent mentioning to duty judges
- **Procedure:**
  - Email brief note with nature of urgency
  - Attach application and affidavit
  - Duty judge reviews and lists for VC hearing if satisfied
- **Timeframe:** Same day or next working day hearing possible
- **Impact:** Access to justice improved; geographical barriers reduced

**3. AI and Predictive Analysis**

- **Emerging Tool:** AI analysis of past judgments to predict injunction grant/refusal
- **Usage:** Some law firms using AI to:
  - Identify relevant precedents
  - Draft applications
  - Assess strength of case
- **Limitation:** AI cannot replace judicial discretion in equity

**4. Blockchain for Injunction Enforcement**

- **Proposal:** Use blockchain to record injunction orders
- **Benefit:** Immutable record; parties cannot claim non-awareness
- **Status:** Experimental; not yet mainstream

### B. Harmonization with International Practices

**1. UNCITRAL Model Law Influence**

- **Section 9 & 17 Arbitration Act:** Align with Model Law on interim measures
- **Recognition:** Indian courts recognize foreign interim measures in arbitration

**2. Hague Convention on Civil Procedure**

- **Relevance:** Service of injunction orders on parties abroad
- **India Status:** Not signatory, but follows principles for international comity

**3. Cross-Border Injunctions**

- **Anti-Suit Injunctions:** Indian courts grant injunctions restraining proceedings abroad in certain cases
  - Breach of arbitration agreement
  - Forum shopping
  - Oppressive foreign proceedings
- **Limitation:** Enforcement depends on foreign court's respect for comity

### C. Emerging Issues and Challenges

**1. Injunctions in Cryptocurrency Disputes**

**Current State:**
- Courts granting restraint orders on crypto exchanges
- Difficulty enforcing against decentralized protocols
- Cross-border enforcement nearly impossible

**Need:** Legislative clarity on crypto asset attachment/injunction procedures

**2. Injunctions Against AI/Automated Systems**

**Emerging Questions:**
- Can injunction be granted against AI system's automated actions?
- Who is proper party - AI owner, developer, user?
- How to frame injunction against algorithm?

**Example:** Injunction against algorithm that automatically reposts defamatory content - how to enforce?

**3. Data Protection and Injunctions**

**Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023:**
- New rights to data principals (users)
- Can injunction restrain data processing?
- Balance between data protection and legitimate business interests

**Likely Development:** Specialized procedures for data-related injunctions

**4. Climate Change and Environmental Injunctions**

**Trend:** Increasing injunctions to:
- Halt projects impacting environment
- Mandate environmental clearances
- Prevent pollution activities

**Principle:** Precautionary principle - when in doubt, restrain activity pending full environmental assessment

**Leading Case:** *Vellore Citizens' Welfare Forum v. Union of India* (1996) continues to guide 2020-2024 climate litigation

**5. Injunctions in Insolvency Proceedings**

**IBC Tension:**
- IBC Section 14 moratorium prevents suits/proceedings against corporate debtor
- Does this bar injunctions by creditors?

**Current Position:**
- Generally yes - moratorium overrides civil court injunctions
- Exception: NCLT can grant interim relief under IBC framework

**Future:** Likely further amendments to harmonize IBC and CPC interim relief provisions

## IX. Comparative Analysis - Interim Relief in Other Jurisdictions

### A. England and Wales - CPR Part 25

**Test for Injunctions:**
- **American Cyanamid** test (1975):
  1. Serious question to be tried
  2. Damages not adequate remedy
  3. Balance of convenience

**Similarities to India:** Three-tier structure similar to Dalpat Kumar

**Differences:**
- English courts more willing to grant injunctions restraining business
- "Adequacy of damages" more rigorously applied in England
- English courts have freezing orders (Mareva injunctions) and search orders (Anton Piller) - not explicitly available in India

### B. United States - Federal Rules of Civil Procedure Rule 65

**Test for Preliminary Injunction:**
1. Likelihood of success on merits
2. Irreparable harm
3. Balance of equities
4. Public interest

**Four-Factor Test:** More structured than Indian three-tier test

**Key Difference:** US courts require higher "likelihood of success" than Indian "prima facie case"

**Bond Requirement:** FRCP 65(c) mandates security; India discretionary under Order XXXIX Rule 2-A

### C. Australia - Federal Court Rules

**Test:**
- Similar to Indian three-tier approach
- Emphasis on "serious question"
- Balance of convenience critical

**Unique Feature:** Australian courts more flexible with interlocutory mandatory injunctions than Indian courts

### D. Singapore - Rules of Court

**Approach:**
- Follows English American Cyanamid test
- Very business-friendly; quick disposal of injunction applications
- Strong enforcement mechanisms

**Lesson for India:** Singapore's efficiency in disposal (typically 2-4 weeks) can be model for Commercial Courts

## X. Practical Checklists

### A. Pre-Filing Checklist - Temporary Injunction

**1. Case Assessment**
- [ ] Prima facie case demonstrable?
- [ ] Evidence of breach/threatened breach?
- [ ] Irreparable injury specific and quantifiable?
- [ ] Damages inadequate as remedy?
- [ ] Balance favors plaintiff?

**2. Documentation**
- [ ] Original contract/agreement
- [ ] Correspondence showing breach/threat
- [ ] Photographs/videos if relevant
- [ ] Financial records if property disposal alleged
- [ ] Expert opinion if technical issue

**3. Procedural Requirements**
- [ ] Application drafted (Order XXXIX Rules 1-2 + Section 151)
- [ ] Affidavit verifying facts
- [ ] Prayer clearly specifying relief
- [ ] Court fee calculated and paid
- [ ] Copies for all parties + court

**4. Legal Research**
- [ ] Relevant Supreme Court precedents identified
- [ ] Applicable High Court judgments
- [ ] Statutory provisions cited

**5. Strategic Decisions**
- [ ] Ex parte or on notice?
- [ ] Willing to furnish security?
- [ ] Undertaking as to damages?
- [ ] Alternative/conditional relief if absolute denied?

### B. Post-Grant Checklist - Temporary Injunction

**1. Immediate Actions**
- [ ] Obtain certified copy of order
- [ ] Serve on defendant (maintain proof)
- [ ] If property-related, ensure physical compliance

**2. Compliance Monitoring**
- [ ] Document defendant's actions
- [ ] If breach, collect evidence
- [ ] Prepare contempt/attachment application if needed

**3. Main Suit Progress**
- [ ] File pleadings promptly
- [ ] Respond to defendant's reply
- [ ] Prosecute suit actively
- [ ] Avoid adjournments

**4. Review and Modification**
- [ ] Monitor changed circumstances
- [ ] If conditions change, seek modification
- [ ] If defendant seeks vacation, prepare opposition

### C. Opposing Injunction Checklist - For Defendants

**1. Initial Response**
- [ ] Read order carefully - understand exact restraint
- [ ] Comply immediately
- [ ] Consult counsel within 24-48 hours
- [ ] Assess vacation/modification prospects

**2. Drafting Reply/Objection**
- [ ] Identify procedural defects in application
- [ ] Challenge prima facie case with evidence
- [ ] Show balance favors defendant
- [ ] Prove damages adequate remedy
- [ ] Highlight delay/acquiescence
- [ ] File counter-affidavit

**3. Alternative Strategies**
- [ ] Consider furnishing security for release
- [ ] Offer undertakings
- [ ] Propose modified/conditional relief
- [ ] Prepare for appeal if necessary

**4. Long-Term**
- [ ] Defend main suit vigorously
- [ ] If injunction vacated/suit dismissed, claim damages
- [ ] Document losses from injunction for damage claim

### D. Attachment Before Judgment Checklist

**1. For Plaintiff**
- [ ] Prima facie case on merits
- [ ] Evidence defendant about to dispose/remove property
- [ ] Specific description of property
- [ ] Affidavit with specific facts (not conclusions)
- [ ] Demonstrate no other adequate remedy

**2. Execution of Attachment**
- [ ] Immovable: Proclamation, collector's office entry
- [ ] Movable: Seizure or receiver appointment
- [ ] Bank accounts: Garnishee notice to bank

**3. For Defendant**
- [ ] File objections under Rule 5
- [ ] Challenge grounds for attachment
- [ ] Show plaintiff no prima facie case
- [ ] Consider furnishing security for release
- [ ] Prepare damage claim if wrongful attachment

## XI. Integration with Overall Civil Litigation Strategy

### A. Interim Relief as Part of Litigation Strategy

**Phase 1: Pre-Litigation**
- Assess whether interim relief needed
- Identify property/assets at risk
- Collect evidence for injunction/attachment
- Consider settlement before filing suit

**Phase 2: Filing and Interim Relief**
- File suit and interim application simultaneously (if urgent)
- Or file interim application shortly after suit
- Coordinate plaint and interim application narratives

**Phase 3: Interim Proceedings**
- Prosecute interim application
- Defend main suit not neglected
- Balance resources between interim and main case

**Phase 4: Post-Interim Order**
- If granted: Monitor compliance, progress main suit
- If refused: Focus on main suit merits
- Consider impact on settlement negotiations

**Phase 5: Trial and Decree**
- Interim order continues until final decree
- Final relief may be broader/different from interim
- Discharge interim order after final decree satisfaction

### B. Settlement and ADR Considerations

**Interim Relief as Settlement Leverage:**
- Injunction granted = plaintiff has leverage
- Attachment ordered = defendant pressured to settle
- Use interim stage for mediation discussions

**Mediating Interim Disputes:**
- Commercial Courts encourage mediation of interim applications
- Parties can agree to:
  - Modified restraints
  - Security arrangements
  - Status quo pending mediation

**Consent Orders:**
- Parties can file consent terms for interim relief
- Court passes order by consent
- Saves time and costs of contested hearing

### C. Cost-Benefit Analysis

**Costs of Seeking Interim Relief:**
- Court fees
- Advocate fees (often substantial for urgent applications)
- Time and management attention
- Risk of adverse order on costs
- Security/undertaking costs if required

**Benefits:**
- Preserves subject matter
- Maintains status quo
- Prevents irreparable harm
- Provides leverage for settlement
- Demonstrates seriousness to defendant

**Strategic Decision:** Weigh costs vs. benefits. Not every case requires interim relief.

**Alternative:** Sometimes undertakings by defendant (even informal) may suffice without court involvement.

## XIII. Summary and Key Takeaways

**Core Framework:**
1. Interim relief governed by Orders XXXVIII-XXXIX, Section 94 CPC
2. Three-tier test: Prima facie case + Balance of convenience + Irreparable injury
3. Discretionary equitable remedy - no automatic entitlement

**Types of Interim Relief:**
- Temporary injunctions (prohibitory and mandatory)
- Attachment before judgment
- Appointment of receiver
- Other Section 94 measures (interim sale, detention, inspection)

**Procedural Essentials:**
- File with affidavit supporting facts
- Comply with notice requirements (ex parte exceptional)
- Time-bound disposal in Commercial Courts (30 days)
- Order subject to modification/vacation during suit pendency

**Modern Trends (2020-2024):**
- Virtual hearings for urgent interim applications
- E-filing and digital service
- Stricter standards for business-impacting injunctions
- IBC moratorium limiting civil court interim relief
- Emerging issues: Crypto assets, AI systems, data protection

**Practice Wisdom:**
- **For Plaintiffs:** Act swiftly, document thoroughly, full disclosure, offer undertakings
- **For Defendants:** Comply immediately, challenge on merits, offer alternatives, monitor for vacation grounds
- **For Courts:** Balance competing interests, record reasons, time-bound disposal, monitor compliance

**Strategic Integration:**
- Interim relief part of overall litigation strategy
- Consider settlement leverage
- Cost-benefit analysis essential
- Coordinate with main suit prosecution

## Conclusion

Interim relief under the Code of Civil Procedure represents a delicate balance between protecting rights during litigation pendency and avoiding irreversible harm to defendants. The three-tier test of prima facie case, balance of convenience, and irreparable injury provides a structured yet flexible framework for courts to exercise equitable discretion.

The 2020-2024 period has witnessed significant evolution through:
- Virtual hearings expanding access to urgent relief
- Commercial Courts enforcing time-bound disposal
- Stricter standards for business-impacting orders post-COVID
- Emerging challenges in crypto, AI, and data protection

Effective use of interim relief requires:
- Swift action and thorough documentation
- Full and frank disclosure (especially for ex parte relief)
- Strategic integration with overall litigation approach
- Realistic assessment of costs vs. benefits

As Indian civil procedure continues modernizing through E-Courts Phase III and legislative reforms (including proposed CPC Amendment 2024), interim relief procedures will further streamline while maintaining the fundamental principle: equity favors those who act promptly, honestly, and with clean hands.
