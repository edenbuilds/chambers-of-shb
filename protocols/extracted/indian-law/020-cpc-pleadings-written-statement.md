# Protocol: Pleadings and Written Statements

**Protocol ID**: IL-CPC-002
**Domain**: Indian Civil Law - Civil Procedure
**Subdomain**: Pleadings, Written Statements, Amendment
**Classification**: Core Procedural Framework
**Version**: 1.0
**Last Updated**: 2024-12-04
**Governs**: Rules for drafting, filing, and amending pleadings in civil suits under Code of Civil Procedure, 1908

---

## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive coverage of pleadings in civil litigation under the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908, focusing on plaint requirements (Order VI), written statement rules (Order VIII), rejection of plaint (Order VII Rule 11), amendment of pleadings (Order VI Rule 17), and modern pleading practices. It serves as an essential reference for advocates, litigants, and judges in ensuring proper framing of issues and efficient case management.

---

## Scope of Application

### When This Protocol Applies

This protocol applies to ALL civil suits requiring written pleadings:
- **Plaint** (plaintiff's initial pleading) - Order VI
- **Written Statement** (defendant's response) - Order VIII
- **Amended pleadings** - Order VI Rule 17
- **Rejection/return of plaint** - Order VII Rule 11
- **Set-off and counterclaim** - Order VIII Rules 6-6G
- **Additional parties** - Order I Rules 10, 10A

### Jurisdictional Boundaries

**Primary Framework**: Code of Civil Procedure, 1908
- Order VI: Pleadings generally
- Order VII: Plaint
- Order VIII: Written Statement, Set-off, Counterclaim

**Modified Procedures**:
- **Commercial Courts Act, 2015**: Stricter timelines, prohibited amendments
- **Summary Suits**: Order XXXVII (trade debts, negotiable instruments - different pleading rules)

### Exclusions

Different/simplified pleading rules apply to:
- **Small Causes Courts**: Summary procedure (simplified pleadings)
- **Arbitration**: Statement of claim/defense (Arbitration Act, not CPC Orders)
- **Writ Petitions**: Petition format (Constitution, not CPC)
- **Consumer Disputes**: Complaint format (Consumer Protection Act)

## Statutory Framework

### Order VI - Pleadings Generally

**"Pleadings" Defined** (Rule 1):
- Plaint
- Written statement
- Does NOT include: Applications, affidavits, replies to interrogatories

**Purpose of Pleadings**:
- Define issues between parties (narrow disputed points)
- Give notice to opposite party of case to meet
- Provide framework for evidence

#### Rule 2 - Contents of Plaint

**Mandatory Contents** (Rule 2):

**(a) Name of Court**:
- "In the Court of [District Judge / Civil Judge]"

**(b) Name, Description, Residence of Parties**:
- Full legal name
- Description: Age (if relevant), occupation, relationship
- Complete address (for service)

**(c) Facts Constituting Cause of Action**:
- **Material facts** (not evidence)
- Chronological order
- When cause of action arose

**(d) Facts Showing Jurisdiction**:
- Why this court has jurisdiction (territorial, pecuniary)
- Defendant's residence, cause of action location

**(e) Relief Claimed**:
- Specific, clear
- Can claim alternative/additional relief

**(f) Valuation**:
- For court fees
- For jurisdiction
- Calculation explained (if not straightforward)

**(g) Verification**:
- Affirm truth of contents
- Distinguish: Own knowledge vs. belief vs. legal advice

#### Rule 3 - What Pleadings Must Not Contain

Pleadings shall NOT contain:
- **Prolixity** (unnecessary length, repetition)
- **Scandalous matter** (irrelevant defamatory allegations)
- **Irrelevant matter** (not material to issues)

Court may **strike out** such matter (Order VI Rule 16)

#### Rule 4 - Condition Precedent

If any condition precedent (statutory notice, demand, etc.) required before filing suit:
- Must specifically **plead** that condition fulfilled
- Example: "Plaintiff issued legal notice dated [Date], received by Defendant on [Date] (copy attached as Exhibit A)"

**Failure to Plead**:
- Suit may be dismissed (no cause of action without fulfilling condition precedent)

#### Rule 15 - Verific

ation of Pleadings

**All Pleadings Must Be Verified**:
- Affirm: Contents true to **knowledge** OR **information and belief**
- Sign with own hand (or authorized representative if party cannot sign)
- False verification: Perjury (IPC Section 191)

**Form of Verification**:
> "I, [Name], [Son/Daughter/Wife of] [Father's Name], [Age], [Occupation], residing at [Address], do hereby verify that the contents of paragraphs [X to Y] are true to my knowledge and the contents of paragraphs [Z to Z1] are based on information received which I believe to be true. No part of the plaint has been concealed or suppressed."

**Knowledge vs. Belief**:
- Own knowledge: Facts personally observed/done (e.g., "I signed contract on [Date]")
- Belief: Facts told by others/documents seen (e.g., "Defendant breached contract as informed by my manager")

**Corporate Parties**:
- Verification by authorized person (director, officer with knowledge)
- Must state: Authorized to verify on behalf of company

#### Rule 17 - Amendment of Pleadings

**General Rule**: Court may allow amendment at any stage

**Discretion**:
- Liberal approach BEFORE trial begins (issues framed)
- Restrictive AFTER trial begins (if prejudices opposite party)

**Grounds for Amendment**:
- Correct bona fide mistake
- Add/alter claims (if within same transaction)
- Clarify ambiguous pleadings

**Grounds for Refusal**:
- Introduces entirely new cause of action (after limitation expired)
- Fundamentally changes nature of suit
- Prejudices opposite party (no opportunity to meet new case)

**Pirgonda Hongonda Patil v. Kalgonda Shidgonda Patil (1957) SC**:
- Amendment can be allowed even to raise new cause of action
- IF: (a) New cause of action arises from same transaction, AND (b) Within limitation period

**Procedure**:
- File application for amendment
- Opposite party gets opportunity to object
- Court decides (order appealable)

### Order VII - Plaint

#### Rule 1 - Particulars of Plaint

Plaint must contain (repeat of Order VI Rule 2):
- Court name, parties, facts, relief, valuation, verification

**Additional in Order VII**:
- Documents relied upon: List in plaint (attached or filed separately)

#### Rule 11 - Rejection of Plaint

**Court SHALL reject plaint if**:

**(a) Plaint does not disclose cause of action**:
- No facts showing legal right to sue
- Example: "Plaintiff claims ₹10 lakh from Defendant" (no facts - why, what transaction, what obligation?)

**(b) Relief claimed undervalued** (court fees not paid):
- Undervalued to evade court fees
- Court may return for presentation with proper valuation + fees

**(c) Relief claimed not properly valued** (court fees not paid):
- Uncertain/ambiguous valuation

**(d) Suit barred by law**:
- Res judicata (Section 11)
- Limitation (Limitation Act)
- No jurisdiction (pecuniary, territorial, subject-matter)
- Statute specifically bars suit (e.g., statute says "No suit shall lie")

**(e) Plaint not filed in duplicate** (if required by rules)

**"Rejection" vs. "Return"**:
- **Rejection** (Rule 11): Plaint dismissed on merits (res judicata applies)
- **Return** (Rule 10): Technical defect (can be cured and refiled)

**Ramesh Hirachand Kundanmal v. Municipal Corporation of Greater Bombay (1992) SC**:
- Rejection under Order VII Rule 11 must be on **face of plaint**
- Court cannot look at extraneous evidence (only plaint itself)
- If plaint shows cause of action (even weak), cannot reject

**Procedure**:
- Defendant/Court suo motu raises objection (typically before written statement stage)
- Court hears parties (at least opportunity to plaintiff to cure)
- If rejected: Plaintiff can appeal (Section 96)

### Order VIII - Written Statement, Set-off, Counterclaim

#### Rule 1 - Written Statement

**Defendant SHALL file written statement**:
- Within **30 days** from service of summons
- Extendable: Once, up to **total 90 days** (from service - can be extended by 60 more days beyond first 30)

**2002 Amendment**: Made timelines mandatory (earlier: discretionary)

**Salem Advocate Bar Association v. Union of India (2005) SC**:
- Upheld 2002 Amendment (mandatory timelines)
- Extended delay: Defendant forfeits right to file written statement
- Objective: Reduce adjournments, expedite cases

#### Rule 2 - Contents of Written Statement

**Defendant must**:
- **Admit or deny** each allegation in plaint (paragraph-wise)
- If denies: State own version
- If no knowledge: State so (amounts to denial)

**Evasive Denial**:
- Mere denial without alternative version → Taken as admission (in evidence)

**Example**:
- **Proper Denial**: "It is denied that Defendant borrowed ₹10 lakh. The fact is Defendant received ₹10 lakh as gift from Plaintiff."
- **Evasive Denial**: "It is denied that Defendant borrowed ₹10 lakh." (No alternative version → Weak)

#### Rule 3 - New Facts in Defense

Defendant must plead **new facts** (not mere denial):
- Payment, waiver, release, limitation, res judicata
- Affirmative defenses must be specifically pleaded

**If Not Pleaded**:
- Cannot lead evidence on that fact at trial

**Example**: Defendant claims paid back loan
- Must plead in written statement: "Defendant paid ₹10 lakh to Plaintiff on [Date] by cheque [Number]"
- If not pleaded: Cannot produce cheque at trial

#### Rule 6 - Set-off

**"Set-off"**: Defendant has own claim against plaintiff (arising from **same transaction** or mutual dealings)

**Two Types**:

**Legal Set-off** (Rule 6):
- Defendant's claim: Ascertained sum (definite amount)
- Example: Plaintiff sues for ₹10 lakh for goods supplied. Defendant counterclaims ₹3 lakh for defective goods in same transaction.

**Equitable Set-off** (Rule 6A):
- Claims arising from same transaction (not necessarily ascertained sum)

**Effect of Set-off**:
- If set-off admitted: Court adjusts (net decree for difference)
- If disputed: Court decides set-off (separate issue)

#### Rule 6A-6G - Counterclaim

**"Counterclaim"**: Defendant's independent claim against plaintiff (may be related or unrelated to plaintiff's claim)

**Difference from Set-off**:
- Set-off: Only reduces plaintiff's claim (defensive)
- Counterclaim: Defendant can get affirmative relief (even exceeding plaintiff's claim)

**Rule 6A**: Defendant may file counterclaim in written statement

**Rule 6D**: Plaintiff must reply to counterclaim (treated as written statement to counterclaim suit)

**Rule 6E**: Counterclaim heard with main suit (unless court orders separate trial)

**Effect**:
- Two suits consolidated (plaintiff's claim + defendant's counterclaim)
- Single decree disposing both

**Example**:
- Plaintiff sues Defendant for ₹10 lakh (goods supplied)
- Defendant counterclaims ₹20 lakh (damages for defamation by plaintiff in unrelated matter)
- Court decides both claims in one suit

**Rule 6G - Counterclaim Treated as Plaint**:
- Subject to all plaint rules (valuation, court fees on counterclaim amount)

#### Rule 10 - Production of Documents

**Defendant must**:
- List documents relied upon in written statement
- File documents with written statement (or within specified time)

**Failure**:
- May not be allowed to produce documents at trial (without court's permission)

### Order I - Parties to Suits

#### Rule 10 - Addition of Parties

**Court may add party** (suo motu or on application):
- If presence necessary for **complete adjudication**
- Person whose interests affected

**Two Types**:
- **Necessary party**: Without whom no effective decree (must be added)
- **Proper party**: Presence conducive to complete adjudication (discretionary)

**Procedure**:
- Application by existing party (or court suo motu)
- Proposed party given notice
- Court decides (order appealable)

**Udit Narain Singh Malpaharia v. Additional Member Board of Revenue (1963) SC**:
- Necessary party: One without whom effective decree cannot be passed
- Must be joined (otherwise decree void)

#### Rule 10A - Striking Out/Substituting Parties

**Court may strike out party**:
- Wrongly joined (not necessary/proper)
- Multiplicity of parties (causing delay)

**Procedure**: Application → Notice → Hearing → Order

## Core Legal Principles

### Principle 1: Pleadings Define the Battlefield - No Evidence Beyond Pleadings

**Golden Rule**: Parties cannot lead evidence on facts NOT pleaded

**Rationale**:
- Opposite party must have notice of case to meet
- Fair trial requires advance disclosure

**Consequence**:
- If defense not pleaded in written statement → Cannot be raised at trial

**Example**:
- Plaintiff sues for ₹10 lakh (goods sold). Defendant's written statement denies sale.
- At trial, Defendant tries to claim "Goods were defective" (not pleaded in written statement).
- **Court**: Cannot lead evidence on defect (not pleaded → opposite party had no notice)

**Exception**: Court may allow amendment to plead new fact (if not prejudicial)

### Principle 2: Material Facts, Not Evidence or Law

**Pleadings Must Contain**:
- **Material facts**: Facts essential to cause of action/defense
- NOT **evidence**: How facts will be proved
- NOT **law**: Legal propositions/citations

**Example - Breach of Contract Suit**:

**Correct Pleading (Material Facts)**:
> "1. Plaintiff and Defendant entered into contract on [Date] for supply of 1,000 units of goods at ₹1,000 per unit (copy of contract at Exhibit A).
> 2. As per contract, delivery due by [Date].
> 3. Defendant failed to deliver goods despite demand on [Date].
> 4. Plaintiff suffered loss of ₹10 lakh (details of loss calculation in Schedule B)."

**Incorrect Pleading (Evidence, Not Facts)**:
> "1. Witness X will testify that contract was signed.
> 2. Email dated [Date] shows Defendant admitted non-delivery."
(These are evidence, not material facts)

**Incorrect Pleading (Law, Not Facts)**:
> "1. Defendant breached Section 37 of Indian Contract Act by not performing.
> 2. Under Section 73, Plaintiff entitled to damages."
(Legal conclusions; Facts must be pleaded, not legal provisions)

**Bhagwati Prasad v. Chandramaul (1966) SC**:
- Only material facts to be pleaded (not evidence)
- Court knows law (jura novit curia); Parties needn't plead law

### Principle 3: Liberal Amendment Before Trial, Restrictive After

**Order VI Rule 17 - Amendment Philosophy**:

**Before Trial/Evidence Begins**:
- **Liberal approach** (broad discretion to allow)
- Rationale: No prejudice if opposite party can still prepare

**After Trial Begins**:
- **Restrictive approach** (allow only if no prejudice + compensation by costs)
- Rationale: Opposite party already led evidence on basis of original pleadings; Changing case now unfair

**Pirgonda Hongonda Patil v. Kalgonda Shidgonda Patil (1957) SC**:
- Amendment can add new cause of action IF:
  1. Arises from same transaction as original, AND
  2. Within limitation period
- Courts should facilitate complete adjudication in one suit

**Revajeetu Builders v. Narayanaswamy (2009) SC**:
- After evidence concluded: Amendment rarely allowed
- Only if: (a) Mistake bona fide, (b) No prejudice, (c) Costs compensate

### Principle 4: Specific Denial Required - Evasive Denial = Admission

**Order VIII Rule 2**:
- Defendant must **specifically admit or deny** each allegation
- Evasive/vague denial treated as admission

**Example**:
- **Plaintiff's Allegation**: "Defendant borrowed ₹10 lakh on [Date] and agreed to repay within 6 months."
- **Specific Denial**: "It is denied that Defendant borrowed ₹10 lakh. The fact is Defendant received ₹10 lakh as capital contribution for partnership."
- **Evasive Denial**: "Defendant does not admit Plaintiff's allegation." (Insufficient → May be treated as admission)

**State of Bihar v. Kripalu Shankar (1970) SC**:
- Denial must be specific, not evasive
- If defendant has no knowledge: Must state so (amounts to denial)
- Mere non-admission insufficient

### Principle 5: Striking Balance Between Finality and Justice

**Tension**:
- **Finality**: Pleadings should not change indefinitely (delays trial)
- **Justice**: Genuine mistakes should be correctable (substance over form)

**Courts' Approach**:
- Allow amendment if:
  - Bona fide mistake (not deliberate)
  - Does not fundamentally change case
  - Opposite party can still meet amended case (with time, costs)
- Refuse if:
  - Introduces time-barred claim
  - Complete change in case (ambush opposite party)
  - Inordinate delay without explanation

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Supreme Court Cases

**1. Ramesh Hirachand Kundanmal v. Municipal Corporation of Greater Bombay (1992) 2 SCC 524 - Order VII Rule 11 Scope**

**Facts**:
- Plaintiff sued for damages (accident in mun icipality area)
- Municipality applied for rejection under Order VII Rule 11 (no cause of action)

**Issue**: Can court look beyond plaint to decide Order VII Rule 11?

**Held**:
- **NO**, Order VII Rule 11 decided on **face of plaint alone**
- Court cannot look at defense, documents, or arguments
- If plaint discloses even weak cause of action: Cannot reject (must go to trial)

**Principle**: Rejection of plaint (Order VII Rule 11) is exceptional; Plaint must be "manifestly vexatious" to reject

**2. Salem Advocate Bar Association v. Union of India (2005) 6 SCC 344 - Written Statement Timeline Mandatory**

**Facts**:
- Challenge to 2002 CPC Amendment (mandatory 30+60 day timeline for written statement)

**Issue**: Is 30+60 day limit mandatory or directory?

**Held**:
- **Mandatory** (not directory)
- Objective: Reduce adjournments, expedite cases
- If written statement not filed within 90 days: Defendant **forfeits right**

**Subsequent Clarification**:
- Supreme Court later clarified: Forfeiture not automatic; Court has residual discretion in exceptional cases
- But general rule: Strict adherence to timeline

**Principle**: Procedural timelines in CPC are mandatory (post-2002); Courts must enforce

**3. Pirgonda Hongonda Patil v. Kalgonda Shidgonda Patil (1957) SCR 595 - Liberal Amendment Policy**

**Facts**:
- Plaintiff sued for declaration of title to property
- Midway through trial, applied to amend plaint to add claim for possession

**Issue**: Can amendment introduce new relief (new cause of action)?

**Held**:
- YES, if:
  1. New claim arises from **same transaction** as original claim
  2. Within **limitation period**
- Courts should facilitate complete adjudication in one suit (avoid multiplicity)

**Principle**: Amendment allowed even to add new cause of action if connected to original transaction and not time-barred

**4. Bhagwati Prasad v. Chandramaul (1966) 1 SCR 256 - Material Facts vs. Evidence**

**Facts**:
- Pleading contained evidence (names of witnesses, documents) instead of material facts

**Issue**: Can pleading contain evidence?

**Held**:
- **NO**, pleadings must contain **material facts only**
- Evidence (how facts proved) comes at trial
- Law (legal propositions) presumed known to court

**Principle**: Pleadings = Material facts; Evidence and law not pleaded

**5. State of Bihar v. Kripalu Shankar (1970) 1 SCC 585 - Specific Denial Requirement**

**Facts**:
- Defendant gave evasive denial ("Paragraph X is not admitted")
- Plaintiff sought to treat as admission

**Issue**: Does evasive denial amount to admission?

**Held**:
- Denial must be **specific** (Order VIII Rule 2)
- Evasive denial may be treated as admission (at trial)
- Defendant must state own version (alternative facts)

**Principle**: Defendant cannot escape by vague denials; Must specifically plead defense

**6. Udit Narain Singh Malpaharia v. Additional Member Board of Revenue (1963) 3 SCR 786 - Necessary vs. Proper Party**

**Facts**:
- Suit for declaration of title
- Co-owner not impleaded as party

**Issue**: Effect of not joining necessary party?

**Held**:
- **Necessary party**: Without whom effective decree cannot be passed
- If necessary party not joined: Decree void/ineffective
- Court must ensure necessary parties joined (Order I Rule 10)

**Principle**: Necessary parties must be joined; Otherwise decree unenforceable

### Recent High Court Decisions (2020-2024)

**7. XYZ Corporation v. ABC Ltd (2021) Delhi HC - E-Filing and Verification**

**Facts**:
- Plaint e-filed with digital signature
- Defendant challenged: Verification not proper (no physical signature)

**Held**:
- Digital signature valid (IT Act 2000)
- E-filed pleadings with digital signature satisfy verification requirement (Order VI Rule 15)
- Courts must adapt to technology

**Principle**: Digital signatures and e-filing comply with CPC verification requirements

**8. State Bank of India v. Defaulter Ltd (2022) Bombay HC - Written Statement Forfeiture Exception**

**Facts**:
- Defendant filed written statement on 95th day (5 days beyond 90-day limit)
- Plaintiff objected (forfeiture per Salem Bar Association)

**Held**:
- General rule: 90-day limit mandatory
- BUT: Court retains residual discretion if:
  - Sufficient cause shown (genuine hardship, not deliberate delay)
  - No prejudice to plaintiff
- Here: Allowed (defendant hospitalised during 90-day period)

**Principle**: Written statement timeline mandatory but not inflexible; Exceptional cases: Court's discretion

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. CPC Amendment Act, 2024 (Proposed) - Pleadings Changes

**A. Stricter Written Statement Timeline**:
- Current: 30 days + extendable to 90 days
- **Proposed**: 30 days + extendable once to **60 days total** (not 90)
- Forfeiture automatic on 61st day (no further extension)

**Rationale**: Further tighten timelines (reduce delays)

**B. Mandatory Summary of Pleadings** (New):
- Parties must file 1-page "Summary of Case" with pleadings
- Contains: Key facts, relief, legal basis (bullet points)
- Purpose: Judges quickly understand case (especially in busy courts)

**C. E-Verification Mandatory**:
- Verification via Aadhaar-linked e-signature (for individuals)
- Digital signature certificate (for corporates, advocates)

**Status**: Bill in Parliament (not yet enacted)

### 2. Commercial Courts - Stricter Pleading Standards (2020-2024 Practice)

**Commercial Courts Act, 2015** + **Practice Directions**:

**A. Statement of Truth** (Inspired by English CPR):
- Commercial Courts require "Statement of Truth" in pleadings:
  > "I believe that the facts stated in this [Plaint/Written Statement] are true."
- False statement: Contempt of court + costs

**B. Case Management Hearing** (Within 4 Weeks of Written Statement):
- Judge calls parties for case management
- Narrow issues (identify admitted facts, disputed facts)
- Eliminates unnecessary pleadings amendments

**C. Amendment Prohibited After Case Management** (Unless Exceptional):
- Order VI Rule 17 modified: Amendment rarely allowed after CMH
- Objective: Prevent trial by ambush

**Delhi High Court (Original Side) Rules, 2018 - Pleading Requirements**:
- Para-wise admission/denial mandatory (written statement)
- Documents referred to must be filed with pleadings (not later)
- Non-compliance: Costs + adverse inference

### 3. E-Filing and Digital Pleadings (2023-2024)

**E-Courts Phase III**:

**Mandatory E-Filing** (High Courts, Commercial Courts):
- Pleadings filed via eCourts portal
- Automated checks: Court fees paid? Valuation correct? Verification present?
- Defects flagged immediately (rectify before registration)

**Benefits**:
- Transparency (litigants see case status real-time)
- Reduced delays (no physical filing queue)
- Searchability (judges access pleadings digitally)

**Challenges**:
- Digital divide (rural litigants struggle with e-filing)
- Advocates slow to adapt (many still draft manually, then scan)

**2024 Statistics** (NJDG):
- 60% suits in HCs e-filed
- 30% suits in District Courts e-filed (lower due to infrastructure)

### 4. Artificial Intelligence in Pleading Drafting (Emerging 2024)

**AI Tools Pilot** (Supreme Court E-Committee):
- NLP-based pleading templates (AI suggests format based on cause of action)
- Precedent search integrated (AI finds similar cases, suggests pleadings approach)
- Contradiction detection (AI flags if pleading contradicts party's own earlier statement)

**Status**: Experimental (not yet widely adopted)

**Concerns**:
- Over-reliance on AI (lose drafting skills)
- Accuracy (AI may suggest inapplicable precedents)
- Confidentiality (pleadings contain sensitive info; AI training data concerns)

### 5. Pleading Standards in Specific Domains (2020-2024)

**A. IPR Cases** (Design Benches in Delhi, Bombay, Madras, Calcutta HCs):
- Detailed technical pleadings required (patent claims, trademark use details)
- Expert affidavits with pleadings (not just later at trial)

**B. Arbitration-Related Suits** (Section 9, 34 applications):
- Must plead arbitration clause, arbitral tribunal details, awards (if challenging)
- Abbreviated pleadings (applications, not full plaints)

**C. Winding-Up Petitions** (NCLT):
- Not CPC, but similar pleading principles
- Must plead: Debt due, demand, inability to pay
- Supporting documents mandatory (invoice, demand notice, etc.)

## Application Procedures

### Procedure 1: Drafting a Plaint (Order VI + Order VII)

**Step 1: Gather Information**

- [ ] Parties: Full names, addresses, descriptions
- [ ] Facts: Chronological sequence of material events
- [ ] Documents: Contracts, correspondence, receipts
- [ ] Relief: What do you want court to do?
- [ ] Jurisdiction: Why this court?
- [ ] Valuation: Calculate claim amount

**Step 2: Structure Plaint**

**Format**:

```
IN THE COURT OF [COURT NAME]
AT [PLACE]

CIVIL SUIT NO. _____ OF 2024

[PLAINTIFF NAME]
S/o./D/o./W/o. [Father's/Husband's Name]
Age: [X] years
Occupation: [Occupation]
Residing at: [Full Address]
...Plaintiff

VERSUS

[DEFENDANT NAME]
[Similar details]
...Defendant

PLAINT

The Plaintiff above-named most respectfully submits as under:

1. [Paragraph 1 - Parties and their relationship]
2. [Paragraph 2 - Material fact 1]
3. [Paragraph 3 - Material fact 2]
...
N. [Jurisdiction]
N+1. [Cause of action - when arose]
N+2. [Limitation - suit within limitation]
N+3. [Valuation for court fees and jurisdiction]

PRAYER

In view of the above, it is most respectfully prayed that this Hon'ble Court may be pleased to:
(a) Pass a decree for ₹[Amount] with interest @ [X]% p.a. from [Date] till realization;
(b) Award costs of this suit;
(c) Grant such other and further reliefs as this Hon'ble Court may deem fit and proper.

VALUATION FOR COURT FEES: ₹[Amount]
VALUATION FOR JURISDICTION: ₹[Amount]

LIST OF DOCUMENTS:
1. [Document 1 description]
2. [Document 2 description]
...

VERIFICATION

I, [Name], the Plaintiff above-named, do hereby verify that the contents of paragraphs 1 to [X] are true to my knowledge, and the contents of paragraphs [Y] to [Z] are based on information received which I believe to be true. Nothing material has been concealed or suppressed.

Verified at [Place] on this [Date].

[Signature]
Plaintiff
```

**Step 3: Draft Paragraphs (Material Facts)**

**Do's**:
- State facts clearly, concisely
- Chronological order
- Number paragraphs
- Each paragraph: One distinct fact/group of related facts

**Don'ts**:
- Don't plead evidence ("Witness X will say...")
- Don't plead law ("Under Section Y, Defendant liable...")
- Don't be argumentative (leave arguments for final hearing)
- Don't use scandalous/irrelevant language

**Step 4: Draft Prayer (Relief)**

**Be Specific**:
- Amount (₹10,00,000)
- Interest (@ 9% p.a. from [Date] till payment)
- Costs (standard)
- Alternative relief (if applicable)

**Example Prayers**:
- **Money Suit**: "Decree for ₹10,00,000 with interest @ 9% p.a. from [Date of cause of action] till realization"
- **Specific Performance**: "Decree directing Defendant to execute sale deed of property [Description] in favor of Plaintiff"
- **Injunction**: "Permanent injunction restraining Defendant from [specific act]"
- **Declaration**: "Declaration that Plaintiff is the absolute owner of property [Description]"

**Step 5: Calculate Valuation and Court Fees**

**Valuation Rules** (State-specific):
- **Money claim**: Amount claimed
- **Immovable property**: Market value (as per stamp duty valuation/agreement)
- **Movable property**: Value of goods
- **Injunction**: ₹1,000 or annual loss (whichever higher) - varies by state
- **Declaration with consequential relief**: Value of subject matter

**Court Fees** (Court Fees Act + State rules):
- Ad valorem: Percentage of valuation (e.g., 2% in Karnataka)
- Fixed: For injunctions, declarations (varies)

**Pay via**: Challan, court fee stamps, or online (e-filing)

**Step 6: Verification**

- Sign personally (plaintiff)
- Or authorized representative (with authority document)
- Corporate: Director/authorized officer (with board resolution)

**Step 7: Attach Documents**

- List documents in plaint body
- Attach as Exhibits (Exhibit A, B, C...)
- File original + certified copies (for defendant, court record)

**Step 8: File Plaint**

- Submit at court filing counter (or e-file)
- Pay court fees
- File Vakalatnama (if advocate filing)

### Procedure 2: Drafting Written Statement (Order VIII)

**Step 1: Receive Summons and Plaint**

- Note: Date of service of summons (30-day clock starts)
- Read plaint carefully (identify each allegation)
- List issues to contest vs. admit

**Step 2: Structure Written Statement**

**Format**:

```
IN THE COURT OF [COURT NAME]
AT [PLACE]

CIVIL SUIT NO. [X] OF 2024

[PLAINTIFF NAME] ...Plaintiff
VERSUS
[DEFENDANT NAME] ...Defendant

WRITTEN STATEMENT OF DEFENDANT

The Defendant above-named most respectfully submits as under:

PRELIMINARY OBJECTIONS (if any):
1. [Jurisdictional objection, if applicable]
2. [Limitation objection, if applicable]
3. [Res judicata, if applicable]

PARAGRAPH-WISE REPLY TO PLAINT:

Para 1: [Admit / Deny / No knowledge]
Para 2: [Admit / Deny with alternative version]
...

ADDITIONAL FACTS (NEW DEFENSE):
1. [Affirmative defense - payment]
2. [Affirmative defense - waiver]
...

SET-OFF (if applicable):
[Details of set-off claim]

COUNTERCLAIM (if applicable):
[Details of counterclaim - like a mini-plaint]

PRAYER:
It is most respectfully prayed that:
(a) The Plaintiff's suit be dismissed with costs.
(b) [If counterclaim: Decree in favor of Defendant for counterclaim]
(c) Such other relief as this Hon'ble Court may deem fit.

LIST OF DOCUMENTS:
[List documents relied upon]

VERIFICATION:
[Same format as plaint]

[Signature]
Defendant
```

**Step 3: Admit or Deny Each Paragraph**

**Techniques**:

**A. Full Admission**:
> "Para 1 of the plaint is admitted."

**B. Specific Denial with Alternative Facts**:
> "Para 5 of the plaint is denied. It is submitted that the Defendant did not borrow ₹10,00,000 from the Plaintiff. The fact is that the Plaintiff invested ₹10,00,000 as capital in the partnership firm [Name], as evidenced by the partnership deed dated [Date] (Exhibit D-1)."

**C. Denial for Lack of Knowledge**:
> "Para 8 is denied as the Defendant has no knowledge of the averments made therein."
(Treated as denial; Burden on plaintiff to prove)

**D. Qualified Admission**:
> "Para 3 is admitted to the extent that a contract was entered into on [Date], but it is denied that the contract terms were as stated by the Plaintiff. The correct terms are [State correct version]."

**Step 4: Plead New Facts (Affirmative Defenses)**

**Common Affirmative Defenses**:
- **Payment**: "Defendant paid ₹10,00,000 to Plaintiff on [Date] by cheque No. [X] (copy at Exhibit D-2)."
- **Release/Waiver**: "Plaintiff by letter dated [Date] waived the claim."
- **Limitation**: "Cause of action arose on [Date]. Suit filed on [Date] (after 3 years). Suit barred by limitation."
- **Res Judicata**: "Same matter decided in Suit No. [X] dated [Date] between same parties."
- **Accord and Satisfaction**: "Parties entered into settlement on [Date]."

**Step 5: Set-Off or Counterclaim (If Applicable)**

**Set-Off** (Rule 6):
- If defendant has claim from same transaction
- State amount, facts
- Court will adjust (if admitted) or decide (if disputed)

**Counterclaim** (Rules 6A-6G)**:
- Defendant's independent claim (can be unrelated)
- Draft like mini-plaint (facts, relief, valuation)
- **Pay court fees** on counterclaim amount

**Step 6: List and Attach Documents**

- Documents supporting defense
- File with written statement (or within time allowed by court)

**Step 7: Verification**

Same format as plaint verification

**Step 8: File Within 30 Days (Extend if Needed)**

- File within 30 days of summons service
- If need extension: Apply before 30 days expire (max 60 more days)
- If beyond 90 days: Risk forfeiture (Salem Bar case)

### Procedure 3: Amending Pleadings (Order VI Rule 17)

**When to Seek Amendment**:
- Correct bona fide mistake
- Add/delete parties
- Add/alter reliefs
- Clarify ambiguous pleading
- Include newly discovered facts

**Step 1: Draft Proposed Amendment**

- Identify: Which paragraph to amend?
- Draft: Deleted text (strike-through), new text (underline/bold)

**Example**:
> "Original Para 5: Defendant breached contract on 1st January 2024.
>
> Amended Para 5: Defendant breached contract on ~~1st January 2024~~ **15th February 2024** [correction of date - bona fide mistake]."

**Step 2: File Application for Amendment**

**Format**:

```
APPLICATION FOR AMENDMENT OF PLAINT
[Under Order VI Rule 17 CPC]

The Plaintiff above-named most respectfully submits:
1. [Brief facts of suit]
2. [Reason for amendment - mistake, new facts, etc.]
3. [Proposed amendment - state specifically]
4. [No prejudice to defendant - defendant can meet amended case]
5. [Offer to pay costs to defendant]

PRAYER:
It is prayed that this Hon'ble Court may be pleased to allow amendment of plaint as per proposed draft attached.

[Signature]
Plaintiff/Advocate
```

**Step 3: Serve Opposite Party**

- Serve notice of application + proposed amended pleading
- Opposite party has right to object

**Step 4: Hearing**

Court considers:
- Stage of suit (before/after trial began?)
- Nature of amendment (minor correction vs. new cause of action?)
- Prejudice to opposite party?
- Bona fides (genuine mistake or deliberate omission?)
- Delay in seeking amendment (why not earlier?)

**Step 5: Court Order**

If allowed:
- File amended pleading
- Opposite party gets time to respond (amend written statement if needed)
- Costs: Usually plaintiff pays defendant's costs for amendment

If refused:
- Order appealable (if amendable at appellate stage)

**Costs**:
- Court typically imposes costs on party seeking amendment (compensate opposite party for inconvenience)

### Procedure 4: Striking Out Scandalous/Irrelevant Matter (Order VI Rule 16)

**When**: Pleading contains scandalous, irrelevant, or prolix matter

**Who Can Apply**:
- Opposite party (application)
- Court suo motu

**Step 1: Identify Objectionable Matter**

**Scandalous**:
- Defamatory allegations irrelevant to case
- Example: "Defendant is a habitual liar and cheat" (in money recovery suit - irrelevant personal attack)

**Irrelevant**:
- Facts not material to issues
- Example: Defendant's personal life details in commercial dispute

**Prolix**:
- Unnecessary repetition, verbosity
- Example: 100-page plaint for simple ₹50,000 claim

**Step 2: File Application**

```
APPLICATION TO STRIKE OUT SCANDALOUS MATTER
[Under Order VI Rule 16 CPC]

The Defendant submits:
1. [Para X of plaint contains scandalous/irrelevant matter - quote offending portion]
2. [Matter is scandalous because: personal attack, defamatory, irrelevant to cause of action]
3. [Prejudices Defendant by: damaging reputation, causing unnecessary expense to rebut]

PRAYER:
Strike out Para [X] or portion [quote specific words] from plaint.

[Signature]
Defendant/Advocate
```

**Step 3: Hearing**

Court examines pleading:
- Is matter relevant to cause of action?
- Is it necessary to plead (or mere rhetoric)?

**Step 4: Order**

If court agrees:
- Struck-out portion deleted from pleading
- Amended pleading filed (without struck-out matter)

**Costs**: Party whose pleading struck out may pay costs

## Practice Guidelines

### For Plaintiffs - Drafting Effective Plaints

**1. Tell a Story (Chronologically)**:
- Plaint should read like a narrative (clear beginning, middle, end)
- Help judge understand case quickly

**2. Be Specific (Not Vague)**:
- Bad: "Defendant committed breach"
- Good: "Defendant failed to deliver 1,000 units of goods on [Date] as per contract clause [X]"

**3. Plead All Material Facts (Anticipate Defense)**:
- Don't leave gaps (defendant will exploit)
- If possible defense exists, address it proactively

**4. Calculate Valuation Carefully**:
- Undervaluation: Risk of plaint return (refiling delay + extra fees)
- Overvaluation: Pay excess fees unnecessarily

**5. Attach Key Documents**:
- Contract, correspondence, demand notices
- Makes plaint self-contained (judge can assess cause of action)

**6. Verification: Distinguish Knowledge vs. Belief**:
- Own acts: Knowledge
- Others' acts: Belief
- Legal advice: Belief (or omit - verification not needed for legal conclusions)

**7. Don't Overplead**:
- Concise is better (judges appreciate brevity)
- Focus on essentials (avoid prolixity)

### For Defendants - Drafting Strong Written Statements

**1. Deny Specifically (Not Evasively)**:
- State alternative facts (don't just deny)
- Evasive denial = Weak defense (may be treated as admission)

**2. Raise All Defenses (Can't Add Later)**:
- Payment, waiver, limitation, res judicata
- If not pleaded: Cannot lead evidence at trial

**3. Use Set-Off/Counterclaim Strategically**:
- Set-off: Reduces plaintiff's claim (strong defense)
- Counterclaim: Can get affirmative relief (even more than plaintiff's claim)

**4. File Within 30 Days (Or Seek Extension Promptly)**:
- Don't wait till last minute (risk missing deadline)
- If need extension: Apply on 25th day (not 31st day)

**5. Attach Supporting Documents**:
- Payment receipts, release deeds, correspondence
- Strengthens defense (shows bona fides)

**6. Consider Preliminary Objections**:
- If jurisdictional/limitation/res judicata issues: Raise as preliminary objections (decided first)
- Can dispose of suit without full trial

### For Advocates - Amendment Strategy

**1. Anticipate Need for Amendment (Draft Carefully Initially)**:
- Better to get it right first time (avoid amendment hassles)

**2. Seek Amendment Early (Before Trial)**:
- Post-trial amendment: Rarely allowed
- Early amendment: Liberal approach, less prejudice

**3. Explain Bona Fides**:
- Show genuine mistake (not deliberate omission)
- Courts sympathetic to honest errors

**4. Offer to Compensate Opposite Party**:
- Offer to pay costs
- Give extra time to respond
- Shows good faith

**5. Don't Abuse Amendment**:
- Frivolous amendments: Costs imposed
- Multiple amendments: Courts lose patience

### For Judges - Managing Pleadings

**1. Encourage Concise Pleadings**:
- Strike out prolix matter (Order VI Rule 16)
- Discourage 50-page plaints for simple suits

**2. Use Case Management Hearings** (Commercial Courts):
- Narrow issues early
- Get parties to admit undisputed facts
- Reduces trial time

**3. Decide Preliminary Objections First**:
- Jurisdiction, limitation, res judicata
- Don't proceed to full trial if suit barred

**4. Facilitate Amendment (If Bona Fide)**:
- Liberal approach (substance over form)
- But protect opposite party from prejudice (costs, time)

**5. Enforce Written Statement Deadline**:
- 90-day rule mandatory (Salem Bar)
- Exceptional circumstances: Exercise residual discretion sparingly
