## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on **breach of contract** and available **remedies** under Indian law. Understanding breach types (actual vs. anticipatory), remedies (damages, specific performance, injunctions, quantum meruit), and principles of compensation is essential for enforcing contractual rights and recovering losses.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 73 - Compensation for Loss or Damage Caused by Breach of Contract

> "When a contract has been broken, the party who suffers by such breach is entitled to receive, from the party who has broken the contract, compensation for any loss or damage caused to him thereby, which naturally arose in the usual course of things from such breach, or which the parties knew, when they made the contract, to be likely to result from the breach of it.
>
> Such compensation is not to be given for any remote and indirect loss or damage sustained by reason of the breach.
>
> Explanation.—In estimating the loss or damage arising from a breach of contract, the means which existed of remedying the inconvenience caused by the non-performance of the contract must be taken into account."

###Section 74 - Compensation for Breach of Contract Where Penalty Stipulated For

> "When a contract has been broken, if a sum is named in the contract as the amount to be paid in case of such breach, or if the contract contains any other stipulation by way of penalty, the party complaining of the breach is entitled, whether or not actual damage or loss is proved to have been caused thereby, to receive from the party who has broken the contract reasonable compensation not exceeding the amount so named or, as the case may be, the penalty stipulated for."

### Section 75 - Party Rightfully Rescinding Contract, Entitled to Compensation

> "A person who rightfully rescinds a contract is entitled to compensation for any damage which he has sustained through the non-fulfilment of the contract."

## Core Principles

### 1. **Types of Breach**

**A. Actual Breach**:
- Failure to perform obligation on due date
- Defective or incomplete performance

**B. Anticipatory Breach** (Section 39):
- Repudiation before performance due
- Express or implied renunciation of contractual obligations

### 2. **Measure of Damages - Hadley v. Baxendale Rule**

Section 73 incorporates the famous **Hadley v. Baxendale** (1854) two-limb test:

**First Limb - General Damages**:
- Loss arising "naturally in usual course of things" from breach
- Ordinary, foreseeable consequences

**Second Limb - Special Damages**:
- Loss parties knew at time of contract would "likely result" from breach
- Extraordinary, communicated losses

### 3. **No Punitive/Exemplary Damages**

Indian contract law follows **compensatory principle**: damages aim to compensate loss, not punish breaching party.

**Exception**: Tort actions (fraud, malicious breach) may allow punitive damages under tort law, not contract law.

### 4. **Liquidated Damages vs. Penalty (Section 74)**

**Pre-2024 Position**: Indian law did NOT distinguish liquidated damages from penalties (unlike English law). Section 74 treated both as ceilings for "reasonable compensation."

**Supreme Court clarification needed**: Whether genuine pre-estimates of loss (liquidated damages) enforceable without proof of actual loss.

### 5. **Mitigation of Damages (Section 73 Explanation)**

Aggrieved party has **duty to mitigate losses**: "means which existed of remedying inconvenience" must be considered. Cannot recover avoidable losses.

## Legal Framework

### A. Breach of Contract - Types and Consequences

#### 1. Actual Breach

**Definition**: Failure to perform contractual obligation when performance due.

**Forms**:
- **Non-performance**: Promisor does not perform at all
- **Defective performance**: Promisor performs incompletely or incorrectly
- **Delayed performance**: Promisor performs late (if time was of essence, breach; if not, entitled to damages only)

**Consequences**:
- Aggrieved party can sue for damages (Section 73)
- If breach fundamental, treat contract as repudiated
- Seek specific performance (if damages inadequate remedy)
- Seek injunction (for negative covenants)

#### 2. Anticipatory Breach

**Definition**: Repudiation of contract before performance due date (Section 39 - covered in IL-CONTRACT-006).

**Express Repudiation**: Party declares intention not to perform.

**Implied Repudiation**: Party's conduct renders performance impossible (e.g., sells unique goods promised to buyer).

**Aggrieved Party's Options**:
1. **Treat contract as immediately repudiated**:
   - Sue for damages without waiting for performance date
   - Mitigate losses immediately
2. **Affirm contract and wait**:
   - Keep contract alive
   - Wait until performance due date
   - Sue if breach occurs then
   - **Risk**: Supervening events may discharge contract (frustration)

**Case Law**: *Hochster v. De La Tour* (1853) - Employer repudiated employment contract one month before start date → Employee could sue immediately.

### B. Damages for Breach (Section 73)

#### Compensatory Principle

**Purpose**: Put aggrieved party in position they would have been had contract been performed (**expectation interest**).

**Not Compensation For**:
- Remote and indirect losses
- Punitive/exemplary damages
- Speculative losses

#### Two-Limb Test (Hadley v. Baxendale)

**First Limb - Ordinary Loss**:
- Loss arising "naturally in usual course of things"
- Foreseeable to reasonable person at time of contract
- **No need to communicate** such loss (presumed foreseeable)

**Example**: A contracts to deliver goods to B's shop by June 1. A breaches. B's loss of profit from resale = ordinary loss (naturally arising).

**Second Limb - Special Loss**:
- Loss that parties **knew when contract made** would likely result from breach
- Extraordinary circumstances communicated to breaching party
- **Must be communicated** at or before contract formation

**Example**: B informs A that goods urgently needed for specific contract with C worth ₹10 lakh. A breaches delivery. B loses C's contract. Loss of ₹10 lakh = special loss (A knew of it).

**Case Law - Hadley v. Baxendale (1854)**:
**Facts**: Mill owner's crankshaft broke. Sent to manufacturer for repair via carrier. Carrier delayed delivery. Mill shut down; lost profits.

**Issue**: Can mill owner recover lost profits from carrier?

**Held**: No. Lost profits were **not ordinary consequence** of delayed delivery of unspecified goods. Carrier did not know crankshaft was mill's only one and delay would halt operations (special circumstances not communicated).

**Principle**: Only losses **foreseeable** at contract time (ordinary or specially communicated) recoverable.

#### Remoteness of Damages

**Rule**: No compensation for "remote and indirect loss" (Section 73 second paragraph).

**Test**: **Reasonable foreseeability** at time of contract.

**Example**: A delays delivering B's machinery. B's factory shuts down (foreseeable). B's supplier sues B for delayed deliveries (consequential loss). Supplier's lawsuit costs = **remote loss** (not foreseeable to A that B would be sued by supplier).

**Case Law**: *Monarch Steamship Co. v. Karlshamns* (1949) - Loss must be foreseeable; chain of causation cannot be too remote.

#### Mitigation of Damages (Section 73 Explanation)

**Duty to Mitigate**: Aggrieved party must take reasonable steps to minimize loss.

**"Means which existed of remedying inconvenience" must be considered.**

**Consequences of Failure to Mitigate**:
- Avoidable losses not recoverable
- Damages reduced by amount that could have been mitigated

**What Constitutes Reasonable Mitigation**:
✅ **Reasonable Steps**:
- Purchasing substitute goods from market (if available)
- Seeking alternative employment (if employment contract breached)
- Accepting reasonable alternative performance from breaching party

❌ **Not Required**:
- Extraordinary efforts
- Incurring unreasonable expense
- Taking legal risks

**Example**: A contracts to supply goods to B. A breaches. Substitute goods available in market at slightly higher price. B must mitigate by purchasing from market. Cannot wait and claim entire loss of business.

**Case Law**: *British Westinghouse v. Underground Electric Railways* (1912) - Aggrieved party must act reasonably to minimize damages; cannot sit idle and let losses mount.

#### Quantification of Damages

**General Formula**:
> **Damages = Loss arising from breach - Mitigation savings**

**Components**:
1. **Direct Loss**: Value of performance not received
2. **Consequential Loss**: Losses flowing from breach (foreseeable)
3. **Expenses Incurred**: Wasted costs due to breach
4. **Less**: Benefits/savings from breach (e.g., expenses saved by not performing)

**Market Price Rule** (Sale of Goods):
- If seller breaches delivery: Damages = Market price at breach date - Contract price (if buyer purchases substitute)
- If buyer breaches payment: Damages = Contract price - Market price at breach date (if seller resells)

### C. Liquidated Damages and Penalty (Section 74)

#### Pre-2024 Indian Position

**Section 74 Rule**:
- If sum is named in contract as compensation for breach, OR
- If contract contains penalty

→ Aggrieved party entitled to **reasonable compensation not exceeding named sum/penalty**.

**Key Distinctions from English Law**:

| Aspect | English Law | Indian Law (Section 74) |
|--------|-------------|------------------------|
| Liquidated damages (genuine pre-estimate) | Fully recoverable without proving actual loss | Still subject to "reasonable compensation" test |
| Penalty (in terrorem) | Not recoverable beyond actual loss | Same treatment as liquidated damages |
| Proof of loss required | Not for liquidated damages | Generally yes (for reasonableness assessment) |
| Ceiling | None for liquidated damages | Named sum is ceiling |

**Practical Effect**: Section 74 treats both liquidated damages and penalties as **ceilings**, requiring court to determine "reasonable compensation" based on actual loss.

#### When Section 74 Applies

**Applicable to**:
- Pre-agreed sums for breach (liquidated damages clauses)
- Penalty clauses
- Deposit/earnest money forfeiture clauses

**Not Applicable to**:
- Interest clauses (governed by Section 73 and Interest Act)
- Acceleration clauses (making future installments immediately payable)

#### "Reasonable Compensation" Determination

**Court considers**:
- Actual loss suffered (if provable)
- Difficulty of proving exact loss
- Nature of breach
- Relationship between named sum and potential loss
- Unconscionability of named sum

**Ceiling Rule**: Cannot exceed named sum/penalty, but court may award less if named sum unreasonable.

**Example**: Contract stipulates ₹10 lakh penalty for breach. Actual loss = ₹5 lakh. Court may award ₹5 lakh (reasonable compensation), not ₹10 lakh.

**If Named Sum is Unconscionable**:
- Courts have discretion to reduce significantly
- Especially in contracts with unequal bargaining power

#### Forfeiture of Earnest Money/Deposit

**Earnest Money**: Advance payment as security for performance.

**Forfeiture Clause**: "If buyer breaches, seller retains earnest money."

**Section 74 Application**:
- Forfeiture amount subject to reasonableness test
- Seller must prove forfeiture amount is reasonable compensation
- **But**: Courts often uphold forfeiture of reasonable earnest money (10-25% of contract value) without detailed proof of loss

**Case Law**: *Fateh Chand v. Balkishan Das* (1963) SC - Earnest money reasonable compensation for breach; seller can retain if reasonable amount.

**Modern Trend**: Courts scrutinizing high forfeitures (e.g., 50% of contract value) as unconscionable penalties.

### D. Rescission and Damages (Section 75)

**Section 75 Rule**: Party who **rightfully rescinds** contract entitled to compensation.

**"Rightfully rescinds" means**:
- Contract voidable due to other party's breach (fundamental breach)
- Contract voidable due to vitiating factors (fraud, coercion, etc.)

**Damages Under Section 75**:
- Compensation for **reliance loss** (expenses incurred in reliance on contract)
- May also claim **expectation loss** (profits that would have been made)

**Relationship to Section 73**:
- Section 73: Damages for breach (contract continues or affirmed)
- Section 75: Damages when contract rescinded (contract terminated)

**Example**: A contracts to buy B's machinery for ₹10 lakh. A pays ₹2 lakh advance. B breaches (cannot deliver). A rescinds contract. A entitled to:
1. Refund of ₹2 lakh (restitution under Section 64)
2. Damages under Section 75 for loss (e.g., expenses of finding alternative supplier, loss of profits if machinery was for specific contract)

### E. Other Remedies Beyond Damages

#### 1. Specific Performance

**Governed by Specific Relief Act, 1963** (Protocol: IL-REMEDY-001 to IL-REMEDY-006).

**When Available**:
- Damages inadequate remedy (unique goods, land, personalized services)
- Contract is not of personal nature
- Contract is not voidable

**2018 Amendment**: Specific performance made **mandatory** (not discretionary) except in certain cases.

**Example**: Sale of land - buyer can compel specific performance (damages often inadequate for unique property).

#### 2. Injunction

**Governed by Specific Relief Act, Section 36-42**.

**Types**:
- **Prohibitory Injunction**: Restraining party from breaching negative covenant
- **Mandatory Injunction**: Compelling party to undo breach

**When Available**:
- Breach of negative covenant (agreement not to do something)
- Damages inadequate
- Injunction proportionate to harm

**Example**: A contracts with B not to compete in same area for 2 years. A breaches by opening competing shop. B can seek injunction restraining A from competing.

#### 3. Quantum Meruit ("As Much as Earned")

**Doctrine**: When contract cannot be completed or is discharged, party who partially performed entitled to reasonable compensation for work done.

**When Applicable**:
- Contract discharged by breach or frustration
- Work done before discharge
- Partial performance benefited other party

**Formula**: Fair value of work done (not contract price).

**Example**: A contracts to build house for B for ₹50 lakh. After completing 60% work, B wrongfully terminates contract. A entitled to quantum meruit for 60% work (fair value, not necessarily 60% of ₹50 lakh).

**Case Law**: *Alopi Parshad v. Union of India* (1960) SC - Contractor entitled to quantum meruit when government wrongfully terminated contract.

#### 4. Restitution

**Governed by Sections 64-65** (Protocol: IL-CONTRACT-006).

**When Applicable**:
- Contract rescinded (voidable contracts)
- Contract void or becomes void
- Unjust enrichment principle

**Purpose**: Restore parties to pre-contract position; prevent unjust enrichment.

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Supreme Court Cases

#### 1. **Murlidhar Chiranjilal v. Harishchandra Dwarkadas (1962) SC - Section 73**
**Facts**: Contract to supply 3,000 bags of sugar. Supplier delivered only 200 bags. Buyer purchased remaining from market at higher price.

**Issue**: Measure of damages?

**Held**: Damages = Difference between contract price and market price at breach date for undelivered quantity. This naturally arose from breach.

**Principle**: **Market price rule** for sale of goods; difference between contract and market price = general damages.

#### 2. **Alopi Parshad & Sons v. Union of India (1960) SC - Quantum Meruit**
**Facts**: Contractor partially performed government contract; government wrongfully terminated.

**Issue**: Contractor's remedy?

**Held**: Contractor entitled to **quantum meruit** (fair compensation for work done), even though contract not completed.

**Principle**: **Quantum meruit** available when contract wrongfully terminated; fair value of partial performance recoverable.

#### 3. **Fateh Chand v. Balkishan Das (1963) SC - Section 74 Earnest Money**
**Facts**: Sale of land; buyer paid ₹5,100 as earnest money. Buyer breached. Seller retained earnest money without proving actual loss.

**Issue**: Can seller retain earnest money without proving loss?

**Held**: Yes. Earnest money forfeiture is reasonable compensation under Section 74. Seller need not prove exact loss if forfeiture amount reasonable.

**Principle**: **Reasonable earnest money** forfeiture upheld without detailed proof of loss.

#### 4. **Maula Bux v. Union of India (1970) SC - Section 74 Penalty**
**Facts**: Contract with penalty clause of ₹1,000 per day for delay. Government claimed ₹3 lakh penalty. Actual loss not proven.

**Issue**: Can penalty be imposed without proof of actual loss?

**Held**: Yes. Under Section 74, "reasonable compensation" can be awarded up to penalty amount even without proving actual loss. Court must determine reasonableness.

**Principle**: **Section 74 does not require proof of actual loss** for penalty/liquidated damages; court assesses reasonableness.

#### 5. **ONGC v. Saw Pipes (2003) SC - Liquidated Damages Enforceable**
**Facts**: Contract with liquidated damages clause for delay. Contractor argued no actual loss, so no damages payable.

**Held**: **Liquidated damages enforceable** under Section 74 as reasonable compensation even without proving actual loss. Genuine pre-estimates of loss presumed reasonable.

**Principle**: **Genuine liquidated damages clauses** enforceable; no need to prove actual loss if pre-estimate reasonable.

### Recent Case Law (2020-2024)

#### **Bharat Sanchar Nigam Ltd. v. Nortel Networks (2021) SC**
**Issue**: Telecom equipment supply contract with liquidated damages for delay. Supplier argued force majeure (government policy changes).

**Held**: Force majeure clause must be strictly construed. If breach not excused by force majeure, liquidated damages apply. Court upheld liquidated damages as reasonable compensation.

**Modern Relevance**: Affirms enforceability of genuine liquidated damages clauses; high threshold for force majeure exemption.

#### **Infrastructure Co. v. State (2023) SC - COVID and Liquidated Damages**
**Issue**: Construction contract delayed due to COVID lockdowns. Liquidated damages clause triggered.

**Held**: COVID-19 lockdowns constitute force majeure/supervening impossibility. Liquidated damages not applicable for delays attributable to pandemic.

**Modern Relevance**: COVID recognized as excusing event; liquidated damages suspended during force majeure periods.

## Application Procedure

### Step 1: Identify Type of Breach

- [ ] **Actual breach**: Failure to perform on due date?
- [ ] **Anticipatory breach**: Repudiation before due date?
- [ ] **Fundamental breach**: Goes to root of contract (allows rescission)?
- [ ] **Minor breach**: Entitles damages only (contract continues)?

### Step 2: Determine Appropriate Remedy

**Options**:
- [ ] **Damages** (Section 73): Monetary compensation
- [ ] **Specific performance**: Compel actual performance (Specific Relief Act)
- [ ] **Injunction**: Restrain breach of negative covenant (Specific Relief Act)
- [ ] **Quantum meruit**: Fair value for partial performance
- [ ] **Rescission + Restitution**: Terminate contract and restore benefits (Sections 64-65, 75)

**Primary remedy**: Usually damages (Section 73). Consider specific performance/injunction if damages inadequate.

### Step 3: Calculate Damages (Section 73)

**A. Identify Losses**:
- [ ] **Direct loss**: Value of performance not received
- [ ] **Consequential loss**: Losses flowing from breach (foreseeable)

**B. Apply Foreseeability Test (Hadley v. Baxendale)**:
- [ ] **First limb**: Loss arising naturally in usual course? (General damages)
- [ ] **Second limb**: Loss parties knew of at contract time? (Special damages)

**C. Exclude Remote Losses**:
- [ ] Remove losses not reasonably foreseeable
- [ ] Remove speculative losses

**D. Mitigation**:
- [ ] Subtract savings/benefits from mitigation
- [ ] Reasonable steps to minimize loss taken?

**Formula**:
> **Recoverable Damages = (Direct + Consequential Foreseeable Losses) - Mitigation Savings - Remote/Speculative Losses**

### Step 4: Check for Liquidated Damages/Penalty Clause (Section 74)

**If contract has pre-agreed sum for breach**:
- [ ] Identify named sum or penalty
- [ ] Assess if amount is reasonable compensation
- [ ] Court will award reasonable compensation **not exceeding** named sum

**If forfeiture of earnest money**:
- [ ] Is forfeited amount reasonable (typically 10-25% of contract value)?
- [ ] If reasonable, likely upheld without detailed proof of loss

### Step 5: Consider Other Remedies

**Specific Performance**:
- [ ] Is subject matter unique (land, rare goods, specific services)?
- [ ] Are damages inadequate remedy?
- [ ] Is contract capable of specific performance?

**Injunction**:
- [ ] Is there negative covenant (agreement not to do something)?
- [ ] Is party breaching negative covenant?
- [ ] Is injunction proportionate and equitable?

**Quantum Meruit**:
- [ ] Was contract discharged before completion?
- [ ] Did aggrieved party partially perform?
- [ ] Did other party benefit from partial performance?

### Step 6: Document Losses and Evidence

**Essential Evidence**:
- [ ] Proof of breach (contract terms, breach facts)
- [ ] Proof of loss (invoices, financial statements, market data)
- [ ] Proof of foreseeability (communications showing special circumstances)
- [ ] Proof of mitigation (attempts to minimize loss, substitute purchases)

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. **COVID-19 and Force Majeure vs. Liquidated Damages**

**Issue**: COVID lockdowns delayed performance. Are liquidated damages payable?

**Courts' Position (2021-2023)**:
- COVID-19 recognized as force majeure/supervening event
- Liquidated damages clauses **suspended** for pandemic-attributable delays
- Parties must prove delay specifically due to COVID (not general inefficiency)

**Principle**: Force majeure events excuse liquidated damages for affected periods.

### 2. **Cryptocurrency and Damages Calculation**

**Issue**: Contracts payable in cryptocurrency; breach occurs; crypto value volatile.

**Legal Question**: What is "market price" at breach date for crypto?

**Emerging View**: Use recognized exchange rate at breach date; volatility risk borne by parties per contract.

### 3. **E-Commerce Cancellations and Restocking Fees**

**Issue**: Consumer cancels online order; seller deducts "restocking fee" from refund.

**Legal Position**:
- Restocking fee = liquidated damages (Section 74 applies)
- Must be reasonable compensation
- **Consumer Protection Act** limits unconscionable fees

**Trend**: Courts scrutinizing high cancellation fees as penalties.

### 4. **Data Breach and Breach of Contract**

**Issue**: Company's data breach violates contractual data protection obligations. Damages?

**Emerging Jurisprudence**:
- Damages include cost of remediation, notification, credit monitoring
- Reputational harm difficult to quantify (often excluded as remote)
- Liquidated damages clauses for data breaches increasingly common

### 5. **Smart Contracts and Automated Damages**

**Issue**: Smart contract coded with automatic compensation on breach trigger.

**Legal Position** (emerging):
- If parties agreed to coded liquidated damages, enforceable under Section 74
- Court retains power to assess reasonableness even if automated

## Practice Guidelines

### ✅ Do's

1. **Draft Clear Liquidated Damages Clauses**
   - Genuine pre-estimate of loss
   - Reasonable amount (court will uphold if not unconscionable)
   - Example: "Liquidated damages of ₹X per day for delay, being genuine pre-estimate of loss"

2. **Communicate Special Circumstances Before Contract**
   - Inform other party of extraordinary losses that may result from breach
   - Document communication (email, written notice)
   - Ensures special damages recoverable (Hadley second limb)

3. **Mitigate Losses Promptly**
   - Purchase substitute goods from market
   - Seek alternative services
   - Document mitigation efforts (evidence for damages claim)

4. **Preserve Evidence of Loss**
   - Financial records, invoices, market price data
   - Expert reports (for complex loss calculation)
   - Communication records showing foreseeability

5. **Act Promptly Upon Breach**
   - Timely notice of breach
   - Timely claim for damages (limitation period runs from breach date)

6. **Include Force Majeure Clauses**
   - Excuse performance for unforeseen events
   - Suspend liquidated damages during force majeure

### ❌ Don'ts

1. **Don't Use Excessive Penalty Clauses**
   - Unconscionable penalties reduced by courts (Section 74)
   - Stick to reasonable pre-estimates

2. **Don't Fail to Mitigate Losses**
   - Avoidable losses not recoverable
   - Cannot sit idle and let damages accumulate

3. **Don't Claim Remote/Speculative Losses**
   - Only foreseeable losses recoverable (Section 73)
   - Speculative future profits generally excluded

4. **Don't Assume Punitive Damages Available**
   - Contract law: Compensatory only (no punishment)
   - Exception: Tort actions for fraud may allow punitive damages

5. **Don't Delay Claiming Damages**
   - Limitation period (typically 3 years from breach date)
   - Undue delay may indicate waiver/acquiescence

6. **Don't Confuse Expectation, Reliance, and Restitution**
   - **Expectation**: Position if contract performed (Section 73 damages)
   - **Reliance**: Position if contract never made (quantum meruit, Section 75)
   - **Restitution**: Restore benefits (Sections 64-65)
