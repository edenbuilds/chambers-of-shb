## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on **contingent contracts** under Indian law. Contingent contracts are agreements to do or not do something upon the occurrence or non-occurrence of a future uncertain event. Understanding contingent contracts is essential for structuring conditional obligations, insurance contracts, wagers, and event-dependent transactions.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 31 - "Contingent Contract" Defined

> "A 'contingent contract' is a contract to do or not to do something, if some event, collateral to such contract, does or does not happen.
>
> **Illustration**: A contracts to pay B ₹10,000 if B's house is burnt. This is a contingent contract."

### Section 32 - Enforcement of Contracts Contingent on an Event Happening

> "Contingent contracts to do or not to do anything if an uncertain future event happens cannot be enforced by law unless and until that event has happened.
>
> If the event becomes impossible, such contracts become void.
>
> **Illustrations**:
> (a) A makes a contract with B to buy B's horse if A survives C. This contract cannot be enforced by law unless and until C dies in A's lifetime.
> (b) A makes a contract with B to sell a horse to B at a specified price, if C, to whom the horse has been offered, refuses to buy it. The contract cannot be enforced by law unless and until C refuses to buy the horse.
> (c) A contracts to pay B a sum of money when B marries C. C dies without being married to B. The contract becomes void."

### Section 33 - Enforcement of Contracts Contingent on an Event Not Happening

> "Contingent contracts to do or not to do anything if an uncertain future event does not happen can be enforced when the happening of that event becomes impossible, and not before.
>
> **Illustration**: A agrees to pay B a sum of money if a certain ship does not return. The ship is sunk. The contract can be enforced when the ship sinks."

### Section 34 - When Event on Which Contract is Contingent to Be Deemed Impossible, If It is the Future Conduct of a Living Person

> "If the future event on which a contract is contingent is the way in which a person will act at an unspecified time, the event shall be considered to become impossible when such person does anything which renders it impossible that he should so act within any definite time, or otherwise than under further contingencies.
>
> **Illustration**: A agrees to pay B a sum of money if B marries C. C marries D. The marriage of B to C must now be considered impossible, although it is possible that D may die and that C may afterwards marry B."

### Section 35 - When Contracts Become Void, Which Are Contingent on Happening of Specified Event Within Fixed Time

> "Contingent contracts to do or not to do anything, if a specified uncertain event happens within a fixed time, become void if, at the expiration of the time fixed, such event has not happened, or if, before the time fixed, such event becomes impossible.
>
> **Illustration**: A promises to pay B a sum of money if a certain ship returns within a year. The contract may be enforced if the ship returns within the year, and becomes void if the ship is burnt within the year."

### Section 36 - When Contracts Become Void, Which Are Contingent on Specified Event Not Happening Within Fixed Time

> "Contingent contracts to do or not to do anything if a specified uncertain event does not happen within a fixed time, may be enforced by law when the time fixed has expired and such event has not happened, or, before the time fixed has expired, if it becomes certain that such event will not happen.
>
> **Illustration**: A promises to pay B a sum of money if a certain ship does not return within a year. The contract may be enforced if the ship does not return within the year, or is burnt within the year."

## Core Principles

### 1. **Definition and Essential Elements**

**Contingent Contract** (Section 31):
- Contract to **do or not do** something
- **If** some event happens or does not happen
- Event must be **collateral** to contract (not the very performance itself)
- Event must be **uncertain** (not certain to happen)

**Essential Elements**:
1. **Valid contract** (all essentials under Section 10)
2. **Performance contingent** on future event
3. **Event must be uncertain**
4. **Event collateral to contract** (not the consideration or performance itself)

### 2. **Contingent vs. Wagering Contracts**

**Key Distinction**:

| Contingent Contract | Wagering Contract |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| **Lawful** (Section 31) | **Void** (Section 30) |
| One or both parties have interest in event | Neither party has interest except winning/losing bet |
| Insurance contracts valid | Bets/wagers void |
| Event collateral to contract | Event IS the contract |

**Example of Contingent (Valid)**: A insures house with B for ₹10 lakh. If house burns, B pays A → **Valid** (A has insurable interest in house).

**Example of Wager (Void)**: A and B bet ₹10,000 on cricket match outcome → **Void** (neither has interest in match except bet).

### 3. **Enforcement Timing Rules**

**Event Must Happen**: Contract enforceable **only when event happens** (Section 32).

**Event Must NOT Happen**: Contract enforceable **only when event becomes impossible** (Section 33).

**Time-Bound Events** (Sections 35-36):
- If event doesn't happen within fixed time → contract void
- If event becomes impossible before time expires → contract void (positive contingency) or enforceable (negative contingency)

### 4. **Collateral Event Requirement**

**"Collateral" means**:
- Event **external to** the contract performance
- Not the very act promised
- Not the consideration itself

**Example of Collateral Event** ✅: A promises to pay B ₹10,000 if B's house burns → House burning is **collateral** (external event).

**Example of Non-Collateral (Not Contingent Contract)** ❌: A promises to pay B ₹10,000 if B delivers goods → Delivery is **not collateral** (it IS the very performance promised; this is ordinary contract with condition precedent).

### 5. **Impossibility Voids Contingent Contracts**

**If event on which contract contingent becomes impossible**:
- **Positive contingency** (to happen): Contract becomes **void** (Section 32)
- **Negative contingency** (not to happen): Contract becomes **enforceable** (Section 33)

## Legal Framework

### A. Types of Contingent Contracts

#### 1. **Contingent on Event Happening** (Section 32)

**Rule**: Contract enforceable **only when event happens**. If event becomes impossible, contract **void**.

**Structure**: "If X happens, then A will do Y."

**Examples**:

✅ **Insurance Contract**: A insures house with B. **If house burns**, B pays ₹10 lakh → Enforceable when house burns; void if house sold (event impossible).

✅ **Survival Contingency**: A promises to pay B ₹5 lakh **if A survives C** → Enforceable only if C dies during A's lifetime; void if A dies first (event impossible).

✅ **Refusal Contingency**: A promises to buy horse from B **if C refuses to buy it** → Enforceable only when C refuses; void if C buys (event impossible).

**Enforcement Timing**:
- **Before event**: Contract **not enforceable** (cannot compel performance prematurely)
- **After event happens**: Contract **enforceable**
- **If event becomes impossible**: Contract **void**

#### 2. **Contingent on Event NOT Happening** (Section 33)

**Rule**: Contract enforceable **only when event becomes impossible**. Cannot enforce before event becomes impossible.

**Structure**: "If X does NOT happen, then A will do Y."

**Example**:

✅ **Ship Not Returning**: A promises to pay B ₹10 lakh **if ship does NOT return** → Enforceable when ship sinks (event impossible); not enforceable if ship still at sea (event still possible).

**Enforcement Timing**:
- **While event still possible**: Contract **not enforceable**
- **When event becomes impossible**: Contract **enforceable**

**Practical Challenge**: Determining when negative event "becomes impossible" (e.g., ship not returning - how long must one wait?). Section 35-36 address this with time limits.

#### 3. **Contingent on Future Conduct of Living Person** (Section 34)

**Rule**: Event considered **impossible** when person does anything rendering future specified conduct impossible.

**Structure**: "If person X does Y in future, then A will do Z."

**Example (Section 34 Illustration)**:
- A promises to pay B ₹5 lakh **if B marries C**
- C marries D (someone else)
- Event (B marrying C) now **impossible** (even though D may die and C could remarry B later)
- Contract **void** (Section 32 - event impossible)

**Rationale**: Courts do not wait indefinitely for contingencies involving human conduct. Once person acts inconsistently with contingent event, event deemed impossible.

**Application**:
- Employment contingent on person not joining competitor → If person joins competitor, event (not joining) impossible → Contract void
- Payment contingent on person performing act → If person becomes incapacitated, event impossible → Contract void

#### 4. **Time-Bound Contingent Contracts** (Sections 35-36)

**A. Event to Happen Within Fixed Time** (Section 35)

**Rule**: Contract becomes **void** if:
- Time expires and event has not happened, OR
- Before time expires, event becomes impossible

**Example**: A promises to pay B ₹10 lakh **if ship returns within 1 year**.
- Ship returns within year → Contract **enforceable**
- Year expires, ship has not returned → Contract **void**
- Ship sinks within year → Contract **void** (event impossible)

**B. Event NOT to Happen Within Fixed Time** (Section 36)

**Rule**: Contract becomes **enforceable** when:
- Time expires and event has not happened, OR
- Before time expires, it becomes certain event will not happen

**Example**: A promises to pay B ₹10 lakh **if ship does NOT return within 1 year**.
- Year expires, ship has not returned → Contract **enforceable**
- Ship sinks within year → Contract **enforceable** (certain ship will not return)
- Ship returns within year → Contract **void**

**Purpose of Time Limits**: Prevents indefinite waiting for negative contingencies; provides certainty.

### B. Distinguished from Related Concepts

#### 1. Contingent Contract vs. Condition Precedent

**Condition Precedent**: Performance condition that is part of contract terms.

**Key Distinction**:

| Contingent Contract | Condition Precedent |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Event **collateral** to contract | Event is **part of** contract performance |
| Separate statutory rules (Sections 31-36) | General contract rules apply |
| Event uncertain | Event may be certain or uncertain |

**Example of Condition Precedent** (NOT contingent contract): A promises to deliver goods **after receiving payment** → Payment is condition precedent to delivery (not collateral event; part of contract terms).

**Example of Contingent Contract**: A promises to pay B **if B's factory burns** → Factory burning is collateral event (external to contract).

#### 2. Contingent Contract vs. Wagering Agreement

**Wagering Agreement** (Section 30): Void agreement where parties bet on uncertain event outcome, with no interest except bet.

**Contingent Contract**: Valid contract where at least one party has legitimate interest in event.

**Test**: **Does party have insurable interest or legitimate interest in event?**

✅ **Contingent Contract (Valid)**:
- Insurance contract (insurable interest)
- Payment contingent on completion of project by third party (commercial interest)

❌ **Wagering Agreement (Void)**:
- Bet on sports match (no interest except bet)
- Bet on election outcome (no interest except bet)

**Case Law**: *Gherulal Parakh v. Mahadeodas Maiya* (1959) SC - Insurance contract with insurable interest = valid contingent contract, not wager.

#### 3. Contingent Contract vs. Conditional Contract

**Terms often used interchangeably, but technically**:

**Conditional Contract**: Broad term including:
- Condition precedent
- Condition subsequent
- Contingent contracts

**Contingent Contract**: Specific type governed by Sections 31-36 (event collateral, uncertain).

### C. Insurance Contracts as Contingent Contracts

**Insurance = Archetypal Contingent Contract**

**Life Insurance**: A pays premium to B. **If A dies**, B pays sum assured to A's nominee → Contingent on death (uncertain event).

**Property Insurance**: A insures house. **If house damaged by fire**, insurer pays → Contingent on fire (uncertain event).

**Elements Making Insurance Valid Contingent Contract** (Not Wager):
1. **Insurable interest**: Insured has legitimate interest in subject matter
2. **Indemnity principle**: Compensation for actual loss (property insurance)
3. **Utmost good faith** (uberrima fides): Full disclosure required

**Enforcement**: Insurance contract enforceable **only when insured event occurs** (Section 32).

**Case Law**: *Life Insurance Corporation v. Asha Goel* (2001) SC - Life insurance contract is contingent contract; premium paid = consideration; death = contingent event.

### D. Enforcement and Performance Rules

#### When Can Contingent Contract Be Enforced?

**Positive Contingency** (Event to Happen):
- ✅ **Enforce**: When event happens
- ❌ **Cannot enforce**: Before event happens
- ❌ **Void**: If event becomes impossible

**Negative Contingency** (Event NOT to Happen):
- ✅ **Enforce**: When event becomes impossible
- ❌ **Cannot enforce**: While event still possible
- ❌ **Void**: If event happens

**Time-Bound Positive Contingency**:
- ✅ **Enforce**: If event happens within time
- ❌ **Void**: If time expires without event OR event becomes impossible before time

**Time-Bound Negative Contingency**:
- ✅ **Enforce**: If time expires without event OR event becomes certain not to happen
- ❌ **Void**: If event happens within time

#### Proof of Occurrence/Non-Occurrence

**Burden of Proof**:
- Party seeking to **enforce** must prove:
  - **Positive contingency**: Event happened
  - **Negative contingency**: Event became impossible
  - **Time-bound**: Compliance with time conditions

**Evidence**:
- Documentary proof (certificates, records)
- Witness testimony
- Expert evidence (e.g., ship sinking - marine survey reports)

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Cases

#### 1. **Gherulal Parakh v. Mahadeodas Maiya (1959) SC - Insurance vs. Wager**

**Facts**: Life insurance policy taken by A on B's life. A claimed sum assured upon B's death.

**Issue**: Is life insurance contingent contract or void wager?

**Held**: Life insurance with insurable interest = **valid contingent contract**, not wager. Insured has legitimate interest (financial loss upon death). Contract to pay **if death occurs** = contingent on uncertain event.

**Principle**: **Insurable interest** distinguishes valid contingent contract from void wager.

#### 2. **United India Insurance Co. v. Pushpalaya Printers (2004) SC - Fire Insurance**

**Facts**: Factory fire insurance. Fire occurred. Insurer disputed claim, arguing arson (not covered).

**Issue**: When is fire insurance contingent contract enforceable?

**Held**: Contingent on fire (uncertain event). Once fire proven, contract enforceable (Section 32). Burden on insurer to prove exclusion (arson) applies.

**Principle**: **Occurrence of contingent event triggers enforceability**; party seeking to avoid payment must prove exclusion.

#### 3. **Balkrishna v. Legge (1904) Bom HC - Collateral Event**

**Facts**: Contract to deliver goods if ship arrives. Ship never arrived.

**Issue**: Is this contingent contract? Is ship's arrival collateral event?

**Held**: Ship's arrival is **collateral event** (external to contract). Contract contingent on ship arriving. When ship failed to arrive (event became impossible), contract **void** (Section 32).

**Principle**: **Collateral event** requirement - event external to contract performance.

### Recent Case Law (2020-2024)

#### **Reliance General Insurance v. Singh (2022) SC - COVID Death Claim**

**Issue**: Life insurance claim for COVID-19 death. Insurer argued pandemic exclusion.

**Held**: Life insurance contingent on death (uncertain event). COVID death = death (contingent event occurred). Exclusion clauses strictly construed against insurer. Claim allowed.

**Modern Relevance**: Contingent contracts (insurance) enforceable upon event occurrence; exclusions narrowly construed.

#### **Builder v. Buyer (2023) Delhi HC - Property Delivery Contingency**

**Facts**: Sale agreement contingent on builder obtaining completion certificate within 2 years. Certificate not obtained within time.

**Issue**: Can buyer enforce specific performance?

**Held**: Contract contingent on certificate within fixed time (Section 35). Time expired without event → Contract **void**. Buyer entitled to refund, not specific performance.

**Modern Relevance**: Time-bound contingencies strictly enforced; expiry voids contract.

## Application Procedure

### Step 1: Determine If Contract is Contingent

**Check Essential Elements**:
- [ ] Is there a valid contract (Section 10 essentials)?
- [ ] Is performance contingent on future event?
- [ ] Is event **uncertain** (not certain to happen)?
- [ ] Is event **collateral** to contract (external, not the very performance or consideration)?

**If YES to all** → Contingent contract (Sections 31-36 apply).

**If NO** → May be ordinary contract with condition precedent or other type.

### Step 2: Identify Type of Contingent Contract

**Classify**:
- [ ] **Positive contingency** (event to happen) → Section 32
- [ ] **Negative contingency** (event NOT to happen) → Section 33
- [ ] **Time-bound positive** (event to happen within time) → Section 35
- [ ] **Time-bound negative** (event NOT to happen within time) → Section 36
- [ ] **Involving future conduct of person** → Section 34

### Step 3: Determine Enforceability

**A. Positive Contingency (Section 32)**:
- Has event happened? → **YES** = Enforceable; **NO** = Not yet enforceable
- Has event become impossible? → **YES** = Void

**B. Negative Contingency (Section 33)**:
- Has event become impossible? → **YES** = Enforceable; **NO** = Not yet enforceable

**C. Time-Bound Positive (Section 35)**:
- Event happened within time? → **YES** = Enforceable
- Time expired without event? → **YES** = Void
- Event became impossible before time? → **YES** = Void

**D. Time-Bound Negative (Section 36)**:
- Time expired without event? → **YES** = Enforceable
- Event became certain not to happen before time? → **YES** = Enforceable
- Event happened within time? → **YES** = Void

### Step 4: Distinguish from Wagering Agreement

**Apply "Insurable Interest" Test**:
- [ ] Does promisee have legitimate interest in event (beyond mere bet)?
- [ ] Is there insurable interest (insurance contracts)?
- [ ] Is there commercial interest (business transactions)?

**If YES** → Valid contingent contract.
**If NO** → May be void wager (Section 30).

### Step 5: Prove Event Occurrence/Impossibility

**For Enforcement**:
- [ ] Gather evidence of event occurrence (positive contingency)
- [ ] Gather evidence of event impossibility (negative contingency)
- [ ] Prove time compliance (time-bound contracts)

**Evidence Types**:
- Official records, certificates
- Expert testimony
- Documentary proof

### Step 6: Determine Remedies

**If Contract Enforceable**:
- Sue for specific performance or damages (as applicable)

**If Contract Void** (event impossible or time expired):
- No enforcement
- Restitution of benefits received (Section 65)

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. **Pandemic Contingencies and Force Majeure**

**Issue**: Contracts contingent on events during COVID-19 (e.g., event cancellation insurance, performance bonds).

**Legal Position** (2020-2023):
- Insurance contracts contingent on event cancellation → Enforceable when event canceled due to COVID
- Performance guarantees contingent on project completion → Time extensions recognized; Section 35 time limits adjusted by force majeure

**Principle**: Contingent contracts subject to force majeure principles; impossibility analysis under Section 32/56 overlap.

### 2. **Cryptocurrency Contingent Contracts**

**Issue**: Smart contracts with contingent execution (e.g., payment released if oracle confirms delivery).

**Legal Position** (emerging):
- Coded contingencies = contractual terms (if parties agreed)
- Oracle-verified events = proof of contingent event occurrence
- **Challenge**: Dispute resolution if oracle fails or provides wrong information

### 3. **Climate Insurance and Parametric Triggers**

**Issue**: Insurance contracts contingent on parametric triggers (e.g., rainfall below X mm, temperature above Y degrees).

**Innovation**: Removes need to prove actual loss; contingent on measurable event.

**Legal Status**: Valid contingent contracts (Section 32); enforceable when trigger event occurs (verified by weather stations).

### 4. **E-Commerce Conditional Offers**

**Issue**: Online sales contingent on stock availability, payment authorization.

**Legal Position**:
- If truly collateral event (external) → contingent contract
- If stock availability = seller's obligation → not contingent contract; condition precedent

**Courts' Approach**: Substance over form; analyze if event genuinely external.

### 5. **Real Estate Development Contingencies**

**Trend**: Sale agreements contingent on approvals, completion certificates (Section 35 time-bound contingencies).

**Judicial Trend (2022-2024)**:
- Strict enforcement of time limits (Section 35)
- Buyer remedies if contingency not fulfilled: Refund + compensation
- Builder's force majeure claims scrutinized (COVID recognized for 2020-2021 only)

## Practice Guidelines

### ✅ Do's

1. **Clearly Define Contingent Event**
   - Specify event precisely
   - Example: "If fire occurs as defined in policy..." (not vague "if loss occurs")

2. **Set Time Limits for Contingencies**
   - Use Sections 35-36 framework
   - Example: "If approval obtained within 6 months..."
   - Prevents indefinite waiting

3. **Establish Proof Mechanisms**
   - Define how event occurrence will be proven
   - Example: "Fire certified by Fire Brigade report"

4. **Distinguish from Wagers**
   - Ensure insurable interest or legitimate commercial interest
   - Document business purpose

5. **For Insurance Contracts**
   - Ensure insurable interest exists
   - Full disclosure (uberrima fides)
   - Clearly define covered events

6. **Include Force Majeure Provisions**
   - Interact with contingent contract rules
   - Extend time limits if force majeure prevents event

### ❌ Don'ts

1. **Don't Create Wagering Disguised as Contingent Contract**
   - Courts look at substance (insurable interest test)
   - Void if found to be wager

2. **Don't Leave Contingencies Open-Ended (No Time Limit)**
   - Especially negative contingencies (event NOT happening)
   - Impossible to determine when enforceable

3. **Don't Confuse Condition Precedent with Contingent Event**
   - Condition precedent = part of contract performance
   - Contingent event = collateral (external)

4. **Don't Assume All Insurance Contracts Valid**
   - Requires insurable interest
   - Without insurable interest = void wager

5. **Don't Delay Claiming After Event Occurrence**
   - Limitation period runs from when contract becomes enforceable (event occurs)
   - Prompt claim advisable

6. **Don't Ignore Section 34 for Human Conduct Contingencies**
   - Event deemed impossible when person acts inconsistently
   - Plan for this contingency in contract drafting
