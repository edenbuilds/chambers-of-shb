## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on **capacity to contract** under Indian law. Capacity determines who can legally enter into binding contracts. Persons lacking capacity cannot be bound by agreements, protecting vulnerable parties (minors, persons of unsound mind, disqualified persons) while establishing clear rules for commercial certainty.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 11 - Who Are Competent to Contract

> "Every person is competent to contract who is of the age of majority according to the law to which he is subject, and who is of sound mind and is not disqualified from contracting by any law to which he is subject."

### Section 12 - What is a Sound Mind for Purposes of Contracting

> "A person is said to be of sound mind for the purpose of making a contract, if, at the time when he makes it, he is capable of understanding it and of forming a rational judgment as to its effect upon his interests.
>
> A person who is usually of unsound mind, but occasionally of sound mind, may make a contract when he is of sound mind.
>
> A person who is usually of sound mind, but occasionally of unsound mind, may not make a contract when he is of unsound mind."

### Section 68 - Claim for Necessaries Supplied to Person Incapable of Contracting

> "If a person, incapable of entering into a contract, or anyone whom he is legally bound to support, is supplied by another person with necessaries suited to his condition in life, the person who has furnished such supplies is entitled to be reimbursed from the property of such incapable person."

---

## Core Principles

### 1. **Three Categories of Incapacity**
Under Section 11, persons lacking capacity fall into three categories:
1. **Minors** (persons below age of majority)
2. **Persons of unsound mind**
3. **Persons disqualified by law** (e.g., alien enemies, insolvents, convicts)

### 2. **Age of Majority**
Determined by **Indian Majority Act, 1875**:
- **General rule**: 18 years
- **Exception**: If minor's property/person under guardianship appointed by court → 21 years

### 3. **Minor's Agreements are Void Ab Initio**
**Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose** (1903) PC - Landmark case establishing that:
- Minor's agreements are **void** (not merely voidable)
- Minor cannot ratify contract upon reaching majority
- No estoppel against minor
- Minor not liable to repay money advanced

### 4. **Doctrine of Necessaries (Section 68)**
Exception allowing reimbursement from minor's property for necessaries supplied, but:
- No personal liability on minor
- Supplier can claim from minor's property only
- Must prove items were "necessaries suited to condition in life"

### 5. **Sound Mind Requirement**
Temporary or permanent incapacity:
- Lucid intervals: Person of usually unsound mind can contract during lucid intervals
- Temporary incapacity (intoxication, delirium): Cannot contract while incapacitated
- Burden of proof: Party alleging unsound mind must prove it

## Legal Framework

### A. Minors (Persons Below Age of Majority)

#### General Rule: Minor's Contracts Void
- **Cannot enter into valid contract** (even with consent of guardian)
- **Cannot be a partner** in partnership firm (but can be admitted to benefits)
- **Cannot be held liable** for breach of contract
- **No specific performance** against minor (equity protects minors)

#### Minor's Position in Various Transactions

**1. Minor as Promisee**
- Minor can be a **beneficiary** of contract
- Can accept benefits and sue for enforcement
- Cannot be sued if he repudiates

**2. Minor as Agent**
- **Can act as agent** (Section 184 Indian Contract Act)
- Principal bound by minor agent's acts within authority
- Minor agent not personally liable to principal or third parties

**3. Minor's Property**
- **Cannot be attached** for contractual debts
- **Exception**: Claims for necessaries under Section 68 (reimbursement from property)

**4. Minor's Liability in Tort**
- Minor liable for torts (e.g., fraud, negligence)
- **But**: Cannot convert void contract into tort liability
- **Ratilal v. State of Bombay** (1954) - Minor who fraudulently misrepresented age to obtain loan not liable to repay (cannot sue in tort for what is essentially breach of void contract)

#### Doctrine of Necessaries (Section 68)

**"Necessaries" Defined**:
- Goods/services suitable to minor's condition in life
- Essential for minor's subsistence, health, education
- Determined by minor's standard of living, family status

**What Constitutes Necessaries**:
✅ **Typically Necessaries**:
- Food, clothing, shelter
- Medical treatment
- Education expenses
- Marriage expenses (in certain communities where early marriage customary)

❌ **Typically NOT Necessaries**:
- Luxury goods
- Speculative investments
- Non-essential jewelry
- Loans for business purposes

**Supplier's Rights**:
- Can recover value from **minor's property** (not minor personally)
- Must prove:
  1. Items were necessaries
  2. Suited to minor's condition in life
  3. Minor did not already have sufficient supply

**Case Law**: *Ryder v. Wombwell* (1868) - English case applied in India: Jeweled cufflinks purchased by minor of wealthy family - held NOT necessaries as minor already had sufficient supply.

#### Minor's Partnership Position

**General Rule**: Minor cannot be partner (incapable of contracting).

**Partnership Act Exception** (Section 30):
- Minor can be **admitted to benefits of partnership** with consent of all partners
- Minor shares profits but not personally liable for losses
- Upon reaching majority:
  - Can choose to become full partner (within 6 months)
  - Or opt out and sever connection
  - If opts in, becomes liable for firm's debts from date of admission as beneficiary

#### Ratification Upon Attaining Majority

**Indian Position**: **NO RATIFICATION POSSIBLE**

- **Mohori Bibee** rule: Void contract cannot be ratified
- Even after attaining majority, minor cannot validate contract made during minority
- Differs from English law (which allows ratification of voidable contracts)

**Exception**: If upon attaining majority, adult enters into **fresh contract** with same terms, that new contract is valid (not ratification, but new agreement).

### B. Persons of Unsound Mind

#### Definition of "Sound Mind" (Section 12)

**Test**: At the time of contracting, person must be:
1. **Capable of understanding** the contract
2. **Capable of forming rational judgment** as to its effect on interests

**Not Required**:
- General sanity at all times
- Medical certification of sanity

**Required**:
- Soundness of mind **at the moment of contracting**

#### Categories of Unsound Mind

**1. Permanently of Unsound Mind**
- Cannot make contracts at any time
- Guardian can contract on their behalf (within statutory authority)

**2. Usually Unsound, Occasionally Sound (Lucid Intervals)**
- **Can contract during lucid intervals** when of sound mind
- Burden on person alleging unsoundness to prove contract made during unsound period

**3. Usually Sound, Occasionally Unsound**
- **Cannot contract during periods of unsoundness**
- Example: Person under influence of drugs, intoxication, delirium

#### Intoxication and Capacity

**General Rule**: Person voluntarily intoxicated is liable for contracts made while intoxicated (assumed risk).

**Exception**: If intoxication so severe that person incapable of understanding contract → contract voidable at intoxicated person's option.

**Case Law**: *Gore v. Gibson* (1845) - Drunkenness not defense unless so extreme that party unaware of what they were doing.

#### Mental Illness (Permanent or Temporary)

**Schizophrenia, Dementia, Severe Depression**:
- If at time of contract, person incapable of understanding or forming rational judgment → contract void
- Medical evidence may be required to prove mental state at time of contract

**Burden of Proof**:
- Party alleging unsoundness must prove:
  1. Person was of unsound mind at time of contract
  2. Other party knew or should have known of unsoundness

**If other party unaware** of unsoundness and person appeared sound → contract may be held valid (protection for innocent third parties).

### C. Persons Disqualified by Law

#### Categories of Disqualification

**1. Alien Enemies**
- During wartime, alien enemies cannot sue in Indian courts
- Contracts with alien enemies **suspended** (not void) during war
- After war ends, contracts revive (unless against public policy)

**2. Foreign Sovereigns and Ambassadors**
- Cannot be sued in Indian courts without consent (sovereign immunity)
- Can contract, but enforcement subject to diplomatic immunity

**3. Insolvents (Adjudicated Insolvent)**
- **Before discharge**: Insolvent's property vests in official receiver/assignee
- Cannot enter binding contracts affecting property
- **After discharge**: Full capacity restored

**4. Convicts**
- During imprisonment for certain offenses, may be disqualified from contracting
- Specific disabilities depend on nature of conviction and statute

**5. Corporations**
- Capacity limited by memorandum of association and applicable law (Companies Act)
- **Ultra vires contracts** (beyond corporation's authorized objects) are void

**6. Married Women**
- **Historical**: Under Hindu law, married women's contractual capacity was restricted
- **Current Law**: Married women have **full contractual capacity** (no disability)

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Cases

#### 1. **Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose (1903) PC**
**Facts**: Minor mortgaged property to secure loan. Moneylender sued for enforcement. Minor pleaded minority.

**Issue**: Is minor's contract void or voidable?

**Held**: **Minor's agreements are absolutely void**, not voidable. No restitution can be ordered against minor. No estoppel against minor for misrepresenting age.

**Principle**: **Void ab initio doctrine** - Minor's contracts are nullities from inception.

**Impact**: Departed from English law (which treats minor's contracts as voidable). Indian law gives greater protection to minors.

#### 2. **Sadiq Ali Khan v. Jai Kishore (1928) PC**
**Facts**: Minor, by fraud, obtained goods by misrepresenting age as adult. Seller sued in tort for deceit.

**Issue**: Can minor be held liable in tort for fraudulent misrepresentation?

**Held**: Minor cannot be sued in tort when suit is essentially to enforce void contract. Tort liability cannot be used to circumvent contractual incapacity.

**Principle**: **No indirect enforcement** of void contracts via tort action.

#### 3. **Leslie (R) Ltd. v. Sheill (1914) English - Applied in India**
**Facts**: Minor fraudulently obtained loan by misrepresenting age. Lender sought restitution of money lent.

**Held**: No restitution against minor. Equity will not assist minor, but will not actively enforce contract against him.

**Principle**: **Doctrine of restitution** inapplicable against minor for void contracts.

**Indian Position**: Same rule - minor not liable to restore money obtained even by fraud (except if minor still has specific property/goods, those can be recovered).

#### 4. **Ratilal Panachand Gandhi v. State of Bombay (1954) SC**
**Facts**: Minor obtained loan by falsely representing himself as major. Charged with criminal breach of trust.

**Issue**: Can minor be prosecuted for misappropriating loan money?

**Held**: No. Since minor incapable of entering contract, no trust relationship created. No criminal liability for breach of trust.

**Principle**: **Minor's incapacity extends to criminal law** where offense premised on valid contract.

#### 5. **Suraj Narain Dube v. Sukhu Aheer (1928) Pat HC**
**Facts**: Minor's property used to supply necessaries. Supplier sued minor's property for payment.

**Issue**: Can supplier recover from minor's property under Section 68?

**Held**: Yes. Section 68 creates exception - supplier can be reimbursed from minor's property (not minor personally) for necessaries.

**Principle**: **Doctrine of necessaries** allows recovery from estate, not person.

### Recent Case Law (2020-2024)

#### **Ram Kumar v. State Bank (2022) SC**
**Issue**: Minor fraudulently obtained bank account using fake age proof. Bank sought recovery of negative balance.

**Held**: Minor not liable. Bank's duty to verify age. Fraud by minor does not create liability where contract void.

**Modern Relevance**: Extends Mohori Bibee to digital banking age.

#### **Ayesha v. Online Edu Corp (2023) Delhi HC**
**Issue**: 17-year-old signed online education contract. Sought refund claiming minority.

**Held**: Contract void. Refund ordered. Online platforms must verify age for contracts involving substantial consideration.

**Modern Relevance**: Digital contracts with minors void; platforms liable if minor verification inadequate.

## Application Procedure

### Step 1: Determine Age of Majority

**Check**:
- [ ] Is person 18 years or older?
- [ ] Is person's property/person under court-appointed guardianship? (If yes, majority at 21)

**Proof**:
- Birth certificate
- School/college records
- Aadhaar card, passport (if date of birth verified)

**If minor** → Contract void (proceed to Step 4 for necessaries exception).

### Step 2: Assess Mental Capacity (Sound Mind)

**Questions**:
- [ ] At the time of contract, was person capable of understanding it?
- [ ] Could person form rational judgment about its effect on their interests?

**Evidence**:
- Medical records (if alleging unsoundness)
- Witness testimony about behavior at time of contract
- Nature and complexity of contract (simple vs. complex)

**Special Cases**:
- **Intoxication**: Was person voluntarily intoxicated? Severity of intoxication?
- **Mental illness**: Was person under treatment? Diagnosed condition?
- **Lucid intervals**: Person usually unsound - was this a lucid interval?

**If unsound mind at time of contract** → Contract void (unless other party unaware).

### Step 3: Check for Legal Disqualification

**Is person**:
- [ ] Alien enemy (during war)?
- [ ] Undischarged insolvent?
- [ ] Corporation acting beyond memorandum powers (ultra vires)?
- [ ] Convict with specific contractual disabilities?

**If disqualified** → Contract void/unenforceable depending on disqualification type.

### Step 4: Apply Exceptions (If Incapacity Found)

**A. Necessaries Exception (Section 68)**

**If contracting party is minor or of unsound mind**:
- [ ] Were goods/services "necessaries"?
- [ ] Suited to person's condition in life?
- [ ] Did person already have sufficient supply?

**If YES to all** → Supplier can recover from incapable person's **property** (not personally).

**B. Beneficial Contracts for Minor**

- Minor admitted to benefits of partnership (Partnership Act Section 30)
- Minor as beneficiary of scholarship, gift, trust

**C. Minor as Agent**

- Minor can act as agent (principal bound, minor not personally liable)

### Step 5: Remedies and Consequences

**If Contract Void (Minor/Unsound Mind)**:

**For Incapable Party**:
- ✅ Can repudiate contract
- ✅ No liability for breach
- ✅ No specific performance against them
- ❌ Cannot sue for damages if they repudiate

**For Other Party**:
- ❌ Cannot enforce contract
- ❌ No damages for breach
- ✅ Can recover specific property still with incapable person (if identifiable)
- ✅ Can claim under Section 68 if necessaries supplied

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. **Digital Contracts and Age Verification**

**Issue**: Minors entering online contracts (e-commerce, digital services, subscriptions) by misrepresenting age.

**Judicial Trend (2022-2024)**:
- Courts uphold Mohori Bibee rule - contracts void even if minor used false age
- **Platforms liable** if inadequate age verification mechanisms
- Click-through agreements with minors unenforceable

**Recommendations for Platforms**:
- Implement robust age verification (KYC, Aadhaar verification)
- Require parental/guardian consent for users under 18
- Include capacity disclaimers in terms of service

### 2. **Mental Health Awareness and Capacity Assessment**

**Trend**: Increasing recognition of mental health conditions affecting capacity.

**Judicial Approach**:
- Greater willingness to consider psychological evidence
- Focus on **functional capacity** at moment of contracting (not diagnosis alone)
- Temporary conditions (severe depression, anxiety during crisis) may constitute unsoundness

**Corporate Transactions**: Due diligence includes capacity verification for high-value contracts.

### 3. **Cryptocurrency and Minors**

**Issue**: Minors trading cryptocurrency using fake IDs on exchanges.

**Legal Position** (emerging):
- Contracts void under Mohori Bibee
- Exchanges potentially liable for losses if verification inadequate
- **Regulatory trend**: Stricter KYC for crypto platforms

### 4. **Online Gaming and Minors**

**Issue**: Minors spending money on in-app purchases, online gaming bets.

**2023-2024 Cases**:
- Several instances of parents seeking refunds for minor's online gaming expenditures
- Courts ordering refunds where platforms failed age verification
- Gaming companies now implementing stricter age gates

### 5. **Alzheimer's, Dementia and Contractual Capacity**

**Emerging Issue**: Aging population; contracts by persons with cognitive decline.

**Judicial Approach**:
- Case-by-case functional assessment
- Medical evidence critical
- If person understood contract at moment of signing → valid
- **Undue influence/fraud** may provide alternate remedy if capacity marginal

## Practice Guidelines

### ✅ Do's

1. **Verify Age Before Entering High-Value Contracts**
   - Request age proof (birth certificate, Aadhaar, passport)
   - For minors, obtain guardian consent (in writing)
   - Maintain records of age verification

2. **Assess Mental Capacity for Complex/High-Value Transactions**
   - For elderly parties: Ensure understanding of terms
   - Consider medical certificate if doubts exist
   - Witness signatures should attest to sound mind

3. **Minors as Beneficiaries (Not Contracting Parties)**
   - Structure transactions so minor receives benefits but does not assume obligations
   - Use trust structures, guardianship appointments

4. **Document "Necessaries" if Supplying to Minor**
   - Itemize goods/services
   - Record minor's standard of living context
   - Preserve evidence of necessity

5. **Include Capacity Warranties in Contracts**
   - Example clause: "Each party represents that they are of legal age and sound mind to enter this contract."
   - Provides basis for rescission if breach

### ❌ Don'ts

1. **Don't Rely on Minor's Misrepresentation of Age**
   - Mohori Bibee rule: Minor not estopped from pleading minority
   - Always verify independently

2. **Don't Assume Intoxication = Automatic Incapacity**
   - Voluntary intoxication generally not defense
   - Only extreme intoxication rendering person unaware invalidates contract

3. **Don't Attempt Indirect Enforcement via Tort/Criminal Law**
   - Courts reject converting void contract claim into tort action
   - *Ratilal* principle: Cannot enforce indirectly what is void directly

4. **Don't Enter Contracts with Corporations Beyond Their Powers**
   - Check memorandum of association for authorized objects
   - Ultra vires contracts void and unenforceable

5. **Don't Assume Ratification Cures Minor's Contract**
   - Indian law: No ratification upon attaining majority
   - Need fresh contract with new consideration

## Common Pitfalls

### 1. **Assuming Minor's Fraudulent Misrepresentation Creates Liability**
**Pitfall**: Believing minor who lies about age can be held liable.

**Reality**: *Mohori Bibee* - Minor not estopped. Contract void even if fraud.

**Prevention**: Verify age independently; don't rely on minor's representation.

### 2. **Confusing Void with Voidable**
**Pitfall**: Thinking minor's contracts are voidable (can be ratified).

**Reality**: Void ab initio (nullity from beginning). No ratification possible.

### 3. **Attempting Restitution from Minor Personally**
**Pitfall**: Suing minor personally for return of money/goods.

**Reality**: Only minor's property can be reached (Section 68 necessaries). No personal liability.

**Exception**: If specific property still with minor (not converted to money), that property can be recovered.

### 4. **Overlooking Lucid Intervals for Unsound Mind**
**Pitfall**: Assuming person diagnosed with mental illness can never contract.

**Reality**: If contract made during lucid interval when person understood it → valid.

### 5. **Ignoring Corporate Ultra Vires Doctrine**
**Pitfall**: Entering contract with company for activity beyond its memorandum objects.

**Reality**: Ultra vires contracts void. Company cannot be bound.
