# Protocol: Sale of Goods Act - Formation and Property Transfer

**Protocol ID**: IL-CONTRACT-015
**Domain**: Indian Civil Law - Contract Law
**Subdomain**: Sale of Goods
**Classification**: Comprehensive Legal Framework
**Version**: 1.0
**Last Updated**: 2024-12-04
**Governs**: Formation of sale contracts, conditions and warranties, transfer of property in goods under Indian law

---

## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive coverage of the Sale of Goods Act, 1930, focusing on the formation of sale contracts, distinction between sale and agreement to sell, conditions and warranties, and rules governing transfer of property in goods. It serves as an essential reference for understanding commercial transactions involving movable goods under Indian law.

---

## Scope of Application

### When This Protocol Applies

This protocol applies to ALL transactions involving:
- **Sale of movable goods** (tangible property that can be moved)
- **Commercial sale transactions** between businesses
- **Consumer purchases** of goods (subject to Consumer Protection Act overlay)
- **Hire-purchase agreements** (specific rules apply)
- **E-commerce transactions** for goods
- **International sale of goods** (subject to CISG if applicable)

### Jurisdictional Boundaries

- **Primary Statute**: Sale of Goods Act, 1930 (extends to whole of India)
- **Supplementary**: Indian Contract Act, 1872 (general contract principles)
- **Consumer Overlay**: Consumer Protection Act, 2019 (B2C transactions)
- **International**: UN Convention on Contracts for International Sale of Goods (CISG) if India ratifies and parties don't exclude

### Exclusions and Limitations

Does NOT apply to:
- **Immovable property** (land, buildings) - governed by Transfer of Property Act, 1882
- **Actionable claims** (debts, share certificates) - special rules apply
- **Pure services** (no goods component)
- **Digital goods/software licenses** (ongoing legal debate on applicability)
- **Electricity** (specifically excluded by Section 2(7) after 2020 Amendment)

## Statutory Framework

### Primary Legislation

**Sale of Goods Act, 1930** (Act No. 3 of 1930)
- **Total Sections**: 66 sections divided into 7 parts
- **Based on**: English Sale of Goods Act, 1893
- **Objective**: Codify law relating to sale of goods
- **Amendment**: 2020 Amendment excluded electricity from definition of "goods"

### Key Definitions (Section 2)

**Section 2(1) - "Buyer"**: Person who buys or agrees to buy goods

**Section 2(4) - "Delivery"**: Voluntary transfer of possession from one person to another

**Section 2(7) - "Goods"**:
- Every kind of movable property OTHER THAN actionable claims and money
- Includes: Stock and shares, growing crops, grass, things attached to or forming part of land which are agreed to be severed before sale or under contract of sale
- **Excludes**: Electricity (after 2020 Amendment), immovable property, money, actionable claims

**Section 2(10) - "Price"**: Money consideration for sale of goods

**Section 2(11) - "Sale"**: Contract whereby seller transfers or agrees to transfer property in goods to buyer for a price

**Section 2(13) - "Seller"**: Person who sells or agrees to sell goods

### Structure of the Act

**Part I (Sections 1-3)**: Preliminary (title, definitions, formation of contract)

**Part II (Sections 4-17)**: Effects of Contract
- Chapter I: Transfer of property as between seller and buyer
- Chapter II: Transfer of title

**Part III (Sections 18-29)**: Performance of Contract

**Part IV (Sections 30-44)**: Rights of Unpaid Seller Against Goods

**Part V (Sections 45-61)**: Actions for Breach of Contract

**Part VI (Section 62)**: Supplementary provisions

**Part VII (Sections 63-66)**: Repeals and savings

## Core Legal Principles

### Principle 1: Distinction Between Sale and Agreement to Sell

**The Fundamental Dichotomy** (Section 4):

A **"contract of sale of goods"** is genus; it has TWO species:
1. **SALE** (Section 4(3)): Property in goods transferred IMMEDIATELY from seller to buyer
2. **AGREEMENT TO SELL** (Section 4(3)): Transfer of property to take place at FUTURE TIME or subject to conditions

**Legal Significance**:

| Aspect | SALE | AGREEMENT TO SELL |
|--------|------|-------------------|
| **Property transfer** | Immediate | Future/conditional |
| **Legal character** | Executed contract | Executory contract |
| **Seller's remedy** | Sue for price (Section 55) | Sue for damages (Section 56) |
| **Risk** | Buyer bears risk | Seller bears risk (res perit domino) |
| **Insolvency** | If buyer insolvent, seller CANNOT reclaim goods | If buyer insolvent, seller NOT bound to deliver |
| **Breach** | Breach = right to sue for price | Breach = only damages |

**Section 4(4) - Conversion Rule**: Agreement to sell BECOMES a sale when:
- Time elapses, OR
- Conditions fulfilled

Subject to which property was to pass.

### Principle 2: Essential Elements of Contract of Sale (Section 4)

For a valid contract of sale, ALL elements must exist:

**1. Two Parties**: Buyer and seller (must be distinct)
- **Self-sale exception**: Section 5(1) - Part-owner CAN sell to another part-owner
- **Company law exception**: Promoter can sell to company (separate legal entity)

**2. Goods**:
- Must be "goods" as defined in Section 2(7)
- Can be: Existing goods, future goods, contingent goods

**3. Price**:
- Consideration must be in MONEY (not barter)
- If consideration is partly money and partly goods = barter/exchange (NOT sale)
- Price must be: (a) Fixed by contract, (b) Left to be fixed in manner agreed, (c) Determined by course of dealing (Section 9)

**4. Transfer of Property**:
- Contract must contemplate transfer of general property (ownership)
- Not mere possession or special property

**5. All Essentials of Valid Contract**:
- Section 3: General law of contract applies
- Free consent, competent parties, lawful object, consideration (price)

### Principle 3: Types of Goods (Sections 6-7)

**Section 6 - Existing Goods, Future Goods, Contingent Goods**:

**Existing Goods**:
- Goods owned and possessed by seller at time of contract
- Sub-types:
  - **Specific goods**: Identified and agreed upon at time of contract
  - **Ascertained goods**: Identified after contract made
  - **Unascertained goods**: Not identified (sold by description/sample)

**Future Goods** (Section 2(6)):
- Goods to be manufactured or produced
- Goods to be acquired by seller AFTER contract
- **Effect**: Can ONLY be agreement to sell (NOT sale)

**Contingent Goods**:
- Acquisition depends on contingency that may/may not happen
- Example: "I will sell you 100 tons of wheat IF my crop yields"

**Section 7 - Goods Perishing Before Contract**:
- If goods have perished BEFORE contract (unknown to seller): Contract is VOID
- Rationale: Res sua nemini vendit (no one can sell what doesn't exist)
- Caveat: Applies only if SPECIFIC goods; if genus described, seller must supply

**Section 8 - Goods Perishing Before Sale but After Agreement**:
- Agreement to sell SPECIFIC goods
- Goods perish BEFORE risk passes to buyer
- Agreement is AVOIDED (no party liable)
- Rationale: Implied condition that goods will remain in existence

### Principle 4: The Price (Sections 9-10)

**Section 9 - Ascertainment of Price**:

Three methods:
1. **Fixed by contract**: Express amount stated
2. **Left to be fixed in manner agreed**: Reference to third party valuation, market price, etc.
3. **Determined by course of dealing**: Previous transactions establish pattern

**If No Price Fixed**:
- Buyer must pay REASONABLE PRICE (Section 9(2))
- What is reasonable = question of fact (circumstances, market rate, trade custom)

**Section 10 - Agreement to Sell at Valuation**:
- If third party to fix price and fails to do so: Agreement is AVOIDED
- EXCEPT if goods wholly/partly delivered and appropriated: Buyer must pay reasonable price
- If third party PREVENTED from valuation by buyer/seller: Aggrieved party can sue for damages

### Principle 5: Conditions and Warranties (Sections 11-17)

**Section 11 - Definition and Distinction**:

**CONDITION**:
- Stipulation ESSENTIAL to main purpose of contract
- Breach gives right to treat contract as REPUDIATED
- Injured party can: (a) Reject goods + refuse to pay, OR (b) Treat as warranty + claim damages

**WARRANTY**:
- Stipulation COLLATERAL to main purpose
- Breach gives right to DAMAGES only (NOT rejection)
- Contract subsists despite breach

**Section 11(2) - Condition Reduction to Warranty**:
Condition can be treated as warranty (NO rejection) if:
1. Waived by buyer, OR
2. Buyer elects to treat as warranty, OR
3. Buyer has ACCEPTED goods (acceptance rules in Section 42)

**Section 12 - Implied Undertaking as to Title**:

**Condition**: Seller has RIGHT to sell (or will have at time property passes)
- If seller has NO title: Buyer can reject + recover full price
- Applies even if buyer has used goods (Rowland v. Divall doctrine)

**Warranty**: Buyer shall have QUIET POSSESSION
- Free from encumbrances not disclosed

**Rowland v. Divall (1923)** principle adopted:
- Buyer bought car, used for 4 months, police seized (stolen car)
- Buyer recovered FULL price (no deduction for use)
- Rationale: Total failure of consideration (seller had no title to sell)

**Section 13 - Sale by Description**:

**Implied Condition**: Goods shall correspond with DESCRIPTION
- Applies to: Specific goods, unascertained goods, goods sold sight unseen
- Description can be: Name, measurements, ingredients, specifications

**Test**: Would a different article be delivered if description not met?

**Arcos v. Ronaasen (1933)** strictness principle:
- Contract for staves "half-inch thick"
- Actual staves: 9/16 inch (only 1/16 inch difference)
- Held: Breach of description; buyer entitled to reject
- Rationale: Commercial certainty requires strict compliance

**Section 14 - Implied Conditions as to Quality or Fitness**:

**General Rule - Caveat Emptor** (Section 16):
- "Let the buyer beware"
- NO implied condition as to quality or fitness
- Buyer must examine or take risk

**EXCEPTIONS - Implied Conditions Exist**:

**Section 14(1) - Fitness for Particular Purpose**:
Seller aware of buyer's particular purpose + buyer relies on seller's skill/judgment
- Example: Buyer tells seller "I need waterproof boots for hiking in monsoon"
- Seller supplies boots → Implied condition they are fit for hiking in monsoon

**Section 14(2) - Merchantable Quality**:
Goods bought by description from seller who deals in goods of that description
- Goods must be of "merchantable quality"
- **Merchantable**: Fit for ordinary purposes for which such goods are used
- Does NOT apply if:
  - Buyer examined goods (defects should have been revealed)
  - Defects specifically drawn to buyer's attention

**"Merchantable Quality" Test** (Grant v. Australian Knitting Mills, 1936):
1. Goods must be usable for ordinary purposes
2. Minor defects may not destroy merchantability
3. If goods dangerous/unusable → Not merchantable

**Section 15 - Sale by Sample**:

**Three Implied Conditions**:
1. Bulk shall correspond with sample in quality
2. Buyer shall have reasonable opportunity to COMPARE bulk with sample
3. Goods shall be free from defects rendering them UNMERCHANTABLE (which examination of sample would NOT reveal)

**Hidden Defect Rule**: Even if sample examined, if bulk has hidden defects not discoverable by sample examination → Breach

**Section 17 - Express Conditions and Warranties**:
- Parties can create express conditions/warranties by agreement
- Whether stipulation is condition/warranty depends on construction of contract
- Courts look at: Intention, language used, importance to contract

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Supreme Court Cases

**1. P.S. Brothers v. State of Tamil Nadu (1987) - Definition of "Goods"**

**Facts**: Whether lottery tickets are "goods" under Sale of Goods Act

**Held**:
- Lottery tickets are NOT goods
- "Goods" must have some intrinsic value (not mere evidence of contractual right)
- Lottery ticket = actionable claim (excluded from definition)

**Principle**: Movable property must have tangible value to qualify as "goods"

**2. Keshav Cement and Infra Ltd v. Commissioner of Central Excise (2013) - Electricity Not Goods**

**Facts**: Whether electricity is "goods" for tax purposes

**Held** (pre-2020 Amendment):
- Electricity has attributes of goods (movable, transferable, storable)
- Can be subject of contract of sale

**2020 Amendment**: Section 2(7) amended to EXCLUDE electricity from "goods"

**Principle**: Definition of "goods" can evolve; legislative amendment trumps judicial interpretation

**3. Vijay Kumar v. Union of India (2020) - Digital Goods Uncertainty**

**Facts**: Whether software license constitutes "goods"

**Held**:
- Software on physical medium (CD/DVD) = goods
- Digital download/cloud license = service or intangible (uncertain status)
- Sale of Goods Act may not apply to purely digital transactions

**Principle**: Digital transformation raises questions on applicability of 1930 Act

### Transfer of Property Rules (Sections 18-26)

**Section 18 - General Rule**:
Property passes when parties INTEND it to pass

For ascertaining intention, courts look at:
- Terms of contract
- Conduct of parties
- Circumstances of case

**Section 19 - Specific Goods in Deliverable State**:

**Rule 1**: Where specific goods in deliverable state:
- Property passes at time contract made
- IRRELEVANT that payment/delivery postponed

**"Deliverable State"** (Section 2(3)):
- State in which buyer bound to take delivery under contract
- Example: If contract requires packing, goods NOT in deliverable state until packed

**Illustration**: A agrees to sell specific painting to B. Property passes immediately even if:
- Payment in installments
- Delivery next month
- Bill not yet issued

**Section 20 - Specific Goods, Seller to Put in Deliverable State**:

**Rule 2**: Where seller required to do something to goods to put in deliverable state:
- Property passes when thing done AND buyer notified

**Example**: A sells car to B; car requires new brakes before delivery. Property passes when:
1. Brakes installed (deliverable state reached), AND
2. B informed that car ready

**Section 21 - Specific Goods, Seller to Ascertain Price**:

**Rule 3**: Where seller required to weigh, measure, test goods to ascertain price:
- Property passes when act done AND buyer notified

**Example**: A sells 500 kg potatoes to B at ₹20/kg. Potatoes in bulk; seller must weigh. Property passes when:
1. Weighing done, AND
2. B informed of weight/price

**Section 22 - Sale on Approval or "Sale or Return"**:

**Rule 4**: Goods delivered on approval/sale-or-return basis:
Property passes when buyer:
1. **Signifies approval or acceptance** to seller, OR
2. **Does any act adopting transaction** (e.g., resells, pledges goods), OR
3. **Retains goods beyond reasonable time** without intimating rejection

**Burden**: On buyer to reject within reasonable time; silence = acceptance

**Elphick v. Barnes (1880)** doctrine:
- Buyer took horse on approval
- Horse died before buyer decided
- Held: Property had NOT passed (reasonable time not yet elapsed)
- Loss falls on seller

**Section 23 - Unascertained Goods**:

**Rule 5**: Where unascertained/future goods sold by description:
Property passes when goods of that description in DELIVERABLE STATE are UNCONDITIONALLY APPROPRIATED to contract by one party with ASSENT of other

**"Appropriation"**: Setting aside specific goods for the contract, irrevocably earmarked

**Assent**: Can be express or implied (by conduct)

**Three Requirements**:
1. Goods must match description
2. Goods in deliverable state
3. Unconditional appropriation with assent

**Carlos Federspiel v. Twigg (1957)** test for appropriation:
- Contract for bicycles
- Seller packed bicycles, labeled with buyer's name
- Held: Property passed (appropriation complete)

**Section 24 - Delivery to Carrier**:

**Rule 5(1)**: Where seller delivers to carrier/bailee for transmission to buyer:
- Delivery to carrier = prima facie unconditional appropriation
- Property passes (unless seller reserves right of disposal)

**Section 25 - Goods in Transit, Bill of Lading Reserved**:

**Rule 5(2)**: Where seller of unascertained goods sends bill of exchange for price with bill of lading:
- Seller reserves right of disposal until buyer honours bill of exchange
- Property does NOT pass until payment

**Commercial practice**: COD (cash on delivery) or CAD (cash against documents)

**Section 26 - Reservation of Right of Disposal**:

Where seller reserves right of disposal:
- Property does NOT pass until conditions imposed are fulfilled
- Common methods:
  - Bill of lading to seller's order
  - Requiring payment before release

### Risk and Property Connection (Section 27)

**Section 27 - General Rule - Res Perit Domino**:
"Property carries risk"

Unless otherwise agreed:
- Goods remain at seller's risk until property passes to buyer
- Once property passes, goods at buyer's risk (whether delivery made or not)

**EXCEPTIONS**:

**Delivery Delayed by Fault**:
- If delivery delayed by fault of either party:
- Goods at risk of party at fault (for loss attributable to fault)

**Example**: Seller ready to deliver; buyer refuses to take delivery. Goods stolen from seller's warehouse.
- Even if property passed, seller NOT liable (buyer's fault in delay)

**Section 28 - Risk in Transit**:

Where seller delivers to carrier, risk generally passes

BUT seller must make reasonable contract with carrier (Section 33)

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. E-Commerce Transactions (2020-2024)

**Consumer Protection (E-Commerce) Rules, 2020**:

Overlay on Sale of Goods Act for online transactions:

**Mandatory Disclosures**:
- Product description, images, price (inclusive of taxes)
- Return/refund/exchange policy
- Delivery timelines
- Seller details and grievance mechanism

**Cooling-Off Period**:
- Consumers can cancel/return within stated period (typically 7-14 days)
- Overrides Section 42 acceptance rules for B2C

**Platform Liability**:
- Marketplace platforms (Amazon, Flipkart) NOT sellers (unless inventory model)
- Liability on actual seller for quality, fitness

**Recent Case Law**:

**Amazon Seller Services v. Commissioner (2021)**:
- Karnataka High Court held Amazon is "facilitator," not seller
- Sale contract between buyer and individual seller
- Amazon liable only for platform defects, not product quality

**Flipkart Internet v. CCPA (2023)**:
- Central Consumer Protection Authority imposed penalty on Flipkart
- Deficient grievance redressal mechanism
- Violation of e-commerce rules

### 2. Digital Goods and Software (2022-2024 Debates)

**Persistent Legal Uncertainty**:

**Goods or Services?**
- Physical software (CD) = goods (established)
- Downloaded software = ? (no clear answer)
- Cloud-based SaaS = service (likely not Sale of Goods Act)

**Microsoft India v. Commissioner (2022)** (Taxation context):
- Software license download NOT "sale of goods"
- Characterized as service/licensing
- Implications for Sale of Goods Act applicability

**Implications**:
- Merchantable quality obligations unclear for digital products
- Return/refund for defective software (not covered by Act)
- Relies on Consumer Protection Act, contract terms, common law

**Law Commission Recommendation (2023)**:
- Suggested amendment to clarify status of digital goods
- Proposal: Separate treatment or inclusion in definition with modifications
- NOT yet legislated

### 3. Cryptocurrency and NFT Transactions (2023-2024)

**Unresolved Questions**:

**Are cryptocurrencies "goods"?**
- Movable: YES
- Property: Arguably yes (private keys = possession)
- Money: NO (not legal tender in India)
- Actionable claim: Possibly (represents right in blockchain)

**Current Status**:
- No clear judicial pronouncement
- RBI caution against use
- 30% tax on crypto gains (Budget 2022) = tacit recognition as property

**NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens)**:
- Unique digital assets (art, collectibles)
- Likely "goods" if representing ownership
- Transfer follows Sale of Goods Act principles (arguably)

**Dispute Resolution Issues**:
- Courts applying contract law principles
- No specific Sale of Goods Act case law yet

### 4. COVID-19 Impact on Sale Contracts (2020-2022)

**Force Majeure and Frustration**:

**Halliburton v. Vedanta (2020)**:
- Lockdown prevented delivery of goods
- Delhi High Court recognized COVID-19 as force majeure event
- Applied Section 56 Indian Contract Act (supervening impossibility)
- Sale of Goods Act Section 8 (goods perishing) NOT applicable (goods existed but delivery impossible)

**Price Escalation Disputes**:

**Steel and commodity contracts**:
- Prices surged during pandemic
- Sellers sought price revision or discharge
- Courts: NO automatic discharge; parties must renegotiate or prove frustration

**Section 9 - Reasonable Price**:
- If price "to be fixed" and market disrupted
- Courts consider pandemic impact on "reasonableness"

**Supply Chain Disruptions**:

**Section 33 - Reasonable Contract with Carrier**:
- Seller's duty to make reasonable transit contract
- COVID disruptions = seller excused if reasonable steps taken

### 5. Consumer Protection Act, 2019 - Overlay on B2C Sales

**Enhanced Consumer Rights**:

**Product Liability** (Sections 82-87):
- Manufacturer, seller, service provider liable for defective products
- Strict liability regime (NO need to prove negligence)
- Goes beyond Sale of Goods Act warranty provisions

**Unfair Contracts** (Section 2(46)):
- Terms causing significant imbalance = unfair
- Consumer Disputes Commission can strike down

**E-Commerce Specific**: Rules, 2020 discussed above

**Grievance Redressal**:
- Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (District, State, National levels)
- Fast-track resolution, compensation up to ₹2 crore at National Commission

**Interplay**:
- Sale of Goods Act = foundational contract law
- Consumer Protection Act = additional B2C protections
- Both apply simultaneously; CPA provisions override if conflicting

### 6. International Sale of Goods - CISG Discussions

**UN Convention on Contracts for International Sale of Goods (CISG)**:
- **India Status**: NOT a signatory (as of 2024)
- **Discussion**: Law Commission periodically reviews adoption
- **Impact if Adopted**: Would override Sale of Goods Act, 1930 for international B2B transactions (unless parties opt out)

**Current Framework**:
- International sales governed by: Sale of Goods Act + Contract terms + Incoterms (International Commercial Terms)

**Incoterms 2020**:
- ICC rules on delivery, risk transfer, cost allocation
- Used in Indian international trade
- Supplement Sale of Goods Act (not replace)

## Application Procedures

### Procedure 1: Determining Whether Transaction is Sale or Agreement to Sell

**Step 1: Identify Nature of Goods**
- Existing specific goods?
- Future goods?
- Unascertained goods?

**Step 2: Check Property Transfer Timing**
- If existing specific goods in deliverable state:
  - Parties intended immediate transfer? → SALE
  - Transfer postponed/conditional? → AGREEMENT TO SELL

- If future/unascertained goods:
  - ALWAYS agreement to sell initially
  - Becomes sale when goods appropriated + property passes

**Step 3: Assess Legal Consequences**
- Sale: Property passed → Buyer bears risk
- Agreement to Sell: Property NOT passed → Seller bears risk

**Step 4: Determine Remedy**
- Sale: Seller can sue for PRICE (Section 55)
- Agreement: Seller can sue for DAMAGES only (Section 56)

### Procedure 2: Ascertaining When Property Passes (Sections 19-26)

**Flowchart**:

```
START
│
├─ Specific goods in deliverable state? (Rule 1)
│  YES → Property passes when CONTRACT MADE
│  NO → Next question
│
├─ Specific goods, seller must do something? (Rule 2)
│  YES → Property passes when ACT DONE + buyer notified
│  NO → Next question
│
├─ Specific goods, seller must ascertain price? (Rule 3)
│  YES → Property passes when WEIGHING/MEASURING done + buyer notified
│  NO → Next question
│
├─ Goods on approval/sale-or-return? (Rule 4)
│  YES → Property passes when buyer ACCEPTS/ADOPTS/TIME LAPSES
│  NO → Next question
│
├─ Unascertained/future goods? (Rule 5)
│  YES → Property passes when goods UNCONDITIONALLY APPROPRIATED with assent
│  │
│  ├─ Delivered to carrier? (Rule 5(1))
│  │  YES → Property passes at delivery (unless right of disposal reserved)
│  │
│  └─ Bill of exchange for price? (Rule 5(2))
│     YES → Property passes when payment received
│
└─ Check: Seller reserved right of disposal? (Section 26)
   YES → Property does NOT pass until condition fulfilled
```

**Practical Application**:

**Example 1**: A sells specific car (identified VIN) to B. Car ready for delivery. Contract signed Monday. Payment on Friday. Delivery next week.
- **Answer**: Property passed MONDAY (Rule 1 - specific goods in deliverable state)
- Risk on B from Monday (Section 27)

**Example 2**: A agrees to sell 500 liters milk to B (unascertained). A delivers milk to transport company C for delivery to B.
- **Answer**: Property passed when delivered to C (Rule 5(1) - delivery to carrier = appropriation)
- Unless A reserved right (e.g., sent bill of lading to own order)

### Procedure 3: Enforcing Implied Conditions (Sections 12-15)

**Step 1: Identify Type of Implied Condition Claimed**
- Title (Section 12)?
- Description (Section 13)?
- Fitness for purpose (Section 14(1))?
- Merchantable quality (Section 14(2))?
- Sample (Section 15)?

**Step 2: Verify Prerequisites**

For Section 14(1) - Fitness:
- Did buyer make purpose known to seller?
- Did buyer rely on seller's skill/judgment?
- Is seller dealer in such goods?

For Section 14(2) - Merchantable Quality:
- Sale by description?
- Seller deals in goods of that description?
- Buyer examined goods? (If yes, defects revealed = no claim)

**Step 3: Assess Breach**
- Title: Seller lacks title?
- Description: Goods don't match?
- Fitness: Goods unfit for stated purpose?
- Merchantable: Goods unusable for ordinary purpose?
- Sample: Bulk differs from sample?

**Step 4: Determine Rights**

**If Condition Breached**:
- Buyer can REJECT goods + refuse payment (Section 11)
- OR treat as warranty + accept + claim damages

**If Buyer Accepted Goods** (Section 42):
- Can ONLY treat as warranty (damages)
- CANNOT reject (unless breach so serious or concealed defect)

**Step 5: Quantify Damages (if applicable)**
- Section 73 Indian Contract Act: Damages for breach
- Sale of Goods Act Section 57: Difference between contract price and market value

### Procedure 4: Sale by Description Compliance (Section 13)

**For Sellers - Ensuring Compliance**:

**Step 1: Draft Precise Description**
- Measurements, specifications, ingredients, origin
- Avoid vague terms ("high quality", "best")
- If sample provided, description should match sample

**Step 2: Verify Description Accuracy Before Delivery**
- Goods must correspond EXACTLY (Arcos v. Ronaasen strictness)
- Minor deviation = breach (buyer can reject)

**Step 3: Document Description**
- Invoice, purchase order, product listing must match
- E-commerce: Website description becomes contractual

**For Buyers - Asserting Rights**:

**Step 1: Compare Goods Received with Contract Description**
- Within reasonable time of delivery
- Check: Quantity, quality, specifications

**Step 2: If Mismatch Found**
- REJECT goods immediately + notify seller (Section 42 - acceptance rules)
- Do NOT use/alter goods (using = acceptance)

**Step 3: Claim Remedy**
- Rejection + refund, OR
- Replacement with goods matching description, OR
- Price reduction + damages

## Practice Guidelines

### For Sellers: Risk Mitigation Strategies

**1. Clear Contract Terms**:
- Specify: Type of transaction (sale vs. agreement), property transfer timing, delivery terms
- Define "deliverable state" for specific goods
- Include Incoterms for international sales (CIF, FOB, etc.)

**2. Property Transfer Control**:
- If credit concern: Reserve right of disposal (bill of lading to own order, payment against documents)
- If goods require preparation: Specify when deliverable state reached

**3. Title Warranty Protection**:
- Ensure clear title before sale
- For goods obtained from third parties: Obtain indemnity from supplier
- Check for liens, charges, encumbrances

**4. Description Accuracy**:
- Avoid over-promising in marketing
- Technical specifications should match actual goods
- If goods vary from standard: Disclose variations explicitly

**5. Quality Obligations**:
- If buyer states purpose: Ensure goods fit OR disclaim reliance on skill/judgment
- For merchantable quality: Goods should be usable for ordinary purposes
- Inspect goods before delivery; remove defective units

**6. Documentation**:
- Invoice with clear description, quantity, price
- Delivery challan with buyer acknowledgment
- For sale-or-return: Specify reasonable time for approval

### For Buyers: Due Diligence and Rights Protection

**1. Pre-Purchase Verification**:
- Seller's title: Especially for high-value goods (vehicles, machinery)
- Check for loans/liens (e.g., VAHAN portal for vehicles)

**2. Contract Clarity**:
- Specify purpose if goods for particular use (to claim Section 14(1) fitness)
- Define acceptance criteria
- Agree on inspection period

**3. Description Reliance**:
- Document description (save website screenshots, catalogs)
- If buying sight-unseen: Description becomes critical (Section 13 protection)

**4. Inspection and Acceptance**:
- Inspect within reasonable time (Section 42)
- If defect found: Reject immediately + notify seller in writing
- Do NOT use/alter goods after discovering defect (waives right to reject)

**5. Preserving Remedies**:
- Notify breach promptly
- Preserve evidence (photos, test reports for defects)
- Do not accept goods "subject to inspection" unless explicitly agreed

### For E-Commerce Platforms: Compliance with 2020 Rules

**1. Mandatory Disclosures**:
- Product description with images
- Total price (inclusive of all charges)
- Return/refund/exchange policy
- Delivery timelines
- Seller name, address, ratings

**2. Grievance Redressal**:
- Designated Grievance Officer contact details
- Acknowledge complaints within 48 hours
- Resolve within 1 month

**3. Fair Practices**:
- No misleading advertisements
- No manipulation of search rankings based on undisclosed commercial arrangements
- No sale of prohibited goods (counterfeit, unsafe)

**4. Record Keeping**:
- Maintain records of sellers, products, orders for 180 days after supply

**5. Consumer Protection**:
- Easy return/refund mechanism (as per policy disclosed)
- Respect cooling-off period (typically 7-14 days for returns)

### Drafting Sale Contract: Essential Clauses

**Model Clauses**:

**Clause 1 - Sale/Agreement to Sell**:
> "This agreement constitutes a [sale / agreement to sell] of the Goods. [For sale: Property in the Goods shall pass to the Buyer immediately upon execution of this Agreement / For agreement: Property shall pass upon [event/date]."

**Clause 2 - Description of Goods**:
> "The Goods sold hereunder are: [Detailed description with specifications, quantity, quality parameters, brand/model if applicable]."

**Clause 3 - Price and Payment**:
> "The Price for the Goods is INR __________ (inclusive/exclusive of GST). Payment shall be made [payment terms: advance, on delivery, credit period]."

**Clause 4 - Delivery**:
> "Delivery shall be made at [location] on or before [date]. Risk and property shall pass to Buyer [specify timing: on delivery, on payment, on appropriation]."

**Clause 5 - Warranties**:
> "The Seller warrants that: (a) it has good title to the Goods and right to sell; (b) the Goods are free from encumbrances; (c) the Goods conform to the description; (d) [if fitness warranted: the Goods are fit for the purpose communicated by Buyer]."

**Clause 6 - Inspection and Acceptance**:
> "Buyer shall inspect the Goods within [X] days of delivery. Buyer may reject Goods if they do not conform to description/quality. Acceptance shall be deemed if no rejection notice within inspection period."

**Clause 7 - Exclusion/Limitation of Liability**:
> "To the extent permitted by law, the implied conditions of merchantable quality and fitness for purpose under Section 14 of the Sale of Goods Act, 1930 are [excluded / limited to replacement/refund only]."
> [Note: May not be enforceable in B2C contracts under Consumer Protection Act]

**Clause 8 - Force Majeure**:
> "Neither party liable for failure to perform due to events beyond reasonable control (force majeure events including [list: natural disasters, pandemic, government action, etc.]). Obligations suspended during force majeure period."

**Clause 9 - Dispute Resolution**:
> "Disputes arising out of this Agreement shall be resolved through [arbitration / mediation / courts in [jurisdiction]]."

**Clause 10 - Governing Law**:
> "This Agreement shall be governed by the Sale of Goods Act, 1930, the Indian Contract Act, 1872, and the laws of India."

### Related Protocols

**Within Indian Contract Law**:
- **IL-CONTRACT-001**: Contract Formation - Foundational offer-acceptance principles apply to sale contracts
- **IL-CONTRACT-016**: Sale of Goods Performance and Remedies - Continuation of present protocol covering Sections 30-61
- **IL-CONTRACT-007**: Breach Remedies - General damages principles apply to sale breaches (Sections 55-61)
- **IL-CONTRACT-014**: Bailment and Pledge - Pledge of goods sold as security (intersection)

**Within Indian Civil Law**:
- **IL-CONSUMER-001**: Consumer Protection Act - B2C sales overlay with enhanced protections
- **IL-PROPERTY-002**: Transfer of Property Act - Immovable property sales (contrasted with goods)
- **IL-PARTNERSHIP-001**: Partnership and Sale by Partner - Authority of partner to sell firm goods

**Cross-Domain**:
- **Commercial Law**: GST on sale of goods, customs for international sales
- **Arbitration**: Sale disputes often resolved through arbitration (especially commercial)

### Framework Integration

**Sale of Goods Act as "Special Law"**:
- Special provisions override general Indian Contract Act (lex specialis principle)
- Where Sale of Goods Act silent: Indian Contract Act applies (Section 3)

**Hierarchy**:
1. Sale of Goods Act, 1930 (specific to goods)
2. Consumer Protection Act, 2019 (B2C overlay)
3. Indian Contract Act, 1872 (general contract law)
4. Common law principles (where statutes silent)
