# Protocol: Discovery, Inspection, and Evidence in Civil Procedure

**Protocol ID**: IL-CPC-003
**Domain**: Indian Civil Law - Civil Procedure
**Subdomain**: Discovery, Document Production, Evidence
**Classification**: Core Procedural Framework
**Version**: 1.0
**Last Updated**: 2024-12-04
**Governs**: Discovery of documents, inspection, interrogatories, admission of facts, and evidence presentation under CPC 1908 and Evidence Act 1872

---

## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive coverage of pre-trial discovery mechanisms and evidence presentation in civil litigation, focusing on Order XI (Discovery and Inspection), Order XII (Admissions), Order XIII (Production of Documents), interrogatories, commissions, and integration with Indian Evidence Act, 1872. It serves as an essential reference for gathering and presenting evidence in civil suits.

---

## Scope of Application

### When This Protocol Applies

This protocol applies to ALL stages of civil litigation involving:
- **Pre-trial discovery** (Order XI - document discovery, inspection)
- **Interrogatories** (Order XI Rules 1-5)
- **Admissions** (Order XII - admission of documents, facts)
- **Document production at trial** (Order XIII)
- **Examination of witnesses** (Orders XVI, XVIII)
- **Commissions** (Order XXVI - local investigation, examination abroad)

### Framework Integration

**Primary Sources**:
- **Code of Civil Procedure, 1908**: Orders XI, XII, XIII (discovery and evidence procedure)
- **Indian Evidence Act, 1872**: Substantive law of evidence (admissibility, relevance, proof)

**Interplay**:
- CPC: **Procedure** for obtaining and presenting evidence
- Evidence Act: **Substantive rules** on what evidence is admissible

## Statutory Framework

### Order XI - Discovery and Inspection of Documents

**Objective**: Enable parties to know opponent's case before trial (prevent surprise, narrow issues)

#### Rule 1 - Discovery by Interrogatories

**Either party may serve** written interrogatories on opponent:
- Questions related to **matters in question** in suit
- Must be relevant to issues pleaded

**Procedure**:
1. Party files interrogatories in court + serves on opponent
2. Opponent must answer **within 10 days** (extendable)
3. Answers on oath (verification)

**Scope Limits**:
- Cannot ask: Irrelevant questions, fishing expeditions
- Cannot compel: Privileged communications (solicitor-client, "without prejudice" negotiations)

#### Rule 12 - Discovery by Production of Documents

**Either party may apply** for order requiring opponent to produce documents:
- Documents referred to in pleadings
- Documents relevant to matters in question
- Documents in opponent's **possession or power**

**"Possession or Power"**:
- Physical possession, OR
- Legal right to obtain (e.g., from agent, banker)

**Procedure**:
1. Party applies for discovery order
2. Court may order opponent to file **affidavit of documents**:
   - List I: Documents in possession (willing to produce)
   - List II: Documents in possession (claiming privilege)
   - List III: Documents formerly in possession (now elsewhere)

**Objections to Production**:
- **Privilege**: Legal professional privilege, "without prejudice" communications, state secrets
- **Irrelevance**: Not relevant to matters in question
- **Oppressive**: Excessively burdensome (e.g., demand for 10,000 emails spanning 20 years)

#### Rule 14 - Inspection of Documents

Once documents listed:
- Opposite party may **inspect** within specified time
- Take copies (at own expense)
- Court may restrict inspection (if privilege claimed)

**"Perusal" vs. "Inspection"**:
- Perusal: Reading document for information
- Inspection: Examining original (verify authenticity, alterations, handwriting)

### Order XII - Admissions

**Purpose**: Simplify trial by admitting undisputed facts/documents (no need to prove)

#### Rule 1-2 - Notice to Admit Documents

**Party may serve** notice requiring opponent to **admit genuineness** of documents:
- If opponent does not admit: Produces documents + proves at trial (costs may follow if document proved genuine later)

**Effect of Admission**:
- Document's genuineness admitted (signature, execution)
- Does NOT admit: Truthfulness of contents (must still prove facts stated in document)

#### Rule 3 - Notice to Admit Facts

**Party may serve** notice requiring opponent to **admit specific facts**:
- Facts relevant to matters in question

**Effect if Admitted**:
- No need to prove that fact at trial (saves time, evidence)

**Effect if Not Admitted** (and fact later proved):
- Court may order costs against party who refused admission (wasted court time)

**Judicial Admissions vs. Admissions in Pleadings**:
- Admission in pleading: Binding (cannot contradict later)
- Admission under Order XII: Binding for trial (but can be withdrawn with court's permission if mistake)

### Order XIII - Production, Impounding, and Return of Documents

#### Rule 1-2 - Document Lists and Production

**Parties must produce**:
- Documents referred to in pleadings
- Documents ordered for production (Order XI)

**Time**: At or before settlement of issues (before trial begins)

**Failure to Produce**:
- Cannot rely on document at trial (without court's permission)

#### Rule 5 - Impounding Documents

**Court may impound** (retain in custody):
- Documents admitted in evidence
- Purpose: Prevent tampering, preserve for appellate court

### Order XVI - Summoning and Attendance of Witnesses

#### Rule 1 - Summons to Witness

**Party may apply** for witness summons:
- Requires person to attend court to testify
- Produced documents in possession

**Summons Contents**:
- Name of witness, address
- Date and time of attendance
- Documents to produce (if any)

**Service**: By court process server or registered post

**Disobedience**:
- Criminal contempt (Section 174 IPC - non-attendance)
- Court may issue bailable/non-bailable warrant

#### Rule 6 - Examination of Witness

**Standard Procedure**:
1. **Examination-in-Chief**: Party calling witness asks questions (open-ended)
2. **Cross-Examination**: Opposite party asks questions (leading questions allowed)
3. **Re-Examination**: Party calling witness clarifies points arising from cross-examination

**Leading Questions**:
- Examination-in-Chief: NOT allowed (except formal/undisputed facts)
- Cross-Examination: Allowed (purpose: test credibility)

### Order XVIII - Hearing of Suit and Examination of Witnesses

#### Rule 4 - Recording of Evidence

**Evidence recorded by**:
- Judge's own hand or dictation (open court)
- OR electronic recording (video/audio - with transcription)

**2002 Amendment**: Encouraged electronic recording (reduce time)

**2024 Practice**: Most courts use digital recording (HCs, Commercial Courts mandatory)

#### Rule 17 - Court's Power to Examine Witness

**Court may** examine witness **at any stage**:
- Clarify doubts
- Elicit material facts not brought out by parties

**Purpose**: Court not passive; Can actively seek truth (inquisitorial element in adversarial system)

### Order XXVI - Commissions

#### Rule 1 - Commission to Examine Witness

**Court may issue commission**:
- To examine witness residing outside court's jurisdiction
- Or unable to attend (old age, sickness)

**Procedure**:
1. Party applies for commission
2. Court appoints Commissioner (usually judicial officer of place where witness resides)
3. Commissioner examines witness, records evidence
4. Returns evidence to court (sealed)

#### Rule 9 - Local Investigation

**Court may issue commission**:
- For local investigation (inspection of property, site)
- Appointing Commissioner or expert

**Use Cases**:
- Boundary disputes (measure land on-site)
- Building defects (engineer inspects)
- Accident reconstruction (examine site)

## Integration with Indian Evidence Act, 1872

### Relevance and Admissibility

**CPC**: Provides **procedure** to obtain evidence
**Evidence Act**: Determines **what evidence admissible**

**Key Evidence Act Provisions**:

**Section 5 - Relevance**:
- Evidence must be relevant to facts in issue
- Irrelevant evidence inadmissible

**Sections 24-30 - Admissions and Confessions**:
- Admission by party binding (can be used against them)
- Confession to police officer inadmissible (criminal context; Less relevant in civil)

**Sections 32-33 - Dying Declaration, Statements by Persons Deceased**:
- Hearsay exceptions (if declarant deceased, statement admissible)

**Sections 45-51 - Expert Opinion**:
- Opinion of expert admissible on matters requiring special knowledge
- Example: Handwriting expert, valuer, medical expert

**Sections 61-90 - Documentary Evidence**:
- Primary evidence: Original document
- Secondary evidence: Certified copy, photocopy (if original lost/destroyed or in opponent's possession)

**Section 65B - Electronic Evidence**:
- Computer-generated records admissible if accompanied by certificate (Section 65B(4))
- Certificate states: Computer produced output, conditions of Section 65B satisfied

### Burden of Proof (Sections 101-106)

**Section 101**: Burden to prove fact on party alleging it
- Plaintiff proves claim; Defendant proves defense

**Section 102**: Initial burden on plaintiff (preponderance of probabilities in civil cases)

**Section 103**: Burden on defendant for affirmative defenses (payment, release, limitation)

**Shifting of Burden**:
- Once party discharges initial burden (prima facie case), burden shifts to opponent to rebut

## Core Legal Principles

### Principle 1: Parties Entitled to Know Opponent's Case (Prevent Trial by Surprise)

**Objective of Discovery**: No "trial by ambush" (Anglo-American tradition)

**Order XI Philosophy**:
- Parties must disclose relevant documents
- Narrow issues before trial
- Focus on genuinely disputed facts

**Contrast with Continental Systems**:
- Civil law countries: Less extensive discovery (judge leads investigation)
- Common law (India follows): Party-driven discovery (adversarial)

**Modern Trend**: Discovery becoming more restrictive (prevent fishing expeditions, protect privacy)

### Principle 2: Privilege Protects Certain Communications

**Legal Professional Privilege** (Section 126 Evidence Act):
- Communications between client and lawyer **during professional employment**
- Privileged (cannot be compelled to disclose)

**Rationale**: Encourage full disclosure to lawyer (effective legal advice)

**Scope**:
- Covers: Legal advice, litigation communications
- Does NOT cover: Lawyer as witness to transaction (not in professional capacity)

**"Without Prejudice" Privilege**:
- Settlement negotiations privileged
- Cannot be used in court (encourage parties to negotiate freely)

**Exception**: Fraud, misrepresentation, or admission of independent fact

### Principle 3: Admissions Simplify Trial

**Order XII Admissions**:
- Admit undisputed facts/documents → No need to prove → Trial faster

**Judicial Admissions** (in pleadings): Binding, conclusive
**Admissions under Order XII**: Can be withdrawn (with court permission if mistake)

**Cost Consequences**:
- Party refuses to admit → Opponent proves at trial → Refusing party may pay costs (wasted time)

### Principle 4: Electronic Evidence Requires Certificate (Section 65B)

**Anvar P.V. v. P.K. Basheer (2014) SC**:
- Electronic records admissible ONLY with **Section 65B(4) certificate**
- Certificate states: Computer produced output properly, conditions satisfied
- Secondary evidence rules (Sections 63-65) do NOT apply to electronic records

**Certificate Requirements** (Section 65B(4)):
- Computer used regularly
- Data fed in regular course
- Computer functioning properly
- Output reproduces original data

**Practical Issue**: Many parties produce electronic evidence (emails, digital documents) without certificate → Inadmissible (unless court relaxes - exceptional)

### Principle 5: Court's Inherent Power to Elicit Truth (Order XVIII Rule 17)

**Indian System**: Mix of adversarial and inquisitorial
- Parties lead evidence (adversarial)
- BUT: Court can examine witnesses sua sponte (inquisitorial)

**Rationale**: Truth-seeking (not mere umpire in contest)

**Supreme Court**: Encouraged courts to actively participate (especially if parties ill-represented or case has public interest element)

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. E-Discovery and Digital Documents (2020-2024)

**Challenge**: Voluminous digital data (emails, WhatsApp chats, cloud documents)

**Courts' Approach**:
- Allow e-discovery BUT with **proportionality** (balance relevance vs. burden)
- Use technology-assisted review (TAR) - AI filters relevant documents

**Delhi High Court Practice Direction (2023)**:
- Parties to provide electronic documents in **searchable PDF format**
- Metadata preservation (date/time stamps, sender/receiver info)
- Privileged documents logged separately (privilege log)

**Challenges**:
- Privacy concerns (personal data in business emails)
- Cost (sifting through terabytes of data expensive)
- Technical competence (judges, lawyers unfamiliar with e-discovery tools)

### 2. Video-Recorded Evidence (2020-2024 Practice)

**COVID-19 Accelerated**:
- Witness examination via video conference (Zoom, Cisco Webex)
- Recorded digitally (replaces stenographic notes)

**Advantages**:
- Preserves demeanor evidence (body language, hesitation)
- Faster trial (no time for dictation)
- Appellate review easier (watch recording vs. read transcript)

**Supreme Court Directions** (Post-COVID):
- Video recording mandatory for Commercial Courts
- High Courts encouraged (not yet mandatory for all)
- District Courts: Gradual rollout (infrastructure challenges)

**Concerns**:
- Authenticity (deepfakes, edited videos)
- Digital divide (poor quality internet in rural areas)

### 3. Section 65B Certificate - Relaxation Trends (2020-2024)

**Strict Rule** (Anvar P.V. case 2014): Section 65B certificate mandatory

**Practical Problem**: Parties often cannot obtain certificate (third-party servers, lost device)

**Judicial Relaxation** (2020-2024 High Court cases):
- If opponent does not object: Court may admit without certificate
- If original device unavailable: Court may accept secondary evidence (if best evidence available)
- Forensic expert affidavit may substitute certificate (some courts)

**Shafhi Mohammad v. State of Himachal Pradesh (2018) SC**:
- Section 65B certificate mandatory even if original unavailable
- BUT: If document not challenged, non-production of certificate not fatal

**Trend**: Courts balancing strict rule with practical realities (avoid technical rejections)

### 4. Proportionality in Discovery (2020-2024)

**Issue**: Discovery requests becoming oppressive (demand for years of emails, millions of documents)

**Commercial Courts Approach**:
- Apply **proportionality test** (similar to U.S. FRCP Rule 26):
  - Discovery burden proportional to importance of issues
  - Cost-benefit analysis (relevance vs. expense)
- Phased discovery (initial limited discovery, expand if needed)

**Example**:
- Contract dispute (₹50 lakh claim)
- Defendant demands plaintiff's all emails for 10 years (1 TB data)
- Court: Disproportionate; Narrows to emails related to specific contract (6-month period)

### 5. Expert Evidence and Concurrent Evidence ("Hot Tubbing") (2023-2024 Pilots)

**Traditional**: Each party calls own expert → Contradictory opinions → Judge confused

**Hot Tubbing** (Australian innovation):
- Both parties' experts examined **together** (concurrently)
- Experts discuss points of agreement/disagreement
- Judge asks questions to both

**Delhi High Court Pilot (2023)**:
- Patent disputes involving technical experts
- IP Division experimented with hot tubbing

**Results (Preliminary)**:
- Experts reached consensus on some technical points (narrowed issues)
- Judge gained better understanding

**Not Yet Widespread** (2024): Limited to complex technical cases

## Application Procedures

### Procedure 1: Applying for Document Discovery (Order XI Rule 12)

**Step 1: Identify Documents Needed**

- [ ] Relevant to matters in question (pleadings)
- [ ] In opponent's possession or power
- [ ] Not privileged

**Step 2: Draft Application**

```
APPLICATION FOR DISCOVERY OF DOCUMENTS
[Under Order XI Rule 12 CPC]

The Plaintiff submits:
1. [Brief facts of suit]
2. [Documents needed - specify]
3. [Relevance - how documents relevant to issues]
4. [Opponent has documents - state basis for belief]
5. [Not privileged]

PRAYER:
Order Defendant to file affidavit of documents listing [specify documents] and allow inspection.

[Signature]
```

**Step 3: Hearing**

- Opponent may object (privilege, irrelevance, oppressive)
- Court balances: Relevance vs. burden

**Step 4: Compliance**

If ordered:
- Opponent files affidavit of documents (within time specified)
- Applicant inspects (at specified time/place)

**Costs**: Usually each party bears own costs; If frivolous application, costs on applicant

### Procedure 2: Serving Interrogatories (Order XI Rule 1)

**Step 1: Draft Interrogatories**

- **Specific questions** (not vague fishing)
- Relevant to pleaded issues

**Example Interrogatories (Contract Breach Suit)**:
> 1. State the date on which Defendant received Plaintiff's goods under invoice [No.].
> 2. State the reasons for non-payment of invoice amount.
> 3. Identify all persons who handled negotiations for the contract dated [Date].

**Step 2: File and Serve**

- File interrogatories in court
- Serve on opponent

**Step 3: Opponent Answers**

- Within 10 days (extendable)
- On oath (verification)

**Step 4: If Answers Inadequate**

- Apply for further and better particulars
- Court may compel adequate answers

**Objections**:
- Oppressive (too many questions)
- Irrelevant
- Privileged

### Procedure 3: Requesting Admissions (Order XII)

**For Documents** (Rule 1-2):

**Step 1: Serve Notice to Admit**

> "NOTICE TO ADMIT DOCUMENTS
>
> TAKE NOTICE that you are required to admit the genuineness of the following documents (copies attached):
> 1. Contract dated [Date] (Exhibit A)
> 2. Demand letter dated [Date] (Exhibit B)
>
> If you do not admit within [X] days, you shall be liable for costs of proving these documents at trial."

**Step 2: Opponent Response**

- Admits: No need to prove at trial
- Does not admit: Must prove at trial (costs may follow if later proved genuine)

**For Facts** (Rule 3):

**Step 1: Serve Notice to Admit Facts**

> "NOTICE TO ADMIT FACTS
>
> TAKE NOTICE that you are required to admit the following facts:
> 1. Defendant received invoice No. [X] on [Date].
> 2. Payment was due on [Date] as per contract terms.
>
> If you do not admit within [X] days, you shall be liable for costs of proving these facts at trial."

**Step 2: Opponent Response**

- Admits: Fact taken as proved (no evidence needed)
- Does not admit: Plaintiff must prove (witness, documents)

### Procedure 4: Summoning Witnesses (Order XVI Rule 1)

**Step 1: Identify Witnesses**

- Who has material evidence?
- Friendly witness (will attend voluntarily) vs. Hostile (needs summons)

**Step 2: File Application for Witness Summons**

```
APPLICATION FOR WITNESS SUMMONS

The Plaintiff submits:
1. [Witness name, address]
2. [Evidence witness will give - briefly]
3. [Relevance to issues]
4. [Documents witness has (if any)]

PRAYER:
Issue summons to witness [Name] to attend court on [Date] and produce [Documents].
```

**Step 3: Court Issues Summons**

- Summons served on witness (registered post or process server)

**Step 4: Witness Attendance**

- If attends: Examine (examination-in-chief, cross, re-examination)
- If does not attend: Apply for bailable warrant

**Step 5: Examination**

**Examination-in-Chief**:
- Open-ended questions ("What happened on [Date]?")
- Lead witness through facts

**Cross-Examination**:
- Opposite party tests credibility
- Leading questions allowed ("You did not see Defendant on that day, correct?")

**Re-Examination**:
- Clarify points from cross-examination
- Cannot introduce new facts (only clarify existing)

**Step 6: Recording**

- Evidence recorded (judge's notes or digital recording)
- Witness signs deposition (if written)

### Procedure 5: Applying for Commission (Order XXVI)

**Scenario**: Witness resides abroad or distant place (expensive to bring to court)

**Step 1: Application for Commission**

```
APPLICATION FOR COMMISSION TO EXAMINE WITNESS

The Plaintiff submits:
1. [Witness name, address in [Country/City]]
2. [Evidence witness will give]
3. [Witness unable to attend court - resides abroad/too ill/too far]
4. [Propose: Commissioner or Indian Consulate to record evidence]

PRAYER:
Issue commission to [Judicial Officer/Indian Consulate at [Place]] to examine witness and return sealed evidence.
```

**Step 2: Court Issues Commission**

- Letter rogatory (if abroad - through diplomatic channels)
- OR commission to judicial officer of that place (if within India)

**Step 3: Commissioner Examines Witness**

- Records evidence (examination-in-chief, cross-examination by parties' representatives/lawyers present)
- Seals evidence, returns to court

**Step 4: Evidence Filed in Court**

- Becomes part of trial record
- Parties can use in arguments

**For Local Investigation** (Rule 9):
- Similar procedure (Commissioner appointed to inspect property, measure land, etc.)
- Commissioner's report filed (with photos, measurements)

## Practice Guidelines

### For Parties - Discovery Strategy

**1. Be Proactive**:
- Disclose relevant documents early (good faith)
- Don't wait for opponent to demand (shows transparency)

**2. Organized Document Management**:
- Maintain chronological file (contracts, correspondence, invoices)
- Digital: Organize in folders (easy retrieval for discovery)

**3. Assert Privilege Carefully**:
- Don't over-claim privilege (weakens genuine claims)
- Log privileged documents (describe generally without waiving privilege)

**4. Proportionality**:
- Don't demand irrelevant documents (oppressive discovery = costs against you)
- Focus on key documents (5-10 critical docs better than 10,000 peripheral)

### For Witnesses - Preparation

**1. Review Documents Before Testifying**:
- Refresh memory (dates, amounts, events)
- Don't memorize script (sounds rehearsed)

**2. Testify Truthfully**:
- If don't remember: Say "I don't recall" (don't guess)
- If uncertain: Say "I believe..." or "To the best of my knowledge..."

**3. Listen to Question Carefully**:
- Answer only what asked (don't volunteer)
- Pause before answering (gives own lawyer time to object if improper question)

**4. Cross-Examination Tips**:
- Stay calm (opposing counsel may try to provoke)
- Yes/No questions: Answer directly (don't argue)
- If question unclear: Ask for clarification

**5. Body Language**:
- Maintain eye contact with judge (not lawyers)
- Sit upright (conveys confidence)
- Avoid fidgeting (suggests nervousness/dishonesty)

### For Advocates - Evidence Presentation

**1. Lead Evidence Systematically**:
- Chronological order (easier for judge to follow)
- Build narrative (connect facts logically)

**2. Use Documents Effectively**:
- Mark as exhibits (Exhibit A, B, C...)
- Refer to document by exhibit number in examination (not just "the contract" - say "Exhibit A, the contract dated [Date]")

**3. Cross-Examination Goals**:
- Undermine opponent's witness credibility
- Elicit favorable facts (if witness cannot deny)
- Impeach (if prior inconsistent statement)

**4. Don't Ask Question You Don't Know Answer**:
- Cross-examination golden rule
- Witness may give damaging answer

**5. Use Leading Questions in Cross-Examination**:
- Control witness ("You did not pay on [Date], correct?" - Not "When did you pay?")

### For Judges - Evidence Management

**1. Active Case Management**:
- At issues stage: Identify genuinely disputed facts
- Direct parties to admit undisputed facts (Order XII)

**2. Time Limits for Evidence**:
- Set deadlines for witness lists, document lists
- Enforce (late evidence requires permission + explanation)

**3. Rule on Objections Promptly**:
- Evidence objections during trial: Decide immediately (don't reserve)
- If complex: Brief oral reasons (detailed in judgment if needed)

**4. Use Technology**:
- Digital evidence bundles (searchable PDFs - easier than 500-page physical bundle)
- Video conferencing for distant witnesses

**5. Encourage Admissions**:
- Point out to parties: "This fact seems undisputed - will you admit?"
- Reduces trial time significantly
