## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on the essential elements of contract formation in Indian law: offer (proposal) and acceptance. It covers the rules governing communication, revocation, and validity of proposals and acceptances under the Indian Contract Act, 1872.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Key Definitions

**Section 2(a) - Proposal (Offer)**:
> "When one person signifies to another his willingness to do or to abstain from doing anything, with a view to obtaining the assent of that other to such act or abstinence, he is said to make a proposal."

**Section 2(b) - Acceptance**:
> "When the person to whom the proposal is made signifies his assent thereto, the proposal is said to be accepted. A proposal, when accepted, becomes a promise."

**Section 2(h) - Contract**:
> "An agreement enforceable by law is a contract."

---

## Elements of a Valid Offer

### 1. Communication
- Offer must be communicated to the offeree
- Uncom

municated offer cannot be accepted
- **Lalman Shukla v. Gauri Dutt** (1913): Cannot claim reward for act done without knowledge of offer

### 2. Intention to Create Legal Relations
- Offeror must intend to be bound by acceptance
- Must be serious, not jest or invitation to treat
- Commercial agreements presumed to have intention

### 3. Definite and Clear Terms
- Terms must be clear, definite, and certain
- Vague or ambiguous offers may be void for uncertainty
- **Taylor v. Portington** (1855): "Tons of iron" too vague

### 4. Capacity to Make Binding Agreement
- Offeror must have capacity to contract (Sections 11-12)
- Minors, persons of unsound mind, disqualified persons lack capacity

## Types of Offers

### 1. Express vs. Implied Offer

**Express Offer**:
- Communicated through words (written or oral)
- Example: "I offer to sell my car for ₹5 lakhs"

**Implied Offer**:
- Communicated through conduct or circumstances
- Example: Boarding a bus implies offer to pay fare

### 2. Specific vs. General Offer

**Specific Offer** (Section 4):
- Made to specific person(s)
- Can only be accepted by that person
- Example: Offer to sell land to X

**General Offer**:
- Made to world at large
- Can be accepted by anyone with knowledge
- **Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co.** (1893): Leading case
- Example: Reward offer in newspaper

### 3. Cross Offers

**Definition**: Two identical offers cross each other
**Rule**: Do NOT constitute acceptance
**Reason**: No knowledge of other's offer when made
**Example**: A mails offer to B; B simultaneously mails identical offer to A

### 4. Counter Offer

**Definition**: Response that varies terms of original offer
**Effect**: Rejection of original offer + new offer
**Rule**: Original offer stands rejected; cannot be accepted later
**Hyde v. Wrench** (1840): Counter-offer destroys original offer

## Communication of Offer

### Section 3: Communication

**How Proposal is Communicated**:
> "The communication of a proposal is complete when it comes to the knowledge of the person to whom it is made."

### Rules:
1. Offer must actually reach the offeree
2. Offeree must have knowledge of offer
3. Mere publication insufficient; offeree must be aware
4. Offer to specific person: must reach that person
5. Offer to public: publication in appropriate medium

---

## Invitation to Treat vs. Offer

### Invitation to Treat

**Definition**: Invitation to others to make offers, not an offer itself

**Examples**:
- Display of goods in shop window
- Advertisements (generally)
- Auction announcements
- Price lists and catalogs
- Tenders invited

**Key Case - Pharmaceutical Society v. Boots** (1953):
- Goods on shelf = invitation to treat
- Customer selecting goods = offer
- Cashier accepting payment = acceptance

### Distinguishing Factors

| Factor | Offer | Invitation to Treat |
|--------|-------|-------------------|
| **Intention** | Willing to be bound | Inviting others to offer |
| **Definiteness** | Clear, definite terms | General, indefinite |
| **Effect of acceptance** | Binding contract | No contract; just an offer received |
| **Examples** | "I sell X for ₹Y" | "Are you interested in buying?" |

## Acceptance

### Requirements for Valid Acceptance (Section 7)

#### 1. Absolute and Unqualified
> "In order to convert a proposal into a promise, the acceptance must be absolute and unqualified."

**Rules**:
- Cannot accept part and reject part
- Cannot add conditions or modifications
- Any variation = counter-offer, not acceptance

#### 2. Expressed in Usual and Reasonable Manner
- According to mode prescribed by offeror; OR
- If no mode prescribed, in usual and reasonable manner

#### 3. Within Prescribed or Reasonable Time
- If time prescribed: within that time
- If no time prescribed: within reasonable time
- What is "reasonable" depends on circumstances

#### 4. Before Offer Lapses or is Revoked
- Acceptance must be while offer is alive
- Offer lapses after:
  - Prescribed time
  - Reasonable time (if no time prescribed)
  - Failure of condition precedent
  - Death/insanity of offeror (if known to offeree before acceptance)
  - Revocation

#### 5. By the Person to Whom Made
- Specific offer: only by that person
- General offer: by anyone with knowledge

#### 6. Communicated to Offeror
- Mere mental assent insufficient
- Must be communicated to offeror (subject to exceptions)

## Communication of Acceptance

### Section 4: When Communication is Complete

**Communication of Acceptance Complete**:

**As Against Proposer**:
> "When it is put in a course of transmission to him, so as to be out of the power of the acceptor."

**As Against Acceptor**:
> "When it comes to the knowledge of the proposer."

### Practical Implications

**Postal Rule (Implied from Section 4)**:
- **Against proposer**: Complete when letter posted
- **Against acceptor**: Complete when received by proposer

**Example**:
- Monday 10 AM: A posts acceptance
- Wednesday 3 PM: A's letter received by B
- **Contract formed**: Monday 10 AM (against A - cannot revoke)
- **B's knowledge**: Wednesday 3 PM (against B - revocation before this effective)

### Electronic Communications
- Email: Generally when enters recipient's server (capable of retrieval)
- Instant messaging: When received and read
- Information Technology Act, 2000: Section 13 (time and place of dispatch)

## Revocation of Offer and Acceptance

### Section 5: Revocation Rules

**Proposal may be Revoked**:
1. At any time before acceptance is complete against proposer
2. By communication of notice of revocation to other party

**Acceptance may be Revoked**:
1. At any time before acceptance is complete against acceptor
2. By communication of notice of revocation to proposer

### Section 6: Modes of Revocation

**Revocation Made By**:
1. Communication of notice of revocation
2. Lapse of time prescribed or reasonable time
3. Failure of acceptor to fulfill condition precedent
4. Death or insanity of proposer (if before acceptance, and known to acceptor)

## Special Rules

### 1. Silence as Acceptance

**General Rule**: Silence ≠ Acceptance
- Offeror cannot impose obligation by saying "if no reply, deemed accepted"
- **Felthouse v. Bindley** (1862): Silence cannot be construed as acceptance

**Exception**: Where parties have prior dealings establishing silence as acceptance

### 2. Prescribed Mode of Acceptance (Section 7)

**If mode prescribed**:
- Acceptance in that mode = valid
- Acceptance in different mode:
  - Valid IF gives equally expeditious and advantageous notice
  - Invalid otherwise (unless offeror waives compliance)

### 3. Acceptance of General Offer

**Performance of condition** in general offer = acceptance
- **Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball**: Performance constituted acceptance
- No need to communicate in advance
- Knowledge of offer essential

## Contract Formation Timeline

```
OFFER → COMMUNICATION → ACCEPTANCE → COMMUNICATION → CONTRACT

Step 1: Offer made
Step 2: Offer communicated (complete when comes to knowledge of offeree)
Step 3: Acceptance given
Step 4: Acceptance communicated:
        - Complete against acceptor: when dispatched
        - Complete against proposer: when comes to knowledge
Step 5: CONTRACT FORMED (when acceptance complete against proposer)
```

## Common Pitfalls

❌ **Invalid Acceptances**:
1. Conditional acceptance ("I accept if you reduce price")
2. Partial acceptance ("I accept 10, not 20 items")
3. Modified acceptance ("I accept but deliver next month instead")
4. Acceptance after revocation
5. Acceptance by unauthorized person (for specific offers)
6. Acceptance without knowledge of offer

✅ **Valid Acceptances**:
1. Absolute and unqualified
2. Communicated to offeror
3. Within prescribed/reasonable time
4. Before revocation
5. By authorized person
6. With knowledge of offer

## Practice Guidelines

### Drafting Offers
1. **Be specific and clear** about terms
2. **State time limit** for acceptance
3. **Specify mode of acceptance** if particular method required
4. **Include conditions precedent** explicitly
5. **Preserve right to revoke** before acceptance

### Accepting Offers
1. **Accept absolutely** without conditions
2. **Use prescribed mode** if specified
3. **Accept promptly** within time limit
4. **Confirm in writing** even if oral acceptance valid
5. **Document acceptance** for evidence

### Advising Clients
1. **Verify offer is still open** before accepting
2. **Check for conditions** in offer
3. **Ensure capacity** of both parties
4. **Preserve evidence** of offer and acceptance
5. **Consider revocation** timing carefully
