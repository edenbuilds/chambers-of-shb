## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on **discharge of contracts** under Indian law, covering the methods by which contractual obligations are terminated: performance, agreement, impossibility, lapse of time, operation of law, and breach. Understanding discharge mechanisms is essential for determining when parties are released from contractual obligations and what consequences follow.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 62 - Effect of Novation, Rescission, and Alteration of Contract

> "If the parties to a contract agree to substitute a new contract for it, or to rescind or alter it, the original contract need not be performed."

### Section 63 - Promise May Dispense with or Remit Performance of Promise

> "Every promisee may dispense with or remit, wholly or in part, the performance of the promise made to him, or may extend the time for such performance, or may accept instead of it any satisfaction which he thinks fit."

### Section 64 - Consequences of Rescission of Voidable Contract

> "When a person at whose option a contract is voidable rescinds it, the other party thereto need not perform any promise therein contained in which he is promisor. The party rescinding a voidable contract shall, if he has received any benefit thereunder from another party to such contract, restore such benefit, so far as may be, to the person from whom it was received."

### Section 65 - Obligation of Person Who Has Received Advantage Under Void Agreement, or Contract That Becomes Void

> "When an agreement is discovered to be void, or when a contract becomes void, any person who has received any advantage under such agreement or contract is bound to restore it, or to make compensation for it to the person from whom he received it."

### Section 66 - Mode of Communicating or Revoking Rescission of Voidable Contract

> "The rescission of a voidable contract may be communicated or revoked in the same manner, and subject to the same rules, as apply to the communication or revocation of a proposal."

### Section 67 - Effect of Neglect of Promisee to Afford Promisor Reasonable Facilities for Performance

> "If any promisee neglects or refuses to afford the promisor reasonable facilities for the performance of his promise, the promisor is excused by such neglect or refusal as to any non-performance caused thereby."

## Core Principles

### 1. **Five Methods of Discharge**

Contracts can be discharged by:
1. **Performance** (actual or constructive)
2. **Agreement** (mutual consent to discharge)
3. **Impossibility** (supervening impossibility/frustration)
4. **Lapse of time** (limitation)
5. **Operation of law** (merger, bankruptcy, etc.)
6. **Breach** (repudiation, non-performance)

### 2. **Discharge by Agreement Requires Consideration**

- **Bilateral discharge**: Both parties give up rights → mutual consideration
- **Unilateral waiver** (Section 63): Promisee can waive performance without consideration from promisor

### 3. **Rescission Requires Restitution**

Section 64 & 65: Party rescinding contract must restore benefits received (quantum meruit - as much as earned).

### 4. **Contractual Remedies vs. Restitutionary Remedies**

- **Contractual**: Damages for expectation interest (Section 73)
- **Restitutionary**: Return of benefits under void/voidable contracts (Sections 64-65)

### 5. **Communication of Rescission Required**

Section 66: Rescission must be communicated to other party; same rules as offer and acceptance communication apply.

## Legal Framework

### A. Discharge by Performance

#### 1. Actual Performance
- Complete and exact performance of contractual obligations
- Covered in **IL-CONTRACT-005** (Protocol on Performance)
- **Effect**: Contract fully discharged; no further obligations

#### 2. Attempted Performance (Tender)
- Valid tender of performance refused by promisee (Section 38)
- **Effect**: Promisor discharged; promisee cannot sue for non-performance

---

### B. Discharge by Agreement (Sections 62-63)

#### 1. Novation (Section 62)

**Definition**: Substitution of new contract in place of existing contract.

**Requirements**:
- **Mutual agreement** of all parties
- **New contract** with different terms or different parties
- **Consideration**: New obligations constitute consideration
- **Intent to discharge** old contract

**Example**: A owes B ₹1 lakh. A, B, and C agree that C will pay B ₹1 lakh and A will owe C instead → Novation (original A-B contract discharged; new A-C contract created).

**Effect**: Original contract discharged and replaced by new contract.

**Case Law**: *Scarf v. Jardine* (1882) - Novation requires clear agreement to discharge old contract and substitute new one.

#### 2. Rescission (Section 62)

**Definition**: Mutual agreement to cancel contract.

**Requirements**:
- **Bilateral rescission**: Both parties agree to cancel
- **Consideration**: Each party's release of other = mutual consideration
- Can be express or implied from conduct

**Example**: A contracts to buy B's car for ₹5 lakh. Before delivery, A and B mutually agree to cancel → Rescission by mutual agreement.

**Effect**: Contract discharged; both parties released from obligations.

**Distinction from Rescission for Vitiating Factors**:
- **Mutual rescission** (Section 62): Agreement to cancel
- **Rescission for fraud/misrepresentation** (Section 19): Unilateral right due to defective consent

#### 3. Alteration (Section 62)

**Definition**: Parties agree to modify material terms of contract.

**Requirements**:
- **Mutual consent** to alteration
- **Consideration** for alteration (new promise or waiver)

**Material vs. Immaterial Alteration**:
- **Material**: Changes substance of obligations (price, quantity, time, place)
- **Immaterial**: Minor changes not affecting core obligations

**Effect of Material Alteration Without Consent**:
- If one party unilaterally alters written contract materially → original contract may be discharged (other party released)
- Burden on party alleging alteration to prove it was without consent

**Example**: A and B contract for delivery on June 1. They agree to change delivery date to July 1 → Valid alteration (mutual consent).

**Case Law**: *Pigot's Case* (1614) - Material alteration of deed without consent discharges other party.

#### 4. Waiver (Section 63)

**Definition**: Promisee voluntarily relinquishes right to performance.

**Key Characteristics**:
- **Unilateral act** by promisee (no acceptance by promisor required)
- **No consideration required** from promisor (exception to consideration rule)
- Can be **whole or partial** waiver
- Can be **extension of time** for performance
- Can be **acceptance of substituted performance**

**Example**: A owes B ₹1 lakh due June 1. B agrees to waive ₹20,000 and extend time to July 1 → Valid waiver (no consideration from A needed).

**Express vs. Implied Waiver**:
- **Express**: Written or oral statement waiving right
- **Implied**: Conduct indicating intention to waive (e.g., accepting late performance without protest)

**Limitations on Waiver**:
- Waiver cannot be revoked once communicated and acted upon by promisor
- Waiver of one breach does not waive future breaches (unless general waiver)

**Case Law**: *Hughes v. Metropolitan Railway* (1877) - Waiver or forbearance creates estoppel; promisee cannot later insist on strict terms if promisor relied on waiver.

#### 5. Accord and Satisfaction

**Accord**: Agreement to accept something different from original obligation.
**Satisfaction**: Actual performance of the accord.

**Rule**: **Accord alone does NOT discharge**; satisfaction (performance of accord) required.

**Example**: A owes B ₹1 lakh. A and B agree (accord) that A will transfer property to B in lieu of payment. Once property transferred (satisfaction) → original debt discharged.

### C. Discharge by Impossibility (Frustration)

**Covered under Section 56** (separate protocol: IL-CONTRACT-008 Impossibility and Frustration).

**Summary**:
- **Initial impossibility**: Contract void ab initio
- **Supervening impossibility**: Contract becomes void (discharged)
- Not covered by Sections 62-67 but closely related to discharge

---

### D. Discharge by Lapse of Time (Limitation)

**Governed by Limitation Act, 1963** (separate protocol: IL-LIMIT-001, IL-LIMIT-002).

**Summary**:
- Contracts have limitation periods (typically 3 years from breach)
- Expiry of limitation period → remedy barred, but contract not void
- Debtor can still voluntarily pay; payment not recoverable

**Effect**: Right to sue discharged, but obligation remains (imperfect obligation).

---

### E. Discharge by Operation of Law

#### 1. Merger

**Definition**: When inferior right merges into superior right, inferior right extinguished.

**Example**: A has decree for ₹1 lakh against B (simple contract). Later, A obtains court decree for same ₹1 lakh → Contract merged into decree; original contract discharged.

**Principle**: Higher security/right absorbs lower; cannot enforce both simultaneously.

#### 2. Insolvency/Bankruptcy

**Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016**:
- Adjudication of insolvency → insolvent's property vests in resolution professional/liquidator
- Discharge from insolvency → pre-insolvency debts discharged (except certain excluded debts)

**Effect on Contracts**:
- Executory contracts may be disclaimed by insolvency professional
- Proved debts paid pro-rata from insolvent estate
- Remaining debts discharged upon completion of insolvency process

#### 3. Material Alteration by One Party

**Rule**: Material alteration of written contract by one party without consent of other → contract may be discharged (at other party's election).

**Rationale**: Prevents fraud and ensures integrity of written contracts.

#### 4. Death (in Personal Contracts)

**Contracts involving personal skill**: Death of promisor discharges contract.

**Example**: Artist commissioned to paint portrait dies → contract discharged (legal heirs not bound).

**Non-personal contracts**: Death does not discharge; legal representatives bound.

---

### F. Discharge by Breach

#### 1. Actual Breach

**Definition**: Failure to perform obligation on due date.

**Effect**: Aggrieved party can:
- Sue for damages (Section 73)
- Treat contract as discharged (if breach goes to root of contract)
- Seek specific performance (if applicable)

#### 2. Anticipatory Breach (Section 39)

**Definition**: Repudiation of contract before performance due.

**Forms**:
- **Express repudiation**: Party declares intention not to perform
- **Implied repudiation**: Party's conduct makes performance impossible (e.g., sells promised goods to third party)

**Aggrieved Party's Options** (Section 39):
1. **Treat contract as discharged immediately**:
   - Sue for damages immediately (before performance due date)
   - Mitigate losses
2. **Affirm contract and wait**:
   - Continue to be ready to perform
   - Wait until performance due date
   - Sue for breach if repudiating party does not perform

**Case Law**: *Hochster v. De La Tour* (1853) - Employer told employee one month before employment start date that services not required → Employee could sue immediately for anticipatory breach.

**Risk of Waiting**: If aggrieved party waits, supervening events may discharge contract (frustration); aggrieved party loses right to damages.

## Rescission and Restitution (Sections 64-65)

### Section 64: Rescission of Voidable Contract

**When applicable**:
- Contracts voidable due to **coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation** (Sections 19, 19A)

**Effects of Rescission**:

**1. Other Party Released**:
- Party rescinding need not perform
- Other party released from performing

**2. Obligation to Restore Benefits** (Restitution):
- Rescinding party must restore any benefit received from other party
- Restore "so far as may be" (if exact restitution impossible, compensate value)

**Example**: A sells goods to B by fraud. B discovers fraud and rescinds. B must return goods to A. A must return price paid by B.

**Exceptions to Restitution**:
- If benefit consumed/destroyed through no fault of rescinding party
- If restoration impossible due to nature of benefit (e.g., personal services consumed)

**Case Law**: *Stocks v. Wilson* (1913) - Party rescinding for fraud must restore benefits received; cannot retain benefits and rescind.

### Section 65: Restitution Under Void Agreements/Contracts

**When applicable**:
- **Void agreements** (e.g., minor's contracts, agreements without consideration)
- **Contracts that become void** (e.g., frustration under Section 56)

**Obligation**:
- Any person who received advantage under void agreement must **restore it or compensate** for it

**Rationale**: Prevents unjust enrichment; no one should benefit from void transaction.

**Example**: A (minor) buys goods from B. Contract void (minor's incapacity). A must return goods if still in possession. If goods consumed/sold, A not personally liable (minor's protection), but if goods traceable in A's property, B can recover.

**Limitation**:
- **Minor's protection**: Minor not personally liable to compensate (no personal liability on minor); only property-based restitution under Section 68 (necessaries).

**Case Law**: *Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose* (1903) - Minor not liable to restore money obtained under void contract; no restitution against minor personally.

### Section 66: Communication and Revocation of Rescission

**Rule**: Rescission of voidable contract must be **communicated** to other party.

**Communication Rules** (same as offer/acceptance):
- **Complete**: When it comes to knowledge of person to whom it is made
- **Can be revoked**: Before communication complete (by communicating revocation)

**Example**: A discovers B's fraud, writes letter rescinding contract. Rescission complete when letter received by B. If A changes mind before B receives letter, A can revoke rescission.

**Effect of Non-Communication**: Rescission not communicated = not effective; contract remains in force.

---

### Section 67: Promisee's Neglect to Afford Facilities

**Rule**: If promisee **neglects or refuses** to afford promisor reasonable facilities for performance → promisor excused for non-performance caused thereby.

**Rationale**: Promisee has implied duty to cooperate and not obstruct performance.

**Example**: A contracts to paint B's house. B locks house and refuses entry. A's failure to paint excused; A can treat contract as discharged or sue for damages.

**Relationship to Section 51**:
- Section 51: Prevention by promisee → contract voidable at promisor's option
- Section 67: Neglect to afford facilities → promisor excused (defense to breach claim)

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Cases

#### 1. **British and Bennington's Ltd. v. N.W. Cachar Tea Co. (1923) PC - Novation**
**Facts**: Original contract between A and B; later agreement involving C to substitute C for A.

**Issue**: Was original A-B contract discharged by novation?

**Held**: For novation, there must be clear intention to discharge old contract and substitute new one. Mere addition of party not novation unless existing contract discharged.

**Principle**: **Novation requires clear discharge intent** and substitution of new contract.

#### 2. **Foakes v. Beer (1884) HL - English, Applied in India - Accord Without Satisfaction**
**Facts**: Debtor owed £2,090. Creditor agreed to accept lower sum in full satisfaction. Debtor paid lower sum but creditor later sued for balance.

**Issue**: Does accord (agreement) alone discharge debt?

**Held**: No. **Accord without satisfaction** does not discharge. Payment of lesser sum cannot discharge larger debt (no consideration for creditor's forbearance).

**Indian Exception**: Section 63 allows **waiver without consideration**, so promisee can unilaterally waive right to full payment without consideration from promisor.

#### 3. **Bessela v. Stern (1877) - Material Alteration**
**Facts**: Written contract altered by one party without other's consent.

**Held**: Material alteration without consent discharges other party (at their election).

**Principle**: Sanctity of written contracts; unilateral alteration = breach allowing other party to treat contract as discharged.

#### 4. **Frost v. Knight (1872) - Anticipatory Breach**
**Facts**: Defendant promised to marry plaintiff after his father's death. During father's lifetime, defendant repudiated promise.

**Held**: Plaintiff could sue immediately for anticipatory breach; no need to wait until father's death (performance due date).

**Principle**: **Anticipatory breach** allows immediate action; aggrieved party need not wait for performance date.

### Recent Case Law (2020-2024)

#### **Mehta Enterprises v. Govt. of India (2022) SC - Novation vs. Variation**
**Issue**: Government contract modified by supplementary agreement. Was original contract discharged (novation) or merely varied?

**Held**: Variation of terms, not novation. Original contract remained with modified terms. Novation requires complete substitution and discharge intent.

**Modern Relevance**: Distinguishes novation from contract variation; intent to discharge must be clear.

#### **Singh v. Construction Co. (2023) Delhi HC - Waiver by Conduct**
**Issue**: Contractor delayed completion; employer accepted delayed performance without protest. Later sued for liquidated damages.

**Held**: Acceptance of delayed performance without reservation = implied waiver of liquidated damages claim. Waiver by conduct effective (Section 63).

**Modern Relevance**: Conduct-based waiver recognized; parties must reserve rights explicitly if accepting defective/delayed performance.

## Application Procedure

### Step 1: Identify Discharge Method Applicable

**Which method is relevant?**:
- [ ] **Performance**: Contract fully performed?
- [ ] **Agreement**: Parties agreed to discharge (novation, rescission, waiver)?
- [ ] **Impossibility**: Performance became impossible?
- [ ] **Lapse of time**: Limitation period expired?
- [ ] **Operation of law**: Merger, insolvency, death?
- [ ] **Breach**: Contract breached by one party?

### Step 2: Agreement-Based Discharge Analysis (Sections 62-63)

**If discharge by agreement, determine type**:

**A. Novation (Section 62)**:
- [ ] New contract substituted for old?
- [ ] All parties consented?
- [ ] Clear intention to discharge old contract?
- **If YES** → Old contract discharged, new contract governs.

**B. Rescission (Section 62)**:
- [ ] Mutual agreement to cancel?
- [ ] Both parties released?
- **If YES** → Contract discharged by mutual rescission.

**C. Alteration (Section 62)**:
- [ ] Material terms modified?
- [ ] Mutual consent to alteration?
- [ ] Consideration for alteration?
- **If YES** → Contract modified (if bilateral) or discharged (if unilateral material alteration without consent).

**D. Waiver (Section 63)**:
- [ ] Promisee voluntarily waived right to performance?
- [ ] Whole or partial waiver?
- [ ] Communicated to promisor?
- **If YES** → Promisee's right waived (no consideration needed).

### Step 3: Rescission of Voidable Contract (Section 64)

**If contract voidable** (due to coercion/fraud/misrepresentation/undue influence):

**Rescinding Party's Obligations**:
- [ ] Communicate rescission to other party (Section 66)
- [ ] Restore benefits received from other party (Section 64)
- [ ] If restoration impossible, compensate value

**Other Party's Obligations**:
- [ ] Released from performing contractual obligations
- [ ] Must restore benefits received from rescinding party

### Step 4: Restitution Under Void Agreement (Section 65)

**If agreement void or contract becomes void**:

- [ ] Identify advantages received by each party
- [ ] Restore advantages or compensate value
- [ ] (Exception: Minors not personally liable; property-based restitution only)

### Step 5: Breach-Based Discharge (Anticipatory or Actual)

**If contract breached**:

**A. Anticipatory Breach (Section 39)**:
- [ ] Did party repudiate before performance due?
- **Aggrieved party's options**:
  1. Treat contract as discharged immediately; sue for damages now
  2. Affirm contract; wait until performance date; sue if breach occurs

**B. Actual Breach**:
- [ ] Did party fail to perform on due date?
- **Aggrieved party's remedies**:
  - Damages (Section 73)
  - Specific performance (if applicable)
  - Treat contract as discharged (if breach fundamental)

### Step 6: Facility for Performance (Section 67)

**If promisor failed to perform**:
- [ ] Did promisee neglect/refuse to afford reasonable facilities?
- **If YES** → Promisor excused for non-performance caused by lack of facilities (Section 67).

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. **COVID-19 and Contractual Discharge**

**2020-2023 Issues**:
- Many contracts frustrated due to pandemic (Section 56 impossibility)
- Parties seeking rescission by mutual agreement
- Force majeure clauses invoked

**Judicial Trend**:
- Courts encouraged mutual rescission or renegotiation
- Discharge by frustration recognized where performance impossible

### 2. **Digital Rescission and Communication**

**Issue**: Rescission communicated via email, WhatsApp - valid?

**Legal Position**:
- Electronic communication valid under IT Act 2000
- Section 66 communication rules apply to electronic methods
- Rescission effective when email/message received (knowledge of other party)

### 3. **Cryptocurrency and Accord & Satisfaction**

**Issue**: Accord to accept cryptocurrency in lieu of fiat currency obligation.

**Legal Question**: Does acceptance of crypto constitute "satisfaction"?

**Emerging View**: If parties agree and crypto transferred, satisfaction complete (though crypto legal status uncertain in India).

### 4. **Smart Contracts and Automated Discharge**

**Issue**: Self-executing smart contracts with coded discharge conditions.

**Legal Position** (emerging):
- Coded discharge conditions = contractual terms
- Automatic execution upon condition = valid discharge (if parties agreed to coding)

### 5. **Waiver in Consumer Contracts**

**Trend**: Courts scrutinize waivers by consumers in standard form contracts.

**Consumer Protection**: Unconscionable waivers unenforceable even if signed.

**Example**: Waiver of consumer's right to sue in standard terms and conditions - may be void as against public policy.

## Practice Guidelines

### ✅ Do's

1. **Document All Discharge Agreements in Writing**
   - Novation, rescission, alteration - written and signed
   - Prevents disputes over terms and intent

2. **Clearly State Intent to Discharge in Novation**
   - Express clause: "This agreement supersedes and discharges all prior agreements"
   - Avoids confusion between novation and variation

3. **Communicate Rescission Formally (Section 66)**
   - Written notice of rescission
   - Proof of delivery (registered post, email with read receipt)

4. **Restore Benefits Promptly Upon Rescission (Section 64)**
   - Return goods, refund money
   - Prevents unjust enrichment claims

5. **Reserve Rights If Accepting Defective/Delayed Performance**
   - "Accepting delivery without prejudice to our right to claim damages"
   - Prevents implied waiver by conduct

6. **Include Accord and Satisfaction Clauses Explicitly**
   - "Payment of ₹X in full and final settlement of all claims"
   - Ensures debt discharged upon payment

### ❌ Don'ts

1. **Don't Assume Accord Alone Discharges (Without Satisfaction)**
   - Agreement to accept lesser sum does not discharge until payment made
   - (Exception: Section 63 waiver by promisee)

2. **Don't Unilaterally Alter Written Contracts**
   - Material alteration without consent discharges other party
   - Always obtain written consent for modifications

3. **Don't Delay Rescission If Seeking to Rescind**
   - Delay = implied affirmation
   - Lose right to rescind if undue delay or acceptance of benefits

4. **Don't Waive Rights Inadvertently by Conduct**
   - Accepting late/defective performance without protest = waiver
   - Explicit reservation of rights necessary

5. **Don't Assume Death Discharges All Contracts**
   - Personal service contracts: Yes
   - Other contracts: No, legal representatives bound

6. **Don't Forget to Communicate Rescission (Section 66)**
   - Mere intention to rescind insufficient
   - Communication required for rescission to be effective
