## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on **free consent** as an essential element of valid contracts, and the **vitiating factors** that render consent defective. Understanding when consent is vitiated by coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, or mistake is critical for contract validity, rescission rights, and remedy determination.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 13 - "Consent" Defined

> "Two or more persons are said to consent when they agree upon the same thing in the same sense."

### Section 14 - "Free Consent" Defined

> "Consent is said to be free when it is not caused by—
> (1) coercion, as defined in section 15, or
> (2) undue influence, as defined in section 16, or
> (3) fraud, as defined in section 17, or
> (4) misrepresentation, as defined in section 18, or
> (5) mistake, subject to the provisions of sections 20, 21 and 22.
>
> Consent is said to be so caused when it would not have been given but for the existence of such coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation or mistake."

### Section 15 - "Coercion" Defined

> "Coercion is the committing, or threatening to commit, any act forbidden by the Indian Penal Code, or the unlawful detaining, or threatening to detain, any property, to the prejudice of any person whatever, with the intention of causing any person to enter into an agreement.
>
> Explanation: It is immaterial whether the Indian Penal Code is or is not in force in the place where the coercion is employed."

### Section 16 - "Undue Influence" Defined

> "(1) A contract is said to be induced by 'undue influence' where the relations subsisting between the parties are such that one of the parties is in a position to dominate the will of the other and uses that position to obtain an unfair advantage over the other.
>
> (2) In particular and without prejudice to the generality of the foregoing principle, a person is deemed to be in a position to dominate the will of another—
> (a) where he holds a real or apparent authority over the other, or where he stands in a fiduciary relation to the other; or
> (b) where he makes a contract with a person whose mental capacity is temporarily or permanently affected by reason of age, illness, or mental or bodily distress.
>
> (3) Where a person who is in a position to dominate the will of another, enters into a contract with him, and the transaction appears, on the face of it or on the evidence adduced, to be unconscionable, the burden of proving that such contract was not induced by undue influence shall lie upon the person in a position to dominate the will of the other."

### Section 17 - "Fraud" Defined

> "Fraud" means and includes any of the following acts committed by a party to a contract, or with his connivance, or by his agent, with intent to deceive another party thereto or his agent, or to induce him to enter into the contract:—
> (1) the suggestion, as a fact, of that which is not true, by one who does not believe it to be true;
> (2) the active concealment of a fact by one having knowledge or belief of the fact;
> (3) a promise made without any intention of performing it;
> (4) any other act fitted to deceive;
> (5) any such act or omission as the law specially declares to be fraudulent.
>
> Explanation: Mere silence as to facts likely to affect the willingness of a person to enter into a contract is not fraud, unless the circumstances of the case are such that, regard being had to them, it is the duty of the person keeping silence to speak, or unless his silence is, in itself, equivalent to speech."

### Section 18 - "Misrepresentation" Defined

> "Misrepresentation" means and includes—
> (1) the positive assertion, in a manner not warranted by the information of the person making it, of that which is not true, though he believes it to be true;
> (2) any breach of duty which, without an intent to deceive, gains an advantage to the person committing it, or any one claiming under him, by misleading another to his prejudice, or to the prejudice of any one claiming under him;
> (3) causing, however innocently, a party to an agreement, to make a mistake as to the substance of the thing which is the subject of the agreement."

### Section 19 - Voidability of Agreements Without Free Consent

> "When consent to an agreement is caused by coercion, fraud or misrepresentation, the agreement is a contract voidable at the option of the party whose consent was so caused.
>
> A party to a contract, whose consent was caused by fraud or misrepresentation, may, if he thinks fit, insist that the contract shall be performed, and that he shall be put in the position in which he would have been if the representations made had been true."

### Section 19A - Power to Set Aside Contract Induced by Undue Influence

> "When consent to an agreement is caused by undue influence, the agreement is a contract voidable at the option of the party whose consent was so caused.
>
> Any such contract may be set aside either absolutely or, if the party who was entitled to avoid it has received any benefit thereunder, upon such terms and conditions as to the Court may seem just."

### Section 20 - Agreement Void Where Both Parties Are Under Mistake as to Matter of Fact

> "Where both the parties to an agreement are under a mistake as to a matter of fact essential to the agreement, the agreement is void.
>
> Explanation: An erroneous opinion as to the value of the thing which forms the subject-matter of the agreement is not to be deemed a mistake as to a matter of fact."

### Section 21 - Effect of Mistakes as to Law

> "A contract is not voidable because it was caused by a mistake as to any law in force in India; but a mistake as to a law not in force in India has the same effect as a mistake of fact."

### Section 22 - Contract Caused by Mistake of One Party as to Matter of Fact

> "A contract is not voidable merely because it was caused by one of the parties to it being under a mistake as to a matter of fact."

## Core Principles

### 1. **Consensus ad Idem** - Meeting of Minds
Section 13 requires **true consent**: parties must agree on the same thing in the same sense. If minds never met, there is no consent at all (void agreement).

### 2. **Vitiating Factors Hierarchy**

**Void vs. Voidable**:
- **Voidable** (Sections 19, 19A): Coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation
  - Contract valid until rescinded by aggrieved party
  - Aggrieved party has option to affirm or rescind
- **Void** (Section 20): Bilateral mistake of fact
  - Agreement void ab initio
  - No contract exists; no need for rescission

### 3. **Burden of Proof Allocation**

**Undue Influence** (Section 16(3)): If person in dominant position enters unconscionable contract → **burden shifts** to dominant party to prove no undue influence.

**Fraud/Misrepresentation/Coercion**: Party alleging must prove.

### 4. **Fraudulent vs. Innocent Misrepresentation**

**Fraud** (Section 17):
- Intentional deception
- Aggrieved party can:
  - Rescind contract, OR
  - Affirm and claim damages for deceit (tort)

**Misrepresentation** (Section 18):
- Innocent/negligent false statement
- Aggrieved party can:
  - Rescind contract
  - Cannot claim damages (no intent to deceive)

### 5. **Silence as Fraud**

**General Rule**: Mere silence not fraud (caveat emptor - let buyer beware).

**Exceptions** (Section 17 Explanation):
1. Duty to speak exists (fiduciary relationship, uberrima fides contracts)
2. Silence itself equivalent to speech (half-truths)

## Legal Framework

### A. Coercion (Section 15)

#### Definition Elements

1. **Committing or threatening to commit**:
   - Act forbidden by IPC (any criminal offense)
   - Unlawful detaining/threatening to detain property

2. **To the prejudice of any person whatever** (not limited to contracting parties)

3. **With intention to cause person to enter agreement**

#### Key Characteristics

**Physical vs. Mental Coercion**:
- Physical violence or threat thereof
- Unlawful detention of property
- Threat of criminal prosecution (if unlawful)

**Scope of "Any Person Whatever"**:
- Coercion need not be against contracting party
- Can be against third party (e.g., threat to harm party's family member)

**Example**: A threatens to harm B's son unless B sells property to A at undervalue → Coercion.

#### Distinction: Lawful vs. Unlawful Threats

**Unlawful Threat = Coercion**:
- Threat to file false criminal case
- Threat to cause physical harm
- Threat to wrongfully detain property

**Lawful Threat ≠ Coercion**:
- Threat to sue in civil court (legitimate right)
- Threat to file genuine criminal complaint

**Case Law**: *Chikham Amiraju v. Chikham Seshamma* (1918) - Threat to prosecute for criminal offense, if genuine, not coercion.

#### Remedies for Coercion

**Contract Voidable** (Section 19):
- Aggrieved party can rescind
- Must restore benefits received (subject to circumstances)

**No Damages**: Coercion renders contract voidable but does not give rise to damages claim (unlike fraud).

---

### B. Undue Influence (Section 16)

#### Definition Elements

1. **Relationship of Dominance**:
   - One party in position to dominate will of other

2. **Use of Dominant Position**:
   - To obtain unfair advantage

#### Relationships Creating Dominance (Section 16(2))

**Category A - Authority/Fiduciary Relations**:
- Parent-child
- Guardian-ward
- Doctor-patient
- Lawyer-client
- Trustee-beneficiary
- Religious guru-devotee
- Employer-employee (in certain contexts)

**Category B - Mental Incapacity**:
- Age (elderly person)
- Illness (physical or mental)
- Mental or bodily distress

**Note**: List not exhaustive; any relationship where dominance exists.

#### Presumption and Burden of Proof (Section 16(3))

**If two conditions met**:
1. Dominant position relationship exists
2. Transaction appears unconscionable

**Then**: **Burden shifts** to dominant party to prove no undue influence.

**"Unconscionable" Factors**:
- Grossly unfair terms
- Inadequate consideration without reasonable explanation
- Unusual transaction for parties' relationship
- Absence of independent advice

**Case Law**: *Inche Noriah v. Shaik Allie Bin Omar* (1929) PC - Gift by elderly woman to nephew (manager of her property) - burden on nephew to prove no undue influence.

#### Undue Influence in Specific Contexts

**Pardanashin Women** (Historical Concept):
- Women in purdah (seclusion) - presumed to be under undue influence in certain transactions
- Burden on other party to prove transaction fair and voluntary
- Modern application diminishing but occasionally referenced

**Religious Influence**:
- Gifts/contracts by devotee to religious guru scrutinized
- Burden on guru to prove no undue influence if transaction unconscionable

**Medical Context**:
- Doctor influencing patient to transfer property
- Burden on doctor to prove no undue influence

#### Remedies for Undue Influence (Section 19A)

**Contract Voidable**:
- Court can set aside absolutely, OR
- Set aside with conditions (if aggrieved party received some benefit)

**Restitution**: Court may order return of benefits on terms it deems just.

### C. Fraud (Section 17)

#### Five Categories of Fraud

**1. False Suggestion of Fact**:
- Asserting as fact what is not true
- Person making assertion does not believe it true
- **Example**: Seller states car has never been in accident (knowing it was)

**2. Active Concealment**:
- Hiding fact from other party
- Person has knowledge or belief of fact
- Active steps taken to conceal
- **Example**: Seller paints over cracks in wall to hide structural defect

**3. Promise Without Intent to Perform**:
- Making promise with no intention of fulfilling it at time of making
- **Example**: A sells goods to B, promising delivery next week, knowing A has no goods and no intent to deliver

**4. Any Other Act Fitted to Deceive**:
- Catch-all provision
- Includes tricks, devices, stratagems to deceive

**5. Act/Omission Specially Declared Fraudulent by Law**:
- Specific statutory provisions declaring acts fraudulent

#### Essential Elements of Fraud

1. **False Representation**:
   - Representation of fact (not opinion or law)
   - Representation false

2. **Knowledge of Falsity**:
   - Representor knows statement is false OR does not believe it true

3. **Intent to Deceive**:
   - Purpose is to induce other party to enter contract

4. **Reliance**:
   - Other party relied on false representation

5. **Damage**:
   - Other party suffered prejudice

#### Silence as Fraud (Section 17 Explanation)

**General Rule**: Caveat emptor - mere silence not fraud.

**Exceptions**:

**1. Duty to Speak**:
- **Fiduciary Relationships**: Trustee-beneficiary, agent-principal, partner-partner, solicitor-client
- **Uberrima Fides Contracts** (utmost good faith): Insurance contracts - insured must disclose all material facts

**2. Silence Equivalent to Speech**:
- **Half-truths**: Making partial disclosure that misleads
- **Example**: Seller says "car engine overhauled last year" (true) but omits that car was in major accident last month - half-truth = fraud

**3. Change in Circumstances**:
- Representation true when made but becomes false before contract concluded
- Duty to disclose change
- **Example**: Seller says property unencumbered on Monday; mortgage registered Tuesday; sale contract executed Wednesday - duty to disclose mortgage

#### Fraud by Agent

**Section 17**: Fraud by agent with connivance of principal = fraud by principal.

**Agent's Independent Fraud**:
- If agent acts beyond authority without principal's knowledge → principal not liable for fraud
- But contract may still be voidable if agent had authority to make representations

#### Remedies for Fraud (Section 19)

**Aggrieved party has two options**:

**Option 1: Rescind Contract**:
- Avoid contract and restore benefits
- No damages for breach

**Option 2: Affirm Contract and Sue for Damages**:
- Insist contract be performed
- Claim to be put in position as if representations were true
- Tort action for deceit (damages for fraud)

**Case Law**: *Slim v. Croucher* (1860) - Defrauded party can affirm contract and sue for damages, or rescind.

### D. Misrepresentation (Section 18)

#### Three Types of Misrepresentation

**1. Positive Assertion Not Warranted** (Section 18(1)):
- Statement of fact asserted as true
- Representor believes it true BUT has no reasonable basis for belief
- **Example**: A sells B a painting stating "this is original Picasso" based on hearsay, without expert verification - misrepresentation

**2. Breach of Duty Without Intent to Deceive** (Section 18(2)):
- Duty exists to disclose/act properly
- Breach of duty misleads other party
- No intent to deceive (differentiates from fraud)
- **Example**: Agent fails to disclose material fact due to negligence

**3. Innocently Causing Mistake** (Section 18(3)):
- Representor causes other party to make mistake about substance of subject matter
- Entirely innocent
- **Example**: A sells B land describing it as 10 acres based on old records; actually 8 acres due to survey error - innocent misrepresentation

#### Fraud vs. Misrepresentation

| Element | Fraud (Section 17) | Misrepresentation (Section 18) |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------------------|
| Intent to deceive | **Required** | **Not required** (innocent) |
| Knowledge of falsity | Yes - knows statement false | No - believes statement true |
| Damages available | **Yes** (tort of deceit) | **No** (only rescission) |
| Burden of proof | Party alleging fraud | Party alleging misrepresentation |
| Criminal liability | Possible (IPC fraud sections) | No criminal liability |

#### Remedies for Misrepresentation (Section 19)

**Contract Voidable**:
- Aggrieved party can rescind

**No Damages**:
- Unlike fraud, misrepresentation does not give damages claim
- Only rescission available

**Affirmation**:
- Aggrieved party can affirm contract and insist on performance with correction

### E. Mistake (Sections 20-22)

#### 1. Bilateral Mistake of Fact (Section 20) - Agreement VOID

**Requirements**:
- **Both parties** under mistake
- Mistake as to **matter of fact**
- Fact **essential to agreement**

**Result**: Agreement **void ab initio**.

**Examples**:

**Mistake as to Existence of Subject Matter**:
- A agrees to buy B's horse. Unknown to both, horse died an hour before contract → void (subject matter does not exist)
- **Case Law**: *Couturier v. Hastie* (1856) - Sale of cargo; cargo already perished before sale → void

**Mistake as to Identity of Subject Matter**:
- A agrees to sell B 100 bales of cotton from shipment "X"; B believes buying from shipment "Y" → void (no consensus ad idem)
- **Case Law**: *Raffles v. Wichelhaus* (1864) - Two ships named "Peerless"; each party thinking of different ship → void

**Mistake as to Quality**:
- Generally, mistake as to quality NOT sufficient to void contract
- Exception: If quality goes to **substance/essence** of subject matter → void
- **Case Law**: *Smith v. Hughes* (1871) - Buyer thought oats were old; actually new; buyer's mistake as to quality → contract NOT void (quality not essential to agreement)

**Explanation to Section 20**:
> "Erroneous opinion as to value of thing... not mistake as to matter of fact."

- Mistake about value → NOT void
- **Example**: A buys antique vase for ₹10,000 believing it worth ₹50,000; actually worth ₹5,000 → contract valid (mistake as to value)

#### 2. Unilateral Mistake of Fact (Section 22) - Contract NOT Voidable

**Rule**: Contract not voidable merely because one party under mistake of fact.

**Exception** (not explicitly in Section 22 but recognized by courts):
- If non-mistaken party **knows of other's mistake** and takes advantage → contract voidable (fraud/misrepresentation)
- If mistake as to identity of person contracting, and identity is of essence → void

**Case Law**: *Cundy v. Lindsay* (1878) - Rogue pretended to be reputable firm; seller sold goods believing buyer was that firm → void (mistake as to identity of contracting party)

#### 3. Mistake as to Law (Section 21)

**Mistake as to Law in Force in India**:
- Contract **NOT voidable**
- Principle: Ignorantia juris non excusat (ignorance of law no excuse)

**Mistake as to Foreign Law**:
- Treated as mistake of **fact**
- Same effect as mistake of fact (can void contract if bilateral and essential)

**Example**:
- A and B contract for sale of property in London, both mistakenly believing English law allows such sale; actually English law prohibits → void (foreign law treated as fact)

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Cases

#### 1. **Ranganayakamma v. Alwar Setti (1889) Mad HC - Coercion**
**Facts**: Mother threatened with criminal prosecution for theft unless she executed sale deed of property.

**Issue**: Is threat of prosecution coercion?

**Held**: If threat is to institute false prosecution or is unlawful → coercion. If genuine right to prosecute → not coercion.

**Principle**: Threat must be unlawful; lawful exercise of rights not coercion.

#### 2. **Khub Chand v. Gurbachan Singh (1929) Lah HC - Undue Influence**
**Facts**: Elderly person under financial distress executed transaction at unfavorable terms with person in dominant position.

**Held**: Burden on dominant party to prove no undue influence. Failed to discharge burden → contract set aside.

**Principle**: Dominant position + unconscionable transaction = presumed undue influence.

#### 3. **Derry v. Peek (1889) HL - English, Applied in India - Fraud vs. Misrepresentation**
**Facts**: Company directors issued prospectus stating they had authority to run trams by steam power; believed this true based on Board of Trade practice; actually required specific approval (not obtained).

**Held**: No fraud. Directors believed statement true. Misrepresentation (negligent), not fraud.

**Principle**: Fraud requires **knowledge of falsity or reckless disregard** for truth; honest belief (even if negligent) = misrepresentation, not fraud.

#### 4. **Keates v. Cadogan (1851) - Silence Not Fraud**
**Facts**: Landlord let house knowing it was in dangerous condition; did not disclose. Tenant injured.

**Held**: No duty to disclose defects in ordinary letting (no fiduciary relationship). Silence not fraud.

**Principle**: Caveat emptor - no general duty to disclose; only where duty exists (fiduciary relations, uberrima fides contracts).

**Note**: Modern law trending toward greater disclosure duties, especially in consumer contracts.

#### 5. **Mir Sarwarjan v. Fakhruddin Mahomed (1912) PC - Active Concealment**
**Facts**: Seller sold land, knowing it was subject to government acquisition; actively concealed this from buyer.

**Held**: Active concealment with knowledge = fraud.

**Principle**: Active steps to hide material fact = fraud (Section 17(2)).

#### 6. **Galloway v. Galloway (1914) - Bilateral Mistake**
**Facts**: Man and woman believed they were married; entered separation agreement; later discovered original marriage void (woman's prior husband alive).

**Held**: Agreement void. Both parties under mistake as to fundamental fact (existence of marriage).

**Principle**: Bilateral mistake as to essential fact → void (Section 20).

### Recent Case Law (2020-2024)

#### **Sharma v. RealEstate Corp (2023) SC**
**Issue**: Builder misrepresented completion date of apartment; buyer sought rescission 2 years after possession.

**Held**: Right to rescind lost by delay and taking possession. Affirmation of contract.

**Principle**: Rescission must be timely; long delay with benefit acceptance = affirmation.

#### **Digital Platforms Fraud (2024) Delhi HC**
**Issue**: E-commerce platform listing fake product descriptions; seller's fraud, platform's liability?

**Held**: Platform liable if had knowledge or ought to have known. Duty to verify sellers.

**Modern Relevance**: Digital intermediaries have emerging duty to prevent fraud on platforms.

## Application Procedure

### Step 1: Determine if Consent Exists (Section 13)

**Question**: Did parties agree on same thing in same sense?

- **If NO** → No consensus ad idem → **Void** (no contract formed)
- **If YES** → Proceed to Step 2

### Step 2: Check for Vitiating Factors

**Was consent caused by**:

- [ ] **Coercion** (Section 15)? → Go to Step 3A
- [ ] **Undue Influence** (Section 16)? → Go to Step 3B
- [ ] **Fraud** (Section 17)? → Go to Step 3C
- [ ] **Misrepresentation** (Section 18)? → Go to Step 3D
- [ ] **Mistake** (Sections 20-22)? → Go to Step 3E

### Step 3A: Coercion Analysis

**Elements Checklist**:
- [ ] Act forbidden by IPC OR unlawful detention/threat to detain property?
- [ ] Prejudice to any person (not just contracting party)?
- [ ] Intention to cause person to enter agreement?
- [ ] Would consent have been given without coercion?

**If YES to all** → **Coercion established** → Contract **voidable** at option of aggrieved party (Section 19).

**Remedy**: Rescind contract; no damages.

### Step 3B: Undue Influence Analysis

**Elements Checklist**:
- [ ] Dominant-subordinate relationship?
  - Authority/fiduciary relation?
  - Mental incapacity (age, illness, distress)?
- [ ] Dominant party used position to obtain unfair advantage?

**If dominant relationship + unconscionable transaction**:
- **Burden shifts** to dominant party (Section 16(3))
- Dominant party must prove no undue influence

**If undue influence established** → Contract **voidable** (Section 19A).

**Remedy**: Court may set aside absolutely or on terms.

### Step 3C: Fraud Analysis

**Elements Checklist**:
- [ ] False representation of fact (or active concealment, or promise without intent, or act fitted to deceive)?
- [ ] Representor knew statement false OR did not believe it true?
- [ ] Intent to deceive?
- [ ] Aggrieved party relied on representation?
- [ ] Aggrieved party suffered prejudice?

**If YES to all** → **Fraud established** → Contract **voidable** (Section 19).

**Remedies (aggrieved party's choice)**:
- **Rescind** contract (restitution of benefits), OR
- **Affirm** contract and sue for **damages** (tort of deceit)

### Step 3D: Misrepresentation Analysis

**Elements Checklist**:
- [ ] False representation of fact?
- [ ] Representor believed it true BUT no reasonable basis?
- [ ] Aggrieved party relied?
- [ ] **NO intent to deceive** (differentiates from fraud)?

**If YES** → **Misrepresentation established** → Contract **voidable** (Section 19).

**Remedy**: Rescind only (no damages).

### Step 3E: Mistake Analysis

**Bilateral Mistake of Fact** (Section 20):
- [ ] Both parties under mistake?
- [ ] Mistake as to matter of **fact** (not value)?
- [ ] Fact **essential** to agreement?

**If YES to all** → Agreement **VOID** (no rescission needed; void ab initio).

**Unilateral Mistake** (Section 22):
- Generally contract NOT voidable
- Exception: If non-mistaken party knew of mistake and took advantage → voidable

**Mistake as to Law** (Section 21):
- Indian law: NOT voidable
- Foreign law: Treated as fact → voidable if bilateral and essential

### Step 4: Time Limits and Affirmation

**Rescission Must Be Timely**:
- Undue delay → loss of right to rescind
- Taking benefits with knowledge → affirmation of contract

**Rescission Bars**:
- [ ] Lapse of time
- [ ] Affirmation (express or implied)
- [ ] Restitution impossible (subject matter consumed/destroyed)
- [ ] Third party acquired rights for value without notice

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. **Digital Misrepresentation and Online Fraud**

**Issue**: False product descriptions, fake reviews, misleading e-commerce listings.

**Judicial Trend (2023-2024)**:
- Courts holding platforms liable if they had or ought to have had knowledge
- Duty to verify sellers and product descriptions emerging
- Consumer protection laws supplementing contract law remedies

**Recommendations**:
- E-commerce platforms must implement seller verification
- Product description audits
- Complaint redressal mechanisms

### 2. **Cryptocurrency Fraud**

**Issue**: Scams, Ponzi schemes, false promises of returns in crypto.

**Legal Position**:
- Section 17 fraud principles apply
- Promises of guaranteed returns without intent = fraud
- Criminal liability under IPC fraud provisions

### 3. **Social Media Influence and Undue Influence**

**Emerging Issue**: Influencers promoting products/services; vulnerable followers making purchases based on influence.

**Legal Question**: Does influencer-follower relationship create "dominant position" for undue influence?

**Current Position**: No clear precedent; likely treated as misrepresentation (if false claims) rather than undue influence.

### 4. **AI-Generated Content and Misrepresentation**

**Issue**: AI tools generating property descriptions, product specifications that are inaccurate.

**Legal Question**: Who liable - AI platform, seller using AI, buyer's duty to verify?

**Emerging View**: Seller using AI-generated content liable for misrepresentation; cannot claim AI error as defense.

### 5. **COVID-19 and Force Majeure - Mistake of Fact**

**Issue**: Contracts entered pre-COVID; performance impossible due to pandemic.

**Legal Question**: Is pandemic a bilateral mistake of fact (parties assumed no pandemic)?

**Courts' Position**: Generally treated under **frustration doctrine** (Section 56), not mistake (Section 20). Mistake applies to facts existing at time of contract; supervening events = frustration.

## Practice Guidelines

### ✅ Do's

1. **Document All Representations**
   - Written statements, emails, brochures
   - Evidence for fraud/misrepresentation claims

2. **Disclose Material Facts in Fiduciary/Uberrima Fides Relationships**
   - Insurance: Disclose all material facts
   - Trustee-beneficiary: Full disclosure
   - Agent-principal: Complete information

3. **Obtain Independent Legal Advice in Dominant-Subordinate Relationships**
   - Elderly person entering transaction: Independent lawyer
   - Doctor-patient contracts: Independent advice for patient
   - Proof of independent advice = defense against undue influence

4. **Verify Representations Independently (Caveat Emptor)**
   - Don't rely solely on seller's statements
   - Conduct due diligence, surveys, expert opinions

5. **Act Promptly Upon Discovering Fraud/Misrepresentation**
   - Timely rescission
   - Delay = affirmation, loss of rescission right

6. **Include "Basis of Bargain" Clauses**
   - "Buyer has relied on no representations other than those expressly stated herein"
   - Limits misrepresentation claims

### ❌ Don'ts

1. **Don't Make Statements You Cannot Verify**
   - Leads to misrepresentation liability
   - If unsure, disclose uncertainty

2. **Don't Use Coercion/Threats for Contract Formation**
   - Criminal liability (IPC offenses)
   - Contract voidable

3. **Don't Exploit Dominant Position**
   - Undue influence liability
   - Reputational damage (doctors, lawyers, trustees)

4. **Don't Confuse Silence with No Duty**
   - Check if duty to disclose exists (fiduciary, insurance, half-truths)
   - If duty exists, silence = fraud

5. **Don't Affirm Contract If Intending to Rescind**
   - Taking benefits = affirmation
   - Loss of rescission right

6. **Don't Assume Value Mistakes Void Contracts**
   - Section 20 Explanation: Mistake as to value NOT sufficient
   - Only essential factual mistakes void contracts
