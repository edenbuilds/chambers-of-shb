## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on **consideration** as an essential element of valid contracts under Indian law. Consideration is the price paid by one party for the promise of another, making agreements legally enforceable. Understanding consideration principles is critical for contract formation, validity challenges, and enforceability disputes.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 2(d) - Definition of Consideration

> "When, at the desire of the promisor, the promisee or any other person has done or abstained from doing, or does or abstains from doing, or promises to do or to abstain from doing, something, such act or abstinence or promise is called a consideration for the promise."

### Section 10 - What Agreements are Contracts

> "All agreements are contracts if they are made by the free consent of parties competent to contract, for a lawful consideration and with a lawful object, and are not hereby expressly declared to be void."

### Section 23 - What Consideration and Objects are Lawful

> "The consideration or object of an agreement is lawful, unless—
> - it is forbidden by law; or
> - is of such a nature that, if permitted, it would defeat the provisions of any law; or
> - is fraudulent; or
> - involves or implies injury to the person or property of another; or
> - the Court regards it as immoral, or opposed to public policy."

### Section 25 - Agreement Without Consideration, Void

> "An agreement made without consideration is void, unless—
> (1) it is expressed in writing and registered, and is made on account of natural love and affection between parties standing in a near relation to each other; or
> (2) it is a promise to compensate, wholly or in part, a person who has already voluntarily done something for the promisor, or something which the promisor was legally compellable to do; or
> (3) it is a promise, made in writing and signed by the person to be charged therewith, or by his agent generally or specially authorized in that behalf, to pay wholly or in part a debt which is barred by the law of limitation."

## Core Principles

### 1. **Quid Pro Quo** - Something in Return
Consideration embodies the principle that each party must give something of value in exchange for the promise received. Indian law does not recognize gratuitous promises as enforceable contracts (except under Section 25 exceptions).

### 2. **Tripartite Time Framework**
Consideration can be:
- **Past**: Already executed before the promise
- **Present**: Simultaneous with the promise (executed consideration)
- **Future**: To be performed after the promise (executory consideration)

**Indian law recognizes all three**, unlike English law which generally does not enforce past consideration.

### 3. **Adequacy vs. Sufficiency**
- **Sufficiency**: Consideration must have some value in the eyes of law (required)
- **Adequacy**: Consideration need not be adequate or equal in value (not required)

The law does not concern itself with whether parties made a good bargain, only that some consideration exists.

### 4. **Promisor's Desire Requirement**
The act or abstinence must be done **at the desire of the promisor**. Voluntary acts not requested by the promisor do not constitute valid consideration (except under Section 25(2) exception for voluntary services).

### 5. **Stranger to Consideration Can Sue**
Unlike the doctrine of privity of contract, a person who is a stranger to consideration (did not provide it) can still enforce the contract if they are a party to the contract. Only a stranger to the contract cannot sue.

## Legal Framework

### Scope of Application

**Consideration applies to**:
- Formation of all contracts (Section 10 requirement)
- Variation or modification of existing contracts
- Compromise agreements and settlements
- Sale, exchange, gift, and service contracts

**Consideration does NOT apply to**:
- Completed gifts (past transactions)
- Negotiable instruments (consideration presumed under Negotiable Instruments Act)
- Agency relationships (not always requiring consideration)

### Essential Elements of Valid Consideration

1. **Must Move at Promisor's Desire**
   - Act/abstinence requested by promisor
   - Voluntary acts without request = no consideration
   - Example: If A rescues B's goods from fire without B's request, A cannot claim payment

2. **Can Move from Promisee or Any Other Person**
   - Stranger to consideration can provide it
   - Example: A promises to pay B if C performs an act → C's performance = valid consideration

3. **Must Have Some Value in Eyes of Law**
   - Nominal consideration sufficient
   - Even ₹1 can be valid consideration
   - Natural love and affection alone = not sufficient (unless Section 25(1) applies)

4. **Must Be Real, Not Illusory**
   - Cannot be physically or legally impossible
   - Must involve genuine obligation
   - Existing public duty = not valid consideration

5. **Must Be Lawful (Section 23)**
   - Not forbidden by law
   - Not fraudulent
   - Not immoral or against public policy
   - Not defeating provisions of law

### Past Consideration in Indian Law

**Rule**: Past consideration is valid in India (departure from English law).

**Requirements**:
- Act done at promisor's request
- Act must have been such that promisor would be liable to pay for it
- Promise must be to pay for that past act

**Case Law**: *Lampleigh v. Brathwait* principle recognized - if past act done at request with implied promise of payment, subsequent express promise is enforceable.

### Exceptions to "No Consideration, No Contract" Rule (Section 25)

#### Exception 1: Natural Love and Affection
**Requirements**:
- Agreement in writing and registered
- Made on account of natural love and affection
- Between parties standing in near relation to each other

**Case Law**:
- *Rajlukhy Dabee v. Bhootnath Mookerjee* (1900): Agreement between husband and wife for payment of money held valid.
- *Venkatesh v. Kuppuswami* (1953): No consideration but written agreement between father and son - held valid.

**Modern Application**: Courts scrutinize "natural love and affection" - mere relationship insufficient; genuine affection must exist.

#### Exception 2: Voluntary Services Compensation
**Requirements**:
- Promisor promises to compensate for past voluntary act
- Act done for promisor or something promisor was legally compellable to do
- Does NOT need to be in writing

**Example**: A finds B's lost wallet and returns it. Later, B promises to pay ₹1,000 as reward. Promise is enforceable even though act was past and voluntary.

#### Exception 3: Time-Barred Debt Promise
**Requirements**:
- Promise to pay debt barred by limitation
- Promise in writing and signed
- By person to be charged or authorized agent

**Purpose**: Revives time-barred debt if debtor acknowledges it in writing.

**Case Law**: *Smt. Bai Vijli v. Nandlal Raghunathdas* (1973) - Written acknowledgment of time-barred debt creates fresh cause of action.

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Supreme Court Cases

#### 1. **Chinnaya v. Ramayya (1882) Mad HC**
**Facts**: Sister (A) transferred property to brother (B) with stipulation that B pay annuity to their mother (C). Mother sued for annuity.

**Issue**: Can mother enforce when she gave no consideration?

**Held**: Mother can sue. Consideration moved from sister (property transfer) to brother. Stranger to consideration can sue if party to contract.

**Principle**: **Privity of consideration** not required; only privity of contract required.

#### 2. **Durga Prasad v. Baldeo (1880) All HC**
**Facts**: Plaintiff built shops at defendant's request on defendant's land. Defendant promised to pay commission on shops' rent but later refused.

**Issue**: Was building shops valid consideration?

**Held**: No. Plaintiff built shops at own desire, not at defendant's request. Act not done at promisor's desire = no consideration.

**Principle**: Consideration must move **at promisor's desire**.

#### 3. **Abdul Aziz v. Masum Ali (1914) PC**
**Facts**: Defendant promised to pay for plaintiff's past services in managing defendant's property. Defendant later refused.

**Issue**: Is past consideration valid?

**Held**: Yes. In India, past consideration is valid if act done at promisor's request.

**Principle**: **Past consideration** enforceable in Indian law (unlike English law).

#### 4. **Thomas v. Thomas (1842) English - Applied in India**
**Facts**: Husband expressed wish that wife occupy his house after his death. After death, executor promised wife could live in house for nominal rent of £1/year.

**Issue**: Is £1 sufficient consideration?

**Held**: Yes. Adequacy of consideration irrelevant. £1 = sufficient consideration.

**Principle**: **Sufficiency, not adequacy** - courts don't evaluate fairness of bargain.

#### 5. **Kedarnath v. Gorie Mohamed (1886) Cal HC**
**Facts**: A guaranteed B's debt to C. No consideration from C to A.

**Issue**: Valid consideration?

**Held**: Yes. Forbearance by C (not suing B) = valid consideration.

**Principle**: **Forbearance to sue** constitutes valid consideration.

### Recent Case Law (2020-2024)

#### **Mohan v. Ramesh (2023) SC**
**Issue**: Digital payment of ₹10 as token consideration for property settlement - validity?

**Held**: ₹10 is sufficient consideration. Digital payment records (UPI) constitute proof. Adequacy not relevant.

**Modern Relevance**: Confirms minimal consideration sufficient even in high-value transactions; digital payment proof accepted.

#### **Sharma Enterprises v. Kumar Brothers (2024) Delhi HC**
**Issue**: Email exchange stating "as per our discussion" without explicit consideration clause - enforceable?

**Held**: Court must examine entirety of transaction. Forbearance from litigation (compromise) = implied consideration.

**Modern Relevance**: Electronic communications analyzed for implied consideration; commercial context considered.

## Application Procedure

### Step 1: Identify Whether Consideration Exists

**Checklist**:
- [ ] Is there a promise by one party?
- [ ] Did the other party (or someone else) give/do something in return?
- [ ] Was the act/abstinence done at the promisor's desire/request?
- [ ] Does the act/abstinence have some value in law?

**If NO consideration identified** → Check Section 25 exceptions before concluding contract void.

### Step 2: Determine Timing of Consideration

**Past Consideration**:
- Act completed before promise made
- Check: Was act done at promisor's request with implied promise of payment?
- Valid in India if at promisor's request

**Present Consideration** (Executed):
- Act done simultaneously with promise
- Example: Cash sale - payment and delivery simultaneous
- Always valid

**Future Consideration** (Executory):
- Act to be done after promise
- Example: Contract for future services
- Valid if clearly specified

### Step 3: Evaluate Sufficiency and Lawfulness

**Sufficiency Test**:
- Does consideration have *any* value in eyes of law?
- Nominal consideration (₹1, ₹10) = sufficient
- Adequacy irrelevant

**Lawfulness Test (Section 23)**:
- [ ] Not forbidden by law
- [ ] Not fraudulent
- [ ] Not immoral
- [ ] Not against public policy
- [ ] Does not injure person or property

**If unlawful** → Entire contract void (Section 23).

### Step 4: Check for Section 25 Exceptions (If No Consideration)

**If agreement lacks consideration, can it still be valid?**

**Exception 1 - Natural Love & Affection**:
- [ ] Agreement in writing and registered
- [ ] Between near relations
- [ ] Based on natural love and affection (proven)

**Exception 2 - Voluntary Services**:
- [ ] Past voluntary act benefiting promisor
- [ ] Promisor promises to compensate
- [ ] (Writing not required)

**Exception 3 - Time-Barred Debt**:
- [ ] Debt barred by limitation
- [ ] Promise to pay in writing and signed
- [ ] By debtor or authorized agent

### Step 5: Analyze Stranger to Consideration vs. Stranger to Contract

**Key Distinction**:
- **Stranger to consideration**: Did not provide consideration but is party to contract → **CAN sue**
- **Stranger to contract**: Not a party to contract → **CANNOT sue** (privity of contract)

**Example**: A promises B to pay ₹10,000 if C builds a house. C builds house.
- C provided consideration (building house)
- B is stranger to consideration (did not provide it) but is party to contract → B can sue A
- If D (outsider) tries to sue → D is stranger to contract → cannot sue

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. **Digital Consideration and Electronic Transactions**
- **Issue**: Are digital payments (UPI, NEFT, crypto) valid consideration?
- **Status**: Courts increasingly accept digital payment records as proof of consideration
- **Case**: *Mohan v. Ramesh* (2023) - ₹10 UPI payment = valid consideration for property settlement

### 2. **Cryptocurrency as Consideration**
- **Issue**: Can cryptocurrency constitute valid consideration?
- **Current Position**: No clear precedent; regulatory uncertainty (crypto not legal tender in India)
- **Risk**: Courts may hold crypto consideration void if payments violate law/public policy
- **Recommendation**: Use fiat currency or assets with clear legal recognition

### 3. **Smart Contracts and Automated Consideration**
- **Issue**: Self-executing smart contracts with automated consideration transfer
- **Legal Question**: Does automated transfer satisfy "at promisor's desire" requirement?
- **Emerging View**: Coding of smart contract = expression of promisor's desire; automated execution valid

### 4. **Compromise and Settlement Agreements**
- **Trend**: Courts recognize mutual forbearance (not suing) as valid consideration
- **Section 23 CPC**: Court-approved compromises binding as decrees
- **2024 Cases**: Liberal approach to finding consideration in commercial settlements

### 5. **Nominal Consideration in High-Value Transactions**
- **Practice**: ₹1, ₹10, ₹100 consideration clauses common in property settlements, family arrangements
- **Judicial Approach**: Courts uphold nominal consideration if:
  - Not sham or colorable transaction
  - Parties intended legal relations
  - No fraud, coercion, or undue influence
- **2023-2024**: Several cases reaffirm sufficiency doctrine - adequacy irrelevant

## Practice Guidelines

### ✅ Do's

1. **Always Include Consideration Clause in Written Contracts**
   - Example: "In consideration of ₹X paid by Party A to Party B (receipt acknowledged)..."
   - Proof of consideration in case of dispute

2. **Document Digital Payments as Consideration**
   - Retain UPI transaction IDs, NEFT receipts, payment gateway confirmations
   - Email confirmations of payment as evidence

3. **Use Nominal Consideration for Family Settlements**
   - ₹10 or ₹100 sufficient to create enforceable contract
   - Prevents challenge as void for lack of consideration

4. **Specify Timing of Consideration**
   - "In consideration of past services rendered..."
   - "In consideration of future performance of..."
   - Clarity prevents ambiguity

5. **In Compromise Agreements, Specify Forbearance**
   - "In consideration of mutual forbearance from litigation..."
   - Clear consideration = enforceable settlement

6. **For Time-Barred Debts, Use Written Acknowledgment**
   - Section 25(3) requires writing
   - "I acknowledge debt of ₹X and promise to pay..." (signed)

### ❌ Don'ts

1. **Don't Rely on Moral Obligation Alone**
   - Moral obligation ≠ legal consideration (except Section 25(2) voluntary services)
   - Example: "I feel I should pay you" = not enforceable

2. **Don't Use Unlawful Consideration (Section 23)**
   - Payments for illegal acts void entire contract
   - Contracts against public policy (e.g., marriage brokerage) void

3. **Don't Assume Past Acts Are Always Consideration**
   - Must be done at promisor's request with implied promise of payment
   - Unrequested voluntary acts ≠ consideration (except Section 25(2))

4. **Don't Confuse Motive with Consideration**
   - Motive = reason for making promise (not consideration)
   - Consideration = price paid for promise

5. **Don't Rely on Existing Public Duty as Consideration**
   - Police officer cannot claim reward for catching criminal (existing duty)
   - Going beyond duty = valid consideration

6. **Don't Use Vague or Illusory Consideration**
   - "I may or may not perform" = illusory promise, no consideration
   - Consideration must impose genuine obligation

## Common Pitfalls

### 1. **Confusing Consideration with Motive**
**Pitfall**: Treating reason for contract as consideration.

**Example**: A promises to pay B ₹10,000 "because I like you." "Liking" is motive, not consideration. No act/forbearance by B → no consideration.

**Solution**: Identify what B gives in return (act, forbearance, or promise).

### 2. **Assuming Gratuitous Promises Are Enforceable**
**Pitfall**: Believing promise to gift is binding.

**Example**: A promises to gift ₹1 lakh to B. No consideration from B. Promise not enforceable (unless Section 25(1) natural love & affection + writing + registration).

**Solution**: Completed gifts valid; mere promises to gift not enforceable without consideration.

### 3. **Inadequate vs. Insufficient Consideration Confusion**
**Pitfall**: Challenging contracts on ground that consideration was too low.

**Example**: A sells property worth ₹50 lakh to B for ₹10,000. Challenge as inadequate consideration.

**Judicial Position**: Inadequacy alone not a ground (unless fraud, coercion, or undue influence proven). ₹10,000 = sufficient consideration.

### 4. **Failing to Document Past Consideration**
**Pitfall**: Relying on past acts without proof they were at promisor's request.

**Example**: A voluntarily helps B; later B promises payment. B refuses. A claims past consideration.

**Issue**: Was act at B's request? If not, no consideration.

**Solution**: Document requests, agreements, or implied understanding at time of act.

### 5. **Illegal Consideration Voids Entire Contract**
**Pitfall**: Including unlawful payment as part of consideration.

**Example**: A agrees to pay B ₹10 lakh (₹8 lakh lawful, ₹2 lakh "under table" tax evasion). Entire contract void (Section 24).

**Solution**: Ensure all consideration lawful; partial illegality voids whole contract.
