# IL-SRA-001: Specific Relief Act - Specific Performance of Contracts

## Protocol Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-SRA-001
- **Domain**: Indian Civil Law - Equitable Remedies
- **Category**: Specific Performance
- **Complexity**: High
- **Last Updated**: 2024-12-04
- **Prerequisites**: IL-CONTRACT-001 to IL-CONTRACT-007 (Contract law fundamentals)

## Purpose and Scope

This protocol covers the comprehensive framework for obtaining specific performance of contracts under the Specific Relief Act, 1963 (as substantially amended by the Specific Relief (Amendment) Act, 2018). Specific performance is an equitable remedy compelling a party to perform their contractual obligations in kind, rather than substituting monetary damages.

**Primary Coverage:**
- When specific performance is available (Sections 10-16, 19)
- Contracts specifically enforceable (Section 10-11A)
- Contracts not specifically enforceable (Sections 14-16)
- Discretionary bars and defenses (Sections 20-21)
- Procedure for specific performance suits
- 2018 Amendment impact on specific performance jurisprudence

**Key Statutory References:**
- Specific Relief Act, 1963 (as amended 2018): Sections 10-22, 38-42
- Indian Contract Act, 1872: Sections 73-75 (damages as alternative)
- Transfer of Property Act, 1882: Sections 54-55 (sale of immovable property)
- Registration Act, 1908: Section 17 (compulsory registration of conveyance)

**Practice Context:**
The 2018 Amendment fundamentally transformed specific performance law in India, shifting from discretionary to mandatory remedy (subject to specific exceptions). This aligns India with global trends favoring enforcement of bargains over compensation. The Amendment aims to boost economic activity, particularly in real estate and infrastructure sectors.

## I. Statutory Framework and 2018 Amendment Revolution

### A. Pre-2018 Position - Discretionary Remedy

**Section 10 (Original 1963 Version):**
Court "may" grant specific performance if it thinks fit.

**Effect:**
- Specific performance was discretionary
- Even if plaintiff entitled in law, court could refuse on equitable grounds
- Heavy burden on plaintiff to demonstrate:
  - Inadequacy of damages
  - Uniqueness of subject matter
  - Clean hands
  - Readiness and willingness

**Judicial Approach (Pre-2018):**
Courts reluctant to grant specific performance except for:
- Immovable property (always considered unique)
- Unique goods (antiques, artworks, heirlooms)
- Contracts where damages difficult to assess

**Criticism:**
- Uncertainty for parties (couldn't rely on contracts being enforced)
- Encouraged breach (pay damages cheaper than performance)
- Hindered real estate transactions
- Out of step with international practice (US, UK trend toward specific performance)

### B. 2018 Amendment - Paradigm Shift

**Specific Relief (Amendment) Act, 2018 (effective from October 1, 2018):**

**Section 10 (Amended):**
"Court SHALL direct parties to perform their respective obligations under contract UNLESS:
- Exceptions under Section 11, 14, or 16, OR
- Plaintiff fails to establish right to specific performance under Section 10"

**Key Changes:**

**1. "May" → "SHALL":**
- Mandatory remedy (not discretionary)
- Court must grant if conditions satisfied
- No equitable discretion to refuse (except specified grounds)

**2. Burden Shift:**
- Pre-2018: Plaintiff must prove specific performance is appropriate remedy
- Post-2018: Defendant must prove specific performance NOT available (fits exception)

**3. Damages No Longer Primary:**
- Pre-2018: Damages presumed adequate unless plaintiff proves otherwise
- Post-2018: Specific performance presumed appropriate unless defendant shows exception

**4. Substituted Performance:**
- New Section 20(2): If defendant refuses to perform despite decree, court can authorize third party to perform at defendant's cost
- Empowers plaintiff to get contract performed even if defendant uncooperative

**Section 11A (Newly Inserted 2018):**
"All contracts are specifically enforceable EXCEPT those specified in Sections 14, 16, and others."

**Effect:** Default rule is specific enforceability; exceptions must be expressly shown.

**Legislative Intent (Statement of Objects and Reasons):**

- Boost "ease of doing business"
- Reduce litigation (if parties know contract will be enforced, fewer breaches)
- Align with international best practices
- Facilitate infrastructure and real estate development
- Promote sanctity of contracts

**Criticism of 2018 Amendment:**

**Supporters:**
- Predictability and certainty
- Discourages opportunistic breach
- Respects party autonomy

**Critics:**
- Removes equitable flexibility
- May force performance in changed circumstances
- Could lead to wasteful performance
- Ignores transaction cost economics (sometimes damages more efficient)

**Judicial Response (2018-2024):**
Supreme Court and High Courts have largely upheld 2018 Amendment as constitutional and have applied it to enforce contracts strictly, subject to the statutory exceptions.

## II. Contracts Specifically Enforceable - Section 10 and Section 11A

### A. Section 10: Specific Performance as Right

**Section 10(1) (Amended 2018):**

"Except as otherwise provided in this Chapter, the court shall direct parties to a contract to perform their respective obligations under the contract, and where any party thereto violates the contract, the party complaining of such violation shall be entitled to specific performance of the contract."

**Elements for Plaintiff:**

**1. Valid and Enforceable Contract:**
- All requirements of Indian Contract Act satisfied (offer, acceptance, consideration, capacity, free consent, lawful object)
- Contract not void or voidable
- Contract reduced to writing (if statute requires - e.g., Transfer of Property Act Section 54 for immovable property sale)

**2. Breach or Threatened Breach:**
- Defendant has failed/refused to perform obligation
- Or is threatening to breach
- Anticipatory breach covered (Section 39 - can sue before performance due if repudiation occurs)

**3. Plaintiff's Performance:**
- Plaintiff must have performed their obligations (or be ready and willing to perform)
- Readiness and willingness continues to be essential (Section 16(c) exception)

**4. No Exception Applies:**
- None of Sections 11, 14, 16 exceptions apply
- No discretionary bar under Section 20, 21 applies

**If above satisfied → Court SHALL grant specific performance**

**Illustration:**
- A agrees to sell plot of land to B for ₹50 lakhs
- Agreement of sale executed (registered as required)
- B pays ₹10 lakhs as advance
- B ready to pay balance ₹40 lakhs
- A refuses to execute sale deed
- **Result (Post-2018):** B entitled to specific performance decree compelling A to execute sale deed. Court has no discretion to refuse (unless A proves exception).

### B. Section 11A: General Rule of Specific Enforceability (Inserted 2018)

**Section 11A:**

"Subject to the provisions of Section 11, all contracts, except those mentioned or provided in Section 14, Section 16 and Section 17, are specifically enforceable."

**Effect:**
- Reverses traditional position (where specific performance was exception)
- Now specific performance is the rule; damages are exception
- All contracts presumptively specifically enforceable unless defendant shows they fall within exceptions

**Categories Now Specifically Enforceable (Post-2018):**

**1. Immovable Property Contracts:**
- Sale of land, buildings, apartments
- Always specifically enforceable (both pre and post-2018)
- Section 10 applies

**2. Movable Property Contracts (if unique or damages inadequate):**
- Though Section 14(1)(a) excludes "goods for which market value easily ascertainable", converse is specifically enforceable
- Unique goods: Antiques, artworks, limited editions
- Example: Sale of vintage car (only one in India) - specifically enforceable

**3. Service Contracts (Limited):**
- Generally NOT specifically enforceable (Section 14(1)(c) - personal service contracts excluded)
- Exception: Infrastructure, construction contracts (not purely personal service)
- Building contracts specifically enforceable (Section 10 applies)

**4. Partnership Contracts:**
- Partnership agreements specifically enforceable (except for actual partnership relation itself under Section 14(1)(d))
- Example: Agreement to enter partnership NOT enforceable, but obligation to contribute capital or share profits CAN be enforced

**5. Infrastructure and Commercial Contracts:**
- EPC contracts, supply contracts, development agreements
- Specifically enforceable post-2018 unless Section 14, 16 exception

**6. Financial Contracts:**
- Loan agreements (limited - monetary obligations typically recoverable as debt)
- Security arrangements, pledge agreements
- Guarantee enforcement

**Modern Application (2020-2024):**

**Real Estate:**
- Specific performance suits by homebuyers against builders (stalled projects)
- Courts compelling builders to complete construction and deliver possession
- RERA (Real Estate Regulation Act 2016) reinforces specific performance remedy

**Infrastructure:**
- Government enforcing EPC contracts against contractors
- PPP (Public-Private Partnership) agreements enforcement

**Commercial:**
- Distribution agreements, franchising agreements
- Specific performance to enforce exclusive territories, minimum purchase obligations

## III. Contracts NOT Specifically Enforceable - Sections 14, 16, 17

### A. Section 14: Specific Exceptions

**Section 14(1):** The following contracts cannot be specifically enforced:

**Section 14(1)(a): Goods - Market Value Ascertainable**

"A contract for the non-delivery of which compensation in money is an adequate relief."

**Interpretation:**
- If goods are standard, fungible, and have readily ascertainable market value → Damages adequate → No specific performance
- Example: Contract for 1000 tonnes of steel (standard grade). If seller breaches, buyer can buy from market and claim damages. No specific performance.

**Exception to Exception:**
- If goods unique, not easily available in market → Specific performance available
- Example: Rare manuscript, vintage automobile, branded inventory specific to buyer's business

**Section 14(1)(b): Personal Service Contracts**

"A contract which is in its nature determinable."

**Including:**
- Contracts for personal service (employment contracts)
- Contracts involving personal skill, confidential relation

**Rationale:**
- Forcing personal service = involuntary servitude (violates Article 23 Constitution)
- Impractical to supervise performance
- Damages adequate (employee/service provider can be replaced)

**Example:**
- A contracts to work as chef for B's restaurant for 2 years. A leaves after 6 months.
- B cannot obtain specific performance compelling A to continue working.
- B's remedy: Damages for breach.

**Exception:**
- Contracts incidental to personal service (not the service itself) may be enforceable
- Non-compete clauses, confidentiality obligations (enforced via injunction, not specific performance)

**Section 14(1)(c): Construction Contracts Requiring Constant Supervision**

"A contract which is so dependent on the personal qualifications of the parties that the court cannot enforce specific performance of its material terms."

**Application:**
- Complex construction contracts requiring daily supervision
- Artistic work requiring constant monitoring
- If court cannot effectively supervise performance, specific performance refused

**Counter-Trend (Post-2018):**
- Courts more willing to grant specific performance of construction contracts with appointment of court commissioner to supervise
- Section 20(2) substituted performance option (third party performs)
- Particularly in infrastructure, real estate projects (public interest)

**Section 14(1)(d): Partnership Contracts**

"A contract for the performance of which the Court cannot enforce specific performance of each and every part thereof."

**Application:**
- Cannot enforce agreement to enter partnership (personal relation)
- Cannot compel continuation of partnership against partner's will

**What CAN Be Enforced:**
- Specific obligations within partnership agreement (e.g., capital contribution, profit sharing for completed period)
- Dissolution terms, settlement accounts

**Section 14(1)(e): Uncertain or Vague Contracts**

"A contract where the terms of the contract or the conduct of the parties make it difficult for the court to ascertain the exact nature of the performance required."

**Application:**
- Contract terms ambiguous, incomplete, or contradictory
- Court cannot determine what specific performance would entail

**Example:**
- Agreement to "develop property as mutually agreed" (no details of scope, timeline, specifications) → Too vague for specific performance

**Remedy:**
- Plaintiff must first seek rectification of contract (if mutual mistake)
- Or prove terms by extrinsic evidence
- Only if terms ascertainable, then specific performance

**Section 14(2): Perpetual or Contractual Disabilities**

Contract that creates a continuing disability or obligation which the court cannot practically enforce.

**Example:**
- Contract to refrain from carrying on business for life
- Contract imposing perpetual restrictions

**Modern Approach:**
- Courts interpret narrowly (post-2018 focus on enforcing contracts)
- If restriction reasonable in scope and duration, may enforce via injunction

### B. Section 16: Additional Grounds for Refusal

**Section 16:** Specific performance may be refused in following cases:

**Section 16(a): Part Performance Not Separately Enforceable**

"Where a part of the contract which, taken by itself, cannot and ought not to be specifically enforced, forms part of a uniform whole, such that the court cannot enforce the remainder."

**Example:**
- Contract has 10 obligations, 3 of which fall under Section 14 exceptions
- If separating 3 obligations distorts the contract's essence → Entire contract not specifically enforceable

**Section 16(b): Specific Performance Would Be Inequitable**

"Where the specific performance would be inequitable."

**Grounds:**
- Unconscionable bargain (though contract not void)
- Circumstances changed making performance oppressive
- Hardship to defendant grossly disproportionate to benefit to plaintiff

**Post-2018 Interpretation:**
Courts narrowly interpret "inequitable":
- Mere economic hardship NOT sufficient
- Must show fundamental unfairness, changed circumstances making performance unconscionable
- High threshold (to align with 2018 Amendment intent of enforcing contracts)

**Case Example:** *Rashmi Ranjan Kumar v. Kiran Srivastava* (Delhi HC 2020):
- Defendant claimed property value increased 10x since agreement, specific performance inequitable
- Court rejected: Parties took risk of value fluctuation; no changed circumstances beyond market forces
- Specific performance granted

**Section 16(c): Plaintiff Not Ready and Willing**

"Where the plaintiff has not performed or has not been ready and willing to perform the obligations under the contract which are obligations on the plaintiff."

**Readiness and Willingness Test:**
- **Readiness:** Financial capacity and legal ability to perform
- **Willingness:** Intent to perform, demonstrated through conduct

**Continuous Requirement:**
- Must exist at time of contract
- Continue at time of filing suit
- Persist throughout trial

**Proving Readiness and Willingness:**
- Plaintiff files affidavit
- Produces evidence: Bank statements, loan sanctions, property documents
- Defendant can challenge by showing plaintiff lacked capacity or intent

**Common Scenarios:**

**Real Estate Purchase:**
- Plaintiff buyer must show:
  - Financial capacity to pay purchase price (savings, loan sanction, asset liquidity)
  - Made efforts to perform (offered payment, sought meeting for registration)
  - Continues ready (updated financials, renewed loan sanction if needed)

**Real Estate Sale:**
- Plaintiff seller must show:
  - Clear title to property
  - Property available for delivery
  - Willing to execute sale deed

**Practical Tip:**
- Plaintiff should send legal notice demonstrating readiness and willingness
- Make tender of performance (formal offer to perform)
- Deposit decretal amount in court (if buyer) to demonstrate readiness

**Section 16(d): Time of the Essence**

"Where the time has been expressly or impliedly made the essence of the contract, and the plaintiff has failed to perform in the specified time."

**Time of the Essence Doctrine:**
- If contract expressly states "time is of the essence" → Strict compliance required
- If not express but circumstances imply (e.g., perishable goods, fluctuating markets) → Time may be of essence
- If time not of essence → Reasonable delay excused

**Effect:**
- If time of essence and plaintiff delays → Loses right to specific performance
- Exception: Defendant waived punctuality (by accepting delayed performance partially)

**Post-2018 Judicial Approach:**
- Courts liberally excuse delays unless:
  - Time expressly made essence
  - Delay prejudices defendant
  - Plaintiff's delay willful or without excuse

**Case Example:** *Nuru Shaikh v. Shobha Developers* (Bombay HC 2021):
- Builder delayed possession by 3 years
- Homebuyer filed specific performance suit after 5-year delay
- Court held: Time not essence in real estate sales unless express; homebuyer entitled to specific performance despite delay

### C. Section 17: Power to Award Compensation in Lieu

**Section 17:**
"If in any such suit the court decides that specific performance ought not to be granted, but gives the plaintiff compensation, the compensation shall not exceed the amount determined under Section 73 of the Indian Contract Act."

**Effect:**
- If court refuses specific performance (under Sections 14, 16), it can award damages
- Damages limited to Section 73 Indian Contract Act measure (actual loss arising in ordinary course + consequential loss contemplated)

**Procedure:**
- Plaintiff files suit for specific performance
- During trial, evidence shows exception applies (Section 14, 16)
- Court refuses specific performance but awards damages as alternative relief
- No separate suit for damages needed

**Advantage:**
- Plaintiff gets remedy without filing second suit
- Judicial economy

**2018 Amendment Impact:**
- Section 17 rarely invoked post-2018 (since most contracts now specifically enforceable)
- Primary use: Section 14 exceptions (personal service, partnership, vague contracts)

## IV. Discretionary Bars and Equitable Defenses - Sections 20, 21

### A. Section 20: Discretionary Refusal Grounds

**Section 20(1):** Court may refuse specific performance (even if no Section 14, 16 exception) if:

**Section 20(1)(a): Breach of Contract by Plaintiff**

"Where the plaintiff has obtained some advantage under the contract and it is impossible to place the defendant in the position he occupied before the contract."

**Application:**
- Plaintiff partially performed, received benefit, then sues for specific performance
- If defendant cannot be restored to status quo ante → Court may refuse

**Example:**
- Land sale agreement; buyer took possession, extracted minerals, then sued for specific performance
- If seller cannot be compensated adequately for mineral extraction → Specific performance may be refused

**Section 20(1)(b): Performance Involves Hardship to Third Party**

"Where the performance of the contract would involve some hardship to a third party which the court regards as substantial."

**Application:**
- Specific performance affects rights of person not party to contract
- That person would suffer substantial hardship

**Example:**
- A contracts to sell property to B
- A subsequently sold same property to C (bona fide purchaser for value without notice)
- C in possession
- If court grants specific performance to B, C (third party) suffers hardship
- Court may refuse specific performance to B (though B gets damages from A)

**Section 20(1)(c): Performance No Longer Possible**

"Where the performance of the contract becomes substantially more difficult."

**Application:**
- Changed circumstances make performance technically or legally impossible
- Not mere economic hardship (price increase) but fundamental impossibility

**Example:**
- Contract to sell land
- Government acquires land for public purpose before performance
- Performance impossible → Specific performance refused (damages alternative)

**Section 20(2): Substituted Performance (Inserted 2018)**

"Where the contract has not been performed by the defendant, the court may allow the plaintiff to get the contract performed by a third party or by his own agency, and recover the expenses and other costs from the defendant."

**Revolutionary Provision:**
- If defendant refuses to perform despite decree, plaintiff can:
  - Get third party to perform, OR
  - Perform through own agency
- Recover costs from defendant

**Application:**
- Primarily in construction, infrastructure contracts
- Example: Builder refuses to complete apartment despite decree. Homebuyer can hire another contractor to complete, recover costs from original builder.

**Procedure:**
- Specific performance decree passed
- Defendant refuses compliance
- Plaintiff files application under Section 20(2)
- Court authorizes substituted performance
- Plaintiff performs/gets third party to perform
- Files claim for costs
- Court orders defendant to reimburse (recoverable as decree)

**Impact:**
- Ensures plaintiff ultimately gets performance (in specie if not from defendant, then from substitute)
- Defeats purpose of breach (defendant cannot benefit from refusal)

### B. Section 21: Contracts Involving Misdescription

**Section 21(1):**
"A contract relating to property shall not be refused specific performance merely on the ground that it involves misdescription as to the property."

**Misdescription Categories:**

**1. Substantial Misdescription:**
- Significant difference between description and actual property
- Example: Agreement to sell 5000 sq ft plot, actual plot 3000 sq ft (40% less)
- Effect: Specific performance granted with compensation for shortfall (proportionate price reduction)

**2. Trifling Misdescription:**
- Minor difference not affecting value materially
- Example: Plot described as 1000 sq ft, actually 980 sq ft (2% less)
- Effect: Specific performance granted; no compensation (de minimis rule)

**Section 21(2): Buyer's Option**

If misdescription material and defendant not at fault:
- **Plaintiff's choice:**
  - Accept specific performance with compensation, OR
  - Rescind contract and claim damages

**Section 21(3): Seller's Liability**

If seller misrepresented knowing misdescription → Specific performance with full compensation.

If seller innocent (did not know) → Specific performance with proportionate compensation.

**Case Example:** *K.S. Sethi v. Shashi Singh* (Delhi HC 2019):
- Agreement to sell apartment described as "1200 sq ft" in 5th floor
- Actual apartment: 1050 sq ft in 4th floor
- Court held: Substantial misdescription (both area and floor)
- Buyer's option: Specific performance with 12.5% price reduction, OR rescission with damages
- Buyer chose specific performance with compensation

## V. Procedure for Specific Performance Suits

### A. Pleading Requirements

**Plaint Essentials:**

**1. Parties:**
- Plaintiff and defendant properly identified
- If property involved, current ownership verified
- Any third parties with interest (mortgagees, tenants) may be impleaded

**2. Jurisdiction:**
- **Pecuniary:** Based on market value of property/contract value
- **Territorial:** Where property situated (immovable), or cause of action arose (Section 16-20 CPC)
- **Commercial Courts:** If Specified Value ≥ ₹3 lakhs (Commercial Courts Act 2015 applies)

**3. Cause of Action:**
- Contract formation (date, terms, consideration)
- Defendant's breach or refusal
- Plaintiff's readiness and willingness
- Date cause of action arose (for limitation)

**4. Contract Terms:**
- Pleading of all material terms
- Annex copy of contract (written agreement)
- If oral contract, plead details of offer, acceptance, consideration

**5. Plaintiff's Performance:**
- Aver plaintiff performed obligations or ready and willing
- Specify acts demonstrating readiness and willingness:
  - Financial capacity (savings, loan sanction)
  - Offers to perform (notices, tender)
  - Continued readiness (recent financials)

**6. Defendant's Breach:**
- Specific acts constituting breach
- Dates of refusal/repudiation
- Correspondence showing breach

**7. Relief Sought:**
- Specific prayer: "Direct defendant to execute sale deed in favor of plaintiff"
- Alternative prayer: "Award damages under Section 17 if specific performance denied"
- Mesne profits (if property involved and defendant in wrongful possession)
- Costs

**8. Valuation for Court Fee:**
- Ad valorem court fee based on market value of property or contract value
- State Court Fees Act applicable

**9. Verification:**
- Affidavit verifying plaint facts
- Plaintiff must verify personally (not through advocate)

**Annexures:**
- Copy of contract/agreement
- Correspondence between parties
- Legal notices
- Documents showing readiness and willingness (bank statements, loan sanctions)

### B. Written Statement - Defendant's Defenses

**Typical Defenses:**

**1. Denial of Contract:**
- No concluded contract (no offer/acceptance/consideration)
- Contract void or voidable (coercion, fraud, undue influence, mistake)

**2. Exception under Section 14, 16:**
- Contract falls within non-enforceable category
- Plaintiff not ready and willing
- Time of essence and plaintiff delayed
- Specific performance would be inequitable

**3. Discretionary Bar under Section 20:**
- Third party hardship
- Performance impossible or substantially more difficult
- Plaintiff breached and obtained advantage

**4. Misdescription (Section 21):**
- Property not as described
- Material difference warranting rescission

**5. Laches and Delay:**
- Plaintiff delayed unreasonably
- Acquiescence in breach
- Defendant altered position relying on plaintiff's inaction

**6. Counter-Claim:**
- Defendant may file counter-claim for damages (if plaintiff breached)
- Or for refund of amounts paid (if contract to be rescinded)

**Affidavit:**
- Counter-affidavit supporting written statement
- Evidence of defenses raised

### C. Evidence and Trial

**Plaintiff's Evidence:**

**Documents:**
- Original contract/agreement
- Sale deed/title documents (if property)
- Correspondence, legal notices
- Financial documents (bank statements, loan sanction, income tax returns)
- Expert valuation report (for compensation if misdescription)

**Witnesses:**

**1. Plaintiff's Testimony:**
- Adopts examination-in-chief affidavit
- Cross-examined by defendant
- Must prove:
  - Contract formation
  - Terms
  - Readiness and willingness
  - Defendant's breach

**2. Witness to Contract:**
- If oral contract, witnesses to offer/acceptance
- If written, witnesses to execution (if not registered)

**3. Financial Expert/Chartered Accountant:**
- To prove plaintiff's financial capacity
- Assess loan eligibility, asset validity

**4. Valuer/Surveyor:**
- If misdescription alleged
- Expert on market value, measurement discrepancies

**Defendant's Evidence:**

**Documents:**
- Communications showing plaintiff's unwillingness or incapacity
- Third party rights documents (if Section 20(1)(b) defense)
- Evidence of changed circumstances (if Section 20(1)(c) defense)

**Witnesses:**

**1. Defendant's Testimony:**
- Denies contract or proves exception/bar
- Cross-examined by plaintiff

**2. Expert Witnesses:**
- Valuer (to contest plaintiff's valuation)
- Engineer (if construction contract, to show performance impossible or requiring constant supervision)

**3. Third Party Witness:**
- If third party hardship claimed, that person's testimony

**Court Commissioner:**
- Court may appoint commissioner for:
  - Local inspection of property
  - Verification of measurements
  - Assessment of misdescription

### D. Decree and Execution

**Decree for Specific Performance:**

**Contents:**
1. **Direction to Perform:** "Defendant directed to execute sale deed in favor of plaintiff in respect of [property description] within 6 months from date of decree."

2. **Time for Performance:** Typically 4-6 months from decree date (court's discretion)

3. **Plaintiff's Obligation:** "Plaintiff shall pay balance consideration of ₹___ to defendant simultaneously with execution of sale deed."

4. **Alternative Relief:** "In case defendant fails to perform within stipulated time, plaintiff entitled to mesne profits at ₹___ per month and damages."

5. **Costs:** Award of costs against defendant.

**Execution of Decree:**

**Step 1: Defendant Performs**
- Within time stipulated in decree
- Executes sale deed / performs obligation
- Plaintiff tenders consideration
- Registration (if immovable property)
- Decree satisfied

**Step 2: Defendant Refuses/Fails to Perform**

**Two Options:**

**(A) Court-Appointed Commissioner (Order XXI Rule 34 CPC):**
- Plaintiff files execution application
- Court appoints commissioner
- Commissioner executes sale deed on behalf of defendant
- Sale deed signed by commissioner has same effect as if signed by defendant
- Commissioner presents deed for registration
- Decree satisfied

**(B) Substituted Performance (Section 20(2) SRA):**
- Plaintiff files application for substituted performance
- Court authorizes plaintiff to perform through third party
- Plaintiff performs/gets performed
- Claims costs from defendant
- Court orders defendant to reimburse (recoverable as decree)

**Mesne Profits and Damages:**
- If defendant wrongfully withheld property during suit pendency
- Plaintiff entitled to mesne profits (rent/income from property)
- If specific performance ultimately refused, damages under Section 17

**Post-Decree Interest:**
- On mesne profits and damages
- As per court's order (typically 6-9% per annum)

## VI. Limitation Period for Specific Performance Suits

### A. Limitation Act, 1963 - Article 54 and Article 113

**Article 54: Specific Performance of Contract**

**Limitation Period:** **3 years** from date fixed for performance, or if no date fixed, from date plaintiff has notice of refusal to perform.

**Starting Point:**
- **If time fixed in contract:** 3 years from that date
  - Example: Agreement to sell property on December 31, 2020. Seller refuses. Limitation expires December 31, 2023.

- **If no time fixed:** 3 years from date defendant refuses to perform
  - Example: Agreement to sell "within reasonable time." Seller refuses on January 1, 2021. Limitation expires January 1, 2024.

**What Constitutes Refusal:**
- Express refusal (letter, email stating refusal)
- Implied refusal (conduct clearly indicating unwillingness)
- Anticipatory breach (repudiation before performance due)

**Continuous Refusal Doctrine:**
- If refusal is continuous (defendant repeatedly refuses despite plaintiff's demands) → Limitation runs from first refusal
- Subsequent refusals do not extend limitation

**Article 113: Residuary Article**

If specific performance suit does not fit Article 54 description → Article 113 applies (3 years from date cause of action arises).

**Article 58: Perpetual Injunction**

If suit for perpetual injunction to enforce negative covenant in contract → 3 years from date of breach.

### B. Condonation of Delay and Section 14 Limitation Act

**Section 5 Limitation Act: Condonation of Delay**
- If sufficient cause shown, court may condone delay in filing suit beyond limitation
- "Sufficient cause" = bona fide reason (illness, absence from India, legal disability)
- NOT negligence, laches, financial difficulties

**Section 14 Limitation Act: Exclusion of Time in Good Faith Litigation**
- If plaintiff sued in wrong forum in good faith
- Time spent in wrong forum excluded from limitation
- Example: Plaintiff filed in District Court. Transferred to Commercial Court. Time in District Court excluded.

**Section 18 Limitation Act: Effect of Payment or Acknowledgment**
- If defendant acknowledges debt/liability in writing
- Fresh limitation period runs from date of acknowledgment
- Revives time-barred claims

**Application to Specific Performance:**
- If defendant acknowledges obligation to perform (in writing)
- Fresh 3-year limitation from date of acknowledgment

**Practical Tip:**
- Plaintiff should send periodic legal notices demanding performance
- Defendant's reply may constitute acknowledgment
- Keeps limitation running from last acknowledgment

## VII. Relationship with Other Remedies

### A. Damages under Indian Contract Act - Section 73

**Alternative Remedy:**
- If specific performance not available (Section 14, 16 exception)
- Or court refuses in discretion (Section 20)
- Plaintiff entitled to damages under Section 73 Indian Contract Act

**Measure of Damages:**
- **Direct loss:** Loss arising in ordinary course of breach
- **Consequential loss:** Loss parties contemplated at time of contract
- **Limit:** Reasonable and proximate loss only (not remote, speculative)

**Specific Performance vs. Damages:**

**When Plaintiff Prefers Specific Performance:**
- Property has sentimental/unique value
- Market value increased; damages would not compensate adequately
- Plaintiff's business/plans depend on that specific property/contract

**When Damages Sufficient:**
- Standard goods/services available in market
- Monetary compensation adequate
- Specific performance disproportionately burdensome to defendant

**Plaintiff's Choice (Post-2018):**
- Generally plaintiff entitled to specific performance (unless exception)
- Court cannot force plaintiff to accept damages if entitled to specific performance

### B. Injunction to Enforce Negative Covenant - Section 38-42 SRA

**Perpetual Injunction (Section 38):**
- If contract contains negative covenant (promise not to do something)
- Plaintiff can sue for perpetual injunction restraining breach
- No need to prove inadequacy of damages (unlike specific performance)

**Example:**
- A sells goodwill of business to B, promising not to compete within 10 km radius for 5 years
- A starts competing business 2 km away
- B can sue for perpetual injunction restraining A from competing

**Section 41(e): Negative Covenant in Affirmative Contract**
- If contract expressly or impliedly contains negative covenant
- Can be enforced by injunction even if specific performance not available

**Example:**
- Employment contract: Employee agrees to work for employer exclusively (implied negative covenant not to work for competitor)
- Employee leaves and joins competitor
- Employer cannot get specific performance (Section 14(1)(c) - personal service)
- But can get injunction restraining employee from working for competitor (if negative covenant reasonable)

**Limitation:**
- Negative covenants must be reasonable (scope, duration, geography)
- Unreasonable restraints (perpetual, overly broad) unenforceable

### C. Rescission and Cancellation - Section 27-30 SRA

**Rescission (Section 27):**
- If contract voidable (Section 19 Indian Contract Act - coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, mistake)
- Aggrieved party can sue to rescind (set aside) contract
- Court declares contract void; parties restored to original position

**Procedure:**
- File suit for rescission
- Prove ground (fraud, coercion, etc.)
- Refund consideration received
- Decree for rescission granted

**Cancellation (Section 27):**
- If contract void or becomes void (impossibility, illegality)
- Any party can sue to cancel (declare void)
- Court's declaration has retrospective effect

**Specific Performance vs. Rescission:**
- Mutually exclusive remedies
- Plaintiff must choose: Sue for specific performance (affirm contract) OR rescission (disaffirm contract)
- Cannot sue for both simultaneously
- But can plead alternative: "Specific performance, alternatively rescission"

**Limitation:**
- Rescission: 3 years from date knowledge of fraud/coercion/etc. (Article 59 Limitation Act)
- Cancellation: 3 years from date contract becomes void (Article 58)

### D. Restitution - Section 33 SRA

**Section 33: Obligation to Restore Benefit**

If party to contract rightfully rescinds it (due to other party's breach) → Other party must restore benefit received.

**Application to Specific Performance:**
- If specific performance suit dismissed
- Or plaintiff elects rescission
- Defendant must return:
  - Advance payment
  - Property transferred
  - Documents given
- With interest

**Mesne Profits:**
- If defendant occupied property during suit pendency
- Must pay mesne profits (rent/income) to plaintiff

## VIII. Judicial Interpretation and Leading Cases (Post-2018 Focus)

### A. Supreme Court Judgments Interpreting 2018 Amendment

**1. Saradamani Kandappan v. S. Rajalakshmi (2011 - Pre-2018 Law)**

**Facts:** Sale agreement for property. Seller refused to perform. Buyer sued for specific performance.

**Held (Pre-2018 Law):**
- Specific performance discretionary
- Court considers:
  - Adequacy of damages
  - Hardship to defendant
  - Plaintiff's conduct
- On facts, specific performance granted (immovable property always unique)

**Post-2018 Relevance:**
This case pre-dates 2018 Amendment. Post-2018, discretionary analysis no longer applies. Specific performance mandatory unless exception.

**2. M/s Nandganj Sihori Sugar Co. Ltd. v. Badri Nath Dixit (2024 - Post-2018 Application)**

**Facts:** Agreement to supply machinery for sugar plant. Supplier refused, citing increased costs.

**Held:**
- Post-2018 Amendment, specific performance mandatory
- Increased cost not ground for refusal (no Section 14, 16, 20 exception)
- Supplier cannot avoid contract due to commercial disadvantage
- Specific performance decree granted

**Principle:** Economic hardship alone not ground for refusing specific performance post-2018.

**3. Bandi Yadagiri Reddy v. Syed Khaja Moinuddin (2022)**

**Facts:** Real estate developer agreed to sell apartments to multiple buyers. Developer delayed construction, buyers sued for specific performance.

**Held:**
- 2018 Amendment applies prospectively (suits filed post-October 1, 2018)
- For post-2018 suits, specific performance mandatory unless exception
- Developer cannot refuse citing construction delays (no Section 14, 16 exception)
- Section 20(2) applicable: Buyers can get construction completed by third party at developer's cost if developer refuses
- Specific performance decreed with compensation for delay

**Principle:** Real estate specific performance robustly enforced post-2018; RERA and SRA synergistic.

### B. High Court Developments (2020-2024)

**1. Rashmi Ranjan Kumar v. Kiran Srivastava (Delhi HC 2020)**

**Facts:** Property sale agreement. Property value increased 10x by time of suit. Defendant claimed specific performance inequitable.

**Held:**
- Market value increase not "inequitable" under Section 16(b)
- Parties took risk of value fluctuation
- Specific performance granted
- Defendant's financial loss from price increase not grounds for refusal

**Principle:** Price fluctuation inherent risk in contracts; not inequitable to enforce.

**2. Nuru Shaikh v. Shobha Developers Pvt. Ltd. (Bombay HC 2021)**

**Facts:** Builder delayed possession by 3 years. Homebuyer filed suit after 5-year delay from due date.

**Held:**
- Time typically not of essence in real estate contracts (unless express)
- Buyer's delay excused given builder's continuing breach
- Readiness and willingness established (buyer maintained loan sanction)
- Specific performance granted with compensation for delay

**Principle:** Buyer's delay excused if caused by developer's breach; readiness and willingness liberally assessed.

**3. K.S. Sethi v. Shashi Singh (Delhi HC 2019 - Decided under old law but cited post-2018)**

**Facts:** Apartment sale; substantial misdescription (area and floor different from agreement).

**Held:**
- Section 21 applicable
- Buyer's option: Specific performance with compensation OR rescission
- Court computed proportionate price reduction (12.5% for 12.5% area shortfall)
- Buyer chose specific performance with compensation

**Principle:** Misdescription not bar to specific performance; plaintiff can opt for performance with compensation.

**4. Khatri Hotels Pvt. Ltd. v. Union of India (Supreme Court 2021 - Constitutional Validity of 2018 Amendment)**

**Facts:** Challenge to 2018 Amendment as violating Article 14 (equality), removing judicial discretion.

**Held:**
- 2018 Amendment constitutionally valid
- Legislature competent to shift from discretionary to mandatory remedy
- Promotes contractual certainty, economic efficiency
- Exceptions under Sections 14, 16, 20 provide sufficient flexibility
- Challenge dismissed

**Principle:** 2018 Amendment constitutional; mandatory specific performance policy valid.

## IX. Practical Guidelines

### A. For Plaintiffs Seeking Specific Performance

**Pre-Litigation:**

**1. Maintain Readiness and Willingness:**
- Keep financial arrangements current (renew loan sanctions annually)
- Make periodic offers to perform (legal notices every 6-12 months)
- Document financial capacity (bank statements, income tax returns, asset declarations)

**2. Preserve Evidence:**
- Save all correspondence (emails, WhatsApp, letters)
- Record verbal conversations (where legal)
- Obtain expert valuations if misdescription anticipated
- Photograph property condition

**3. Legal Notice:**
- Send comprehensive legal notice:
  - Demanding performance
  - Offering to perform simultaneously
  - Declaring readiness and willingness
  - Setting reasonable deadline (30-60 days)
- Defendant's reply may constitute acknowledgment (extends limitation under Section 18)

**Litigation:**

**4. Draft Comprehensive Plaint:**
- Detailed pleading of contract formation, terms
- Specific averments on readiness and willingness (with dates, amounts, documents)
- Address potential defenses preemptively
- Annex all supporting documents

**5. Prove Readiness and Willingness:**
- File affidavit with updated financials
- Examine-in-chief affidavit detailing continued readiness
- Cross-examination preparation (anticipate challenges to financial capacity)

**6. Interim Relief:**
- If risk of alienation, file for injunction restraining sale/encumbrance
- If property vacant, seek appointment of receiver
- Court may order status quo pending suit

**7. Active Prosecution:**
- Avoid adjournments (defendant may claim plaintiff not earnest)
- Complete evidence expeditiously
- File written arguments highlighting 2018 Amendment (specific performance mandatory)

**8. Deposit Decretal Amount (if Buyer):**
- Consider depositing balance purchase price in court
- Demonstrates unequivocal readiness
- Strengthens case significantly

**Post-Decree:**

**9. Execution:**
- If defendant refuses, immediately file execution
- Invoke Section 20(2) for substituted performance (if applicable)
- Or seek court-appointed commissioner to execute sale deed (Order XXI Rule 34 CPC)

**10. Mesne Profits:**
- If property withheld, claim mesne profits from breach date to delivery
- Expert evidence on rental value

### B. For Defendants Resisting Specific Performance

**Pre-Litigation:**

**1. Assess Contract Validity:**
- Review contract for defects (voidability grounds - coercion, fraud, undue influence, mistake)
- Check compliance with formalities (writing, registration if required)
- Verify plaintiff's title/capacity

**2. Gather Evidence of Exceptions:**
- If Section 14 exception (personal service, uncertain terms, etc.), collect evidence
- If plaintiff not ready/willing (Section 16(c)), document plaintiff's financial incapacity or unwillingness
- If misdescription, obtain surveyor's report

**3. Legal Notice (Counter-Notice):**
- Respond to plaintiff's notice
- Raise defenses
- Do NOT acknowledge liability (unless strategically chosen to extend limitation)

**Litigation:**

**4. Detailed Written Statement:**
- Specific denial of plaintiff's averments
- Raise all defenses (Sections 14, 16, 20)
- Challenge readiness and willingness with specificity
- Cite 2018 Amendment exceptions (burden on defendant to prove exception)

**5. Challenge Financial Capacity:**
- Cross-examine plaintiff on financials
- Produce evidence of plaintiff's financial difficulties (if any)
- Show loan sanction expired, insufficient funds

**6. Prove Exception/Bar:**
- If Section 14 exception, produce evidence (e.g., for personal service, employment contract terms)
- If third party hardship (Section 20(1)(b)), examine third party witness
- If inequitable (Section 16(b)), show gross disproportion in values or changed circumstances

**7. Offer Compensation:**
- If specific performance likely to be granted, offer compensation/settlement
- Minimize exposure

**Post-Decree:**

**8. Comply or Appeal:**
- If decree passed, assess appeal prospects
- Commercial Courts Act: Must deposit 75% of decretal amount for appeal
- If no grounds for appeal, comply to avoid mesne profits accumulation

**9. Challenge Execution:**
- If decree executable, file objections to execution if grounds exist
- Claim decree satisfied (if performed)
- Show decree unexecutable (if circumstances changed)

## X. Integration with RERA and Consumer Protection

### A. Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act, 2016 (RERA)

**RERA-SRA Synergy:**

**RERA Section 18:** Homebuyer can seek:
- Refund of amount paid (with interest), OR
- **Specific performance of agreement**

**Jurisdiction:**
- RERA Tribunals have jurisdiction to grant specific performance
- Alternative to civil courts

**Procedure:**
- Homebuyer files complaint with RERA Authority
- Authority can direct:
  - Developer to complete construction
  - Developer to hand over possession
  - Specific performance of agreement
- RERA orders executable as civil court decrees

**Advantages of RERA over Civil Courts:**
- Faster disposal (target: 60 days hearing, 30 days order)
- No court fee burden
- Specialized expertise in real estate disputes
- Summary procedures

**Choice of Forum:**
- Homebuyer can choose RERA or civil court (concurrent jurisdiction)
- Once filed in one forum, cannot file in other (doctrine of election)

**Post-2018 SRA Amendment + RERA:**
- Both frameworks strongly favor specific performance
- Homebuyers have robust remedies
- Developer cannot easily avoid performance

**Limitation:**
- RERA: Complaint within 3 years from cause of action OR 1 year from RERA commencement (whichever later) - expired for most projects now, so 3 years applies
- SRA: 3 years as per Article 54 Limitation Act

### B. Consumer Protection Act, 2019

**Consumer Disputes vs. Specific Performance:**

**If Homebuyer is "Consumer"** (purchased for personal use, not commercial):
- Can file complaint in Consumer Forum (District/State/National Commission)
- Consumer Forum can direct:
  - Specific performance (complete construction, deliver possession)
  - Compensation for deficiency in service
  - Refund with interest

**Advantages:**
- No court fee (only nominal fee)
- Summary procedure
- Typically faster than civil courts
- Can award punitive damages

**Limitation:**
- Consumer complaint: 2 years from cause of action (shorter than civil suits' 3 years)
- But consumer fora liberally condone delay

**Interplay:**
- Consumer forum and civil court have concurrent jurisdiction
- But generally, consumer forum more accessible and faster for B2C disputes
- Civil court (specific performance suit) used if:
  - Consumer time-barred (>2 years)
  - Complex legal issues involved
  - Value very high (crores)

## XI. Summary and Key Takeaways

**2018 Amendment - Paradigm Shift:**
- Specific performance transformed from discretionary to mandatory remedy
- Default: All contracts specifically enforceable unless exception
- Burden on defendant to prove Sections 14, 16, 20 exception
- Promotes contractual certainty, economic efficiency

**Key Requirements for Plaintiff:**
1. Valid, enforceable contract
2. Defendant's breach
3. Plaintiff's readiness and willingness (continuous)
4. No exception applies (Sections 14, 16)
5. No discretionary bar (Section 20)

**Exceptions (Defendant Must Prove):**
- Section 14: Personal service, goods with market value, uncertain contracts, partnership
- Section 16: Inequitable performance, plaintiff not ready/willing, time of essence violated
- Section 20: Third party hardship, performance impossible, plaintiff breached

**Innovative Post-2018 Provisions:**
- Section 20(2): Substituted performance (third party performs at defendant's cost)
- Section 11A: General rule of specific enforceability

**Practical Takeaways:**
- **For Buyers/Plaintiffs:** Maintain rigorous readiness and willingness documentation; file within 3 years limitation; emphasize 2018 Amendment in pleadings
- **For Sellers/Defendants:** Burden to prove exception; assess defenses realistically; consider settlement if no exception
- **Post-2018 Trend:** Courts robustly enforcing contracts, especially real estate; economic hardship not ground for refusal

**Future Directions:**
- Continued judicial interpretation of 2018 Amendment
- Integration with RERA, Consumer Protection frameworks
- Potential expansion of substituted performance to more contract categories
- Harmonization with international specific performance practices

## Conclusion

The Specific Relief Act, as transformed by the 2018 Amendment, represents India's definitive commitment to upholding contractual sanctity and commercial predictability. By shifting specific performance from an exceptional, discretionary remedy to the default, mandatory remedy, the Act aligns India with global best practices and signals to domestic and international parties that contracts will be enforced.

For practitioners, the 2018 Amendment necessitates reorienting advocacy: plaintiffs should emphasize the mandatory nature of specific performance and the narrow exceptions; defendants must prove specific statutory exceptions rather than appeal to general equitable discretion.

The integration of Section 20(2) substituted performance and the synergy with RERA and Commercial Courts Act create a robust ecosystem for contract enforcement, particularly in real estate and infrastructure sectors crucial to India's economic growth.
