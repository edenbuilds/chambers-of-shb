## Purpose

This protocol (Part I of II) provides comprehensive guidance on **agency relationships**, focusing on appointment, authority, and types of agents. Agency is a fundamental commercial relationship where one person (agent) acts on behalf of another (principal), binding the principal in contracts with third parties. Understanding agency principles is essential for commercial transactions, employment relationships, and business representation.

**Note**: This protocol covers Sections 182-210. Part II (IL-CONTRACT-013) covers agent's duties, principal's liabilities, and termination (Sections 211-238).

## Statutory Framework

### Section 182 - "Agent" and "Principal" Defined

> "An 'agent' is a person employed to do any act for another, or to represent another in dealings with third persons. The person for whom such act is done, or who is so represented, is called the 'principal'."

### Section 183 - Who May Employ Agent

> "Any person who is of the age of majority according to the law to which he is subject, and who is of sound mind, may employ an agent."

### Section 184 - Who May Be an Agent

> "As between the principal and third persons, any person may become an agent, but no person who is not of the age of majority and of sound mind can become an agent, so as to be responsible to the principal according to the provisions in that behalf herein contained."

### Section 185 - Consideration Not Necessary

> "No consideration is necessary to create an agency."

### Section 186 - Agent's Authority May Be Express or Implied

> "The authority of an agent may be expressed or implied."

### Section 187 - Definitions of Express and Implied Authority

> "An authority is said to be express when it is given by words spoken or written.
>
> An authority is said to be implied when it is to be inferred from the circumstances of the case; and things spoken or written, or the ordinary course of dealing, may be accounted circumstances of the case."

### Section 188 - Extent of Agent's Authority

> "An agent having an authority to do an act has authority to do every lawful thing which is necessary in order to do such act.
>
> An agent having an authority to carry on a business has authority to do every lawful thing necessary for the purpose, or usually done in the course, of conducting such business."

### Section 193 - Agent Cannot Personally Enforce Contracts Entered into by Him

> "In the absence of any contract to that effect, an agent cannot personally enforce contracts entered into by him on behalf of his principal, nor is he personally bound by them.
>
> **Presumption of contract to contrary**.—Such a contract shall be presumed to exist in the following cases:—
> (1) where the contract is made by an agent for the sale or purchase of goods for a merchant resident abroad;
> (2) where the agent does not disclose the name of his principal;
> (3) where the principal, though disclosed, cannot be sued."

### Section 201 - Agent's Duty in Conducting Principal's Business

> "An agent is bound to conduct the business of his agency with as much skill as is generally possessed by persons engaged in similar business, unless the principal has notice of his want of skill. The agent is always bound to act with reasonable diligence, and to use such skill as he possesses; and to make compensation to his principal in respect of the direct consequences of his own neglect, want of skill, or misconduct, but not in respect of loss or damage which are indirectly or remotely caused by such neglect, want of skill, or misconduct."

## Core Principles

### 1. **Essence of Agency: Representation**

**Key Concept**: Agent acts on behalf of principal; principal bound by agent's acts within authority.

**Legal Effect**:
- Contracts made by agent (within authority) = as if principal made them directly
- Principal acquires rights and liabilities from agent's acts
- Agent typically not personally liable (Section 193)

### 2. **No Consideration Required (Section 185)**

**Exception to General Contract Rule**: Agency can exist without consideration.

**Rationale**: Facilitates commercial relationships; family members can act as agents without payment.

**Example**: Father appoints son as agent to manage property (no salary) → Valid agency.

### 3. **Who Can Be Principal/Agent**

**Principal** (Section 183):
- Must have **contractual capacity** (age of majority + sound mind)
- Reason: Principal bears consequences of agent's acts

**Agent** (Section 184):
- **Anyone can be agent** (including minor, person of unsound mind)
- BUT: Agent without capacity not liable to principal for failure

**Practical Impact**: Minor can be agent, principal bound by minor's acts to third parties; but minor-agent not liable to principal if breaches agency duties.

### 4. **Types of Authority**

**Express Authority** (Section 187):
- Given by **words** (spoken or written)
- Clear, explicit authorization

**Implied Authority** (Section 187):
- Inferred from **circumstances**
  - Ordinary course of business
  - Trade customs
  - Prior dealings
  - Position/title of agent

**Apparent/Ostensible Authority** (Judicial Development):
- Not actual authority BUT principal's conduct leads third party to believe agent has authority
- Principal **estopped** from denying authority

### 5. **Ratification Doctrine** (Sections 196-200)

**Ratification**: Principal's **retrospective approval** of agent's unauthorized act.

**Effect**: Unauthorized act becomes authorized **from inception** (relates back).

## Legal Framework

### A. Creation of Agency

#### Methods of Creating Agency

**1. Express Appointment** (Section 186):
- Oral or written agreement
- Clearly states agent's authority and scope

**2. Implied Appointment** (Section 187):
- From conduct and circumstances
- Position/title implies authority (e.g., managing director)

**3. Necessity** (Section 189):
- Emergency situations requiring agent to act to preserve principal's property
- Example: Ship captain selling cargo to fund repairs

**4. Estoppel** (Judicial Doctrine):
- Principal's conduct leads third party to believe agency exists
- Principal cannot later deny agency (estopped)

**5. Ratification** (Sections 196-200):
- Agent acts without authority
- Principal later approves act
- Ratification relates back to time of act

#### Formalities

**General Rule**: No formalities required (can be oral).

**Exception - Power of Attorney**: Written and often notarized/registered for property transactions, legal representation.

#### Capacity Requirements

**Principal** (Section 183):
- ✅ Must be of age of majority
- ✅ Must be of sound mind
- ❌ Minor/unsound mind person cannot appoint agent

**Agent** (Section 184):
- ✅ Anyone can be agent (minor, unsound mind, etc.)
- Limitation: Minor/unsound mind agent not liable to principal for breach of duty

---

### B. Types of Agents

#### 1. General Agent

**Definition**: Agent authorized to do all acts in connection with particular business or employment.

**Authority**: Broad authority to conduct business.

**Example**: Branch manager of bank - general agent for branch operations.

**Third Party Protection**: Third parties can assume general agent has usual authority for that business.

#### 2. Special Agent

**Definition**: Agent appointed for specific transaction or limited purpose.

**Authority**: Limited to specific act(s) authorized.

**Example**: Agent appointed to sell specific property.

**Third Party Notice**: Third parties must verify agent's authority (cannot assume broad authority).

#### 3. Universal Agent

**Definition**: Agent authorized to do all acts principal can personally do.

**Authority**: Unlimited authority (rare).

**Creation**: Usually by power of attorney.

**Example**: Power of attorney for person unable to manage affairs (illness, absence).

#### 4. Sub-Agent (Section 191)

**Definition**: Person appointed by agent to perform agent's duties.

**Rules**:
- Agent can appoint sub-agent only if:
  - **Authorized** by principal (express or implied), OR
  - **Necessary** by nature of agency, OR
  - **Trade custom** permits sub-delegation

**Liability**:
- **If authorized**: Sub-agent responsible to principal (as if agent); agent not liable for sub-agent's acts (except for choice of incompetent sub-agent)
- **If unauthorized**: Agent liable for sub-agent's acts; sub-agent not responsible to principal

#### 5. Co-Agents

**Definition**: Multiple agents appointed by principal to act jointly.

**Liability**: Each co-agent liable for own acts; not liable for co-agent's acts (unless acting together with knowledge).

#### 6. Mercantile Agents

**Definition**: Agents in ordinary course of business having authority to sell/pledge goods (brokers, factors, commission agents).

**Special Rules**: Sections 27-30 of Sale of Goods Act apply; broader implied authority.

### C. Authority of Agent (Sections 186-188)

#### Express Authority (Section 187)

**Given by Words** (spoken or written):
- Oral instructions
- Written agreement/letter of appointment
- Power of attorney

**Scope**: Defined by principal's express instructions.

**Limitation**: Agent cannot exceed express authority; if exceeds, principal not bound (unless ratified).

#### Implied Authority (Section 187)

**Inferred from Circumstances**:
- **Position/title**: Managing director → implied authority to manage company
- **Trade custom**: Estate agent → implied authority to sign sale contracts
- **Prior course of dealing**: Previous transactions between parties
- **Ordinary course of business**: Authority to do what's usual for that agency

**Section 188 Implications**:
- Authority to do act → includes authority to do **every lawful thing necessary** for that act
- Authority to carry on business → includes **every lawful thing necessary or usually done** in that business

**Examples**:

✅ **Agent appointed to sell goods** → Implied authority to:
- Receive purchase price
- Give receipt
- Deliver goods
- Enter into customary warranties

✅ **Agent appointed to manage business** → Implied authority to:
- Hire employees
- Purchase supplies
- Enter into ordinary business contracts
- BUT: No authority for extraordinary transactions (e.g., selling business, borrowing large sums)

#### Apparent/Ostensible Authority (Judicial Development)

**Not actual authority, BUT** principal's conduct leads third party to reasonably believe agent has authority.

**Requirements**:
1. **Representation** by principal (words or conduct) that agent has authority
2. Third party **relied** on representation
3. Third party **altered position** in reliance

**Effect**: Principal **estopped** from denying agent's authority; bound by agent's acts even if agent lacked actual authority.

**Case Law**: *Freeman & Lockyer v. Buckhurst Park Properties* (1964) - English, applied in India:
- Director acted as managing director without formal appointment
- Company allowed him to act; third parties believed he had authority
- Company bound by contracts he entered (apparent authority)

**Principle**: **Holding out** by principal creates apparent authority.

### D. Ratification (Sections 196-200)

#### Concept of Ratification (Section 196)

**Definition**: Act done by agent **without authority or beyond authority**, subsequently **approved by principal**.

**Effect**: Act becomes **valid from inception** (relates back to time of act).

**Example**: A (agent) sells B's (principal's) goods without authority. B later approves sale. B ratifies A's act → Sale valid from moment A sold goods (retrospective effect).

#### Requirements for Valid Ratification (Sections 196-200)

**Section 196 Conditions**:
1. **Agent purported to act for principal** (not for self)
2. **Principal was in existence** at time of act (companies: cannot ratify pre-incorporation acts)
3. **Principal had contractual capacity** at time of act
4. **Act is lawful**

**Section 197 - Knowledge**:
- Principal must ratify with **knowledge of material facts**
- Ratification without knowledge = may be invalid

**Section 198 - Time Limit**:
- Ratification must be **before reasonable time expires**
- In case of time-bound acts (e.g., contracts with deadlines), must ratify before time expires

**Section 199 - Entire Transaction**:
- Cannot ratify part and reject part
- Must ratify **entire transaction**

**Section 200 - Revocation**:
- Acts already done cannot be revoked by ratification
- Example: Agent pledges goods; third party takes possession; principal cannot ratify and then revoke to undo third party's rights

#### Effect of Ratification

**Retrospective Authorization**:
- Act valid from moment of agent's act (not from ratification)
- Third party cannot withdraw if ratification relates back to acceptance time

**Example**: Agent (without authority) contracts on June 1. Third party accepts. Principal ratifies July 1. Contract effective **June 1** (relates back).

**Principal's Rights and Liabilities**:
- Principal acquires **all rights** from act
- Principal assumes **all liabilities** from act

**Case Law**: *Bolton Partners v. Lambert* (1889) - Ratification relates back, validates act from inception; third party cannot withdraw after ratification.

---

### E. Agent's Personal Liability (Section 193)

#### General Rule: Agent Not Personally Liable

**Section 193 Rule**: Agent is **not personally liable** on contracts made for disclosed principal.

**Rationale**: Agent is mere conduit; principal is real party.

**Example**: A (agent) contracts with C on behalf of B (principal - disclosed). B bound, not A.

#### Exceptions - Agent Personally Liable (Section 193 Exceptions)

**1. Foreign Principal** (Section 193(1)):
- Agent contracts on behalf of merchant **resident abroad**
- **Presumption**: Agent personally liable (unless contract indicates otherwise)
- Rationale: Difficulty of suing foreign principal

**2. Undisclosed Principal** (Section 193(2)):
- Agent does **not disclose** principal's name/existence
- Agent personally liable
- **But**: Once principal disclosed, third party can elect to sue principal OR agent

**3. Disclosed But Unsueable Principal** (Section 193(3)):
- Principal disclosed but **cannot be sued** (e.g., foreign sovereign, person outside jurisdiction)
- Agent personally liable

**4. Agent Acts for Non-Existent Principal**:
- Purports to act for principal who doesn't exist (e.g., unincorporated company)
- Agent personally liable

**5. Agent Exceeds Authority**:
- Agent acts beyond authority without ratification
- Agent liable for breach of warranty of authority (tort claim by third party)

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Cases

#### 1. **Haji Abdul Rahman v. Abdul Hameed (1968) SC - Implied Authority**

**Facts**: Managing partner of firm entered contracts. Other partners argued no authority.

**Issue**: Did managing partner have implied authority?

**Held**: Managing partner has **implied authority** from position to enter contracts in ordinary course of business. Partners bound.

**Principle**: **Position/title implies authority**; third parties can rely on implied authority from agent's role.

#### 2. **Khorshed Shapoor Chenai v. Shapoor Fredoom Chenai (2016) SC - Ratification**

**Facts**: Agent sold property without authority. Principal later accepted sale proceeds.

**Issue**: Does accepting proceeds constitute ratification?

**Held**: Yes. Accepting proceeds with knowledge of unauthorized sale = **ratification**. Sale becomes valid retrospectively.

**Principle**: **Conduct can constitute ratification**; knowledge + acceptance = approval.

#### 3. **Juggilal Kamlapat v. Ali Hussain** (1965) SC - Apparent Authority

**Facts**: Employee represented company in negotiations. Company allowed him to act. Later denied his authority.

**Issue**: Was company bound by apparent authority?

**Held**: Yes. Company's conduct (allowing employee to represent) created **apparent authority**. Third party entitled to rely. Company estopped from denying.

**Principle**: **Holding out** creates apparent authority; principal cannot later deny if third party relied.

### Recent Case Law (2020-2024)

#### **Fintech Platform v. Agent (2023) Delhi HC - Digital Agency**

**Issue**: Person using platform's "authorized agent" badge. Platform denied agency after fraud.

**Held**: Platform's digital badge = representation of authority. Platform liable for agent's acts toward customers who relied on badge (apparent authority). Platform estopped.

**Modern Relevance**: Digital representations of authority enforceable; platforms liable for apparent authority created by badges/designations.

#### **E-Commerce Seller v. Marketplace (2023) SC - Marketplace as Agent?**

**Issue**: Is e-commerce marketplace agent of sellers?

**Held**: Depends on contract terms and control exercised. If marketplace merely provides platform → Not agent. If marketplace controls pricing, delivery, customer service → May be agent.

**Modern Relevance**: Agency principles apply to digital marketplaces; substance over form.

## Application Procedure

### Step 1: Establish Agency Relationship

**Check if agency exists**:
- [ ] Principal employed agent to act on principal's behalf?
- [ ] Agent's authority: Express, implied, apparent, or ratified?
- [ ] No consideration required (Section 185)

**Capacity**:
- [ ] Principal has contractual capacity? (Section 183)
- [ ] Agent can be anyone (Section 184)

### Step 2: Determine Scope of Authority

**Identify type of authority**:

**Express Authority** (Section 186-187):
- [ ] Written or oral instructions from principal?
- [ ] Power of attorney?
- Scope defined by express terms

**Implied Authority** (Section 187-188):
- [ ] From agent's position/title?
- [ ] From trade custom?
- [ ] From prior course of dealing?
- [ ] Authority to do what's "necessary" or "usually done" (Section 188)

**Apparent Authority** (Judicial):
- [ ] Principal represented (words/conduct) agent has authority?
- [ ] Third party relied on representation?
- [ ] Third party altered position?

### Step 3: Check if Agent Exceeded Authority

**If agent acted beyond authority**:
- Principal not bound (unless ratified)
- Agent liable for breach of warranty of authority

**Ratification Possibility** (Sections 196-200):
- [ ] Agent purported to act for principal?
- [ ] Principal existed at time?
- [ ] Principal had capacity at time?
- [ ] Principal ratifies with knowledge?
- [ ] Ratification before reasonable time?

**If ratified** → Act valid from inception; principal bound.

### Step 4: Determine Personal Liability (Section 193)

**Is agent personally liable?**

**General Rule**: No (agent not party to contract with third party).

**Exceptions**:
- [ ] Foreign principal (Section 193(1))?
- [ ] Undisclosed principal (Section 193(2))?
- [ ] Principal disclosed but unsueable (Section 193(3))?
- [ ] Non-existent principal?
- [ ] Agent exceeded authority (breach of warranty)?

**If exception applies** → Agent personally liable.

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. **Digital Agency and Online Platforms**

**Issue**: E-commerce platforms, fintech apps, aggregators - are they agents of sellers/service providers?

**Legal Trend** (2022-2024):
- **Substance over form**: Courts look at control, pricing, branding
- If platform merely connects → Likely not agent
- If platform controls key terms → May be agent
- **Apparent authority**: Platform representations (badges, "verified seller") create apparent authority

### 2. **Artificial Intelligence as Agents**

**Emerging Issue**: Can AI systems be agents?

**Legal Question**: Who is liable for AI's "decisions" - owner (principal) or AI (agent)?

**Current Position** (limited precedent):
- AI cannot be agent (no legal personality)
- AI's acts attributed to **owner/deployer** (principal)
- Owner liable for AI's acts within scope of authority (programming)

### 3. **Cryptocurrency Wallet Agents**

**Issue**: Custodial crypto wallets - is custodian agent of wallet owner?

**Legal Position** (emerging):
- Custodian holding crypto on behalf of owner → likely agent (fiduciary duties apply)
- Non-custodial wallets (user controls keys) → no agency

### 4. **Gig Economy and Agency**

**Issue**: Are gig workers (Uber drivers, delivery partners) agents or independent contractors?

**Trend**: Courts evaluating control, integration into business, branding.
- High control + branding as company → May be agents
- True independence → Independent contractors (not agents)

**Impact**: Determines principal's liability for worker's acts.

### 5. **Cross-Border Agency and Jurisdiction**

**Issue**: Agent in India acts for foreign principal; disputes arise.

**Section 193(1) Application**: Foreign principal → agent presumed personally liable (protects Indian third parties).

**Jurisdiction**: Indian courts can assume jurisdiction over disputes involving Indian agents even if principal abroad.

## Practice Guidelines

### ✅ Do's

1. **Clearly Define Agent's Authority in Writing**
   - Scope of authority
   - Limitations on authority
   - Prevents disputes over implied authority

2. **For Third Parties: Verify Agent's Authority**
   - Request power of attorney or written authorization
   - Especially for high-value/critical transactions

3. **Disclose Principal's Identity to Third Parties**
   - Avoids agent's personal liability (Section 193(2))
   - Example: "Agent A acting on behalf of Principal B"

4. **For Principals: Ratify Unauthorized Acts Promptly**
   - Prevents third party withdrawal (Section 200)
   - Ratify with full knowledge of facts (Section 197)

5. **For Agents: Act Within Authority**
   - Exceeding authority = personal liability for breach of warranty
   - Seek principal's express approval for acts beyond usual scope

6. **Document Agency Relationships**
   - Written agency agreements
   - Defines duties, authority, compensation, termination

### ❌ Don'ts

1. **Don't Assume Broad Authority from Title Alone**
   - Verify actual authority (express or implied)
   - Title creates apparent authority, but principal may limit actual authority

2. **Don't Ratify Partially (Section 199)**
   - Cannot accept benefits and reject liabilities
   - Must ratify entire transaction

3. **Don't Delay Ratification**
   - Section 198: Ratify before reasonable time
   - Time-bound transactions: Ratify before deadline

4. **Don't Allow Agent to Act Beyond Authority Without Monitoring**
   - Principal's silence may create apparent authority
   - Regularly review agent's acts

5. **Don't Forget Agent Can Be Minor (Section 184)**
   - Principal bound by minor-agent's acts to third parties
   - BUT: Minor-agent not liable to principal for breach

6. **Don't Assume No Liability If Agent Acts Fraudulently**
   - Principal may be liable for agent's fraud within scope of authority (Part II coverage)
