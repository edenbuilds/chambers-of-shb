## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on **quasi-contracts** (also called "constructive contracts" or "implied contracts in law") under Indian law. Quasi-contracts are not actual contracts but legal obligations imposed by law to prevent unjust enrichment. Understanding quasi-contractual obligations is essential for restitution claims, recovery of benefits conferred, and preventing unjust enrichment.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Chapter V Heading - Certain Relations Resembling Those Created by Contract

> "The relations resembling those created by contract which are dealt with in this Chapter are as follows:—"

### Section 68 - Claim for Necessaries Supplied to Person Incapable of Contracting, or on His Account

> "If a person, incapable of entering into a contract, or anyone whom he is legally bound to support, is supplied by another person with necessaries suited to his condition in life, the person who has furnished such supplies is entitled to be reimbursed from the property of such incapable person.
>
> **Illustrations**:
> (a) A supplies B, a lunatic, with necessaries suitable to his condition in life. A is entitled to be reimbursed from B's property.
> (b) A supplies the wife and children of B, a lunatic, with necessaries suitable to their condition in life. A is entitled to be reimbursed from B's property."

### Section 69 - Reimbursement of Person Paying Money Due by Another, in Payment of Which He is Interested

> "A person who is interested in the payment of money which another is bound by law to pay, and who therefore pays it, is entitled to be reimbursed by the other.
>
> **Illustration**: B holds land in Bengal, on a lease granted by A, the zamindar. The revenue payable by A to the Government being in arrear, his land is advertised for sale by the Government. Under the revenue law, the consequence of such sale will be the annulment of B's lease. B, to prevent the sale and the consequent annulment of his own lease, pays to the Government the sum due from A. A is bound to make good to B the amount so paid."

### Section 70 - Obligation of Person Enjoying Benefit of Non-Gratuitous Act

> "Where a person lawfully does anything for another person, or delivers anything to him, not intending to do so gratuitously, and such other person enjoys the benefit thereof, the latter is bound to make compensation to the former in respect of, or to restore, the thing so done or delivered.
>
> **Illustrations**:
> (a) A, a tradesman, leaves goods at B's house by mistake. B treats the goods as his own. He is bound to pay A for them.
> (b) A saves B's property from a fire. A is not entitled to compensation from B, if the circumstances show that he intended to act gratuitously."

### Section 71 - Responsibility of Finder of Goods

> "A person who finds goods belonging to another, and takes them into his custody, is subject to the same responsibility as a bailee.
>
> **[See bailment provisions for detailed obligations]**"

### Section 72 - Liability of Person to Whom Money is Paid, or Thing Delivered, by Mistake or Under Coercion

> "A person to whom money has been paid, or anything delivered, by mistake or under coercion, must repay or return it.
>
> **Illustrations**:
> (a) A and B jointly owe ₹100 to C. A alone pays the amount to C, and B, not knowing this fact, pays ₹100 over again to C. C is bound to repay the amount to B.
> (b) A railway company refuses to deliver up certain goods to the consignee, except upon the payment of an illegal charge for carriage. The consignee pays the sum charged in order to obtain the goods. He is entitled to recover so much of the charge as was illegally excessive."

## Core Principles

### 1. **Nature of Quasi-Contracts**

**"Quasi" = As If**:
- Not actual contracts (no offer, acceptance, or agreement)
- **Legal fiction**: Law treats as if contract exists
- Imposed by law to achieve justice
- Based on **equity** principle: No one should unjustly benefit at another's expense

**Key Characteristics**:
- No actual agreement between parties
- Obligations imposed by law (statutory or judicial)
- Prevent unjust enrichment
- Restitutionary in nature (restore benefits, not compensate expectations)

### 2. **Restitution vs. Contract Damages**

**Quasi-Contract Remedy** (Restitution):
- Restore benefit received
- **Quantum meruit** ("as much as earned")
- Value of benefit, not contract price

**Contract Damages**:
- Compensate expectation interest
- Put party in position as if contract performed
- May exceed value of benefit received

**Example**: A mistakenly paints B's house (no contract). Quasi-contract remedy = fair value of painting (quantum meruit), not what A would have charged if contracted.

### 3. **Unjust Enrichment Doctrine**

**Foundation of Quasi-Contracts**:
- Rooted in Roman law concept: *Nemo debet locupletari ex aliena jactura* (No one should be enriched unjustly at another's expense)

**Elements**:
1. **Enrichment**: Defendant received benefit
2. **At plaintiff's expense**: Benefit came from plaintiff
3. **Unjust**: No legal justification for defendant keeping benefit
4. **No other remedy**: Plaintiff has no contractual claim

### 4. **Five Categories (Sections 68-72)**

**Section 68**: Necessaries supplied to incapable person
**Section 69**: Payment of another's debt in which payer has interest
**Section 70**: Benefit of non-gratuitous act enjoyed
**Section 71**: Finder of goods (bailee obligations)
**Section 72**: Money/thing delivered by mistake or coercion

### 5. **Not Exhaustive List**

Courts recognize **additional quasi-contractual obligations** beyond Sections 68-72 based on unjust enrichment principles.

**Judicial Recognition**:
- Restitution for work done before contract frustrated
- Recovery for benefits conferred before contract discovered void
- Subrogation rights

## Legal Framework

### A. Section 68 - Necessaries Supplied to Incapable Person

#### Scope and Application

**When Applicable**:
1. Person **incapable of contracting** (minor, person of unsound mind, etc.)
2. **Necessaries** supplied to incapable person OR their dependents
3. Necessaries **suited to condition in life**

**Effect**: Supplier entitled to **reimbursement from property** of incapable person.

#### "Necessaries" Defined

**Necessaries**: Goods/services essential for subsistence, health, education, or reasonable comfort according to person's station in life.

**Factors Determining "Necessaries"**:
- Social status, wealth, family background of incapable person
- Whether incapable person already has sufficient supply
- Customary needs of person's community/class

**Examples - Typically Necessaries**:
✅ Food, clothing, shelter
✅ Medical treatment, medicines
✅ Education expenses
✅ Reasonable conveyance
✅ Legal services (in certain cases)

**Examples - Typically NOT Necessaries**:
❌ Luxury goods beyond station in life
❌ Jewelry, expensive watches (unless customary for family)
❌ Speculative investments
❌ Non-essential entertainment

#### Reimbursement Limitations

**From Property Only**: Supplier can recover from **incapable person's property**, not personally.

**No Personal Liability**: Incapable person (especially minor) not personally liable to pay.

**Proof Required**:
- Items were necessaries
- Suited to incapable person's condition in life
- Incapable person did not already have sufficient supply

**Case Law**: *Nash v. Inman* (1908) English, applied in India - University student (minor) supplied fancy waistcoats; held NOT necessaries (already had sufficient clothing).

### B. Section 69 - Payment of Another's Debt

#### When Obligation Arises

**Elements**:
1. Person A **interested** in payment of debt
2. Debt owed by person B
3. A **pays** B's debt
4. Payment necessary to protect A's interest

**Effect**: B bound to **reimburse** A.

#### "Interested in Payment" Defined

**Interest means**: Legal or equitable interest in ensuring debt paid (not mere volunteer).

**Examples of Interest**:

✅ **Tenant paying landlord's property tax** (to prevent lease cancellation - Section 69 Illustration)
✅ **Junior mortgagee paying senior mortgage** (to prevent foreclosure of property)
✅ **Surety paying principal debtor's debt** (surety then has right of reimbursement)
✅ **Co-owner paying property charges** (to preserve common property)

❌ **Stranger with no interest**: Cannot recover (e.g., A pays B's credit card bill out of goodwill → Cannot recover).

#### Extent of Reimbursement

**Amount Recoverable**: Actual amount paid (not more, not less).

**No damages**: Only restitution of payment made.

**Case Law**: *Exall v. Partridge* (1799) - Surety paid debt; entitled to reimbursement from principal debtor (Section 69 principle).

### C. Section 70 - Benefit of Non-Gratuitous Act

#### Elements of Section 70 Claim

**Five Requirements**:
1. **Lawful act**: Plaintiff did something lawfully for defendant OR delivered something to defendant
2. **Non-gratuitous**: Did NOT intend to act gratuitously (free of charge)
3. **Benefit enjoyed**: Defendant enjoyed benefit
4. **No contract**: No actual contract between parties
5. **Unjust enrichment**: Unjust for defendant to retain benefit without payment

**Effect**: Defendant bound to **compensate** plaintiff or **restore** thing delivered.

#### "Non-Gratuitous" Intent

**Test**: Did plaintiff intend to act free of charge, or did plaintiff expect payment?

**Presumption**: In **commercial context**, presumed non-gratuitous (expected payment).

**Rebuttal**: If circumstances show **gratuitous intent** (e.g., friendly favor, emergency rescue), no Section 70 claim.

**Illustration (b)**: A saves B's property from fire → If circumstances show A intended to act gratuitously (emergency rescue by neighbor), A **not entitled** to compensation.

**Contrast**: If A is professional fire-fighter hired to guard area, and saves B's property → Non-gratuitous (expected payment); A may claim under Section 70.

#### "Benefit Enjoyed" Requirement

**Subjective vs. Objective Benefit**:
- **Objective**: Service/goods have market value
- **Subjective**: Defendant actually wanted/benefited from service

**Indian Position**: Generally **objective test** - if reasonable person would consider it beneficial, sufficient (even if defendant did not subjectively want it).

**Exception**: If defendant expressly refused service beforehand, cannot impose benefit and claim payment.

**Example**: A mistakenly delivers goods to B's house (thinking it's customer C's house). B treats goods as own → B enjoyed benefit; must pay A (Section 70 Illustration (a)).

#### Quantum Meruit - Measure of Compensation

**Compensation = Fair value of benefit**, not:
- What plaintiff would have charged under contract
- Plaintiff's profit margin
- Defendant's savings

**Quantum Meruit**: "As much as earned" - reasonable value for work done.

**Factors in Valuation**:
- Market rates for similar services
- Customary charges
- Actual benefit to defendant
- Quality and extent of work

**Case Law**: *Munshi Ram v. Municipal Committee, Delhi* (1968) SC - Work done under void contract; plaintiff entitled to quantum meruit based on reasonable value, not contract price.

### D. Section 71 - Finder of Goods

#### Obligations of Finder

**Section 71 Rule**: Finder of goods = **Bailee** (same responsibilities).

**Finder's Duties** (as bailee):
1. **Take reasonable care** of goods (as careful owner would be)
2. **Not use goods** for own purposes (except limited use for preservation - e.g., riding lost horse to shelter)
3. **Search for true owner** (reasonable efforts)
4. **Return goods** to true owner when found
5. **Not mix** goods with own property (if divisible goods)

**Rights of Finder**:
- Retain goods until expenses of preservation recovered
- Sell goods (if perishable OR owner cannot be found despite reasonable search OR owner refuses to pay finder's expenses)
- Sue third party for damage/loss to goods (bailee's right)

**Case Law**: *Amichand Payrelal v. Union of India* (1969) SC - Finder has qualified ownership (bailee rights); can maintain suit for goods against third parties.

### E. Section 72 - Money/Thing Delivered by Mistake or Coercion

#### Two Situations

**A. Delivery by Mistake**
**B. Delivery under Coercion**

#### A. Mistake (Section 72)

**Rule**: Money/thing paid by **mistake** must be **repaid/returned**.

**Types of Mistake**:
- **Mistake of fact**: Payment based on incorrect factual belief
- **Mistake of law** (special treatment)

**Mistake of Fact Examples**:
✅ A pays debt twice, thinking first payment not made → Second payment recoverable (Section 72 Illustration (a))
✅ A pays B's debt, believing A is legally obligated → If A was NOT obligated, payment recoverable

**Mistake of Law**:
- **General Rule**: Mistake of Indian law = **no recovery** (ignorantia juris non excusat)
- **Exception**: Mistake of **foreign law** treated as mistake of **fact** → recoverable

**Limitation**: Cannot recover if:
- Payment was **due** (even if paid by mistake, if debt existed → no recovery)
- Payee **changed position** in reliance on payment (estoppel)

**Case Law**: *Durga Prasad v. Baldeo* (1880) - Payment under mistake of law not recoverable; payment under mistake of fact recoverable.

**Modern Position**: *Hydro Engineering* (2015) SC - Clarified that mistake of foreign law = mistake of fact; recoverable under Section 72.

#### B. Coercion (Section 72)

**Rule**: Money/thing delivered under **coercion** must be repaid/returned.

**Coercion Defined**: Unlawful pressure compelling payment (Section 15 coercion applies).

**Example (Section 72 Illustration (b))**:
Railway refuses to deliver goods unless illegal charge paid. Consignee pays to get goods. **Illegal excess recoverable** under Section 72.

**Forms of Coercion**:
- Threat of violence
- Unlawful detention of property
- Abuse of legal process (e.g., threat of false criminal case)
- Economic duress (in certain circumstances)

**Reimbursement**: Full amount paid under coercion (or value of thing delivered).

**Case Law**: *Great Western Railway Co. v. Sutton* (1869) - Payment to avoid unlawful detention of goods recoverable.

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Supreme Court Cases

#### 1. **Maula Bux v. Union of India (1969) SC - Section 70**

**Facts**: Contractor partially performed government contract; government terminated contract. Contractor claimed quantum meruit.

**Issue**: Can contractor claim under Section 70 for work done?

**Held**: Yes. Work done under contract later terminated → quasi-contractual obligation under Section 70. Contractor entitled to **quantum meruit** (fair value of work done), not contract price.

**Principle**: **Section 70 applies** when contract cannot be completed; prevents unjust enrichment of beneficiary.

#### 2. **Munshi Ram v. Municipal Committee, Delhi (1968) SC - Quantum Meruit**

**Facts**: Contract to construct drains; contract discovered void (incorrect tender process). Work already done.

**Issue**: Can contractor recover for work done under void contract?

**Held**: Yes. Under Section 70 and Section 65, contractor entitled to **quantum meruit** - reasonable value of work, not contract price.

**Principle**: **Quantum meruit** compensates fair value even if contract void.

#### 3. **State of West Bengal v. M/s B.K. Mondal & Sons (1962) SC - Section 69**

**Facts**: Contractor paid government dues on behalf of another contractor to prevent machinery seizure (contractor had interest in machinery).

**Issue**: Can contractor recover payment under Section 69?

**Held**: Yes. Contractor had **interest in payment** (protecting machinery). Entitled to reimbursement from party whose debt was paid.

**Principle**: **Section 69 interest** includes commercial/proprietary interest justifying payment.

### Recent Case Law (2020-2024)

#### **Builder v. Purchaser (2022) SC - Quantum Meruit on Termination**

**Facts**: Flat purchase agreement; builder partially constructed; agreement canceled by mutual consent.

**Issue**: Is buyer entitled to refund only, or builder entitled to retain portion for work done?

**Held**: Builder entitled to **reasonable compensation** for work done (quantum meruit under Section 70 principle), not full retention of advance. Buyer entitled to refund minus reasonable value of completed work pro-rated to buyer's unit.

**Modern Relevance**: Quasi-contract principles applied to real estate disputes; prevents unjust enrichment of either party.

#### **Bank v. Account Holder (2023) SC - Mistaken Payment (Section 72)**

**Facts**: Bank mistakenly credited ₹50 lakh to A's account. A withdrew and spent money. Bank discovered mistake, demanded return.

**Issue**: Can bank recover under Section 72?

**Held**: Yes. Payment by **mistake** (bank error) → A must return under Section 72. A's change of position (spending) not defense if A should have known payment was erroneous (large unexpected sum).

**Modern Relevance**: Digital banking errors recoverable under Section 72; recipient cannot unjustly benefit from bank mistakes.

## Application Procedure

### Step 1: Identify Applicable Quasi-Contract Provision

**Determine which section applies**:

- [ ] **Section 68**: Necessaries supplied to incapable person?
- [ ] **Section 69**: Payment of another's debt in which payer has interest?
- [ ] **Section 70**: Non-gratuitous act benefiting another without contract?
- [ ] **Section 71**: Finder of goods?
- [ ] **Section 72**: Money/thing delivered by mistake or coercion?

### Step 2: Establish Elements of Claim

**Section 68 Elements**:
- [ ] Person incapable of contracting (minor, unsound mind)
- [ ] Necessaries supplied (suited to condition in life)
- [ ] Incapable person or dependents received benefit

**Section 69 Elements**:
- [ ] Debt owed by another person
- [ ] Payer has **interest** in payment (not mere volunteer)
- [ ] Payment made to protect payer's interest

**Section 70 Elements**:
- [ ] Lawful act done or thing delivered
- [ ] **Non-gratuitous** intent (not free/charitable)
- [ ] Defendant **enjoyed benefit**
- [ ] No actual contract

**Section 72 Elements**:
- [ ] Money paid or thing delivered
- [ ] By **mistake** OR under **coercion**
- [ ] Recipient received benefit

### Step 3: Prove Benefit and Quantum

**For Sections 68, 69, 70**:
- Establish value of benefit conferred
- **Quantum meruit**: Fair market value (not contract price or plaintiff's asking price)

**Evidence**:
- Market rates for similar services/goods
- Expert valuation
- Customary charges in trade

**For Section 72**:
- Establish amount paid or value of thing delivered

### Step 4: Check for Defenses

**Common Defenses**:
- **Gratuitous intent** (Section 70): Circumstances show plaintiff intended to act free
- **No benefit** (Section 70): Defendant did not actually benefit; services unsolicited and unwanted
- **Payment was due** (Section 72 mistake): Even if paid by mistake, if debt existed, no recovery
- **Change of position** (Section 72): Recipient changed position in good faith reliance
- **Statute of limitations**: Quasi-contract claims subject to limitation (typically 3 years from cause of action)

### Step 5: Calculate Remedy

**Quantum Meruit** (Sections 68, 70):
- Fair value of services/goods provided
- **Not**: Contract price, plaintiff's normal charges, or defendant's profits

**Reimbursement** (Section 69):
- Exact amount paid (not more, not less)

**Restitution** (Section 72):
- Exact amount/value delivered by mistake or coercion

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. **Digital Payment Errors and Section 72**

**Issue**: Bank/UPI errors resulting in wrong recipient receiving money.

**Trend (2022-2024)**:
- Recipients must return mistaken payments under Section 72
- Banks can recover even if recipient spent money (unless recipient proves good faith change of position)
- **RBI guidelines**: Recipients refusing to return subject to legal action

**Principle**: Section 72 applies to digital payment errors; unjust enrichment principles enforced.

### 2. **Cryptocurrency Transfers and Unjust Enrichment**

**Issue**: Crypto sent to wrong wallet address by mistake.

**Legal Question**: Can Section 72 apply to crypto transfers?

**Emerging Position**: If transfer by **identifiable mistake** and recipient **identifiable** → quasi-contractual obligation to return. **Challenge**: Anonymity of crypto wallets.

### 3. **COVID-19 Contract Terminations and Quantum Meruit**

**Issue**: Contracts terminated due to COVID frustration; work partially done.

**Courts' Position (2020-2023)**:
- Supplier entitled to **quantum meruit** for work done before frustration (Section 70 principle)
- Fair value, not contract price (if contract had profit margins)

### 4. **Real Estate Quasi-Contract Claims**

**Trend**: Builders/buyers seeking quantum meruit when agreements canceled.

**Judicial Approach**:
- **Builder's claim**: Reasonable value of construction completed (minus delays/defects)
- **Buyer's refund**: Full advance minus builder's quantum meruit entitlement
- Prevents both parties' unjust enrichment

### 5. **Finder's Rights in Digital Assets**

**Emerging Issue**: Person recovers lost cryptocurrency wallet/NFT for owner.

**Legal Question**: Does Section 71 apply to digital "goods"?

**Conceptual Challenge**: Are cryptocurrencies/NFTs "goods"? If yes, finder may have quasi-contractual rights (expenses, limited possessory rights).

## Practice Guidelines

### ✅ Do's

1. **Document Non-Gratuitous Intent (Section 70)**
   - Invoices, quotes, commercial correspondence
   - Proves intent to charge, not act gratuitously

2. **Prove Benefit Actually Conferred**
   - Evidence that defendant used/consumed services or goods
   - Not enough that plaintiff performed work; defendant must have benefited

3. **Establish Fair Market Value (Quantum Meruit)**
   - Expert valuations, market surveys, industry standards
   - Avoid inflated claims (courts award reasonable value, not wishful pricing)

4. **Act Promptly on Mistaken Payments (Section 72)**
   - Notice recipient immediately upon discovering mistake
   - Prevents "change of position" defense

5. **For Section 69 Claims, Prove Interest in Payment**
   - Document legal/equitable interest
   - Not mere volunteer; must show necessity of payment to protect interest

6. **Search for Owner (Section 71 Finders)**
   - Reasonable efforts to locate owner (police report, advertisements)
   - Maintains rights as finder

### ❌ Don'ts

1. **Don't Assume All Unpaid Work Recoverable**
   - Only if non-gratuitous and defendant benefited (Section 70)
   - Volunteered/unwanted services generally not recoverable

2. **Don't Claim Contract Price as Quantum Meruit**
   - Quantum meruit = fair value (may be less than contract price)

3. **Don't Delay Claiming Mistaken Payment**
   - Recipient's change of position may defeat claim
   - Limitation period runs from discovery of mistake

4. **Don't Pay Another's Debt Without Interest (Section 69)**
   - Mere volunteers cannot recover
   - Must have legal/equitable interest

5. **Don't Assume Mistake of Law Recoverable**
   - General rule: Mistake of Indian law = no recovery
   - Exception: Mistake of foreign law (treated as fact)

6. **Don't Use Found Goods for Own Benefit (Section 71)**
   - Finder = bailee; must preserve, not use for own purposes
   - Using found goods = breach of bailee duty
