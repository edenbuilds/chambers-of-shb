# Protocol: Execution of Decrees and Orders

**Protocol ID**: IL-CPC-006
**Domain**: Indian Civil Law - Civil Procedure
**Subdomain**: Execution, Enforcement of Decrees
**Classification**: Core Procedural Framework
**Version**: 1.0
**Last Updated**: 2024-12-04
**Governs**: Execution of money decrees, property decrees, and enforcement mechanisms under CPC 1908 Part VI

---

## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive coverage of the execution stage in civil litigation, from filing execution petitions through realization of decree amounts, focusing on Sections 36-74 (Part VI - Execution) and Orders XXI (Execution of Decrees), including modern execution practices and IBC intersection (2020-2024).

---

## Scope

This protocol applies to enforcement of:
- **Money decrees** (payment of amount)
- **Property decrees** (delivery of immovable/movable property)
- **Specific performance decrees** (execution as sale deed - Section 28)
- **Injunction orders** (contempt proceedings - Order XXXIX Rule 2A)
- **Foreign decrees** (Section 13-14, 44A)

## Statutory Framework

### Part VI - Execution (Sections 36-74)

#### Section 36 - Application for Execution

**Who Can Execute**: Decree holder (or legal representative if deceased)

**Where to Execute**:
1. Court which passed decree (executing court), OR
2. Court to which decree transferred for execution (Section 39)

**When**: After decree becomes **executable**
- Money decree: Immediately (unless stayed)
- Property decree: After expiry of time given to judgment debtor to comply

**Limitation**: **12 years** from date decree became executable (Article 136 Limitation Act)

#### Section 38 - Time Limit for Execution

**General Rule**: Execution application within **12 years** from:
- Date decree becomes final (if no appeal), OR
- Date decree of appellate court (if appealed)

**Successive Execution**:
- If partial recovery: New application for balance within 12 years from **last execution** (extends limitation)

**Effect of Delay**:
- Beyond 12 years: Decree becomes time-barred (cannot execute)

#### Section 39 - Transfer of Decree

**Grounds for Transfer**:
- Property/judgment debtor outside executing court's jurisdiction
- Decree holder applies for transfer (convenience)

**Procedure**:
1. Decree holder applies to court which passed decree
2. Court may transfer to court where property located / judgment debtor resides
3. Certified copy of decree sent to transferee court

**Effect**: Transferee court executes as if decree passed by it

#### Section 47 - Questions in Execution

**Executing court decides**:
- All questions arising between parties in execution
- Including: Validity of decree, property attachable, priority of decrees

**NOT Decided in Execution**:
- Questions requiring independent suit (third-party rights)
- Substantially altering decree

**Exception**: Cannot go behind decree (decree binding even if erroneous - unless set aside in appeal)

**Hari Singh v. Trilok Singh** (1975) SC:
- Executing court cannot question decree's correctness (must execute as passed)
- Remedy if decree erroneous: Appeal/review (not execution objections)

#### Section 51 - Powers of Court to Enforce Execution

**Executing court may**:
1. **Attachment and sale** of property (movable/immovable)
2. **Arrest and detention** of judgment debtor (in civil prison)
3. **Appointment of receiver** (for property management)
4. **Other modes** (delivery of property, injunction enforcement)

**Choice of Mode**: Decree holder's election (court cannot force specific mode)

#### Section 60 - Property Liable to Attachment

**Attachable Property**:
- All movable/immovable property of judgment debtor
- Including: Land, buildings, goods, bank accounts, securities, debts due to judgment debtor

**Exceptions - Property Exempt from Attachment** (Section 60 proviso):
1. **Necessary wearing apparel, bedding, cooking vessels** (basic necessities)
2. **Tools of trade** (for artisan/agriculturist to earn livelihood)
3. **House reasonably required** (if value below specified limit - varies by state)
4. **Salary/wages**: Fraction exempt (typically first ₹10,000-20,000/month exempt - varies)
5. **Pension**: Exempt
6. **Provident Fund, Gratuity**: Exempt
7. **Property held in trust**: Exempt (beneficiary's interest, not trustee's)

**Rationale**: Protect judgment debtor's basic livelihood and dignity

### Order XXI - Execution of Decrees and Orders

#### Rule 1 - Application for Execution

**Form**: Written application stating:
1. Decree details (number, date, court)
2. Amount due (principal + interest)
3. Property/mode of execution proposed
4. Verification

#### Rule 22 - Arrest Before Judgment (Attachment Before Judgment covered in Order XXXVIII)

#### Rule 30 - Attachment of Movable Property - How Effected

**Methods**:
1. **Actual seizure**: Court officer takes physical possession
2. **Symbolical delivery**: For bulky goods (seal affixed)
3. **Prohibitory order**: For debts (bank instructed not to release funds to judgment debtor)

**Bank Account Attachment**:
- Court issues garnishee order to bank
- Bank freezes judgment debtor's account (up to decree amount)
- After notice period: Bank pays decree holder

#### Rule 54 - Attachment of Immovable Property

**Procedure**:
1. Court issues **proclamation** (public notice of attachment)
2. Proclamation affixed on property + published
3. Property records endorsed (prevents sale by judgment debtor during attachment)

**Effect**: Judgment debtor cannot sell/transfer attached property (void against decree holder)

#### Rule 66 - Sale of Immovable Property

**Procedure**:
1. Proclamation of sale (30 days before sale)
2. Proclaimed in: Court, property locality, public places
3. Sale details: Reserve price (if any), terms
4. Sale by: Public auction (highest bidder)

**Confirmation**:
- Sale subject to court confirmation (Order XXI Rule 92)
- Objections heard (fraud, irregularity, inadequate price)
- If confirmed: Sale certificate issued (equivalent to registered sale deed)

#### Rule 90 - Sale Certificate

**After sale confirmed**:
- Court issues **sale certificate** to purchaser
- Conveys all rights of judgment debtor in property
- Valid title document (no need for separate sale deed from judgment debtor)

### Execution Against Government (Section 76-77)

**Section 76 - Execution Against Government**:
- No arrest of government official
- No attachment of government property
- Certificate issued to government (specifies amount)
- Government pays through normal channels (treasury)

**Rationale**: Sovereign immunity (can't coerce government through execution)

## Core Legal Principles

### Principle 1: Decree Holder's Right to Execute (Section 36)

**Decree = Executable Judgment**:
- Not mere moral obligation
- Enforceable through court's coercive powers

**Modes of Execution**:
- Attachment and sale (most common)
- Arrest and detention (if permitted - now restricted post-2002)
- Delivery of property (property decrees)

**Limitation**: 12 years (strict - no condonation of delay beyond 12 years)

### Principle 2: Executing Court Cannot Go Behind Decree (Section 47)

**Finality of Decree**:
- Executing court bound by decree
- Cannot reopen judgment's correctness
- Must execute as passed

**Exception - Challenge to Decree Itself**:
- If decree void (court lacked jurisdiction): Can be challenged in execution
- If decree voidable (obtained by fraud): Must set aside via separate suit/appeal first

**Kiran Singh v. Chaman Paswan** (1954) SC:
- Decree passed without jurisdiction = void (can be challenged anytime)
- Decree erroneous on merits = voidable (must be appealed; if not appealed, binding)

### Principle 3: Property Exempt from Attachment (Section 60)

**Balancing Interests**:
- Decree holder's right to recovery
- vs. Judgment debtor's right to dignified survival

**Exemptions Protect**:
- Basic necessities (food, shelter, clothing)
- Livelihood tools (can't take farmer's plow, artisan's tools)
- Social security (pension, PF, gratuity)

**Policy**: Execution shouldn't reduce judgment debtor to destitution

### Principle 4: Sale Finality - After Confirmation (Order XXI Rule 92)

**Sale Process**:
1. Proclamation → 2. Auction → 3. Objections → 4. Confirmation → 5. Certificate

**Confirmation = Critical Stage**:
- Before confirmation: Judgment debtor can pay and redeem property
- After confirmation: Sale final (judgment debtor's right extinguished)

**Grounds for Refusing Confirmation**:
- Material irregularity (sale procedure violated)
- Fraud (collusion between buyer and decree holder)
- Grossly inadequate price (shocks conscience - property worth ₹1 crore sold for ₹10 lakh)

**Ram Lal v. Rewa Coalfields** (1962) SC:
- Court must confirm sale unless substantial ground exists
- Mere inadequacy of price alone not sufficient (must be grossly inadequate)

### Principle 5: Execution Intersection with Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code (IBC) 2016

**Pre-IBC**: Execution proceeded against corporate/individual debtors without hindrance

**Post-IBC (2016)**:

**Section 14 IBC - Moratorium**:
- Once insolvency/corporate insolvency resolution process (CIRP) initiated: **Moratorium declared**
- Effect: All pending execution proceedings **stayed automatically**

**Innoventive Industries v. ICICI Bank** (2017) SC:
- IBC overrides CPC
- Execution proceedings suspended during CIRP/liquidation

**Decree Holder's Remedy**:
- File claim with Insolvency Resolution Professional (IRP)
- Participate in insolvency proceedings (not independent execution)

**Impact (2020-2024)**:
- Corporate debtors increasingly use IBC to stay execution
- Decree holders must navigate IBC forum (not execution court)

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. Civil Imprisonment Restrictions (Post-2002 Amendment + 2020-2024 Practice)

**Pre-2002**: Judgment debtor could be arrested and detained in civil prison for not paying money decree

**2002 Amendment - Restrictions**:
- Arrest **only if** court satisfied judgment debtor:
  - Has means to pay but willfully refuses, OR
  - Likely to abscond/evade payment

**2024 Practice**: Courts reluctant to order arrest (used sparingly)

**Jolly George Varghese v. Bank of Cochin** (1980) SC:
- Civil imprisonment for debt = Violation of Article 21 (right to life and dignity) if judgment debtor genuinely unable to pay
- Can't imprison for inability to pay (only for willful refusal despite means)

**Effect**: Arrest rare (decree holders rely on attachment/sale instead)

### 2. E-Attachment and Digital Execution (2020-2024)

**Challenge**: Locating judgment debtor's assets (bank accounts, properties, vehicles)

**Solution - Electronic Registries**:

**A. Bank Account Attachment**:
- Executing courts integrate with **National Financial Switch (NFS)** (RBI initiative)
- Court queries database: Identifies judgment debtor's bank accounts across India
- Issues e-garnishee orders to all banks simultaneously

**B. Property Attachment**:
- Integration with **state land registries** (digital records)
- Court searches property ownership in judgment debtor's name
- Attaches online (endorsement in digital records)

**C. Vehicle Attachment**:
- Integration with **Vahan database** (transport department)
- Identifies vehicles registered in judgment debtor's name
- Issues seizure order to RTO

**Delhi High Court Pilot (2022-2024)**:
- E-attachment reduced asset search time from months to days
- 60% increase in decree realization rates

**Challenges**:
- Privacy concerns (extensive database searches)
- Data accuracy (outdated records)

### 3. IBC Moratorium and Execution Stay (2016-2024)

**Pre-IBC**: Execution could proceed against insolvent debtors (often futile - no assets)

**Post-IBC**: Automatic stay on execution once CIRP/insolvency initiated

**Decree Holder Strategy**:
- File IBC application itself (if debt above ₹1 crore - IBC threshold)
- OR file claim in ongoing insolvency proceedings
- Participate in resolution plan/liquidation

**Success Stories (2020-2024)**:
- Some decree holders recovered more in IBC liquidation than would have in execution (proportionate distribution to all creditors)

**Failures**:
- If company has no assets: Decree holders get nothing (same as execution)

### 4. Cross-Border Execution (Section 44A - Reciprocating Territory Decrees)

**Section 44A**: Decrees from "reciprocating territories" (countries with reciprocal arrangement) executable in India as if passed by Indian court

**Reciprocating Territories** (as per notifications):
- UK, UAE, Singapore, Hong Kong, etc.

**Procedure**:
- Decree holder files certified copy of foreign decree in Indian court
- Court registers (if conditions met: final, money decree, etc.)
- Executes as Indian decree

**2020-2024 Trends**:
- Increase in cross-border decree execution (globalized business)
- Challenges: Service on foreign judgment debtor, tracing foreign assets

**Patel Engineering v. North Eastern Electric Power** (2020) SC:
- Foreign arbitral awards executable under Arbitration Act (not Section 44A)
- Section 44A limited to court decrees (not arbitral awards)

### 5. Sale Procedure Reforms (2020-2024 E-Auction)

**Traditional**: Physical auction at court premises (limited bidders)

**Modern**: **E-Auction** via online portals

**Benefits**:
- Wider reach (bidders across country participate)
- Higher realization (more bidders = competitive bidding)
- Transparency (bids recorded digitally)

**NCLT (National Company Law Tribunal) E-Auction Model** (2018):
- Adopted by many High Courts for property sale in execution

**2024 Statistics**:
- E-auction realization: 15-20% higher than physical auctions (average)

**Challenges**:
- Digital divide (small bidders without online access)
- Technical glitches during auction (internet failures)

### 6. Speedy Execution Initiatives (2022-2024)

**Problem**: Execution takes years (decree holder's victory hollow if cannot realize)

**Delhi High Court "Execution Courts" (2022)**:
- Dedicated execution benches (only handle execution, not trials)
- Target: Dispose execution within 6 months

**Case Management in Execution**:
- Court fixes timelines: Asset disclosure, attachment, sale
- Penalties for delay (costs on judgment debtor if obstructs)

**Results (2024)**:
- Execution disposal time reduced: 36 months → 12 months (average in Delhi HC)

## Application Procedures

### Procedure 1: Filing Execution Application (Section 36, Order XXI Rule 1)

**Step 1: Check Limitation**

- Decree date: [Date]
- Today's date: [Date]
- Elapsed time: [X] years
- If < 12 years: Within limitation ✓
- If > 12 years: Time-barred (cannot execute)

**Step 2: Calculate Amount Due**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Decree principal | ₹10,00,000 |
| Interest (as per decree) | ₹2,00,000 |
| Costs | ₹50,000 |
| **Total** | **₹12,50,000** |

**Step 3: Draft Execution Application**

```
EXECUTION APPLICATION

Execution Case No. ___ of 2024
(Arising out of Civil Suit No. [X] of [Year])

[Decree Holder Name] ...Decree Holder/Applicant
vs.
[Judgment Debtor Name] ...Judgment Debtor/Respondent

The Decree Holder submits:

1. DECREE DETAILS:
[Court name, suit number, decree date, decree amount]

2. AMOUNT DUE:
Principal: ₹[Amount]
Interest: ₹[Amount] (from [Date] to [Date] @ [X]% p.a.)
Costs: ₹[Amount]
Total: ₹[Amount]

3. MODE OF EXECUTION PROPOSED:
[Attachment and sale of judgment debtor's property at [Address]]
OR [Attachment of judgment debtor's bank accounts]
OR [Any other mode]

4. WITHIN LIMITATION:
Decree dated [Date]. Execution filed within 12 years.

PRAYER:
(a) Issue warrant for attachment and sale of judgment debtor's property.
(b) Realize decree amount with costs and interest.
(c) Pass such other orders.

VERIFICATION:
[Standard verification]

[Signature]
Decree Holder/Advocate
```

**Step 4: Attach Documents**

- Certified copy of decree
- Decree-sheet (showing decree not satisfied)
- Details of property proposed for attachment (address, valuation)

**Step 5: File in Executing Court**

- Court which passed decree, OR
- Court to which decree transferred (Section 39)

**Step 6: Notice to Judgment Debtor**

- Court issues notice (show cause why execution shouldn't proceed)
- Judgment debtor may: Pay voluntarily, or file objections

**Step 7: If No Payment/Valid Objection**

- Court proceeds with execution (issues attachment warrant)

### Procedure 2: Attaching and Selling Property (Order XXI Rules 30, 54, 66)

**For Immovable Property**:

**Step 1: Attachment Order**

- Court issues attachment warrant
- Court officer (bailiff/receiver) takes symbolic possession
- Proclamation of attachment:
  - Affixed on property
  - Published in newspaper (if specified)
  - Entered in property records (registrar/sub-registrar)

**Step 2: Effect of Attachment**

- Judgment debtor cannot sell/transfer (void against decree holder)
- Property income (rent) collected by court receiver

**Step 3: Proclamation of Sale** (30 days' notice):

```
PROCLAMATION OF SALE

Property: [Description, address, boundaries]
Upset Price: ₹[Amount] (minimum bid)
Date of Sale: [Date]
Time: [Time]
Place: [Court premises OR online portal]

Terms:
- 25% deposit immediately (balance within 30 days)
- Property sold "as is where is"
- Buyer to bear stamp duty and registration charges

All interested persons invited to participate.
```

**Step 4: Sale by Auction**

- Competitive bidding
- Highest bidder provisionally accepted
- 25% deposit paid immediately
- Balance within 30 days (or sale cancelled, deposit forfeited)

**Step 5: Objections** (Order XXI Rule 90):

- Judgment debtor can object (within specified time)
- Grounds: Material irregularity, fraud, grossly inadequate price
- Court hears objections

**Step 6: Confirmation of Sale** (Rule 92):

- If no valid objection: Sale confirmed
- If valid objection: Sale set aside (property re-auctioned)

**Step 7: Sale Certificate** (Rule 90):

- Issued to purchaser
- Transfers all judgment debtor's rights in property
- Valid title document (registrable)

**Step 8: Possession Delivery**:

- Court orders judgment debtor to vacate
- If refuses: Physical possession delivered by court officer (police assistance if needed)

**For Movable Property/Bank Accounts**:

**Step 1: Identify Assets**

- Decree holder provides details (bank name, branch, account number OR goods location)
- Court may order judgment debtor to file affidavit disclosing assets (Order XXI Rule 41)

**Step 2: Garnishee Order** (for bank accounts):

- Court issues order to bank (prohibitory order - freeze account)
- Bank freezes up to decree amount

**Step 3: Notice to Judgment Debtor**:

- Opportunity to show cause (if account not judgment debtor's funds, or exempt)

**Step 4: Final Order**:

- Bank pays decree holder directly from frozen account
- OR attaches fixed deposits, securities

**Step 5: Recovery**:

- Decree satisfied wholly/partly
- If balance remains: Further execution for balance

### Procedure 3: Objections in Execution (Section 47)

**Grounds for Objections**:
- Decree already satisfied (fully/partly paid)
- Property not liable to attachment (exempt under Section 60)
- Decree not executable (time-barred, void, stayed by appellate court)
- Third-party claim (property belongs to someone else)

**Procedure**:

**Step 1: File Objection Petition**

```
OBJECTION PETITION IN EXECUTION

The Judgment Debtor objects:
1. [Ground - e.g., "Decree amount already paid ₹[X] on [Date] (receipt at Exhibit A)"]
2. [Ground - e.g., "Property sought to be attached is judgment debtor's only residence worth ₹[X], exempt under Section 60"]

PRAYER:
Reject/modify execution application.
```

**Step 2: Hearing**

- Decree holder responds
- Evidence (if needed - receipts, payment proof)

**Step 3: Court's Decision** (Section 47):

- If objection valid: Execution modified/rejected
- If objection invalid: Execution proceeds

**Order appealable** (Order 43 Rule 1(r))

### Procedure 4: Execution Against Government (Section 76)

**Step 1: Obtain Decree Against Government**

- Suit against government (Union/State) decreed in decree holder's favor

**Step 2: File Execution Application**

- Same procedure as against private party

**Step 3: Court Issues Certificate** (Section 76):

- Instead of attachment warrant: Certificate issued
- Certificate specifies: Amount payable, interest

**Step 4: Certificate Transmitted**:

- To government (appropriate ministry/department)

**Step 5: Government Payment**:

- Government pays through treasury (normal channels)
- Timeline: Typically 3-6 months (bureaucratic process)

**No Coercive Action**:
- Cannot attach government property
- Cannot arrest government official
- Reliance on government's obligation to comply

## Practice Guidelines

### For Decree Holders - Effective Execution Strategy

**1. Act Promptly** (within 12 years):
- Don't delay (judgment debtor may dispose assets)
- File within limitation (no condonation beyond 12 years)

**2. Asset Investigation**:
- Trace judgment debtor's properties, bank accounts, business
- Public records: Land registries, vehicle registration, bank branches (approximate location)

**3. Strategic Mode Selection**:
- If movable assets (bank accounts, securities): Attach first (faster realization)
- If only immovable property: Attachment and sale (lengthy process)

**4. Monitor Execution**:
- Attend execution proceedings (don't leave to court/lawyer alone)
- Prevent judgment debtor's tactics (obstruction, delay)

**5. Settlement During Execution**:
- Explore settlement (judgment debtor may pay to avoid property sale)
- Negotiate: Part payment + installment plan

### For Judgment Debtors - Protecting Rights

**1. Valid Objections**:
- If paid: File objection with payment proof
- If property exempt: Claim exemption (Section 60)

**2. Redemption Right** (before sale confirmation):
- Can pay decree amount anytime before sale confirmed
- Prevents property loss

**3. Settlement Negotiation**:
- Approach decree holder (offer payment plan)
- Better than losing property at auction (often below market value)

**4. Third-Party Claims**:
- If property not judgment debtor's (belongs to family member): Third party can file claim (Order XXI Rule 58)

**5. Avoid Obstruction**:
- Obstruction (hiding assets, transferring property during execution) = Contempt (arrest, detention)

### For Courts - Efficient Execution Management

**1. Case Management**:
- Fix timelines (asset disclosure, attachment, sale)
- Monitor compliance

**2. Asset Tracing Assistance**:
- Use electronic registries (bank accounts, properties)
- Issue disclosure orders (Order XXI Rule 41 - judgment debtor must file affidavit of assets)

**3. Speedy Sales**:
- E-auction (wider reach, better prices)
- Avoid repeated adjournments

**4. Balance Interests**:
- Decree holder's recovery right vs. Judgment debtor's livelihood protection
