# Protocol: Sale of Goods Act - Performance, Delivery, and Remedies

**Protocol ID**: IL-CONTRACT-016
**Domain**: Indian Civil Law - Contract Law
**Subdomain**: Sale of Goods - Performance and Remedies
**Classification**: Comprehensive Legal Framework
**Version**: 1.0
**Last Updated**: 2024-12-04
**Governs**: Performance obligations, delivery, acceptance, unpaid seller's rights, and remedies for breach under Sale of Goods Act, 1930

---

## Purpose

This protocol provides comprehensive coverage of Sections 27-61 of the Sale of Goods Act, 1930, focusing on performance obligations, delivery and acceptance rules, rights of unpaid sellers against goods, and remedies available to both parties for breach of contract. It serves as a complete reference for enforcing sale contracts and resolving disputes.

---

## Scope of Application

### When This Protocol Applies

This protocol applies to ALL sale of goods transactions after contract formation, covering:
- **Performance obligations** of seller and buyer
- **Delivery mechanics** and acceptance procedures
- **Transit and carriage** issues
- **Unpaid seller's remedial rights** (lien, stoppage, resale)
- **Buyer's remedies** for non-delivery or breach of warranty
- **Seller's remedies** for non-acceptance or non-payment
- **Damages calculation** for breach

### Jurisdictional Boundaries

- **Primary Statute**: Sale of Goods Act, 1930 (Sections 27-61)
- **Supplementary**: Indian Contract Act, 1872 (Sections 73-75 on damages)
- **Procedural**: Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 (for suit enforcement)
- **Consumer Overlay**: Consumer Protection Act, 2019 (enhanced B2C remedies)

### Exclusions and Limitations

Does NOT cover:
- **Formation and property transfer** (covered in IL-CONTRACT-015)
- **Specific performance** of sale contracts (Specific Relief Act, 1963)
- **International sales** (governed by Incoterms + CISG if applicable)
- **Auction sales** (special rules in Section 64)
- **Hire-purchase agreements** (distinct from sale)

## Statutory Framework

### Risk and Goods in Transit (Section 27-29)

**Section 27 - Goods at Risk of Owner**:

**General Rule**: Goods remain at seller's risk until property passes; thereafter at buyer's risk

**Rationale**: "Res perit domino" - thing perishes at owner's risk

**EXCEPTIONS**:

1. **Delivery Delayed by Fault**:
   - Goods at risk of party at fault (for loss attributable to that fault)
   - Example: Buyer refuses to take timely delivery; goods stolen → Buyer bears risk

2. **Agreement to Contrary**:
   - Parties can allocate risk differently (e.g., CIF contracts - risk passes on shipment, property on payment)

**Section 28 - Transfer of Risk in Transit**:

Where goods delivered to carrier for transmission:
- Prima facie risk passes to buyer (Section 27 applies)
- Seller must make **reasonable contract** with carrier (Section 33)
- If seller fails reasonable contract duty: Seller liable for deterioration in transit

**Section 29 - Part Delivery**:

Where part delivery made:
- Risk for delivered portion: Buyer
- Risk for undelivered portion: Seller
- Unless otherwise agreed

### Performance of Contract (Sections 30-37)

**Section 30 - Seller's Duty to Deliver Exact Quantity**:

**Three Rules**:

**1. Delivery of Less Quantity** (Section 30(1)):
- Buyer MAY reject entire lot, OR
- Accept delivered quantity + pay pro-rata

**2. Delivery of Excess Quantity** (Section 30(2)):
- Buyer MAY:
  - Accept contract quantity + reject excess, OR
  - Reject entire lot, OR
  - Accept entire lot + pay for excess at contract rate

**3. Mixed Goods** (Section 30(3)):
- Contract goods mixed with goods of different description
- Buyer MAY: Accept contract goods + reject rest, OR reject entire lot

**Exceptions** (Section 30(4)):
- Custom/usage of trade
- **De minimis** principle: Insignificant deviation disregarded

**Modern Application**:
- Courts apply "commercial practicability" (e.g., 1000 kg delivered as 999.5 kg = acceptable)
- B2C e-commerce: Strict compliance expected (consumer can reject if quantity short)

**Section 31 - Delivery by Installments**:

**General Rule**: Buyer NOT bound to accept delivery by installments (unless agreed)

**If Installment Contract**:
- Each installment = separate contract (for some purposes)
- Breach in one installment does NOT auto-repudiate entire contract
- Test: Does breach go to root of contract?

**Repudiation Test**:
1. How much of contract unperformed?
2. How likely breach will recur?

**Munroe v. Meyer (1930)** principle:
- Contract for 1,500 tons wheat in installments
- First installment defective
- Held: Buyer entitled to repudiate entire contract if defect indicates inability to perform

**Section 32 - Delivery to Carrier**:

**Section 32(1) - Carrier as Buyer's Agent**:
- Where seller authorized/required to send goods to buyer
- Delivery to carrier = prima facie delivery to buyer
- Risk passes (unless otherwise agreed)

**Section 32(2) - Notice to Buyer**:
- Seller must give buyer sufficient notice to enable insurance
- If seller fails: Goods at seller's risk during transit

**Section 32(3) - Reasonable Contract with Carrier**:
- Seller must make contract with carrier on behalf of buyer
- Contract must be **reasonable** having regard to nature of goods and other circumstances
- If unreasonable contract: Seller liable for deterioration

**Section 33 - Delivery at Distant Place**:

Where seller agrees to deliver at place other than sale place:
- Risk passes when goods delivered to carrier
- Unless otherwise agreed or circumstances indicate contrary

**Section 34 - Buyer's Duty to Accept Delivery**:

Buyer's obligations:
1. **Apply for delivery**: Request delivery when goods ready
2. **Furnish facilities**: Provide unloading assistance if required
3. **Accept delivery**: Within reasonable time

**Failure Consequences**:
- Seller can sue for damages (Section 56)
- Or exercise unpaid seller's rights (if price unpaid)

**Section 35 - Buyer's Right to Examine Goods**:

**Examination Before Acceptance**:
- Where goods not examined before contract: Buyer has right to examine
- Seller must afford reasonable opportunity on delivery
- Purpose: Verify goods conform to contract

**Timing**: At time/place of delivery

**No Deemed Acceptance Until Examination** (Section 42 read with 35)

**Section 36 - Buyer Not Bound to Return Rejected Goods**:

Where buyer rightfully rejects goods:
- Buyer NOT bound to return to seller
- Sufficient to notify rejection
- Goods remain at seller's risk

**Rationale**: Buyer is not seller's agent/bailee after rejection

**But**: Buyer must not use goods post-rejection (using = acceptance)

**Section 37 - Liability of Buyer Refusing Delivery**:

If buyer wrongfully refuses/neglects to accept and pay:
- Seller can sue for:
  1. **Price** (if property passed) - Section 55
  2. **Damages** (if agreement to sell) - Section 56

### Acceptance (Section 42)

**Section 42 - When Buyer Deemed to Have Accepted Goods**:

Buyer deemed to have accepted when:

**1. Express Intimation** (Section 42(1)):
- Buyer intimates to seller that he has accepted goods

**2. Acts Inconsistent with Seller's Ownership** (Section 42(2)):
- Buyer does any act inconsistent with seller's ownership
- Examples: Resale, pledge, consumption, substantial alteration

**3. Retention Beyond Reasonable Time** (Section 42(3)):
- Buyer retains goods beyond reasonable time without intimating rejection
- "Reasonable time" = question of fact (nature of goods, custom, etc.)

**Consequences of Acceptance**:
- Buyer CANNOT reject goods (loses right to repudiate)
- Breach of condition reduced to breach of warranty
- Buyer limited to damages only (Section 11(4))

**EXCEPTION - Latent Defects**:
- If defect not discoverable on reasonable examination
- Acceptance doesn't bar claim for latent defect
- Buyer can claim damages for breach of warranty

**Karsandas v. Sheth Budharmal (1960)**:
- Buyer accepted bales of cotton
- Later discovered internal defects not visible externally
- Held: Acceptance doesn't preclude claim for hidden defects

### Rights of Unpaid Seller Against Goods (Sections 43-54)

#### Definition of Unpaid Seller (Section 45)

**"Unpaid Seller"** includes seller when:
1. **Whole price not paid/tendered**, OR
2. **Bill of exchange/negotiable instrument conditional** (e.g., cheque dishonoured, bill not accepted)

**Section 45(2) - "Seller" Includes**:
- Agent of seller to whom bill of lading pledged
- Consignor/agent who paid price (or liable for it)

#### Seller's Rights (Section 46)

Unpaid seller has **TWO categories** of rights:

**A. Against Goods** (Even if property passed):
1. **Lien** on goods for price (Sections 47-49)
2. **Stoppage in transit** if buyer insolvent (Sections 50-52)
3. **Right of resale** in certain cases (Section 54)

**B. Against Buyer Personally**:
4. **Suit for price** (Section 55)
5. **Suit for damages** (Section 56)

Rights **cumulative**, not alternative (seller can pursue multiple remedies)

#### Lien (Sections 47-49)

**Section 47 - Unpaid Seller's Lien**:

Seller can retain possession of goods until payment when:

**1. Sale Without Credit** (Section 47(1)(a)):
- No credit period agreed
- Payment expected on delivery

**2. Sale on Credit, Term Expired** (Section 47(1)(b)):
- Credit period agreed but expired
- Payment now due

**3. Buyer Insolvent** (Section 47(1)(c)):
- Even if credit period not expired
- Insolvency triggers immediate lien

**Part Delivery** (Section 47(2)):
- Lien on remaining goods in seller's possession
- Unless part delivery shows intent to waive lien on rest

**Section 48 - Termination of Lien**:

Lien terminated when:

**1. Delivery to Carrier** (Section 48(a)):
- Seller delivers to carrier for transmission to buyer
- Without reserving right of disposal
- [Seller may have stoppage in transit right instead]

**2. Buyer Obtains Possession** (Section 48(b)):
- Lawfully or wrongfully

**3. Waiver** (Section 48(c)):
- Express or implied waiver by seller

**Section 49 - Part Delivery**:
- Lien exercisable on remainder unless circumstances show waiver

#### Stoppage in Transit (Sections 50-52)

**Section 50 - Right of Stoppage**:

**When Exercisable**:
- Buyer becomes **INSOLVENT**
- Goods **in transit** (not yet reached buyer)
- Seller can stop goods, resume possession

**Purpose**: Prevent goods from falling into hands of insolvent buyer (creditors would benefit, not seller)

**Section 2(2) - "Insolvent"**:
- Ceased to pay debts in ordinary course of business, OR
- Cannot pay debts as they become due

**Does NOT require formal insolvency proceedings** (unlike English law post-2015)

**Section 51 - Duration of Transit**:

**Transit Begins**: When goods delivered to carrier for transmission to buyer

**Transit Ends** when:

**1. Buyer Takes Delivery** (Section 51(a)):
- From carrier or other bailee

**2. Buyer Obtains Delivery Before Destination** (Section 51(b)):
- Buyer takes possession during transit

**3. Carrier Attorns to Buyer** (Section 51(c)):
- Carrier acknowledges holds goods on behalf of buyer
- Even if further carriage contemplated

**4. Wrongful Refusal by Carrier** (Section 51(d)):
- Carrier wrongfully refuses to deliver to buyer

**5. Part Delivery** (Section 51(e)):
- Remainder still in transit (unless circumstances show intent to deliver entire)

**Section 52 - How Stoppage Effected**:

**Two Methods**:

**1. Actual Possession** (Section 52(1)):
- Seller takes actual possession from carrier

**2. Notice to Carrier** (Section 52(2)):
- Notice given to:
  - Person in actual possession, OR
  - Principal (carrier owner)
- Notice must enable reasonable opportunity to communicate with buyer
- Carrier must redeliver to seller (seller bears cost)

**Carrier's Protection**:
- If proper notice given, carrier must obey
- Carrier NOT liable to buyer if obeys lawful stoppage

#### Resale by Unpaid Seller (Section 54)

**Section 54 - Right of Resale**:

Seller can resell goods in **THREE situations**:

**1. Perishable Goods** (Section 54(2)):
- Goods of perishable nature
- Buyer unreasonably delays acceptance
- Seller can resell to mitigate loss

**2. Notice of Intention Given** (Section 54(3)):
- Seller gives notice to buyer of intention to resell
- Buyer fails to pay/tender price within reasonable time
- Seller can resell

**3. Express Reservation** (Section 54(4)):
- Contract expressly reserves right of resale on buyer's default

**Consequences of Resale**:
- Original contract **rescinded** (not merely breach)
- Seller can recover damages from original buyer for any loss
- New buyer gets good title (even if original contract not rescinded by notice)

**Profit on Resale**:
- If seller makes profit: Generally seller keeps it
- Unless original contract provided otherwise

**R.V. Ward v. Bignall (1967)** principle adopted:
- Resale after reasonable notice = contract rescinded
- Seller can sue for loss (if any) but needn't account for profit

### Remedies Against Buyer (Sections 55-56)

**Section 55 - Suit for Price**:

Seller can sue for price in **TWO situations**:

**1. Property Passed** (Section 55(1)):
- Property in goods has passed to buyer
- Buyer wrongfully neglects/refuses to pay
- Seller can sue for **price** (not damages)

**Rationale**: Debt action (not damages for breach)

**2. Day Fixed for Payment** (Section 55(2)):
- Price payable on specific day
- Irrespective of delivery
- Property may/may not have passed
- Seller can sue for price if day passed and not paid

**Example**: Buyer agrees to buy car, pay ₹10 lakh on 1st May irrespective of delivery date. If buyer doesn't pay on 1st May, seller can sue for price even if delivery not yet made.

**Distinction from Section 56**:
- Section 55: Action for **debt** (price)
- Section 56: Action for **damages** (breach)

**Section 56 - Damages for Non-Acceptance**:

**When Applicable**:
- Buyer wrongfully neglects/refuses to accept and pay
- Property has NOT passed (agreement to sell)

**Measure of Damages**:
- **Prima facie**: Difference between contract price and market price at time of refusal
- **If no market**: Damages assessed by other means (cost incurred, profit lost)

**Section 56(3) - Available Market Test**:
- "Available market": Real and not merely nominal market
- Must be buyers willing to purchase at market price

**No Available Market**:
- Lost profit damages (subject to remoteness rules)
- Wasted expenses (if profit not provable)

**Charter v. Sullivan (1957)** principle:
- Contract for sale of unique Rolls Royce
- No available market
- Damages = seller's loss of profit on that specific sale

**Mitigation Duty**:
- Seller must take reasonable steps to mitigate loss
- Cannot claim for avoidable loss

### Remedies Against Seller (Sections 57-61)

**Section 57 - Damages for Non-Delivery**:

**When Applicable**:
- Seller wrongfully neglects/refuses to deliver goods to buyer

**Measure of Damages**:
- **Prima facie**: Difference between contract price and market price at time goods ought to have been delivered
- **If no market**: Damages assessed by other means

**Section 57(3) - Available Market**: Same test as Section 56

**Special Circumstances** (Section 57(4)):
- If special circumstances known to seller
- Loss flowing from breach of those circumstances = recoverable
- [Hadley v. Baxendale rule from Indian Contract Act Section 73]

**Sub-Sale Loss**:
- If buyer had sub-sold goods at profit and seller knew
- Buyer can recover lost profit on sub-sale
- Leading case: **Re Hall & Pim (1928)**

**Section 58 - Specific Performance**:

**General Rule**: Specific performance NOT granted for sale of goods (damages adequate)

**EXCEPTION** (Section 58):
- Court MAY order specific performance:
  - If goods are **specific or ascertained**, AND
  - Goods have **unique value** (cannot be easily obtained elsewhere)

**Examples of Unique Goods**:
- Antiques, rare art, heirlooms
- Ships (if specific ship required)
- Unique machinery

**Governed by Specific Relief Act, 1963**:
- Section 10: Court may order specific performance if damages inadequate
- Section 14(1)(c): Specific performance granted for movables if unique

**Section 59 - Breach of Warranty**:

**Buyer's Options**:

**1. Set-Up Breach as Defence** (Section 59(1)(a)):
- Seller sues for price
- Buyer pleads breach of warranty in diminution/extinction of price

**2. Sue for Damages** (Section 59(1)(b)):
- Independent suit for breach of warranty

**Measure of Damages** (Section 59(2)):
- **Prima facie**: Difference in value between goods as warranted and goods as delivered
- Loss directly and naturally flowing from breach

**Breach of Warranty of Quality** (Section 59(3)):
- Prima facie = diminution in value
- BUT can include consequential losses (e.g., injury from defective goods)

**Bence Graphics v. Fasson UK (1998)** principle adopted:
- Defective labels supplied
- Buyer resold at full price (no loss on resale)
- Held: Damages = nominal (no actual loss despite breach)

**Section 60 - Extinction of Right to Sue**:

Once buyer accepts goods and treats breach of condition as breach of warranty:
- Cannot later reject goods
- Limited to warranty damages (Section 11(4) + Section 59)

**Section 61 - Repudiation**:

Nothing affects buyer's right to treat breach of condition as repudiation BEFORE acceptance (Section 11(3))

## Core Legal Principles

### Principle 1: Perfect Tender Rule vs. Substantial Performance

**Indian Law Position**:
- Sale of Goods Act follows **"Perfect Tender Rule"** (unlike service contracts)
- Seller must deliver goods EXACTLY as per contract (quantity, quality, time)
- Minor deviations → Buyer can reject (Section 30)

**Contrast with Services**:
- Service contracts: Substantial performance doctrine applies
- Substantial compliance + reasonable quality = contract performed

**Rationale for Strictness in Goods**:
- Commercial certainty (buyer relies on precise specifications)
- Easier to verify compliance for tangible goods

**Mitigation - De Minimis**:
- Trifling deviations disregarded ("the law does not concern itself with trifles")
- Example: 1000 units contract, 999 delivered → Likely de minimis

### Principle 2: Doctrine of Election (Section 11)

**Once Buyer Accepts Goods**:
- Breach of condition **treated as breach of warranty** only
- Buyer loses right to reject
- Can only claim damages

**Policy**:
- Finality in commercial transactions
- Seller should know status after reasonable time
- Prevents buyer from using goods and then rejecting

**Exception for Latent Defects**:
- Defects not discoverable on reasonable examination
- Acceptance doesn't bar claim for such defects

### Principle 3: Unpaid Seller's Rights as Security Interests

**Real Rights vs. Personal Rights**:

**Real Rights** (Against Goods):
- Lien: Possessory security interest
- Stoppage: Transitional security interest
- Resale: Realization of security

**Personal Rights** (Against Buyer):
- Sue for price/damages

**Purpose**:
- Protect seller who extended credit or delivered before payment
- Seller preferred over other creditors (to extent of lien/stoppage)

**Limitations**:
- Lien: Lost once possession lost
- Stoppage: Only if buyer insolvent
- Both: Defeated if buyer resold to bona fide purchaser (Section 30 Transfer of Title)

### Principle 4: Measure of Damages - Market Price Rule

**Section 56 and 57 - Available Market Test**:

If available market exists:
- Damages = Contract price - Market price (on date of breach)

Rationale: **Mitigation**
- Buyer can buy substitute in market (non-delivery)
- Seller can resell in market (non-acceptance)
- Neither should profit/lose beyond market replacement cost

**If No Available Market**:
- Actual loss calculation
- Lost profit (if provable)
- Wasted expenses

**Burden**: Party claiming no available market must prove

### Principle 5: Acceptance Finality

**Section 42 - Once Accepted, Cannot Reject**:

Acceptance acts as:
- **Waiver** of right to reject
- **Election** to treat contract as subsisting
- **Reduction** of condition to warranty

**Exceptions**:
- Latent defects (not discoverable on reasonable examination)
- Fraud/concealment by seller
- Fundamental breach (renders goods totally useless)

## Judicial Interpretation

### Landmark Cases on Performance and Remedies

**1. Munroe v. Meyer [1930] 2 KB 312 - Installment Breach**

**Facts**:
- Contract for 1,500 tons wheat in 125-ton installments
- First installment contained wrong quality

**Issue**: Can buyer repudiate entire contract for breach in one installment?

**Held**:
- YES, if breach goes to root of contract
- Test: (1) Ratio of breach to whole, (2) Likelihood of repetition
- Here: Serious breach indicating inability to perform → Repudiation justified

**Principle**: Installment breach doesn't automatically entitle repudiation; depends on severity and probability of recurrence

**2. Karsandas H. Thacker v. Sheth Budharmal Jiramal (1960) - Acceptance and Latent Defects**

**Facts**:
- Sale of cotton bales
- Buyer accepted bales after external inspection
- Later discovered internal defects (water damage not visible)

**Issue**: Does acceptance bar claim for latent defects?

**Held**:
- NO, acceptance doesn't preclude claim for defects not discoverable on reasonable examination
- Buyer entitled to damages for breach of implied condition (merchantable quality)

**Principle**: Section 42 acceptance doesn't bar claims for hidden defects unknown and unknowable at time of acceptance

**3. Union of India v. Madan Gopal Kabra (1954) - Specific Performance of Goods**

**Facts**:
- Contract for sale of specific machinery
- Seller refused to deliver

**Issue**: Can specific performance be granted for sale of goods?

**Held**:
- Generally NO (damages adequate for goods available in market)
- YES if goods specific or ascertained with unique value
- Specific Relief Act Section 10 applies

**Principle**: Specific performance for goods is exceptional; granted only if goods irreplaceable and damages inadequate

**4. New India Sugar Mills v. Commissioner of Income Tax (1963) - Damages Mitigation**

**Facts**:
- Buyer refused to accept sugar
- Seller resold at lower price

**Issue**: What damages can seller claim?

**Held**:
- Seller must mitigate by reselling in available market
- Damages = Contract price - Resale price (if resale reasonable)
- Cannot claim full contract price if mitigation possible

**Principle**: Duty to mitigate losses applies to sale of goods; seller cannot sit idle and claim full contract price

**5. Bennett Coleman v. Rajagopal (2018) - Buyer's Right to Examine**

**Facts**:
- Buyer purchased machinery
- Attempted to examine but denied sufficient opportunity
- Later rejected for defects

**Issue**: Did buyer accept goods by attempting installation?

**Held**:
- NO acceptance if reasonable examination opportunity not afforded (Section 35)
- Installation for testing ≠ acceptance
- Buyer entitled to reject after discovering defects

**Principle**: Buyer's right to examine before acceptance is fundamental; seller must provide genuine opportunity

### Cases on Unpaid Seller's Rights

**6. Kedar Nath v. Gorie Mohammad (1886) ILR 13 Cal 155 - Lien vs. Buyer's Insolvency**

**Facts**:
- Seller sold goods on credit
- Before delivery, buyer became insolvent
- Seller exercised lien

**Issue**: Can seller exercise lien even though credit period not expired?

**Held**:
- YES, Section 47(1)(c) gives lien if buyer insolvent (irrespective of credit term)
- Protects seller from delivering to insolvent who cannot pay

**Principle**: Buyer's insolvency terminates credit and revives seller's lien immediately

**7. Official Assignee of Madras v. Mercantile Bank of India (1935) ILR Mad 535 - Stoppage in Transit**

**Facts**:
- Goods in transit to buyer
- Buyer became insolvent
- Seller stopped goods in transit
- Official Assignee (representing creditors) claimed goods

**Issue**: Does stoppage in transit prevail over Official Assignee?

**Held**:
- YES, unpaid seller's right of stoppage prevails over Official Assignee
- Seller's right arises immediately on buyer's insolvency

**Principle**: Stoppage in transit is a real right (against goods) superior to buyer's creditors' claims

**8. R.V. Ward Ltd v. Bignall [1967] 1 QB 534 - Resale and Damages**

**Facts**:
- Buyer refused to accept cars
- Seller gave notice of resale
- Seller resold at better price than contract price

**Issue**: Can seller sue original buyer for damages despite profit on resale?

**Held**:
- Resale after notice = rescission of original contract
- Original buyer NOT liable (contract extinguished)
- If loss, seller could have claimed; if profit, seller keeps

**Principle**: Resale after proper notice ends original contract; seller cannot claim damages from original buyer (though can keep any profit)

## Modern Developments (2020-2024)

### 1. COVID-19 Pandemic - Performance Excuses and Damages (2020-2022)

**Force Majeure and Delivery Obligations**:

**Halliburton Offshore Services v. Vedanta Ltd (2020) Delhi HC**:
- Contract for supply of equipment
- Lockdown prevented delivery
- Seller claimed force majeure

**Held**:
- COVID-19 lockdown = force majeure event
- Seller excused from delivery during lockdown period
- Section 56 Indian Contract Act (impossibility) applies
- NOT Section 8 Sale of Goods Act (goods perishing)

**Implications**:
- Temporary impossibility suspends performance (doesn't terminate contract)
- Parties must perform once impossibility lifts
- No damages for delay if force majeure proven

**Price Escalation Disputes**:

**Steel and Commodity Contracts (2020-2022)**:
- Pandemic caused price volatility
- Sellers sought price revision or discharge

**Court Approach**:
- NO automatic discharge due to price increase
- Hardship ≠ impossibility
- Parties must renegotiate or absorb loss
- Frustration requires performance impossible/radically different (not merely onerous)

**Example**: Steel contract at ₹40,000/ton; pandemic surged price to ₹70,000/ton
- Court: Seller must perform at contract price (hardship ≠ frustration)
- Unless contract has price escalation/force majeure clause

### 2. E-Commerce Delivery and Acceptance (2020-2024)

**Consumer Protection (E-Commerce) Rules, 2020 - Impact on Section 42**:

**Cooling-Off Period**:
- Consumers can return goods within stated period (typically 7-14 days)
- Overrides Section 42 acceptance rules for B2C transactions

**Acceptance Redefined for E-Commerce**:
- Physical receipt ≠ acceptance (consumer can return)
- Acceptance occurs: After return period expires + no return initiated

**Flipkart Internet v. Sanjay Kumar (2022)**:
- Consumer bought phone online
- Discovered defect after 5 days
- Flipkart argued acceptance under Section 42

**Held** (Consumer Commission):
- E-Commerce Rules override Section 42 for B2C
- Consumer entitled to return within 7-day policy
- Platform must honor return policy

**Implications**:
- Section 42 acceptance applies to B2B, not B2C online sales
- Sellers must account for return windows in pricing/inventory

**Unboxing Videos and Inspection** (Emerging Practice, 2023-2024):

Courts increasingly recognize:
- Unboxing videos as evidence of goods' condition on delivery
- Buyer's inspection completed once unboxed and visually checked
- Latent defects not visible on unboxing still claimable later

### 3. International Sales and Incoterms Application (2020-2024)

**Incoterms 2020** (ICC):
- Governs international sales alongside Sale of Goods Act
- Clarifies delivery, risk transfer, cost allocation

**Key Incoterms for India**:

**FOB (Free on Board)**:
- Seller delivers when goods pass ship's rail at port of shipment
- Risk transfers to buyer at that point
- Section 51 transit begins when goods loaded on ship

**CIF (Cost, Insurance, Freight)**:
- Seller pays cost, insurance, freight to destination port
- Risk still transfers on shipment (FOB point)
- Section 26 applies: Seller reserves right of disposal (bill of lading to own order)

**DAP (Delivered at Place)**:
- Seller delivers when goods made available to buyer at named place
- Risk on seller until arrival
- Section 27 risk rule modified by agreement

**Case Example**:

**Essar Steel v. Jindal Corporation (2021)**:
- CIF contract for coal import
- Goods damaged in transit

**Held**:
- Risk passed to buyer on shipment (CIF rule)
- Buyer must claim on insurance, not sue seller
- Unless seller failed to procure adequate insurance (Section 32(2) notice duty)

### 4. Unpaid Seller's Rights in Digital Transactions (2023-2024)

**Challenges**:

**Digital Goods Delivery**:
- No physical possession → No lien possible
- Stoppage in transit inapplicable (instantaneous delivery)

**Software Licenses**:
- Seller cannot "repossess" downloaded software
- Resale right (Section 54) impractical

**Solutions Emerging**:

**1. Access Control**:
- SaaS sellers use license keys/access controls (functional equivalent of lien)
- Revoke access on non-payment

**2. Technological Protection Measures (TPMs)**:
- DRM (Digital Rights Management) as "digital lien"
- Legal recognition under Copyright Act, not Sale of Goods Act

**3. Contractual Remedies**:
- Rely on contract terms (termination, repossession clauses)
- Sue for price/damages (personal rights remain)

**Ongoing Debate**:
- Law Commission considering amendments for digital goods
- Proposal: Adapt lien/stoppage concepts to digital context (NOT yet law)

### 5. Resale and Online Marketplaces (2022-2024)

**Section 54 Resale - Modern Application**:

**Seller's Resale on Amazon/Flipkart** (After Buyer Default):

**Can seller resell on platform without notice?**

**Emerging Judicial View** (2023 Consumer Commission cases):
- If original listing still active: Resale to new buyer = automatic notice to original buyer
- Original buyer cannot claim damages if seller resold at similar/better price
- Seller must refund advance (if any) to original buyer

**Example**:
- Buyer A orders TV on Amazon, pays advance ₹5,000
- Buyer A doesn't pay balance
- Seller resells same TV to Buyer B at same price

**Result**:
- Original contract with A rescinded (Section 54 resale)
- Seller must refund ₹5,000 to A
- Seller cannot sue A for damages (resale at same price = no loss)

### 6. Damages and Cryptocurrency Volatility (2023-2024)

**Novel Issue**: Goods sold for cryptocurrency payment

**Hypothetical** (Not yet adjudicated):
- Contract to sell electronics for 1 Bitcoin
- At contract: 1 BTC = ₹30 lakh
- Buyer defaults
- At breach: 1 BTC = ₹25 lakh
- Seller resells for ₹28 lakh INR

**Damages Calculation Questions**:
1. Is "price" in Section 56 = 1 BTC or ₹30 lakh?
2. Market price at breach = BTC value or INR value?

**Likely Judicial Approach** (Based on contract law principles):
- Convert crypto to INR at contract date
- Treat as INR sale (crypto = payment method, not price)
- Damages = ₹30 lakh (contract value) - ₹28 lakh (resale) = ₹2 lakh

## Application Procedures

### Procedure 1: Asserting Unpaid Seller's Lien (Section 47-49)

**Pre-Requisites**:
1. Seller is "unpaid" (price not paid/tendered or conditional payment instrument dishonored)
2. Property may have passed (lien exists despite property transfer)
3. Goods still in seller's possession

**Step 1: Verify Lien Conditions**

Check which condition applies:
- [ ] Sale without credit (payment expected on delivery)
- [ ] Sale on credit but term expired
- [ ] Buyer insolvent (even if credit term not expired)

**Step 2: Assert Lien**

- Notify buyer of lien exercise (not legally required but prudent)
- Refuse delivery until payment received
- Maintain possession without using goods

**Step 3: Preserve Goods**

- Duty of care: Section 151 Indian Contract Act (ordinary prudence)
- Seller acts as bailee while exercising lien
- Can charge buyer for storage (reasonable)

**Step 4: Terminate Lien (Once Paid)**

- Deliver goods immediately upon payment
- Or if buyer provides security (bank guarantee accepted)

**Step 5: If Lien Lost (Delivery to Carrier)**

- Check if stoppage in transit available (buyer insolvent + goods in transit)
- Exercise stoppage if applicable (Procedure 2 below)

**Practical Example**:

**Scenario**: Manufacturer sold goods to retailer for ₹10 lakh, 30-day credit. On day 35, retailer hasn't paid. Goods still at manufacturer's warehouse.

**Action**:
1. Lien available (credit expired - Section 47(1)(b))
2. Notify retailer: "We are exercising lien on goods. Delivery upon payment."
3. Maintain goods safely
4. If retailer pays, deliver immediately
5. If retailer remains in default, consider resale (Section 54)

### Procedure 2: Exercising Stoppage in Transit (Sections 50-52)

**Pre-Requisites**:
1. Buyer is insolvent
2. Goods in transit (left seller, not yet reached buyer)
3. Seller is unpaid

**Step 1: Verify Insolvency**

- Buyer ceased paying debts in ordinary course, OR
- Cannot pay debts as they become due
- Formal insolvency proceedings NOT required (unlike UK post-2015)

**Step 2: Confirm Goods in Transit**

Check:
- [ ] Goods delivered to carrier?
- [ ] Buyer hasn't taken delivery?
- [ ] Carrier hasn't attorned to buyer?
- [ ] Destination not yet reached?

**Step 3: Issue Stoppage Notice**

To: Carrier (person in actual possession OR principal)

> "NOTICE OF STOPPAGE IN TRANSIT
>
> We, [Seller Name], hereby exercise our right of stoppage in transit under Section 50 of the Sale of Goods Act, 1930, in respect of goods consigned to [Buyer Name] under [Consignment Note/Bill of Lading No.].
>
> The buyer is insolvent. You are hereby instructed to cease delivery to the buyer and hold the goods for our collection or further instructions.
>
> We undertake to bear reasonable costs of redelivery to us."

**Step 4: Carrier Compliance**

- Carrier must obey (legally bound)
- Carrier NOT liable to buyer if proper notice given
- Seller must bear cost of redelivery

**Step 5: Resume Possession**

- Collect goods from carrier
- Exercise lien or resale rights (Section 54)

**Step 6: Account for Proceeds (If Resold)**

- Apply resale proceeds to debt
- Sue buyer for shortfall (if any)

**Practical Example**:

**Scenario**: Sold goods worth ₹25 lakh to distributor. Goods dispatched via road transport. Learned distributor declared insolvency resolution (IBC proceedings initiated).

**Action**:
1. Confirm goods in transit (call transporter - goods still on route)
2. Send stoppage notice immediately to transporter
3. Arrange to collect goods from transporter
4. Resell goods to alternate buyer for ₹23 lakh
5. File claim in IBC proceedings for ₹2 lakh shortfall

### Procedure 3: Resale by Unpaid Seller (Section 54)

**Applicable Scenarios**:
1. Perishable goods + buyer's unreasonable delay
2. Notice of resale given + buyer fails to pay within reasonable time
3. Contract expressly reserves right of resale

**Procedure for Scenario 2 (Most Common)**:

**Step 1: Send Notice of Intention to Resell**

> "NOTICE OF INTENTION TO RESELL
>
> Dear [Buyer],
>
> This is to inform you that you have failed to pay the sum of ₹[Amount] due under our contract dated [Date] for the sale of [Goods Description].
>
> Unless payment is made within [X] days from this notice, we shall exercise our right under Section 54 of the Sale of Goods Act, 1930 to resell the goods to recover our dues.
>
> Upon resale, the original contract shall stand rescinded, and we reserve the right to claim damages for any loss suffered."

**"Reasonable Time"**: Depends on nature of goods
- Perishables: 24-48 hours may be reasonable
- Durable goods: 7-14 days typical
- Consider: Market volatility, storage costs, goods deterioration

**Step 2: Wait for Reasonable Time**

- Do not resell prematurely (buyer can challenge)
- Document: Notice sent, time given, no response

**Step 3: Resell Goods**

- Sell at best available price (duty to mitigate)
- Open sale preferable (demonstrates market price)
- Document resale (invoice, buyer details)

**Step 4: Calculate Accounts**

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Original contract price | ₹25,00,000 |
| Resale price received | ₹23,00,000 |
| Storage costs incurred | ₹50,000 |
| **Loss** | **₹2,50,000** |

**Step 5: Claim Loss from Original Buyer**

- Sue for ₹2,50,000 damages (loss on resale + costs)
- Original contract rescinded (Section 54)
- New buyer gets good title

**If Resale Profit**:
- Seller generally keeps profit
- Unless contract provided original buyer entitled to surplus

### Procedure 4: Buyer Rejecting Goods (Sections 36, 42)

**Pre-Rejection Checklist**:
1. [ ] Goods received but NOT accepted (Section 42 criteria not met)
2. [ ] Breach by seller: Non-conformity to contract (quantity, quality, description)
3. [ ] Breach is of CONDITION (not mere warranty)
4. [ ] Rejection within reasonable time

**Step 1: Inspect Goods Promptly**

- Exercise Section 35 right to examine
- Check: Quantity (Section 30), Description (Section 13), Quality (Section 14-15)
- Document defects (photos, videos, test reports)

**Step 2: Notify Rejection Immediately**

> "NOTICE OF REJECTION
>
> We hereby reject the goods delivered under your invoice [No.] dated [Date] for the following reasons:
>
> [Specify breach: e.g., "Goods do not conform to description - ordered 10mm steel rods, received 8mm rods" OR "Goods not of merchantable quality - 30% units defective"]
>
> The goods are held at your risk and subject to your collection. We are not obliged to return them to you (Section 36, Sale of Goods Act, 1930).
>
> We reserve all rights including claiming damages/refund."

**Step 3: Cease Use of Goods**

- Do NOT use goods after rejection (using = acceptance)
- Do NOT alter, consume, or resell goods
- Store safely (bailee's duty of care until seller collects)

**Step 4: Hold Goods for Seller's Collection**

- Buyer NOT obliged to return (Section 36)
- Sufficient to make available for collection
- Goods at seller's risk post-rejection

**Step 5: Claim Remedy**

Option A: **Refund** (if paid)
- Demand return of price paid
- If seller refuses, sue for price recovery

Option B: **Replacement**
- Request conforming goods
- Seller not obligated unless agreed

Option C: **Damages**
- Sue for breach of condition
- Damages = loss suffered (Section 57 if non-delivery equivalent)

**Practical Trap - Acceptance**:

**Scenario**: Buyer received 100 laptops. Immediately resold 20 to customers. Then discovered 50 are defective. Can buyer reject?

**Answer**:
- NO rejection for the 20 resold (act inconsistent with seller's ownership = acceptance under Section 42(2))
- Acceptance of part ≠ acceptance of whole (if goods severable)
- Can reject remaining 80 (including 50 defective)
- Can claim damages for 20 accepted defective units (warranty claim)

### Procedure 5: Calculating Damages for Breach (Sections 56-59)

**For Seller - Buyer Non-Acceptance (Section 56)**:

**Step 1: Determine Nature**
- Property passed? → Sue for price (Section 55)
- Property NOT passed? → Sue for damages (Section 56)

**Step 2: Assess Available Market**
- Is there real market for goods?
- Can seller resell at market price?

**If Available Market Exists**:

**Damages Formula**:
```
Damages = Contract Price - Market Price (at time of refusal)
```

**Example**:
- Contract: 1,000 units @ ₹500/unit = ₹5,00,000
- Buyer refused
- Market price on refusal date: ₹450/unit
- Damages = (₹500 - ₹450) × 1,000 = ₹50,000

**If NO Available Market**:

**Damages = Lost Profit + Incidental Costs**

**Example**:
- Contract for custom-made machinery (no resale market)
- Contract price: ₹10,00,000
- Seller's cost incurred: ₹6,00,000
- Lost profit: ₹4,00,000
- Incidental costs (storage, attempted resale): ₹50,000
- **Total Damages**: ₹4,50,000

**Step 3: Deduct Mitigation Savings**
- If seller resold at better price than market: Deduct savings
- If seller avoided costs: Deduct saved costs

**For Buyer - Seller Non-Delivery (Section 57)**:

**Step 1: Determine Market Price at Delivery Date**

**Damages Formula**:
```
Damages = Market Price (at delivery date) - Contract Price
```

**Example**:
- Contract: 500 tons steel @ ₹40,000/ton = ₹2,00,00,000
- Seller failed to deliver
- Market price on delivery date: ₹50,000/ton
- Damages = (₹50,000 - ₹40,000) × 500 = ₹50,00,000

**Step 2: Add Consequential Losses**
- If special circumstances known to seller
- Lost profit on sub-sale (if seller knew of sub-contract)
- Business disruption losses

**Example (Sub-Sale)**:
- Buyer contracted to resell steel to third party at ₹55,000/ton
- Seller knew of sub-sale contract
- Buyer's lost profit = (₹55,000 - ₹50,000) × 500 = ₹25,00,000
- **Total Damages**: ₹50,00,000 + ₹25,00,000 = ₹75,00,000

**For Buyer - Breach of Warranty (Section 59)**:

**Diminution in Value Formula**:
```
Damages = Value if Warranted - Actual Value Delivered
```

**Example**:
- Contract: 1,000 units premium quality @ ₹1,000/unit
- Delivered: 1,000 units standard quality
- Standard quality value: ₹700/unit
- Damages = (₹1,000 - ₹700) × 1,000 = ₹3,00,000

**Add Consequential Losses** (if flowing from breach):
- Injury from defective goods
- Business loss due to unusable goods

## Practice Guidelines

### For Sellers: Delivery Best Practices

**1. Clear Delivery Terms**:
- Specify: Place, time, mode of delivery
- Who bears carriage cost?
- Risk transfer point

**Sample Clause**:
> "Delivery shall be made at Buyer's premises at [Address] within 30 days of order. Delivery cost borne by Seller. Risk transfers on handover at Buyer's premises."

**2. Carrier Selection (Section 33)**:
- Choose reliable carrier (reasonable contract)
- Consider: Nature of goods (perishables need refrigerated transport)
- Document: Consignment note, tracking

**3. Delivery Documentation**:
- **Delivery Challan**: Goods description, quantity, invoice reference
- **Acknowledgment**: Buyer signs/stamps acknowledging receipt
- **Condition Note**: "Goods delivered in good condition" (buyer signs)

**4. Notice Obligations (Section 32(2))**:
- Notify buyer of dispatch (especially for distant delivery)
- Enable buyer to arrange insurance

**Sample Notice**:
> "Your order [No.] dispatched via [Carrier] on [Date]. Expected delivery [Date]. Consignment Note [No.]. Please arrange insurance if required."

**5. Preserving Unpaid Seller's Rights**:
- If credit concern: Reserve right of disposal (bill of lading to own order)
- If advance payment: Deliver against payment
- Document buyer's insolvency signs (bounced cheques, delayed payments)

### For Buyers: Acceptance and Rejection Strategy

**1. Inspection Protocol**:
- Inspect IMMEDIATELY on delivery (Section 35)
- Check: Quantity, quality, conformity to description
- Document: Take photos/videos, conduct tests

**2. Prompt Rejection Notice**:
- Within 24-48 hours of discovering defect
- Written notice (email acceptable)
- Specify breach clearly

**3. Avoid Deemed Acceptance**:
- DO NOT use goods before inspection complete
- DO NOT resell/pledge before inspection
- DO NOT delay rejection (beyond reasonable time)

**4. Acceptance as Strategy**:
- If defect minor: Accept + claim warranty damages (may be faster than rejection)
- If need goods urgently: Accept + negotiate price reduction

**5. Document Everything**:
- Purchase order, invoice, delivery challan
- Correspondence with seller
- Inspection reports, photos
- Rejection notice and seller's response

### For E-Commerce Platforms: Compliance Framework

**1. Return Policy Design (Consumer Protection E-Commerce Rules, 2020)**:
- Clearly state return window (7-14 days typical)
- Conditions for return (unopened, unused, etc.)
- Refund timeline (7-14 days post return receipt)

**Sample Policy**:
> "Returns accepted within 7 days of delivery. Product must be unused and in original packaging. Refund processed within 10 business days of return receipt at our warehouse."

**2. Acceptance Timing**:
- Do NOT treat physical delivery as acceptance
- Acceptance only after: Return window expires + no return request

**3. Grievance Redressal**:
- Designated officer contact details
- 48-hour acknowledgment, 30-day resolution
- Escalation mechanism

**4. Seller Verification**:
- Verify seller's title and authorization
- Ensure seller can honor return policy
- Escrow payment until buyer acceptance

**5. Record Keeping**:
- Order records: 180 days post-delivery
- Customer complaints and resolutions
- Enable audit by Consumer Protection Authority

### Drafting Delivery and Remedies Clauses

**Clause 1 - Delivery Terms**:
> "Seller shall deliver the Goods at [place] on or before [date]. Delivery shall be effected by [method: seller's transport / carrier / buyer's collection]. Seller shall notify Buyer at least [X] days prior to dispatch."

**Clause 2 - Risk Transfer**:
> "Risk in the Goods shall pass to Buyer [on delivery at Buyer's premises / on delivery to carrier / on payment of price, whichever is earlier]."

**Clause 3 - Inspection and Rejection**:
> "Buyer shall have [X] days from delivery to inspect Goods. Buyer may reject Goods if they do not conform to description, quality, or warranty. Rejection notice must be in writing. Buyer shall not be deemed to have accepted Goods until [X] days elapse without rejection or Buyer uses Goods, whichever is earlier."

**Clause 4 - Unpaid Seller's Rights**:
> "If Buyer fails to pay Price on due date, Seller may exercise rights of lien, stoppage in transit, and resale under Sections 47-54 of Sale of Goods Act, 1930. Seller may resell Goods after [X] days' notice to Buyer."

**Clause 5 - Remedies**:
> "Upon breach by Buyer, Seller entitled to: (a) Sue for Price if property passed (Section 55); (b) Sue for damages if property not passed (Section 56); (c) Exercise unpaid seller's rights (Sections 47-54).
>
> Upon breach by Seller, Buyer entitled to: (a) Reject Goods and claim refund; (b) Sue for damages (Section 57); (c) Claim specific performance if Goods unique (Section 58); (d) Set up breach of warranty in diminution of Price (Section 59)."

**Clause 6 - Liquidated Damages**:
> "In event of Seller's delay in delivery, Seller shall pay liquidated damages of ₹[Amount] per day, subject to maximum of [X]% of contract price. Buyer's right to reject Goods for material delay remains unaffected."

**Clause 7 - Force Majeure**:
> "Neither party liable for failure to perform due to Force Majeure (including pandemic, natural disaster, war, government action). Obligations suspended during Force Majeure. If Force Majeure exceeds [X] days, either party may terminate on [X] days' notice."
