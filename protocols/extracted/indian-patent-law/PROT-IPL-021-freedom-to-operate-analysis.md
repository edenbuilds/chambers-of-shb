# Protocol: Freedom-to-Operate (FTO) Analysis

**Protocol ID**: PROT-IPL-021
**Version**: 1.0.0
**Domain**: Indian Patent Law
**Category**: Risk Assessment
**Last Updated**: 2026-01-16

---

## Purpose

Comprehensive guide for conducting Freedom-to-Operate analysis in India, including patent landscape mapping, claim interpretation, infringement risk assessment, and clearance strategies specific to the Indian patent regime.

---

## Activation Triggers

- Launching new product in India
- Entering Indian market with existing product
- Receiving cease and desist letter
- Pre-licensing due diligence
- Investment/M&A assessment
- R&D project evaluation

---

## Prerequisites

- Clear product/technology description
- Knowledge of commercial plans
- Access to patent databases
- Understanding of claim construction
- Budget for comprehensive search

---

## Core Process

### Step 1: Define Scope of FTO Analysis

#### Product Definition:
- **What**: Complete technical description
- **How**: Manufacturing process/method
- **Where**: Geographic markets (India focus)
- **When**: Launch timeline
- **Variations**: Planned modifications

#### Analysis Scope:
| Component | Include |
|-----------|---------|
| Active ingredients | All compositions |
| Formulation | Delivery mechanisms |
| Manufacturing process | Key steps |
| Device/apparatus | Structural features |
| Software/algorithms | Functional elements |
| Packaging | If innovative |

### Step 2: Patent Search Strategy

#### Indian Patent Databases:
1. **InPASS** (Indian Patent Advanced Search System)
   - Official IPO database
   - Free access
   - Updated regularly

2. **Espacenet** (India coverage)
   - International database with Indian patents
   - Full-text search capability

#### Search Parameters:
- **Keywords**: Technical terms, synonyms, variations
- **IPC/CPC codes**: Relevant classifications
- **Applicant/inventor names**: Known competitors
- **Filing dates**: Patents in force (20-year term)

#### Search Scope for India:
- Indian granted patents (IN)
- Indian pending applications
- PCT applications designating India
- Convention applications with India priority

### Step 3: Initial Patent Screening

#### Filter Criteria:

| Factor | Assessment |
|--------|------------|
| Status | Granted and in force |
| Term | Not expired (20 years from filing) |
| Renewal | Fees paid/maintained |
| Relevance | Technical overlap |
| Claims | Potentially covering product |

#### Create Patent Landscape:
- Total patents identified
- Categorize by relevance (high/medium/low)
- Map to product components
- Identify key patent holders

### Step 4: Claim Analysis

#### Indian Claim Construction Principles:

**Purposive Construction** (followed in India):
- Claims read in context of specification
- Technical contribution considered
- Not purely literal, not purely equivalents

#### Key Elements to Analyze:

1. **Independent Claims**: Primary scope
2. **Dependent Claims**: Narrower scope
3. **Method Claims**: Process coverage
4. **Product Claims**: Composition/device
5. **Use Claims**: Application specific

#### Claim Element Mapping:
```
For each relevant claim:
├─ List all claim elements
├─ Map product features to elements
├─ Identify matches/differences
├─ Assess literal infringement
└─ Assess doctrine of equivalents risk
```

### Step 5: Infringement Risk Assessment

#### Literal Infringement Analysis:
- Does product have ALL claim elements?
- Element-by-element comparison
- Any missing element = no literal infringement

#### Doctrine of Equivalents (India):
- Less developed than US
- Ravi Kamal Bali v. Kala Tech (Delhi HC)
- Consider: function-way-result test

#### Risk Categorization:

| Risk Level | Criteria | Action |
|------------|----------|--------|
| **High** | Literal infringement likely | Seek license or design-around |
| **Medium** | Equivalents possible | Detailed analysis, opinion |
| **Low** | Differences exist | Document distinctions |
| **Clear** | No coverage | Proceed with monitoring |

### Step 6: Validity Assessment

#### If High-Risk Patent Identified:

Assess invalidity arguments under:
- **Section 25/64 grounds**: Lack of novelty, obviousness
- **Section 3 exclusions**: 3(d), 3(k), 3(p), etc.
- **Section 10 deficiencies**: Insufficient disclosure
- **Prior art**: Anticipation/obviousness

#### Sources for Prior Art:
- Patent databases (global)
- Scientific literature
- Trade publications
- Conference proceedings
- Products on market before priority date

### Step 7: Design-Around Strategies

#### If Infringement Risk High:

**Option 1**: Modify product to avoid claims
- Change composition/structure
- Alter manufacturing process
- Remove or substitute elements

**Option 2**: Challenge patent validity
- Pre-grant opposition (if pending)
- Post-grant opposition (within 12 months of grant)
- Revocation petition (any time)

**Option 3**: Obtain license
- Voluntary licensing negotiation
- Compulsory licensing (if eligible)
- Cross-licensing opportunities

**Option 4**: Wait for expiry
- Calculate remaining term
- Consider interim strategies

### Step 8: FTO Opinion Preparation

#### Opinion Components:

1. **Executive Summary**: Key findings, risk level
2. **Methodology**: Search strategy, databases
3. **Product Description**: What was analyzed
4. **Patent Landscape**: Relevant patents identified
5. **Claim Analysis**: Element-by-element comparison
6. **Risk Assessment**: For each patent
7. **Validity Assessment**: Potential challenges
8. **Recommendations**: Clear action items
9. **Limitations**: Scope boundaries

#### Opinion Types:
- **Preliminary FTO**: Quick assessment
- **Comprehensive FTO**: Detailed analysis
- **Clearance Opinion**: Formal legal opinion

## Indian-Specific Considerations

### Section 3 Defenses:
- Section 3(d): New form without enhanced efficacy
- Section 3(k): Computer program per se
- Section 3(j): Traditional knowledge/plants
- Section 3(p): Traditional knowledge

### Bolar Exception (Section 107A):
- Regulatory approval activities exempt
- Generic drug development protected
- Pre-expiry R&D permitted

### Compulsory Licensing Option:
- Section 84: After 3 years from grant
- Consider as risk mitigation
- Not guaranteed, requires application

## Common FTO Mistakes

1. **Incomplete search**: Missing key patents
2. **Surface-level analysis**: Not parsing claims
3. **Ignoring pending applications**: Could grant
4. **Outdated information**: Check current status
5. **Over-reliance on abstracts**: Read full claims
6. **Missing Section 3 defense**: Available in India
