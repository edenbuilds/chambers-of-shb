## Purpose

Comprehensive guide to understanding parallel imports and patent exhaustion doctrine in India, including the international exhaustion regime, practical implications, and strategies for patentees and importers.

## Core Process

### Step 1: Understanding Exhaustion Doctrine

#### What is Exhaustion:

When a patentee sells a patented product, their rights over that specific item are "exhausted" - they cannot control subsequent resale.

#### Types of Exhaustion:

| Type | Meaning | Effect |
|------|---------|--------|
| **National** | Exhausted only within country of sale | Can block imports |
| **Regional** | Exhausted within region (e.g., EU) | Can block extra-regional imports |
| **International** | Exhausted globally upon first sale | Cannot block parallel imports |

### Step 2: India's Position

#### Section 107A(b) - International Exhaustion:

"[T]he following acts shall not constitute an infringement... importation of patented products by any person from a person who is duly authorised under the law to produce and sell or distribute the product"

#### Interpretation:

**India Follows INTERNATIONAL EXHAUSTION**

This means:
- Once patentee sells anywhere in world
- That specific product can be imported to India
- Without patentee's consent
- As long as first sale was authorized

### Step 3: Requirements for Parallel Import Defense

#### What Importer Must Show:

| Requirement | Evidence |
|-------------|----------|
| **Legitimate first sale** | Product sold by patentee/authorized party |
| **Authorized production** | Made by or licensed by patentee |
| **Same product** | Identical to patented product |
| **Chain of title** | Legitimate purchase chain |

#### What Defeats the Defense:

| Issue | Effect |
|-------|--------|
| Counterfeit goods | Not parallel import (infringement) |
| Unauthorized manufacture | Not authorized first sale |
| Quality/specification differences | May not be "same product" |
| Repackaged/altered goods | Depends on nature of alteration |

### Step 4: Practical Implications

#### For Patentees:

**Challenges**:
- Cannot prevent parallel imports
- Price differences exploited
- Gray market activity
- Loss of distribution control

**Strategies**:
1. **Contractual restrictions**: In distribution agreements
2. **Trademark protection**: May have different rules
3. **Product differentiation**: Country-specific versions
4. **Service bundling**: Value-added services
5. **Pricing alignment**: Reduce arbitrage opportunity

#### For Importers:

**Opportunities**:
- Legitimate parallel imports allowed
- Price arbitrage possible
- Competition enabled

**Precautions**:
- Verify authorized first sale
- Document chain of title
- Ensure not counterfeit
- Check for contractual restrictions
- Consider warranty/service issues

### Step 5: Distinguishing Situations

#### Parallel Import vs. Infringement:

```
Is the product:
├── Made by patentee/licensee? → Possibly parallel import
│   └── First sold with authorization? → Parallel import OK
│
├── Made without authorization? → INFRINGEMENT
│   └── Not parallel import defense
│
└── Counterfeit/fake? → INFRINGEMENT
    └── Separate issue entirely
```

#### Gray Market Goods:

| Type | Status in India |
|------|-----------------|
| Genuine goods, different market | Likely permitted |
| Genuine goods, contractual restriction | Contractual issue, not patent |
| Altered/repackaged genuine | May depend on alteration |
| Counterfeit goods | Infringement (not parallel import) |

### Step 6: Trademark Considerations

#### Different Rule for Trademarks:

Indian trademark law may NOT follow international exhaustion:
- Section 30(3): National exhaustion possible
- May block parallel imports on trademark grounds
- Even if patent exhaustion applies

#### Strategy:
Patentees may use trademark rights where patent exhaustion fails.

### Step 7: Case Law and Interpretations

#### Key Developments:

| Case | Holding |
|------|---------|
| **Samsung v. Kapil Wadhwa** | Discussed exhaustion in context |
| **Legislative history** | Section 107A intended to allow parallel imports |
| **TRIPS flexibility** | India exercised option for international exhaustion |

#### Interpretation Points:

1. "Duly authorized" = Authorized by patent holder
2. "To produce and sell" = Legitimate manufacturer
3. No requirement of Indian license
4. First sale anywhere in world qualifies

### Step 8: Export Considerations

#### Can Patentee Restrict Exports:

- From India: Patentee can restrict through contract
- To India: Cannot control (international exhaustion)

#### Practical Reality:
Many distribution agreements include:
- Territory restrictions
- Export prohibitions
- Customer restrictions

These are contractual, not patent issues.

## Parallel Import Flowchart

```
Patented Product Imported to India
        │
        ├── Made by patentee or licensee?
        │   │
        │   ├── YES → First sale authorized?
        │   │         │
        │   │         ├── YES → PARALLEL IMPORT (Permitted)
        │   │         │
        │   │         └── NO → INFRINGEMENT
        │   │
        │   └── NO → Counterfeit/unauthorized
        │             │
        │             └── INFRINGEMENT (Not parallel import)
        │
        └── Uncertain origin → Investigate further
            │
            └── Burden on importer to prove legitimacy
```

## Strategic Guidance

### For Patentees Concerned About Parallel Imports:

| Strategy | Implementation |
|----------|---------------|
| **Contractual controls** | Distribution agreements with territory limits |
| **Trademark enforcement** | Where trademark exhaustion narrower |
| **Product differentiation** | Country-specific models/features |
| **Service bundling** | Tie to local service/warranty |
| **Selective distribution** | Careful dealer selection |

### For Importers Planning Parallel Import:

| Step | Action |
|------|--------|
| **Due diligence** | Verify legitimate first sale |
| **Documentation** | Retain purchase chain evidence |
| **Legal review** | Check for contractual exposure |
| **Quality check** | Ensure genuine, not counterfeit |
| **Market analysis** | Assess commercial viability |

## Common Parallel Import Issues

1. **Confusing with counterfeits**: Very different legal status
2. **Ignoring trademark angle**: Different rules may apply
3. **No documentation**: Cannot prove legitimate source
4. **Contractual blindness**: May have separate liability
5. **Quality differences**: Country-specific products
6. **Warranty problems**: May not be honored
