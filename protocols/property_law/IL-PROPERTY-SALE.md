# Indian Property Law: Sale of Immovable Property

## Purpose
This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on sale of immovable property under the Transfer of Property Act 1882 (Sections 54-55) and Registration Act 1908 (Section 17). It covers the distinction between contract to sell vs sale, essential elements of valid sale, registration requirements, transfer of ownership and risk, doctrine of part performance (Section 53A), remedies for breach, and practical considerations for property transactions.

## Activation Triggers
Use this protocol when:
- Client wants to buy/sell immovable property
- Analyzing validity of property sale deed/agreement to sell
- Dispute over transfer of ownership or possession
- Registration issues in property transactions
- Breach of contract to sell (buyer/seller defaulted)
- Specific performance claim for property sale
- Applying Section 53A part performance doctrine
- Title verification and due diligence for property purchase
- Stamp duty and registration fee calculation
- Remedies evaluation for defective property transactions

## Core Principles

### 1. Section 54: Sale Defined

**"Sale is a transfer of ownership in exchange for a price paid or promised or part-paid and part-promised"**

Essential Elements of Sale:
1. **TRANSFER OF OWNERSHIP** - Title passes from seller to buyer
2. **IMMOVABLE PROPERTY** - Land, buildings, hereditary allowances, rights
3. **PRICE** - Money consideration (not barter = exchange)
4. **PAID OR PROMISED** - Present or future payment
5. **TWO PARTIES** - Seller (transferor) and Buyer (transferee)

### 2. Section 54 Registration Requirement

**Immovable Property of Value Rs. 100 or Upwards:**

**Registration is MANDATORY** (Section 17 Registration Act 1908)

```
Section 54 TPA:
"Sale of immovable property of the value of one hundred rupees and upwards 
can be made only by a REGISTERED INSTRUMENT."

Section 49 Registration Act:
"No document required to be registered shall affect any immovable property 
comprised therein, or confer any power to adopt, or be received as evidence 
of any transaction affecting such property, unless it has been REGISTERED."
```

**Effect of Non-Registration:**
- Sale deed is VOID (not voidable)
- No transfer of title
- Cannot be used as evidence
- Buyer gets NO rights in property
- Only remedy: Return of price + damages

**Below Rs. 100:**
- Registration NOT mandatory
- Delivery of possession sufficient

### 3. Contract to Sell vs Sale

| Aspect | Contract to Sell | Sale |
|--------|------------------|------|
| **Nature** | Executory contract | Executed transfer |
| **Ownership** | Remains with seller | Passes to buyer |
| **Risk** | With seller | With buyer |
| **Registration** | NOT required | MANDATORY (Rs. 100+) |
| **Breach Remedy** | Damages (not specific performance usually) | Specific performance available |
| **Rights Created** | Personal right (in personam) | Proprietary right (in rem) |
| **Time of Transfer** | Future date | Immediately on registration |

**Agreement to Sell** = Contract to Sell
- Creates obligation to execute sale deed in future
- Does NOT transfer ownership
- Usually not registrable (no transfer effected)
- Breach → Damages OR specific performance (if property unique)

**Sale Deed** = Sale
- Transfers ownership immediately on registration
- Buyer becomes owner
- Registrable and registration mandatory
- Breach after registration → Different remedies (possession, damages)

### 4. Transfer of Ownership and Risk

**Section 55(4)(b): Seller's Duty to Disclose Material Defects**

```
Seller must disclose:
- Material defects in property
- Material defects in title
- Encumbrances (mortgage, charge, lease)
- Litigation affecting property
```

**Non-Disclosure:**
- Buyer can rescind contract OR
- Claim compensation for loss

**Section 55(6)(a): Risk Passes with Ownership**

```
"The risk of loss from fire, tempest, or other inevitable accident, 
passes to the buyer as soon as the property is transferred"
```

**Unless otherwise agreed:**
- Before registration: Risk with SELLER
- After registration: Risk with BUYER

**Practical Implication:**
- Property damaged before registration → Seller bears loss
- Property damaged after registration → Buyer bears loss
- Insurance should transfer on registration

## Framework

### Phase 1: Pre-Transaction Due Diligence

**Step 1: Title Verification (30-40 Years Chain)**

Verify seller has:
1. **MARKETABLE TITLE** - Free from defects, litigation, encumbrances
2. **CHAIN OF TITLE** - Examine 30-40 years back (preferably to root of title)
3. **NO ADVERSE POSSESSION** - Verify actual possession

Documents to Examine:
- Sale deeds (entire chain)
- Gift deeds, partition deeds, will, succession certificate
- Encumbrance certificate (15 years minimum)
- Property tax receipts
- Possession certificate
- Building approval plans (if construction)
- No Objection Certificates (NOC) from society/authority

**Step 2: Legal Status Check**

1. **Seller's Capacity** - Section 7 TPA
   - Major (18+ years)
   - Sound mind
   - Entitled to transfer (owner, co-owner, authorized agent)

2. **Property Status**
   - NOT agricultural land (if buyer non-agriculturist - state laws)
   - NOT government/municipal land
   - NOT disputed property (pending litigation)
   - NOT subject to statutory restriction

3. **Encumbrances Check** - Encumbrance Certificate
   - Mortgage
   - Charge
   - Lease
   - Easement
   - Pending litigation

**Step 3: Physical Inspection**

1. **Site Visit** - Verify boundaries, possession, encroachment
2. **Survey** - Demarcation of property
3. **Construction Status** - Match approved plans with actual construction

### Phase 2: Agreement to Sell

**Step 1: Draft Agreement to Sell**

Mandatory Clauses:
1. **PARTIES** - Full name, address, age of seller and buyer
2. **PROPERTY DESCRIPTION** - Survey number, area, boundaries, address
3. **SALE PRICE** - Total consideration
4. **ADVANCE/EARNEST** - Amount paid (usually 10-20%)
5. **BALANCE PAYMENT** - Mode and timeline
6. **COMPLETION DATE** - Date for executing sale deed
7. **SELLER'S REPRESENTATIONS**
   - Owner of property
   - Marketable title
   - No encumbrances
   - Vacant possession
8. **BUYER'S OBLIGATIONS**
   - Pay balance on completion
   - Bear stamp duty and registration charges (unless otherwise agreed)
9. **DEFAULT CLAUSES**
   - Seller default: Return double earnest + damages
   - Buyer default: Forfeit earnest
10. **DISPUTE RESOLUTION** - Arbitration/jurisdiction clause

**Step 2: Execute Agreement (Optional Registration)**

Agreement to sell:
- Registration NOT mandatory (no transfer of ownership)
- But ADVISABLE to register if substantial advance paid
- Registered agreement: Admissible as evidence + notice to world

**Step 3: Token Money/Earnest**

Legal Nature:
- Part payment of price
- Secures performance of contract
- Forfeited if buyer defaults (unless otherwise agreed)
- Returned double if seller defaults (customary, not statutory)

Amount: Usually 10-20% of sale price

### Phase 3: Execution of Sale Deed

**Step 1: Draft Sale Deed**

Mandatory Contents:
1. **DATE AND PLACE** - Of execution
2. **PARTIES** - Seller (Vendor) and Buyer (Purchaser)
3. **RECITALS** - Background, title history
4. **PROPERTY DESCRIPTION** - Schedule with survey numbers, boundaries, area
5. **CONSIDERATION** - Total price
6. **RECEIPT CLAUSE** - Acknowledgment of payment
7. **TRANSFER CLAUSE** - "Sell, transfer, convey all rights, title, interest"
8. **POSSESSION CLAUSE** - Delivery of possession
9. **SELLER'S COVENANTS**
   - Marketable title
   - No encumbrances
   - Right to sell
   - Quiet enjoyment
10. **ATTESTATION** - Two witnesses

**Step 2: Stamp Duty Payment**

Governed by Indian Stamp Act 1899 + State Stamp Acts

**Stamp Duty Rate**: Varies by state (3-10% of consideration/market value, whichever higher)

Examples:
- Maharashtra: 5-6% + 1% metro cess
- Karnataka: 5% + 0.1% surcharge
- Delhi: 4-7%
- Tamil Nadu: 7%

**Market Value vs Consideration:**
- Stamp duty on HIGHER of:
  - Agreement value (consideration)
  - Circle rate/guidance value/ready reckoner rate

**Payment Mode:**
- E-stamping (preferred)
- Non-judicial stamp paper (being phased out)

**Penalty for Under-Stamping:**
- 2% penalty per month (maximum 200% of deficit)
- Document inadmissible as evidence

**Step 3: Registration (Section 17 Registration Act)**

**Mandatory Documents for Registration:**

Seller Must Produce:
1. **SALE DEED** - Original + 2 copies
2. **IDENTITY PROOF** - Aadhaar, PAN, passport
3. **TITLE DOCUMENTS** - Previous sale deed, chain documents
4. **ENCUMBRANCE CERTIFICATE** - 15 years
5. **TAX RECEIPTS** - Property tax paid up to date
6. **NOC** - Society NOC (if apartment/flat)
7. **POWER OF ATTORNEY** - If selling through agent

Buyer Must Produce:
1. **IDENTITY PROOF** - Aadhaar, PAN, passport
2. **ADDRESS PROOF**

**Registration Procedure:**

Step 1: **Book Appointment** - Online slot booking
Step 2: **Physical Appearance** - Seller, buyer, 2 witnesses at Sub-Registrar office
Step 3: **Document Verification** - Sub-Registrar verifies documents
Step 4: **Thumb Impression/Signature** - Parties sign sale deed
Step 5: **Registration Fee Payment** - 1% of consideration (usually)
Step 6: **Registration Completion** - Deed entered in register, serial number assigned
Step 7: **Certified Copies** - Collect registered sale deed (usually 1-7 days)

**Limitation for Registration:**
- Section 25 Registration Act: Within 4 MONTHS from execution
- Extension: Additional 4 months (with penalty)
- After 8 months: Registration refused

**Effect of Registration:**
- Transfer of ownership from seller to buyer
- Buyer becomes absolute owner
- Deed admissible as evidence
- Notice to world

### Phase 4: Post-Registration

**Step 1: Possession Delivery**

Methods:
1. **SYMBOLIC** - Delivery of keys
2. **ACTUAL** - Physical handover and vacation by seller
3. **CONSTRUCTIVE** - Attornment (acknowledgment by tenant/occupier)

**Step 2: Mutation of Records**

Update revenue records:
1. **Revenue Department** - Mutation in land records (7/12, 8A, RTC)
2. **Municipal Corporation** - Property tax records
3. **Society** - Transfer of membership (if apartment)
4. **Utility Providers** - Electricity, water connection transfer

**Step 3: Tax Implications**

**For Seller:**
- **Capital Gains Tax** - LTCG/STCG on profit
  - LTCG: 20% with indexation (holding >24 months)
  - STCG: As per income tax slab (holding <24 months)
- **Exemptions** - Section 54, 54EC, 54F Income Tax Act

**For Buyer:**
- **TDS Deduction** - Section 194IA: 1% TDS if consideration >Rs. 50 lakhs
- **Registration Fee** - 1% (approx) of consideration
- **Stamp Duty** - 3-10% (state-specific)

## Section 53A: Doctrine of Part Performance

**Section 53A TPA (Protection to Transferee)**

```
"Where any person contracts to transfer for consideration any immovable 
property by writing signed by him or on his behalf, and the transferee has, 
in part performance of the contract:
(a) Taken possession of the property, OR
(b) Being already in possession, continues in possession in part performance, 
    and has done some act in furtherance,

And the transferee has performed or is willing to perform his part of the 
contract, then the transferor CANNOT enforce against the transferee any 
right in respect of the property inconsistent with the contract."
```

**Purpose**: Prevent fraud/unjust enrichment where buyer acts on agreement but seller refuses to execute registered sale deed

**Essential Conditions (All Must Be Satisfied):**

1. **CONTRACT** - Valid contract to transfer immovable property
2. **CONSIDERATION** - For consideration (not gratuitous)
3. **WRITING** - Contract in WRITING signed by transferor/agent
4. **POSSESSION** - Transferee taken possession OR continues in possession
5. **ACT IN FURTHERANCE** - Transferee done some act in furtherance
6. **READINESS TO PERFORM** - Transferee ready and willing to perform his part

**What Section 53A DOES:**
- Prevents seller from evicting buyer
- Prevents seller from transferring to third party
- Protects buyer's possession

**What Section 53A DOES NOT DO:**
- Does NOT transfer ownership (only registered deed can)
- Does NOT create title in buyer
- Does NOT make unregistered deed valid

**Remedy**: Buyer must file suit for **SPECIFIC PERFORMANCE** to compel seller to execute registered sale deed

### Case Law on Section 53A

**Nathulal v. Phoolchand (SC 1969)**
Holding: Section 53A is shield, not sword - defensive, not offensive remedy

**Ramesh Hirachand v. Sonamal (SC 1988)**
Holding: "Act in furtherance" means substantial investment/alteration beyond mere possession

Examples of "Act in Furtherance":
- Construction on property
- Substantial repairs/renovations
- Payment of substantial part of price
- Payment of property tax, maintenance charges

NOT Sufficient:
- Mere possession alone
- Payment of token amount
- Temporary occupation

## Remedies for Breach

### A. Buyer's Remedies (Seller Defaulted)

**Scenario 1: Seller Refuses to Execute Sale Deed After Agreement**

Remedy 1: **SPECIFIC PERFORMANCE** (Section 10 Specific Relief Act 1963)

Conditions:
- Property is UNIQUE (land/building inherently unique)
- Damages inadequate compensation
- Contract valid and enforceable
- Buyer ready and willing to perform

Decree: Court orders seller to execute registered sale deed

Limitation: 3 YEARS from date fixed for performance (Article 54 Limitation Act)

**Remedy 2: DAMAGES** (Section 73 Contract Act)

If specific performance not granted:
- Return of earnest/advance
- Interest on amount paid
- Damages for loss of bargain (if market price increased)
- Compensation for expenses (stamp duty on agreement, legal fees)

**Remedy 3: COMPENSATION** (Section 55(5) TPA)

Seller wrongfully neglects/refuses to complete sale:
- Compensation for loss/damage from breach
- Return of deposit with interest

**Scenario 2: Seller's Title Defective (Discovered After Agreement)**

Section 55(1)(c) TPA: Seller's Duty to Disclose

Buyer's Options:
1. **RESCIND CONTRACT** - Get back deposit + expenses
2. **CLAIM COMPENSATION** - For diminution in value
3. **SPECIFIC PERFORMANCE WITH ABATEMENT** - Compel sale with price reduction

**Scenario 3: Seller Sells to Third Party (Double Sale)**

If Buyer's Agreement Registered:
- Agreement creates notice to world
- Third party purchaser is subsequent transferee
- Buyer can claim specific performance (priority)

If Buyer's Agreement NOT Registered:
- Third party bona fide purchaser for value gets priority (Section 27 Registration Act)
- Buyer's remedy: Damages against seller only

### B. Seller's Remedies (Buyer Defaulted)

**Scenario 1: Buyer Refuses to Pay Balance/Take Deed**

Remedy 1: **SPECIFIC PERFORMANCE** (Compel Buyer to Complete)

Conditions:
- Seller ready with marketable title
- Buyer without valid excuse
- Court's discretion (rarely granted against buyer)

Usually NOT granted because:
- Contract of sale is personal
- Cannot force buyer to purchase unwillingly
- Damages adequate remedy for seller

**Remedy 2: DAMAGES** (Section 73 Contract Act)

Seller can claim:
- Forfeit earnest (if contract permits)
- Damages for loss of bargain (if market price declined)
- Interest on unpaid price
- Expenses incurred (stamp duty on readiness deed)

**Remedy 3: FORFEIT DEPOSIT**

If contract contains forfeiture clause:
- Forfeit earnest/deposit
- Additional damages if actual loss exceeds deposit

Section 74 Contract Act Ceiling:
- Liquidated damages enforceable
- Court may reduce if unreasonable

**Scenario 2: Buyer Delays Payment**

Seller's Rights:
1. **CHARGE INTEREST** - As per agreement OR 6% p.a. if not specified
2. **RESCIND CONTRACT** - After reasonable notice
3. **FORFEIT DEPOSIT** - If contract permits

Time is of Essence:
- If contract says "time is of essence" → Strict timelines
- If not stated → Reasonable time allowed

## Common Scenarios and Solutions

### Scenario 1: Unregistered Sale Deed

**Facts**: 
- Buyer paid full price
- Seller executed sale deed
- Possession delivered
- But sale deed NOT registered

**Legal Position**:
- Sale is VOID (Section 54 + Section 49 Registration Act)
- No transfer of ownership
- Seller remains legal owner
- Buyer has NO title

**Buyer's Remedies**:
1. **File suit for specific performance** - Compel seller to register deed OR execute fresh registered deed
2. **Claim under Section 53A** - If conditions satisfied (possession + writing + consideration)
3. **Claim damages** - Return of price + interest + compensation

**Risk**: If seller sells to third party and registers deed, third party gets title (unless buyer's claim registered)

### Scenario 2: Agreement to Sell + Possession + Part Payment

**Facts**:
- Agreement to sell executed (not registered)
- Buyer paid 50% price
- Buyer in possession for 3 years
- Buyer constructed boundary wall
- Now seller refuses to execute sale deed

**Legal Position**:
- No ownership transferred (only agreement)
- Section 53A protection available (writing + possession + consideration + act in furtherance)

**Buyer's Remedies**:
1. **File suit for specific performance** - Compel registered sale deed
2. **Rely on Section 53A** - Protect possession against seller's eviction
3. **Claim damages** - If specific performance refused

**Limitation**: 3 years from date fixed for completion OR 3 years from date seller repudiated contract

### Scenario 3: Power of Attorney Sale

**Facts**:
- Seller gives Power of Attorney to Agent
- Agent executes "sale deed" on PoA basis
- Buyer pays full price and takes possession
- Sale deed registered
- But property NOT directly transferred (PoA holder sold)

**Legal Position**:
- **PoA Sale is NOT Sale** (Suraj Lamp & Industries v. State of Haryana - SC 2012)
- Power of Attorney does NOT transfer ownership
- Only principal (owner) can transfer property
- Registration of PoA deed does NOT validate transfer

**Risk**: 
- Seller (principal) can revoke PoA anytime
- Seller can sell property to third party
- Buyer has NO title

**Valid PoA Transaction**:
- PoA holder acts on behalf of principal
- Sale deed executed in NAME of principal (not PoA holder)
- Principal must sign sale deed (or PoA must be irrevocable)

**Buyer's Protection**:
- Insist on sale deed signed by OWNER (not PoA holder)
- Verify PoA is registered and irrevocable
- Take indemnity from principal

### Scenario 4: Circle Rate vs Agreement Value

**Facts**:
- Agreement value: Rs. 50 lakhs
- Circle rate/guidance value: Rs. 70 lakhs
- Buyer pays stamp duty on Rs. 50 lakhs only

**Legal Position**:
- Stamp duty payable on HIGHER of agreement value or circle rate
- Under-stamping attracts penalty

**Consequences**:
- Sub-Registrar may refuse registration OR
- Register with deficiency notice
- Penalty: 2% per month (max 200% of deficit)
- Document inadmissible as evidence until deficiency paid

**Solution**:
- Pay stamp duty on circle rate (Rs. 70 lakhs)
- Avoid litigation/penalty

### Scenario 5: Joint Property Sale by One Co-Owner

**Facts**:
- Property owned by A and B (co-owners)
- A sells entire property to C without B's consent
- C pays full price, gets registered sale deed from A
- B refuses to recognize sale

**Legal Position**:
- Section 7 TPA: Co-owner can transfer only his SHARE
- A can validly sell only his 50% share
- Transfer of B's share VOID (A not entitled to transfer)

**C's Rights**:
- Becomes co-owner with B (owns A's 50% share)
- Cannot claim exclusive ownership
- Can file partition suit to separate shares

**B's Rights**:
- Remains co-owner (50% share intact)
- Can file suit to cancel sale deed qua B's share

**Prevention**:
- Buyer must verify ALL co-owners consent
- All co-owners must execute sale deed

### Scenario 6: Sale During Pending Litigation

**Facts**:
- Property subject to pending partition suit
- During pendency, A sells property to B
- B gets registered sale deed
- Later, court orders partition

**Legal Position**:
- Section 52 TPA: Transfer pending litigation is subject to outcome
- B purchases subject to litigation risk
- If court rules against A, B's purchase fails

**Lis Pendens (Section 52 TPA)**:
```
"During pendency of any suit or proceeding relating to immovable property, 
transfer by any party to the suit does NOT affect rights of other party 
under decree or order."
```

**Effect**:
- B's purchase valid ONLY if A ultimately succeeds
- If A loses, B loses property

**Prevention**:
- Search for litigation (encumbrance certificate, court records)
- Avoid purchasing property under litigation
- If necessary, wait for litigation conclusion

## Integration with Other Protocols

### Criminal Law Integration

**Property Sale Frauds - IPC Offenses**:
- **Cheating (Section 420 IPC)** - Seller conceals material defects, sells without title
- **Forgery (Section 464-471 IPC)** - Forged sale deeds, title documents
- **Criminal Breach of Trust (Section 406 IPC)** - Seller takes price but refuses sale

**Prevention**:
- Title verification
- Document authentication
- Police verification (in high-value transactions)

### Contract Law Integration

**Indian Contract Act 1872 Applicability**:
- Agreement to sell is CONTRACT (governed by Contract Act)
- Sections 10, 23 (valid contract requirements)
- Section 73 (damages for breach)
- Section 74 (liquidated damages ceiling)
- Section 55 (time and place of performance)

### Civil Procedure Integration

**Specific Performance Suit - Procedure**:
- Pecuniary jurisdiction: Based on property value
- Territorial jurisdiction: Where property situated (Section 16 CPC)
- Plaint contents: Order VII CPC
- Limitation: 3 years (Article 54 Limitation Act)
- Decree: Specific performance OR damages (if SP refused)

### Registration Act Integration

**Section 17 Registration Act 1908**:
- Mandatory registration for immovable property sale (Rs. 100+)
- Section 49: Unregistered document cannot affect property
- Section 27: Priority between registered and unregistered documents

### Limitation Act Integration

**Articles Applicable**:
- **Article 54** - Specific performance: 3 years from date fixed for performance
- **Article 113** - Damages for breach of contract: 3 years from breach
- **Article 65** - Possession based on title: 12 years

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Relying on Unregistered Sale Deed
**Mistake**: Buyer pays price, takes possession, but does not register sale deed, thinking possession is sufficient

**Consequence**: No title transferred; seller can sell to third party

**Solution**: ALWAYS register sale deed (mandatory for property Rs. 100+)

### 2. Paying Without Title Verification
**Mistake**: Buyer pays full price based on seller's representation without verifying title documents

**Consequence**: Discovers defect in title later; cannot get refund if seller disappears

**Solution**: Conduct thorough due diligence BEFORE payment (30-40 years chain of title)

### 3. Ignoring Encumbrances
**Mistake**: Buyer does not obtain encumbrance certificate; later discovers mortgage/charge on property

**Consequence**: Property may be attached/sold by creditor

**Solution**: Obtain 15-year encumbrance certificate; verify no pending litigation/mortgage

### 4. PoA Sale Without Owner's Signature
**Mistake**: Buyer accepts sale deed signed by PoA holder (not owner) thinking PoA sale is valid

**Consequence**: Sale invalid (Suraj Lamp case); no title transferred

**Solution**: Insist on owner's signature OR irrevocable registered PoA with principal's acknowledgment

### 5. Under-Valuing to Save Stamp Duty
**Mistake**: Buyer and seller show lower consideration in sale deed to reduce stamp duty

**Consequence**: 
- Penalty for under-stamping (200% of deficit)
- Capital gains tax issue for seller (black money presumption)
- Registration may be refused

**Solution**: Show actual consideration OR circle rate (whichever higher); pay correct stamp duty

### 6. Not Registering Agreement to Sell
**Mistake**: Buyer pays substantial advance (30-50%) but does not register agreement to sell

**Consequence**: If seller sells to third party, third party gets priority (no notice of buyer's claim)

**Solution**: Register agreement to sell if substantial amount paid; creates notice to world

### 7. Accepting Possession Without Verification
**Mistake**: Buyer takes possession without verifying seller's actual possession rights (may be tenant, not owner)

**Consequence**: Discovers later that seller was tenant; no ownership rights

**Solution**: Verify seller's possession documents (title deeds, tax receipts, possession certificate)

### 8. Ignoring Building Approvals
**Mistake**: Buyer purchases property/flat without verifying building approvals, completion certificate

**Consequence**: 
- Illegal construction (may be demolished)
- Cannot get home loan
- Cannot register electricity/water connection

**Solution**: Verify building plan approval, completion certificate, occupancy certificate

### 9. Not Conducting Physical Survey
**Mistake**: Buyer relies on documents alone without physical survey/demarcation

**Consequence**: Discovers encroachment, boundary disputes after purchase

**Solution**: Conduct physical survey; verify boundaries match sale deed description

### 10. Delaying Registration Beyond 4 Months
**Mistake**: Buyer executes sale deed but delays registration beyond 4 months (thinking extension automatic)

**Consequence**: 
- Penalty for late registration (4-8 months)
- Registration refused after 8 months
- Must execute fresh deed

**Solution**: Register within 4 months of execution; if delayed, apply for extension immediately

## Case Law Digest

### 1. Specific Performance - Property is Unique

**Nathulal v. Phoolchand (SC 1969)**

**Facts**: Buyer sought specific performance for purchase of land. Seller argued damages adequate remedy.

**Holding**: 
- "Every piece of land or house property is unique, and no two pieces of land or houses are identical."
- Specific performance is normal remedy for land/building contracts
- Damages inadequate because property has special value to buyer

**Principle**: Specific performance readily granted for immovable property (presumed unique)

**Application**: Buyer can file specific performance suit; court will likely decree performance

---

### 2. Section 53A - Part Performance Doctrine

**Ramesh Hirachand Kundanmal v. Municipal Corporation (SC 1988)**

**Facts**: Buyer took possession under agreement to sell, paid substantial amount, and made constructions. Seller refused to execute sale deed.

**Holding**:
- Section 53A requires "act in furtherance" beyond mere possession
- Construction/substantial investment qualifies as act in furtherance
- Seller cannot evict buyer who has Section 53A protection

**Principle**: "Act in furtherance" means substantial alteration/investment, not mere possession

**Application**: Payment of price + construction = Section 53A protection; seller cannot evict

---

### 3. PoA Sale Invalid

**Suraj Lamp & Industries Pvt. Ltd. v. State of Haryana (SC 2012)**

**Facts**: Property sold through Power of Attorney without owner executing sale deed. Buyer claimed valid sale based on registered PoA deed.

**Holding**:
- "General Power of Attorney (GPA) sale is NOT a sale at all"
- Only registered sale deed by OWNER can transfer title
- PoA holder acts on behalf of principal; cannot transfer property in his own name
- Registration of PoA does not validate transfer

**Principle**: PoA sale does NOT transfer ownership; only registered sale deed by owner valid

**Application**: Insist on sale deed signed by owner (not PoA holder); PoA sale creates no title

---

### 4. Stamp Duty on Circle Rate

**Rajasthan High Court - Multiple Cases**

**Facts**: Sale deeds executed showing lower consideration than circle rate. Registrar demanded stamp duty on circle rate.

**Holding**:
- Stamp duty payable on HIGHER of actual consideration or circle rate/market value
- Under-stamping makes document inadmissible as evidence
- Penalty of 2% per month (max 200%) on deficit

**Principle**: Stamp duty based on circle rate, not agreement value (if circle rate higher)

**Application**: Pay stamp duty on circle rate even if agreement value lower; avoid penalty

---

### 5. Lis Pendens - Section 52 TPA

**Jayaram Mudaliar v. Ayyaswami (SC 1973)**

**Facts**: Property sold during pending partition suit. Buyer claimed bona fide purchaser protection.

**Holding**:
- Section 52 TPA: Transfer during pending litigation subject to outcome
- Doctrine of lis pendens applies from suit filing to decree
- Buyer's rights subject to court's final order

**Principle**: Purchase during litigation is at buyer's risk; subject to court decree

**Application**: Verify no pending litigation (encumbrance certificate); avoid purchasing litigated property

---

### 6. Co-Owner's Sale Invalid Qua Other Co-Owner

**Atma Ram v. Tek Singh (SC 1973)**

**Facts**: One co-owner sold entire property without consent of other co-owners. Buyer claimed entire property.

**Holding**:
- Co-owner can transfer only his UNDIVIDED SHARE
- Transfer of other co-owners' shares VOID (Section 7 TPA)
- Buyer becomes co-owner (with share of selling co-owner only)

**Principle**: Co-owner cannot transfer more than his share; consent of ALL co-owners required for entire property sale

**Application**: Verify ALL co-owners execute sale deed; single co-owner sale transfers only his share

---

### 7. Seller's Duty to Disclose Defects

**Gulam Abbas v. State of U.P. (Allahabad HC 1981)**

**Facts**: Seller sold property without disclosing pending acquisition proceedings. Buyer discovered later that property was being acquired.

**Holding**:
- Section 55(1)(c) TPA: Seller must disclose material defects in title
- Non-disclosure of pending acquisition is material defect
- Buyer entitled to rescind contract and claim damages

**Principle**: Seller has duty to disclose ALL material defects affecting property/title

**Application**: Seller must disclose litigation, acquisition, encumbrances; non-disclosure = rescission right

---

### 8. Time Not of Essence (Unless Specified)

**Saradamani Kandappan v. S. Rajalakshmi (SC 2011)**

**Facts**: Agreement to sell fixed completion date. Buyer delayed payment by 3 months. Seller rescinded contract, claiming time was of essence.

**Holding**:
- Time is NOT of essence in property contracts (unless expressly stated)
- Reasonable delay acceptable
- Seller must give notice and reasonable time before rescinding

**Principle**: Mere specification of date does not make time of essence; reasonable delay excusable

**Application**: If time not expressly of essence, buyer gets reasonable time even after deadline

---

### 9. Unregistered Sale Deed + Possession = No Title

**Mangat Ram v. Sardar Meharban Singh (SC 1987)**

**Facts**: Buyer paid full price, took possession, but sale deed not registered. Seller sold to third party and registered deed. First buyer claimed title based on possession.

**Holding**:
- Section 54 TPA + Section 49 Registration Act: Registration MANDATORY
- Unregistered sale deed creates NO title
- Possession without registration does not defeat registered transferee

**Principle**: Unregistered sale deed is void; possession alone insufficient to create title

**Application**: MUST register sale deed; unregistered deed + possession gives no protection against subsequent registered sale

---

### 10. Specific Performance - Discretionary Remedy

**Kailash Nath v. Prabhu Dayal (SC 2000)**

**Facts**: Buyer sought specific performance. Buyer had delayed payment and not performed his part of contract.

**Holding**:
- Specific performance is DISCRETIONARY remedy (Section 20 Specific Relief Act)
- Buyer must come with clean hands
- If buyer defaulted or not ready and willing to perform, court may refuse specific performance

**Principle**: Specific performance not automatic right; court's discretion based on plaintiff's conduct

**Application**: Buyer must show readiness and willingness to perform; delay/default may disentitle specific performance

## Summary Flowchart

```
PROPERTY SALE TRANSACTION
│
├─ PHASE 1: DUE DILIGENCE (30-40 Years Chain)
│  ├─ Title Verification
│  ├─ Encumbrance Certificate (15 years)
│  ├─ Litigation Search
│  ├─ Physical Survey
│  └─ Seller's Capacity Check
│
├─ PHASE 2: AGREEMENT TO SELL (Optional but Recommended)
│  ├─ Draft Agreement
│  ├─ Pay Advance/Earnest (10-20%)
│  ├─ Register Agreement (if substantial amount paid)
│  └─ Fix Completion Date
│
├─ PHASE 3: SALE DEED EXECUTION
│  ├─ Draft Sale Deed
│  ├─ Pay Stamp Duty (On circle rate if higher)
│  ├─ Execute Before Witnesses (2)
│  └─ Register Within 4 Months (Section 17 Registration Act)
│     ├─ Parties Appear at Sub-Registrar
│     ├─ Document Verification
│     ├─ Signature/Thumb Impression
│     └─ Registration Fee Payment (1%)
│
├─ PHASE 4: POST-REGISTRATION
│  ├─ Collect Registered Sale Deed
│  ├─ Possession Delivery (Symbolic/Actual)
│  ├─ Mutation in Revenue Records
│  ├─ Property Tax Transfer
│  ├─ Society Membership Transfer (if flat)
│  └─ TDS Deduction (1% if consideration >Rs. 50 lakhs)
│
└─ REMEDIES FOR BREACH
   ├─ Buyer's Remedies (Seller Defaulted)
   │  ├─ Specific Performance (3 years - Article 54)
   │  ├─ Damages (Section 73 Contract Act)
   │  └─ Section 53A Protection (if conditions satisfied)
   └─ Seller's Remedies (Buyer Defaulted)
      ├─ Forfeit Earnest/Deposit
      ├─ Damages for Loss of Bargain
      └─ Specific Performance (rarely granted against buyer)
```

## Practical Checklist

### For Buyer - Before Agreement to Sell

- [ ] Verify seller's identity (Aadhaar, PAN, address proof)
- [ ] Verify seller's title (examine 30-40 years chain of title documents)
- [ ] Obtain encumbrance certificate (15 years minimum)
- [ ] Search for litigation (court records, encumbrance certificate)
- [ ] Verify property tax paid up to date
- [ ] Conduct physical survey (boundaries, encroachment check)
- [ ] Verify building approvals (if constructed property)
- [ ] Check circle rate/guidance value (for stamp duty calculation)
- [ ] Verify seller's capacity (major, sound mind, entitled to transfer)
- [ ] Check for co-owners (all must consent)

### For Buyer - Agreement to Sell Stage

- [ ] Draft agreement with all mandatory clauses
- [ ] Specify completion date (time of essence if required)
- [ ] Pay earnest/advance (get receipt)
- [ ] Register agreement (if substantial amount paid)
- [ ] Take symbolic possession (if agreed)
- [ ] Ensure default clauses clear (forfeit/return double earnest)

### For Buyer - Sale Deed Stage

- [ ] Verify all title documents again (before balance payment)
- [ ] Draft sale deed with all mandatory contents
- [ ] Calculate stamp duty (on circle rate if higher than agreement value)
- [ ] Pay stamp duty (e-stamping preferred)
- [ ] Pay balance consideration (get receipt)
- [ ] Execute sale deed before 2 witnesses
- [ ] Register within 4 months (both parties + witnesses appear)
- [ ] Pay registration fee (1% approx)
- [ ] Deduct TDS (1% if consideration >Rs. 50 lakhs)

### For Buyer - Post-Registration

- [ ] Collect registered sale deed (certified copy)
- [ ] Take actual possession (keys, handover)
- [ ] Mutation in revenue records (7/12, RTC, land records)
- [ ] Transfer property tax (municipal corporation)
- [ ] Transfer society membership (if flat)
- [ ] Transfer utility connections (electricity, water, gas)
- [ ] Obtain occupancy certificate (if new construction)
- [ ] Update address in bank records, government records

### For Seller - Before Agreement to Sell

- [ ] Gather all title documents (entire chain)
- [ ] Obtain encumbrance certificate (to show property free)
- [ ] Clear any pending dues (property tax, society charges)
- [ ] Obtain NOC from society (if flat)
- [ ] Obtain NOC from bank (if mortgaged - get loan closure)
- [ ] Verify no litigation pending
- [ ] Disclose all material defects (Section 55(1)(c) duty)

### For Seller - Sale Deed Stage

- [ ] Provide all title documents to buyer
- [ ] Provide identity proof, title documents at registration
- [ ] Execute sale deed before witnesses
- [ ] Appear at Sub-Registrar for registration
- [ ] Deliver possession (keys, handover)
- [ ] Provide mutation documents to buyer
- [ ] Calculate capital gains tax liability
- [ ] Pay capital gains tax (or invest in exemption instruments)
