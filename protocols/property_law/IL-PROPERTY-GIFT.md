# Indian Property Law: Gift of Immovable Property

## Purpose
This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on gift of immovable property under the Transfer of Property Act 1882 (Sections 122-129). It covers essential elements of valid gift, registration requirements, delivery/acceptance, revocation principles, onerous gifts, universal donee, suspended/conditional gifts, Hindu law gift concepts (Hiba, musha, musha-bil-iwaz), and practical considerations for gift transactions.

## Activation Triggers
Use this protocol when:
- Client wants to gift property to family member/third party
- Analyzing validity of gift deed
- Challenge to gift deed (by donor's legal heirs, creditors)
- Revocation of gift (by donor or court)
- Onerous gift acceptance/rejection
- Conditional gift (condition precedent/subsequent)
- Universal donee determination
- Gift vs Will distinction
- Gift vs Sale determination (inadequate consideration)
- Muslim law Hiba (gift) under TPA framework
- Tax planning through gifts (avoiding capital gains)
- Gift to minor/person of unsound mind

## Core Principles

### 1. Section 122: Gift Defined

**"Gift is the transfer of certain existing movable or immovable property made voluntarily and without consideration, by one person (donor), to another (donee), and accepted by or on behalf of the donee."**

Essential Elements of Gift:
1. **TRANSFER** - Of existing property (not future property)
2. **IMMOVABLE OR MOVABLE** - Any property (land, building, goods, shares)
3. **VOLUNTARY** - Free will (no coercion, fraud, undue influence)
4. **WITHOUT CONSIDERATION** - Gratuitous (if consideration present → Sale)
5. **DONOR** - Transferor (owner of property)
6. **DONEE** - Transferee (recipient)
7. **ACCEPTANCE** - By or on behalf of donee
8. **EXISTING PROPERTY** - Present property (not spes successionis - expectancy)

**Key Distinctions:**

| Aspect | Gift | Sale | Will |
|--------|------|------|------|
| **Consideration** | NO consideration (gratuitous) | Price paid | NO consideration |
| **Transfer** | Inter vivos (during lifetime) | Inter vivos | Testamentary (after death) |
| **Acceptance** | Required during donor's lifetime | Implied by payment | NO acceptance (testator dead) |
| **Revocable** | Generally NO (exceptions) | NO | YES (anytime before death) |
| **Registration** | MANDATORY (immovable) | MANDATORY (immovable Rs. 100+) | Not registered (probate/succession) |
| **Delivery** | Required | Required | NA (possession after death) |
| **Time of Transfer** | Immediately on registration | Immediately on registration | After death and probate |

### 2. Section 123: Registration Requirement

**Section 123 TPA + Section 17 Registration Act:**

**Immovable Property:**
```
"For the purpose of making a gift of immovable property, the transfer must 
be effected by a REGISTERED INSTRUMENT signed by or on behalf of the donor, 
and attested by at least TWO WITNESSES."
```

**Registration is MANDATORY** for gift of immovable property (NO exception for value threshold)

**Effect of Non-Registration:**
- Gift deed VOID (Section 49 Registration Act)
- No transfer of ownership
- Donee gets NO rights
- Donor remains owner

**Movable Property:**
- Registration NOT required
- Delivery of possession sufficient (Section 123)

### 3. Essential Requirements for Valid Gift

**Requirement 1: Existing Property Only (Section 124)**

```
Section 124: "The gift of a thing not in existence at the date of the gift is VOID."
```

**Valid:**
- Gift of house (exists)
- Gift of land (exists)
- Gift of specific crops standing on land (exists)

**VOID:**
- Gift of future crops (not yet grown)
- Gift of property to be acquired in future
- Gift of expectancy of succession (spes successionis)

**Requirement 2: Voluntarily Made (Free Consent)**

Gift must be made:
- Without coercion (Section 15 Contract Act)
- Without fraud (Section 17 Contract Act)
- Without undue influence (Section 16 Contract Act)
- Without misrepresentation (Section 18 Contract Act)

If consent vitiated:
- Gift VOIDABLE at donor's option (Section 126)
- Can be rescinded

**Requirement 3: Acceptance by Donee (Section 122)**

**Acceptance Required:**
- During donor's lifetime
- Before or at time of execution (contemporaneous)
- Can be express OR implied

**Express Acceptance:**
- Donee signs gift deed (accepts transfer)
- Common practice: Donee as party to deed

**Implied Acceptance:**
- Donee takes possession
- Donee acts as owner (pays taxes, makes alterations)
- Presumed if deed registered and possession delivered

**Rejection:**
- If donee rejects, no transfer
- Must reject during donor's lifetime
- After acceptance, cannot reject (unless onerous gift)

**Acceptance by Guardian (Donee Minor/Unsound Mind):**
- Guardian can accept on behalf of minor/incapacitated person
- Section 122 proviso: Natural guardian OR court-appointed guardian

**Requirement 4: Transfer Must Be Effected (Delivery)**

**Immovable Property:**
- Registration of gift deed (Section 123)
- Delivery of possession (symbolic or actual)

**Symbolic Delivery:**
- Handing over keys
- Handing over title documents
- Attornment (acknowledgment by occupier)

**Actual Delivery:**
- Physical handover of property
- Donee takes possession

**Registration + Possession:**
- Both required for complete transfer
- Registration alone insufficient (if possession not delivered)
- Possession alone insufficient (if gift not registered)

### 4. Section 126: Gifts May Be Suspended or Revoked

**General Rule: Gift is IRREVOCABLE**

Once gift validly made and accepted:
- Donor CANNOT revoke unilaterally
- Gift cannot be taken back
- Donee becomes absolute owner

**Exceptions (Gift Can Be Revoked/Set Aside):**

**Exception 1: Consent Vitiated (Section 126(a))**
- Coercion, fraud, misrepresentation, undue influence
- Donor can rescind gift
- Must act within reasonable time

**Exception 2: Condition Precedent Fails (Section 126(b))**
- Gift conditional on future event
- Event fails to occur
- Gift fails

Example: "I gift this house to A if he marries within 1 year" - If A does not marry within 1 year, gift fails.

**Exception 3: Donee Predeceases Donor (During Donor's Lifetime)**
- If donee dies before acceptance
- Gift fails (no one to accept)

**Exception 4: Mutual Agreement**
- Donor and donee mutually agree to rescind
- Execute deed of rescission
- Retransfer to donor

**Exception 5: Court Order**
- Creditors' suit to set aside gift (fraudulent transfer)
- Gift to defraud creditors → Can be set aside

**Exception 6: Onerous Gift Rejected (Section 127)**
- Donee rejects onerous gift
- Gift fails

**Section 126 Does NOT Allow:**
- Revocation by donor alone (without donee's consent)
- Revocation for donor's changed circumstances
- Revocation for donee's ingratitude/misconduct

**Muslim Law Exception: Hiba (Gift) Revocability**
- Under Muslim Personal Law, gift (Hiba) by parent to child revocable before delivery
- After delivery: Generally irrevocable (exceptions for parent-child)

## Framework

### Phase 1: Pre-Gift Planning

**Step 1: Donor's Objectives**

Understand purpose:
- Tax planning (avoid capital gains tax)
- Estate planning (distribute property during lifetime)
- Family settlement
- Religious/charitable donation
- Love and affection

**Step 2: Property Valuation**

For immovable property:
- Obtain valuation report
- Determine stamp duty (based on market value or circle rate, whichever higher)

**Step 3: Tax Implications**

**For Donor:**
- **NO Capital Gains Tax** - Gift is not "transfer" for consideration (Section 47 Income Tax Act)
- Exemption from capital gains (even if property appreciated)

**For Donee:**
- **NO Gift Tax** - Gift Tax abolished in India (post-1998)
- But if gift from non-relative → Income taxable under Section 56(2)(x) Income Tax Act
- Exemption: Gifts from relatives (defined list) exempt

**Relatives Defined (Section 56(2)(x)):**
- Spouse
- Brother/sister
- Brother/sister of spouse
- Brother/sister of parents
- Lineal ascendant/descendant of individual or spouse
- Spouse of lineal ascendant/descendant

**If Donee is Non-Relative:**
- Gift of immovable property > Rs. 50,000 → Taxable in donee's hands
- Tax = Fair market value of property as "Income from Other Sources"

**Example:**
- Father gifts house worth Rs. 1 crore to son → NO tax (relative)
- Friend gifts house worth Rs. 1 crore to friend → Rs. 1 crore taxable income for donee

**Step 4: Legal Capacity Check**

**Donor Must Be:**
1. **Competent to Contract** (Section 11 Contract Act)
   - Major (18 years)
   - Sound mind
   - Not disqualified by law

2. **Entitled to Transfer Property** (Section 7 TPA)
   - Owner (sole or co-owner)
   - Authorized agent/guardian/trustee

**Who CANNOT Gift:**
- Minor (gift by minor VOID - Mohori Bibee principle)
- Person of unsound mind
- Person not entitled (non-owner, trespasser)

**Donee Can Be:**
- Any person (including minor)
- Company, trust, society (juristic persons)

### Phase 2: Drafting Gift Deed

**Mandatory Contents:**

**1. TITLE**
   - "GIFT DEED" or "DEED OF GIFT"

**2. DATE AND PLACE**
   - Date of execution
   - Place of execution

**3. PARTIES**
   - **Donor** (Transferor): Full name, age, address, occupation
   - **Donee** (Transferee): Full name, age, address, occupation
   - If donee minor: Guardian's details

**4. RECITALS**
   - Donor's ownership and title
   - Relationship with donee (if any)
   - Motive for gift (love and affection, natural love, consideration of marriage, etc.)

**5. PROPERTY DESCRIPTION (Schedule)**
   - Full address
   - Survey numbers, plot numbers
   - Boundaries (North, South, East, West)
   - Area (sq. ft. / sq. meters / acres)
   - Built-up area (if building)

**6. CONSIDERATION CLAUSE**
   - "Out of natural love and affection"
   - "Without any monetary consideration"
   - Emphasize gratuitous nature

**7. TRANSFER CLAUSE**
   - "Hereby transfer, grant, gift, convey all my right, title, interest, and ownership in the said property described in the Schedule to the Donee absolutely and forever"

**8. DELIVERY OF POSSESSION**
   - "Delivered possession of the said property to the Donee"
   - Donee acknowledges receipt

**9. ACCEPTANCE CLAUSE**
   - "The Donee hereby accepts the gift"
   - Donee's signature

**10. COVENANTS**
   - Donor declares:
     - Absolute owner
     - No encumbrances (mortgage, charge, litigation)
     - Right to gift
     - Property free from liabilities

**11. ATTESTATION**
   - Two witnesses
   - Full name, address, signature

### Phase 3: Stamp Duty and Registration

**Step 1: Stamp Duty Calculation**

**Stamp Duty on Gift Deed:**
- Similar to sale deed (state-specific)
- Based on market value or circle rate/guidance value (whichever higher)
- Rate: 3-8% (varies by state)

**Example (Maharashtra):**
- Property value: Rs. 50 lakhs
- Stamp duty: 5% (for gift to non-relative) OR 3% (for gift to relative)
- Gift to son (relative): Rs. 50 lakhs × 3% = Rs. 1.5 lakhs

**Concessional Stamp Duty (Many States):**
- Gift to blood relative: Reduced rate (2-3%)
- Gift to non-relative: Regular sale deed rate (5-7%)

**Step 2: Registration Procedure**

**Documents Required:**

**Donor Must Produce:**
1. Gift deed (original + 2 copies)
2. Identity proof (Aadhaar, PAN, passport)
3. Address proof
4. Title documents (sale deed, previous chain)
5. Encumbrance certificate (15 years)
6. Tax receipts (property tax paid)
7. NOC (if applicable - society, bank)

**Donee Must Produce:**
1. Identity proof (Aadhaar, PAN, passport)
2. Address proof
3. PAN card (mandatory for registration)

**Registration Steps:**

1. **Book Appointment** - Online slot at Sub-Registrar office
2. **Pay Stamp Duty** - E-stamping OR stamp paper
3. **Appear in Person** - Donor, donee, 2 witnesses
4. **Document Verification** - Sub-Registrar checks documents
5. **Signature/Thumb Impression** - Parties execute deed
6. **Pay Registration Fee** - 1% (approx) of property value
7. **Registration Complete** - Deed entered in register
8. **Collect Certified Copies** - Registered gift deed (1-7 days)

**Limitation for Registration:**
- Section 25 Registration Act: Within 4 MONTHS of execution
- Extension: Additional 4 months (with penalty)
- After 8 months: Registration refused

**Effect of Registration:**
- Transfer of ownership from donor to donee
- Donee becomes absolute owner
- Gift deed admissible as evidence
- Notice to world

### Phase 4: Post-Registration

**Step 1: Delivery of Possession**

Even after registration, deliver possession:
- Physical handover
- Hand over keys, documents
- Donee takes actual possession

**Step 2: Mutation of Records**

Update revenue records:
1. **Revenue Department** - Mutation in land records (7/12, RTC, property card)
2. **Municipal Corporation** - Property tax records
3. **Society** - Membership transfer (if flat)
4. **Utility Providers** - Electricity, water connection transfer

**Step 3: Update PAN/Aadhaar**

Donee updates:
- PAN database (property ownership)
- Aadhaar address (if shifting residence)

## Special Gift Concepts

### 1. Onerous Gift (Section 127)

**Definition:** Gift with attached burden/obligation

**Section 127 TPA:**
```
"If a gift is in the form of a single transfer to the same person of several things 
of which one is, and the others are not, burdened by an obligation, the donee can 
take nothing by the gift unless he accepts it fully."
```

**Example 1:**
- A gifts house + car to B
- House has mortgage debt of Rs. 10 lakhs
- B must accept BOTH (house with debt + car) OR reject BOTH
- Cannot cherry-pick (take car, reject house)

**Example 2:**
- A gifts property worth Rs. 1 crore to B
- Condition: B must pay Rs. 20 lakhs to A's creditor
- B can:
  - Accept fully (take property + pay Rs. 20 lakhs), OR
  - Reject fully (refuse gift)

**All or Nothing Principle:**
- Donee cannot accept beneficial part and reject onerous part
- Must accept entire gift or reject entirely

**Election by Donee:**
- Donee must elect within reasonable time
- If accepts part, deemed to accept all
- If rejects part, deemed to reject all

### 2. Universal Donee (Section 128)

**Section 128:**
```
"Where a gift consists of the donor's whole property, the donee is personally liable 
for all the debts and liabilities of the donor at the time of the gift to the extent 
of the property comprised therein."
```

**Universal Donee = Donee of ENTIRE property**

**Principle:**
- Donor gifts ALL property to donee
- Donee liable for donor's debts (to extent of property value)
- Creditors can sue donee

**Example:**
- A owns property worth Rs. 1 crore (total assets)
- A has debts of Rs. 30 lakhs
- A gifts entire property to son B
- B liable for Rs. 30 lakhs debts (as universal donee)
- Creditors can claim from property

**Limitation:**
- Liability limited to value of property gifted
- Donee not personally liable beyond property value

**Distinction from Onerous Gift:**
- Onerous gift: Specific burden attached to specific property
- Universal donee: General liability for all debts

### 3. Conditional and Contingent Gifts

**A. Condition Precedent**

**Definition:** Gift takes effect ONLY if condition fulfilled

**Example:**
- "I gift this house to A if he completes MBA degree"
- If A completes MBA → Gift takes effect
- If A does not complete MBA → No gift

**Legal Position:**
- Condition must occur before gift takes effect
- If condition fails → Gift fails (reverts to donor/estate)

**B. Condition Subsequent**

**Definition:** Gift already effective, but can be defeated if condition occurs

**Example:**
- "I gift this house to A, but if he sells it within 10 years, gift revoked"
- Gift effective immediately
- But revoked if A sells within 10 years

**Void Conditions (Section 26 Contract Act):**
- Restraint of marriage (void)
- Restraint of trade (void, with exceptions)
- Restraint of legal proceedings (void)
- Uncertain/impossible conditions (void)

**Example (Void):**
- "I gift house to A on condition he never marries" → Condition VOID (restraint of marriage)
- Gift takes effect without condition (A gets house, can marry)

**C. Contingent Gift (Section 124)**

**Definition:** Gift depends on uncertain future event

**Example:**
- "I gift house to A if X dies during my lifetime"
- X's death is uncertain event

**Validity:**
- If contingency clear and possible → Valid
- If contingency impossible → Gift void

### 4. Gift to Unborn Person (Section 13 TPA)

**General Rule: VOID**

Section 13: Transfer to unborn person is VOID (subject to exceptions)

**Exception (Rule of Perpetuity):**
- Transfer for benefit of unborn person valid IF:
  - Transfer is to living person FIRST, and
  - On death of living person, to unborn person
  - Unborn person must come into existence within LIFE INTEREST + 18 years

**Example (Valid):**
- "I gift house to my wife A for her life, and after her death to my grandson B (not yet born)"
- If B born within A's lifetime → Valid
- If B not born by A's death → Gift fails

**Example (Void):**
- "I gift house to my grandson B (not yet born)" - Direct gift to unborn → VOID

### 5. Gift Inter Vivos vs Testamentary Transfer

**Gift Inter Vivos (During Lifetime):**
- Transfer during donor's lifetime
- Acceptance during donor's lifetime
- Irrevocable (once accepted)
- Registered gift deed
- Ownership passes immediately

**Will (Testamentary):**
- Takes effect after testator's death
- No acceptance required (testator dead)
- Revocable anytime before death
- Not registered (probate after death)
- Ownership passes after death + probate

**Distinguishing Test:**
- If transfer intended to take effect during transferor's lifetime → Gift
- If transfer intended to take effect after transferor's death → Will

**Problem:** Donatio Mortis Causa (Gift in Contemplation of Death)
- Gift made in contemplation of imminent death
- Takes effect immediately BUT revocable if donor survives
- Indian law: Treated as gift if registration done (irrevocable)

## Muslim Law Gift Concepts (Hiba)

**Hiba (Gift under Muslim Personal Law):**

Muslim law gift (Hiba) governed by Muslim Personal Law, but TPA applies if:
- Property situated in areas where TPA applicable
- Registration required (Section 123 TPA)

**Essential Elements of Hiba:**

1. **Declaration (Ijab)** - Donor's offer to gift
2. **Acceptance (Qubul)** - Donee's acceptance
3. **Delivery of Possession** - Actual or constructive

**Distinction from TPA Gift:**
- TPA: Registration mandatory (immovable)
- Hiba: Delivery sufficient (traditionally), but registration required by statute

**Types of Hiba:**

**A. Hiba-bil-Iwaz (Gift for Consideration)**
- Gift in exchange for something
- Similar to sale (but called gift)
- Irrevocable

**B. Hiba-ba-Shartul-Iwaz (Gift with Stipulation for Return)**
- Gift with condition of return gift
- If return gift given → Both irrevocable
- If return gift not given → Original gift revocable

**Revocability of Hiba:**

**General Rule:** Hiba irrevocable after delivery

**Exception:** Revocable in following cases:
1. **Parent to Child** - Parent can revoke gift to child (before delivery or after if possession not taken)
2. **Gift Not Delivered** - If possession not delivered, revocable
3. **Gift for Consideration** - Hiba-bil-Iwaz irrevocable

**Musha (Gift of Undivided Share):**
- Gift of undivided share in property
- Valid under Muslim law
- Delivery: Symbolic (joint possession continues)

## Common Scenarios and Solutions

### Scenario 1: Father Gifts Property to Son, Then Changes Mind

**Facts:**
- Father executed registered gift deed gifting house to son
- Possession delivered
- Son living in house for 2 years
- Father now wants property back (financial difficulty)

**Legal Position:**
- Gift validly made (registered + accepted + possession delivered)
- Gift IRREVOCABLE (no exception applies)
- Father cannot unilaterally revoke

**Father's Options:**

**Option 1: Request Son to Re-Gift**
- Request son to voluntarily re-gift property
- Son executes gift deed gifting back to father
- Entirely voluntary (son can refuse)

**Option 2: Purchase Back**
- Father purchases property from son (sale transaction)
- Son agrees to sell for consideration
- Tax implications (capital gains for son)

**Option 3: Court Relief (Difficult)**
- Challenge gift on grounds of coercion/undue influence/fraud
- Burden of proof on father (heavy)
- Unlikely to succeed (2 years passed, possession delivered)

**Son's Position:**
- Absolute owner (gift validly completed)
- No legal obligation to return
- Can refuse father's request

---

### Scenario 2: Gift Deed Executed But Possession Not Delivered

**Facts:**
- Mother executed registered gift deed gifting flat to daughter
- Gift deed registered 1 year ago
- But mother still living in flat (possession not delivered)
- Mother now wants to revoke gift

**Legal Position:**

**Analysis:**
- Gift deed registered (Section 123 satisfied)
- But possession NOT delivered (incomplete transfer)

**Two Views:**

**View 1 (Strict):** Gift incomplete until possession delivered
- Registration alone insufficient
- Donor can revoke before delivery

**View 2 (Liberal):** Registration sufficient (possession formality)
- Once registered, ownership transferred
- Donor's continued possession as licensee/permissive occupier

**Courts Generally Follow View 2:**
- Registration transfers ownership
- Possession delivery is formality (can be enforced)
- Daughter can file suit for possession

**Mother's Position:**
- Difficult to revoke (gift deed registered)
- Can argue non-delivery = incomplete gift
- Better to seek daughter's consent for rescission

**Daughter's Position:**
- File suit for possession (specific performance)
- Compel mother to deliver possession
- Claim ownership based on registered deed

---

### Scenario 3: Gift to Non-Relative vs Relative (Tax Implication)

**Facts:**
- Uncle gifts property worth Rs. 80 lakhs to nephew
- Question: Is nephew taxable?

**Legal Analysis (Income Tax Act Section 56(2)(x)):**

**Nephew as Relative:**
Check if nephew falls within "relative" definition:
- Section 56(2)(x): Nephew NOT in list of relatives
- Therefore, nephew is NON-RELATIVE for IT Act purpose

**Tax Implication:**
- Gift of immovable property > Rs. 50,000 from non-relative → Taxable
- Nephew must pay income tax on Rs. 80 lakhs as "Income from Other Sources"
- Tax rate: As per nephew's income tax slab (say 30% + cess = Rs. 24 lakhs approx)

**Solution:**
- Sale at circle rate/fair market value (instead of gift)
- Uncle pays capital gains tax (may have exemptions - Section 54, 54F)
- Nephew no gift tax (consideration paid)

**If Nephew Were Son:**
- Son is "lineal descendant" → Relative
- Gift exempt from tax in son's hands (NO tax)

### Scenario 4: Gift to Minor

**Facts:**
- Grandfather gifts property to grandson (8 years old)
- Grandfather executes gift deed
- Father (natural guardian) accepts on behalf of grandson

**Legal Position:**

**Validity:**
- Minor can be donee (no restriction)
- Acceptance by natural guardian valid (Section 122 proviso)
- Registration in minor's name with guardian details

**Gift Deed Contents:**
- Donee: Minor grandson (through natural guardian father)
- Father accepts gift on behalf of minor
- Property vests in minor (ownership)
- Father manages as guardian (no ownership)

**Tax Implication:**
- Gift from grandfather (lineal ascendant) to grandson (lineal descendant) → Relative
- NO tax in minor's hands

**Clubbing Provision (Section 64(1A) IT Act):**
- Income from gifted property clubbed with parent's income
- Until minor attains majority (18 years)
- Example: Rental income from gifted house taxed in father's hands

**After Majority:**
- Grandson becomes absolute owner
- Can manage property independently
- Income taxed in grandson's hands

### Scenario 5: Gift Challenged by Creditors

**Facts:**
- A has debts of Rs. 50 lakhs to Bank
- A gifts property worth Rs. 1 crore to son (to avoid creditors)
- Bank challenges gift as fraudulent transfer

**Legal Provisions:**

**Section 53 TPA: Fraudulent Transfer**
```
"Every transfer of immovable property made with intent to defeat or delay creditors 
shall be voidable at the option of any creditor so defeated or delayed."
```

**Essential Elements:**
1. Transfer of property
2. Intent to defeat/delay creditors
3. Creditor defeated or delayed

**Bank's Remedy:**
- File suit to set aside gift
- Prove fraudulent intent
- If succeeded: Gift declared void, property available for creditors

**A's Defense:**
- Gift made out of natural love and affection (no fraudulent intent)
- Debts can be paid from other assets
- Gift not sole asset transfer

**Court's Approach:**
- Examine circumstances:
  - Was A insolvent at time of gift?
  - Did gift render A unable to pay debts?
  - Was gift to close relative (son) suspicious?
  - Temporal proximity (gift immediately before creditor's claim?)

**Likely Outcome:**
- If A has no other assets + gift rendered him insolvent → Gift likely voidable
- If A has sufficient other assets to pay debts → Gift may be upheld

**Universal Donee Principle (Section 128):**
- If gift of ENTIRE property → Son liable for debts (up to property value)
- Bank can claim from son as universal donee

### Scenario 6: Gift vs Sale (Inadequate Consideration)

**Facts:**
- A "sells" property worth Rs. 1 crore to daughter for Rs. 10 lakhs
- Transaction documented as "sale deed"
- Revenue authorities claim it's gift (stamp duty difference)

**Distinction:**

**Sale:** Transfer for consideration (price)
**Gift:** Transfer without consideration

**Test:**
- Is consideration nominal (token) OR genuine price?

**Factors Indicating Gift:**
- Grossly inadequate consideration (Rs. 10 lakhs vs Rs. 1 crore)
- Transferee is close relative (daughter)
- Love and affection expressed

**Factors Indicating Sale:**
- Genuine negotiations
- Market-driven price
- Commercial transaction

**Legal Position:**
- If consideration nominal + close relative → Courts may treat as gift
- If genuine price (even if low) → Sale

**Practical Implication:**
- Gift: Higher stamp duty (5-7% on full value)
- Sale: Stamp duty on consideration (if consideration < circle rate, on circle rate)

**Tax Implication:**
- Gift: No capital gains for donor, gift tax risk for donee (if non-relative)
- Sale: Capital gains tax for seller

**Recommendation:**
- If intent is gift → Execute as gift deed
- If genuine sale → Show fair consideration

### Scenario 7: Conditional Gift - Condition Fails

**Facts:**
- Father gifts property to son on condition: "Son shall not sell property during father's lifetime"
- After 5 years, son sells property
- Father claims gift revoked

**Legal Analysis:**

**Nature of Condition:**
- Condition subsequent (gift already effective, but defeated if condition breached)
- Restraint on alienation

**Validity of Condition:**
- Absolute restraint on alienation = VOID (Section 10 TPA)
- Qualified restraint (during father's lifetime) = Valid? (debatable)

**Section 10 TPA:**
```
"Where property is transferred subject to a condition absolutely restraining the 
transferee from parting with or disposing of his interest, the condition is VOID."
```

**Court's Approach:**
- Absolute restraint (forever) → VOID
- Temporary restraint (limited period/purpose) → May be valid

**In This Case:**
- Restraint during father's lifetime = Reasonable period
- Courts may uphold condition as valid

**Effect of Breach:**
- If condition valid + breached → Gift may revert to father
- Father can file suit to revoke gift and recover property

**Son's Defense:**
- Condition void (restraint on alienation)
- Gift absolute and unconditional

**Likely Outcome:**
- Condition may be held valid (limited restraint)
- Son's sale in breach → Father may recover property

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Not Registering Gift Deed (Immovable Property)
**Consequence:** Gift VOID; no transfer of ownership
**Solution:** ALWAYS register gift deed for immovable property (Section 123)

### 2. Gift Without Donee's Acceptance
**Consequence:** No valid gift (acceptance required)
**Solution:** Obtain express acceptance (donee signs deed) OR implied acceptance (delivery of possession)

### 3. Gift of Future Property
**Consequence:** Gift VOID (Section 124)
**Solution:** Gift only existing property; cannot gift property to be acquired in future

### 4. Not Delivering Possession After Registration
**Consequence:** Incomplete transfer; donor may argue gift not completed
**Solution:** Deliver possession immediately after registration (symbolic or actual)

### 5. Gifting to Defraud Creditors
**Consequence:** Gift voidable (Section 53 TPA); creditors can set aside
**Solution:** Clear all debts before gifting; or gift only surplus property

### 6. Ignoring Tax Implications (Non-Relative Donee)
**Consequence:** Donee taxable on gift value (Section 56(2)(x))
**Solution:** Check if donee is "relative"; if not, consider sale instead

### 7. Onerous Gift Without Clear Acceptance
**Consequence:** Donee may reject onerous part; gift fails
**Solution:** Clearly disclose burden; obtain donee's written acceptance of entire gift

### 8. Gift Deed Without Two Witnesses
**Consequence:** Unattested deed may be challenged; registration may be refused
**Solution:** Ensure two witnesses attest gift deed (Section 123)

### 9. Not Updating Mutation After Gift
**Consequence:** Revenue records show donor as owner; title disputes
**Solution:** Update mutation in revenue records, municipal tax records, society records

### 10. Expecting Revocability (General Gifts)
**Consequence:** Cannot revoke validly made gift (except specific exceptions)
**Solution:** Understand gift is irrevocable; consider will if want revocability
