# Indian Property Law: Easements and Servitudes

## Purpose
This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on easements and servitudes under the Indian Easements Act 1882 (Sections 4-55). It covers essential elements of easements, acquisition by grant/prescription/necessity, rights of way, light and air easements, water rights, termination of easements, licenses vs easements distinction, and practical considerations for property transactions involving easements.

## Activation Triggers
Use this protocol when:
- Client's property has easement (right of way, light, air, drainage)
- Neighbor claiming prescriptive easement (20/30 years use)
- Dispute over access rights/pathway
- Construction blocking neighbor's light or air
- Water flow/drainage issues between properties
- Title verification revealing easement burden
- Creating new easement by deed/agreement
- Termination of easement (abandonment, unity of possession)
- Easement vs license determination
- Quasi-easement in land partitions
- Ancillary rights with property purchase
- Public easement vs private easement
- Extinguishment of easement

## Core Principles

### 1. Section 4: Easement Defined

**"An easement is a right which the owner or occupier of certain land possesses, as such, for the beneficial enjoyment of that land, to do and continue to do something, or to prevent and continue to prevent something being done, in or upon, or in respect of certain other land not his own."**

Essential Elements of Easement:
1. **TWO PROPERTIES** - Dominant heritage (benefited) and Servient heritage (burdened)
2. **BENEFICIAL ENJOYMENT** - For better enjoyment of dominant heritage
3. **RIGHT TO DO OR PREVENT** - Positive (do something) or negative (prevent something)
4. **NOT OWNERSHIP** - Right only, not ownership of servient land
5. **CONTINUES** - Not temporary permission
6. **APPURTENANT** - Attached to land (not personal)

**Key Terminology:**

- **Dominant Heritage/Tenement:** Property benefited by easement
- **Servient Heritage/Tenement:** Property burdened by easement  
- **Dominant Owner:** Owner of dominant heritage
- **Servient Owner:** Owner of servient heritage

**Example:**
- Property A (landlocked) has right of way over Property B to reach public road
- Property A = Dominant heritage (benefited)
- Property B = Servient heritage (burdened)
- Right of way = Easement

**Types of Easements:**

**A. Positive Easements (Right to DO):**
- Right of way (walk, drive over servient land)
- Right to draw water (from well, spring on servient land)
- Right of drainage (discharge water onto servient land)
- Right to use pathway, bridge
- Right to graze cattle

**B. Negative Easements (Right to PREVENT):**
- Right of light (prevent servient owner from blocking light)
- Right of air (prevent blocking air/ventilation)
- Right to lateral support (prevent excavation damaging dominant land)
- Right to prevent construction (beyond certain height)

### 2. Easement vs License

**Critical Distinction:**

| Aspect | Easement | License |
|--------|----------|---------|
| **Interest in Property** | YES (property right) | NO (personal permission) |
| **Transferability** | Transfers with land | NOT transferable |
| **Revocability** | Generally irrevocable | Revocable (Section 60 Easements Act) |
| **Duration** | Permanent (unless terminated) | Temporary |
| **Creation** | Grant, prescription, necessity | Permission/consent |
| **Successor Bound** | Binds successors | Does NOT bind successors |
| **Two Properties Required** | YES (dominant + servient) | NO (licensor's land only) |

**License Defined (Section 52 Easements Act):**
"Where one person grants to another, or to a definite number of other persons, a right to do, or continue to do, in or upon the immovable property of the grantor, something which would, in the absence of such right, be unlawful, and such right does not amount to an easement or an interest in the property, the right is called a LICENSE."

**Example (Easement):**
- A owns Plot 1 (landlocked)
- A has permanent right of way over B's Plot 2 to reach road
- Right transfers when A sells Plot 1
- = EASEMENT

**Example (License):**
- A allows B (neighbor) to park car in A's driveway occasionally
- Permission personal to B
- Revocable by A anytime
- Does not transfer if B sells property
- = LICENSE

### 3. Acquisition of Easements

**Section 15-24: Four Methods**

#### Method 1: By GRANT (Section 15)

**Express Grant:**
- Created by written document
- Deed of easement/grant
- Registered (if immovable property)

**Example:**
- A grants right of way to B (owner of adjoining landlocked plot)
- Execute registered deed granting easement
- Permanent easement created

**Implied Grant:**
- Quasi-easement (Section 15)
- When land divided, existing usage continues as easement
- Example: Single property with pathway to backyard; on partition, pathway becomes easement for backyard owner

**Registration:**
- Easement deed must be REGISTERED if:
  - For period > 1 year, OR
  - Perpetual easement, OR
  - On immovable property
- Section 17 Registration Act 1908

---

#### Method 2: By PRESCRIPTION (Section 15-25)

**Section 15:**
```
"An easement may be acquired by virtue of a local custom and by long and 
uninterrupted enjoyment of the easement."
```

**Prescriptive Easement = Acquisition by Long Use**

**Requirements (ALL Must Be Satisfied):**

1. **CONTINUOUS USE** - Uninterrupted use for required period
2. **OPEN AND PEACEABLE** - Not by force, stealth, or permission
3. **AS OF RIGHT** - Nec vi, nec clam, nec precario (without force, secrecy, permission)
4. **STATUTORY PERIOD** - 20 years (as of right without interruption)

**Section 25 - Prescriptive Period:**
```
"Where the access and use of light or air to and for any building has been 
actually enjoyed therewith for the full period of twenty years, the right to 
such access and use shall be absolute and indefeasible."
```

**20 Years Rule:**
- For light and air: 20 years
- For other easements: 20 years (general rule)

**Exception - Government Land:**
- Section 29: Easement cannot be acquired by prescription against Government land
- 30 years minimum (if at all permissible by statute)

**Evidence of Prescriptive Easement:**
- Witness testimony (long-time residents)
- Photographs showing pathway/usage
- Municipal records, maps
- Property tax documents showing pathway

**Example:**
- Property A owner walking through Property B for 25 years to reach road
- Usage continuous, open, as of right (not by permission)
- After 20 years: Easement acquired by prescription
- B cannot obstruct pathway

**Interruption of Prescription:**
- If servient owner obstructs use for 1 year+ continuously
- Prescription period resets
- Section 17: Interruption for less than 1 year = No effect

---

#### Method 3: By NECESSITY (Section 13)

**Easement of Necessity = Landlocked Property**

**Section 13:**
```
"Where, on the partition of any property, one portion has, and the other has not, 
access to a public way or navigable river, the former portion is subject to an 
easement of way across it in favor of the latter portion to the public way or 
navigable river."
```

**Conditions:**
1. Property landlocked (no access to public road)
2. Necessity arose due to partition/conveyance
3. No alternative access available

**Example:**
- Single property with road access in front
- Divided into front and back portions
- Front portion has road access
- Back portion landlocked
- Easement of way automatically created in favor of back portion across front

**Scope:**
- Necessity route (shortest, least burdensome)
- Cannot claim multiple routes (only one necessary)
- Servient owner can designate route (if reasonable)

**Cessation:**
- If alternative access becomes available
- Easement of necessity terminates

---

#### Method 4: By LOCAL CUSTOM (Section 15)

**Local Custom = Community Practice**

**Conditions:**
1. Custom ancient (from time immemorial)
2. Continuous and certain
3. Reasonable
4. Not against statute

**Example:**
- Village custom allowing all residents to draw water from community well
- Easement by custom

**Rarely Applied:** Modern courts rarely recognize new customs

## Rights and Obligations

### Rights of Dominant Owner (Section 26-40)

**Right 1: Use Easement for Intended Purpose (Section 26)**
- Dominant owner can use easement for purpose granted
- Cannot exceed scope
- Cannot burden servient land beyond original grant

**Example:**
- Right of way granted for pedestrian use
- Dominant owner cannot use for vehicular traffic (excess burden)

**Right 2: Make Necessary Repairs (Section 34)**
- Dominant owner can enter servient land to repair easement
- Must cause least inconvenience
- Example: Repair pathway, clear drainage

**Right 3: Transfer with Property (Section 32)**
- Easement appurtenant (attached to land)
- Automatically transfers with sale/gift of dominant heritage
- Buyer inherits easement rights

### Duties of Dominant Owner (Section 31-33)

**Duty 1: Not Increase Burden (Section 31)**
- Cannot increase burden on servient heritage beyond original grant
- Cannot extend use to other properties
- Example: Right of way for Plot A only; cannot allow Plot C owner to use

**Duty 2: Maintain Easement (Section 33)**
- Dominant owner must maintain easement (if required by grant)
- Pay for repairs (unless grant specifies otherwise)
- Keep pathway/drainage clear

**Duty 3: Use Reasonably (Section 26)**
- Use easement in manner least burdensome to servient owner
- Not cause unnecessary damage

### Rights of Servient Owner (Section 41-45)

**Right 1: Use Servient Heritage (Section 41)**
- Servient owner can use servient land (subject to easement)
- Cannot obstruct easement
- Example: If right of way across corner, servient owner can use rest of land

**Right 2: Alter Route (Section 42)**
- If route becomes inconvenient, servient owner can provide alternative
- Alternative must be equally convenient
- Requires dominant owner's consent OR court approval

**Right 3: Resistance to Excessive Use (Section 44)**
- If dominant owner exceeds scope, servient owner can resist
- File suit for injunction

### Duties of Servient Owner (Section 41-43)

**Duty 1: Not Obstruct Easement (Section 41)**
- Cannot build/plant/excavate obstructing easement
- Must maintain status quo
- Example: Cannot build wall across right of way

**Duty 2: Not Diminish Easement (Section 43)**
- Cannot reduce light/air to dominant property
- Cannot alter condition of servient land adversely affecting easement

## Specific Types of Easements

### 1. Right of Way (Section 13, 15)

**Purpose:** Access from dominant property to public road

**Types:**

**A. Permanent Right of Way:**
- Granted by deed OR acquired by prescription
- Fixed route
- Defines width, mode of use (pedestrian/vehicular)

**B. Easement of Necessity:**
- Arises due to landlocked property
- Shortest route
- Servient owner can designate route (if reasonable)

**Width of Pathway:**
- As specified in grant
- If not specified: Reasonable width for intended use
  - Pedestrian: 3-5 feet
  - Vehicular: 8-12 feet (single lane)

**Mode of Use:**
- Pedestrian only OR
- Vehicular (two-wheeler/four-wheeler)
- Cannot upgrade without consent (e.g., pedestrian to vehicular)

**Maintenance:**
- Usually dominant owner's responsibility
- Grant may specify otherwise

**Common Disputes:**
- Servient owner building gate/wall obstructing path
- Dominant owner widening path without consent
- Vehicular use when grant was for pedestrian

**Remedies:**
- Injunction to remove obstruction
- Mandatory injunction to restore access
- Damages for obstruction

---

### 2. Right of Light and Air (Section 7, 25)

**Section 7:**
```
"Where, by the exercise of a right in or over immovable property, light or 
air is in fact withdrawn from other immovable property, the withdrawal is 
not actionable unless the right is expressly granted or acquired by 
prescription."
```

**Key Principle:**
- NO natural right to light/air
- Must be acquired by grant OR prescription (20 years)

**Acquisition by Prescription (Section 25):**
- Building enjoyed light/air for 20 years uninterruptedly
- Right becomes absolute and indefeasible
- Neighbor cannot obstruct thereafter

**Measurement:**
- Light and air measured at the plane of windows/openings
- Not entire property

**Obstruction:**
- Neighbor building structure blocking light = Actionable (if 20-year right established)
- Remedy: Injunction to demolish OR damages

**Ancient Lights Doctrine:**
- If building enjoyed light for 20+ years
- Neighbor cannot build obstructing light
- Applies to existing openings (not new windows)

**Example:**
- A's house has windows facing east (towards B's land)
- A's house standing for 25 years with windows enjoying light
- B wants to build 3-story building blocking A's light
- A can claim easement of light (20-year prescription)
- Injunction against B's construction

**Limitations:**
- Right extends to light/air actually enjoyed
- Cannot claim light/air over entire plot
- Reasonable obstruction permissible (if does not substantially reduce light)

---

### 3. Right of Water (Section 7A-7D)

**Types:**

**A. Right to Draw Water:**
- Dominant owner can draw water from well/spring on servient land
- Amount: As specified in grant OR reasonable use

**B. Right to Flow Water:**
- Discharge water (rain, waste) onto servient land
- Must follow natural flow OR grant

**C. Right to Irrigate:**
- Use water from servient owner's source for irrigation
- Quantity and timings as per grant

**Riparian Rights (Not Easement):**
- Owner of land abutting river/stream has right to use water
- Natural flow right (not easement)
- Governed by common law

**Surface Water:**
- Natural drainage flow cannot be obstructed
- Lower land must accept natural flow from higher land

**Example:**
- A's property at higher level
- Rainwater naturally drains to B's property (lower level)
- B cannot obstruct natural drainage
- A has natural right (not easement)

---

### 4. Right of Lateral Support (Section 7E)

**Definition:** Right to have one's land supported by adjoining land

**Natural Right (Not Easement):**
- Every landowner has natural right to lateral support from adjoining land
- Neighbor cannot excavate causing subsidence/collapse

**Example:**
- A excavates on his land near boundary
- A's excavation causes B's land to collapse/subside
- A liable for damages (breach of natural right)

**Artificial Support (Buildings):**
- Support to building (not land) is NOT natural right
- Must be acquired by grant or prescription

**Remedies:**
- Injunction to stop excavation
- Mandatory injunction to restore support
- Damages for collapse

## Termination of Easements (Section 46-55)

### Method 1: Release (Section 46)

**Voluntary Surrender:**
- Dominant owner releases easement (in writing)
- Executed release deed
- Easement extinguished

**Example:**
- A has right of way over B's land
- A finds alternative access
- A executes release deed releasing easement
- Easement terminated

---

### Method 2: Unity of Ownership (Section 47)

**Merger Doctrine:**
- When dominant and servient heritages come into same ownership
- Easement extinguished by merger

**Example:**
- Property A has right of way over Property B
- A purchases B (both properties now owned by A)
- Easement merged and extinguished

**Revival:**
- If properties later separated (sold to different owners)
- Easement does NOT automatically revive
- Must be re-created by grant or prescription

---

### Method 3: Permanent Alteration (Section 48)

**Destruction of Servient Heritage:**
- If servient property destroyed (earthquake, flood)
- Easement extinguished

**Permanent Alteration Making Easement Impossible:**
- Example: Right of way path submerged due to reservoir construction
- Easement terminates

---

### Method 4: Abandonment (Section 50)

**Non-Use + Intent to Abandon:**
- Dominant owner must:
  - Cease using easement for long period, AND
  - Demonstrate intent to abandon

**Mere Non-Use Insufficient:**
- 20-year non-use does not automatically extinguish
- Must prove abandonment intent

**Evidence of Abandonment:**
- Blocking own access to servient land
- Building structure making easement unusable
- Written statement of no further use

---

### Method 5: Limitation (Section 51)

**20-Year Rule:**
- If easement not exercised for 20 years continuously
- Easement extinguished by limitation

**Applies Only If:**
- Servient owner obstructed AND dominant owner acquiesced
- Dominant owner did not challenge obstruction for 20 years

**Example:**
- B blocks A's right of way with wall
- A does not object or file suit for 20 years
- Easement extinguished by limitation

---

### Method 6: Expiry of Purpose (Section 49)

**Easement Created for Specific Purpose:**
- When purpose achieved or becomes impossible
- Easement terminates

**Example:**
- Easement granted for construction (temporary access for construction vehicles)
- Construction completed
- Easement expires

## Common Scenarios and Solutions

### Scenario 1: Landlocked Property - Claiming Easement of Necessity

**Facts:**
- A owns Plot 1 (back portion)
- B owns Plot 2 (front portion with road access)
- Both plots were single property earlier (partitioned 5 years ago)
- A has no access to road except through B's Plot 2
- B refuses to allow access

**Legal Analysis:**

**Section 13 - Easement of Necessity:**
- Plot 1 landlocked due to partition
- No alternative access
- Easement of necessity arises automatically

**A's Rights:**
- File suit for declaration and injunction
- Court will grant easement of necessity
- Route: Shortest and least burdensome to B

**B's Position:**
- Cannot deny access (statutory easement)
- Can suggest alternative route (if equally convenient)

**Remedies:**
- Declaration of easement of necessity
- Injunction restraining B from obstructing
- Mandatory injunction to remove obstruction (if built)

---

### Scenario 2: Prescriptive Right of Way - 20-Year Use

**Facts:**
- A's property accessed via pathway through B's property
- A using pathway for 22 years continuously
- B now wants to block pathway (building wall)
- A claims prescriptive easement

**Legal Analysis:**

**Section 15 + 25 - Prescription:**
- 20-year continuous use required
- Use must be as of right (nec vi, nec clam, nec precario)

**A Must Prove:**
1. **Continuous use** for 22 years (uninterrupted)
2. **Open and peaceable** (not by force or stealth)
3. **As of right** (not by permission/license)

**B's Defense:**
- Use was permissive (license, not easement)
- Interruption for 1+ year (breaks prescription)
- A used alternate route intermittently

**Evidence:**
- Witnesses (long-time residents)
- Photographs
- Tax records, maps showing pathway

**Likely Outcome:**
- If A proves 20+ years as of right → Prescriptive easement
- B cannot obstruct
- Injunction against wall construction

---

### Scenario 3: Light and Air - Neighbor's Construction Blocking Windows

**Facts:**
- A's house built in 1995 with east-facing windows
- A enjoyed light/air through windows for 30 years
- B (neighbor) wants to build 4-story building in 2025 blocking A's windows
- A objects

**Legal Analysis:**

**Section 25 - Prescription (Light/Air):**
- A's building enjoyed light for 30 years (>20 years required)
- Absolute and indefeasible right acquired
- B cannot obstruct

**A's Remedies:**
- Injunction to restrain B's construction
- If already built: Mandatory injunction to demolish/alter

**B's Defense:**
- Right limited to light actually enjoyed (not all light)
- Reasonable obstruction permissible
- A can still receive adequate light

**Court's Approach:**
- Measure light before and after construction
- If substantial diminution → Injunction granted
- If minor reduction → May permit with damages

**Quantum of Light:**
- Courts apply "reasonable light" test
- Not absolute darkness required for injunction
- Substantial obstruction sufficient

---

### Scenario 4: Right of Way - Excessive Use (Vehicular vs Pedestrian)

**Facts:**
- A granted right of way to B in 1990 (deed specifies "pedestrian access")
- B now using path for heavy vehicles (trucks)
- Pathway damaged, A's property disturbed
- A claims excessive use

**Legal Analysis:**

**Section 31 - Not Increase Burden:**
- Easement scope defined by grant (pedestrian)
- B cannot upgrade to vehicular use
- Excessive burden on servient heritage

**A's Remedies:**
- Injunction to restrain vehicular use
- Limit use to pedestrian only
- Damages for damage caused

**B's Defense:**
- Usage evolved with needs
- Reasonable expansion
- Vehicular access necessary (no alternative)

**Court's Approach:**
- Strict interpretation of grant
- Pedestrian ≠ Vehicular
- A entitled to injunction
- B must seek permission OR use alternative route

---

### Scenario 5: Easement vs License - Neighbor's Parking Permission

**Facts:**
- A allowed B to park car in A's driveway for 2 years
- No written agreement
- A now wants to revoke permission
- B claims easement acquired

**Legal Analysis:**

**Easement vs License:**
- No grant deed (no written easement)
- Permission appears to be license (personal, revocable)
- 2 years < 20 years (prescription not satisfied)

**License Characteristics Present:**
- Personal permission to B
- No two properties (B parking in A's land)
- Revocable

**Section 60 Easements Act - License Revocable:**
- Grantor can revoke license anytime (unless coupled with interest)
- Notice required (reasonable)

**A's Rights:**
- Revoke license (give reasonable notice)
- File suit for injunction if B refuses

**B's Defense:**
- Claim prescriptive easement (fails - only 2 years)
- Claim irrevocable license (unlikely - no consideration)

**Outcome:**
- License, not easement
- A can revoke
- B must stop parking

---

### Scenario 6: Unity of Ownership - Easement Extinguished

**Facts:**
- Property A had right of way over Property B
- A purchased B in 2020 (both properties now owned by A)
- A sold B to C in 2025
- C blocks pathway claiming no easement exists

**Legal Analysis:**

**Section 47 - Unity of Ownership:**
- When A owned both properties → Easement merged and extinguished
- On sale to C → Easement did NOT automatically revive

**C's Position:**
- Easement extinguished by merger
- No easement burden on C's property

**A's Position:**
- Easement implied by quasi-easement (if existing use continued)
- Section 15 quasi-easement exception

**Quasi-Easement (Section 15):**
- If pathway continued to be used during unified ownership
- On partition/sale, quasi-easement converts to easement

**Likely Outcome:**
- If A continued using pathway during unified ownership → Quasi-easement
- C's property subject to easement (implied)
- If pathway abandoned during unified ownership → No easement

---

### Scenario 7: Drainage - Natural Flow Obstruction

**Facts:**
- A's property at higher level
- Rainwater naturally flows to B's property (lower level)
- B built wall blocking natural drainage
- Water stagnates on A's property

**Legal Analysis:**

**Natural Drainage Right (Not Easement):**
- Natural flow of surface water cannot be obstructed
- Lower land must accept natural flow from higher land
- B has no right to obstruct

**A's Remedies:**
- Injunction to remove wall
- Mandatory injunction to restore drainage
- Damages for water stagnation/damage

**B's Defense:**
- Right to build on own land
- A's drainage excessive (not natural)

**Court's Approach:**
- Natural flow protected (B cannot obstruct)
- If A's drainage artificial (from construction) → Different analysis
- B may divert, not obstruct entirely

## Integration with Other Protocols

### Property Sale Integration

**Due Diligence:**
- Verify easement burdens (encumbrance certificate)
- Check title documents for grants
- Physical inspection for pathways, encroachments

**Sale Deed Disclosure:**
- Seller must disclose easement burdens
- Buyer takes subject to existing easements
- Non-disclosure = Misrepresentation (rescission, damages)

### Registration Act Integration

**Section 17 - Easement Registration:**
- Easement for >1 year OR perpetual → Registration mandatory
- Unregistered easement: Inadmissible as evidence

**Section 27 - Priority:**
- Registered easement binds subsequent buyers
- Constructive notice to world

### Civil Procedure Integration

**Injunction Suits (Order XXXIX CPC):**
- Temporary injunction to restrain obstruction
- Mandatory injunction to remove obstruction
- Suit for declaration and permanent injunction

**Jurisdiction:**
- Where servient heritage situated (Section 16 CPC)

### Limitation Act Integration

**Article 65 - Possession Suits:**
- 12 years from accrual
- Applies to easement obstruction suits

**20-Year Prescription:**
- Section 25 Easements Act: 20 years uninterrupted use

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Assuming Natural Right to Light/Air
**Consequence:** No actionable right if not acquired by grant/prescription
**Solution:** Verify 20-year enjoyment before claiming easement of light/air

### 2. Creating Oral Easement (Immovable)
**Consequence:** Unenforceable (registration required)
**Solution:** Execute registered deed for permanent easements

### 3. Permissive Use Claimed as Prescriptive Easement
**Consequence:** License ≠ Easement; no prescription for permissive use
**Solution:** Use must be as of right (not by permission) for prescription

### 4. Ignoring Easement Burdens in Property Purchase
**Consequence:** Buyer takes subject to easements; no recourse
**Solution:** Check encumbrance certificate, physical inspection

### 5. Blocking Easement Without Legal Action
**Consequence:** Possible contempt, damages
**Solution:** File suit for termination/modification; don't self-help

### 6. Exceeding Scope of Easement
**Consequence:** Dominant owner liable; injunction, damages
**Solution:** Adhere to grant terms; seek variation if needed

### 7. Not Maintaining Easement (Dominant Owner)
**Consequence:** Damage to servient land; liability
**Solution:** Regular maintenance as per grant terms

### 8. Claiming Easement Against Government Land (Prescription)
**Consequence:** Cannot acquire prescriptive easement against Government
**Solution:** Seek formal grant/permission

### 9. Relying on Unity of Ownership to Extinguish Easement
**Consequence:** Quasi-easement may revive on re-partition
**Solution:** Execute release deed if intend to extinguish permanently

### 10. Not Documenting Easement Alteration
**Consequence:** Disputes over changed route/terms
**Solution:** Execute variation deed; register modifications
