# Indian Property Law: Mortgage of Immovable Property

## Purpose
This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on mortgage of immovable property under the Transfer of Property Act 1882 (Sections 58-104). It covers the 6 types of mortgages (simple, conditional sale, usufructuary, English, equitable, anomalous), essential elements, rights and duties of mortgagor and mortgagee, redemption rights, foreclosure, sale procedures, subrogation, marshalling, contribution, and practical considerations for mortgage transactions.

## Activation Triggers
Use this protocol when:
- Client wants to mortgage property for loan/debt security
- Analyzing mortgage deed validity or classification
- Mortgage default/breach by mortgagor (non-payment)
- Mortgagee wants to enforce security (sale/foreclosure)
- Redemption suit by mortgagor (paying off debt)
- Priority disputes between multiple mortgagees
- Subrogation claims by subsequent purchaser/mortgagee
- Marshalling of securities application
- Contribution between co-mortgagors
- Equitable mortgage creation (deposit of title deeds)
- Mortgage by deposit without written document
- Tacking of further advances to existing mortgage

## Core Principles

### 1. Section 58: Mortgage Defined

**"Mortgage is the transfer of an interest in specific immovable property for the purpose of securing the payment of money advanced or to be advanced by way of loan, an existing or future debt, or the performance of an engagement which may give rise to a pecuniary liability."**

Essential Elements of Mortgage:
1. **TRANSFER OF INTEREST** - In immovable property (not ownership)
2. **SPECIFIC PROPERTY** - Identified property (not general assets)
3. **PURPOSE** - Securing debt/loan/engagement
4. **DEBT** - Existing, future, or contingent
5. **TRANSFEROR** - Mortgagor (borrower/debtor)
6. **TRANSFEREE** - Mortgagee (lender/creditor)

**Key Distinction:**

| Aspect | Sale | Mortgage |
|--------|------|----------|
| **Transfer** | Ownership | Interest (security) |
| **Purpose** | Absolute transfer | Security for debt |
| **Redemption** | No right to get back | Right to redeem |
| **Price vs Debt** | Price paid | Debt secured |
| **Permanent** | Yes | Temporary (until debt repaid) |

**Mortgage vs Charge:**

| Aspect | Mortgage | Charge |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **Interest** | Transfer of interest | NO transfer |
| **Right** | Right in rem | Right in personam |
| **Remedy** | Sale of property | Court-ordered sale only |
| **Creation** | Transfer deed | Agreement/operation of law |

### 2. Section 58: Six Types of Mortgages

#### Type 1: SIMPLE MORTGAGE (Section 58(b))

**Definition**: Mortgagor binds himself personally to pay debt and agrees that in default, mortgagee can sell property, but WITHOUT delivering possession.

**Essential Features:**
1. Personal liability of mortgagor (covenant to pay)
2. Agreement that mortgagee can cause sale in default
3. NO delivery of possession
4. NO power of sale without court intervention

**Example**: A borrows Rs. 10 lakhs from B, executes simple mortgage deed creating charge on property. A retains possession. If A defaults, B must file suit for sale.

**Remedy for Mortgagee:**
- File suit for SALE of property (Section 67 TPA)
- Obtain preliminary decree
- Obtain final decree for sale
- Property sold through court

**Advantages:**
- Mortgagor retains possession and use of property
- No risk of extrajudicial foreclosure

**Disadvantages:**
- Lengthy court process for mortgagee
- Cannot take immediate possession

---

#### Type 2: MORTGAGE BY CONDITIONAL SALE (Section 58(c))

**Definition**: Mortgagor ostensibly sells property on condition that:
- On default, sale becomes absolute, OR
- On payment, sale becomes void, OR
- On payment, buyer re-transfers to seller

**Essential Features:**
1. Ostensible sale (appears to be sale)
2. Condition of re-transfer on payment
3. NO delivery of possession (usually)
4. Distinguishable from actual sale by redemption right

**Test to Distinguish from Sale:**
- If transaction intended as SECURITY for debt → Mortgage by conditional sale
- If transaction intended as ABSOLUTE transfer → Sale with repurchase condition

**Example**: A needs Rs. 20 lakhs urgently. Sells property to B for Rs. 20 lakhs with agreement that if A repays within 5 years, B will re-transfer property.

**Remedy for Mortgagee:**
- In default: Claim absolute ownership (no court sale needed)
- But courts apply Section 60 redemption right (mortgagor can redeem even after default)

**Advantages:**
- Appears simpler (like sale)
- Mortgagee claims ownership on default

**Disadvantages:**
- Courts scrutinize heavily (to protect mortgagor)
- Redemption right cannot be excluded
- Potential for dispute over nature (sale vs mortgage)

---

#### Type 3: USUFRUCTUARY MORTGAGE (Section 58(d))

**Definition**: Mortgagor delivers possession or expressly/impliedly binds himself to deliver possession, and mortgagee entitled to receive rents and profits in lieu of interest or in payment of debt or both.

**Essential Features:**
1. Delivery of POSSESSION to mortgagee
2. Mortgagee receives rents/profits
3. NO personal liability of mortgagor (usually)
4. NO power of sale to mortgagee
5. Debt extinguished when rents/profits = debt amount

**Example**: A borrows Rs. 5 lakhs from B, delivers possession of house. B collects rent of Rs. 25,000/month. Debt reduces by rental amount until fully paid.

**Duration:**
- Usually fixed period (e.g., 10 years)
- Or until debt paid from rents/profits

**Remedy for Mortgagee:**
- Possession and enjoyment
- Collect rents and profits
- If mortgagor obstructs possession: File suit for possession

**Remedy for Mortgagor:**
- Redeem mortgage after period/payment complete
- File suit for redemption (Section 60)

**Advantages:**
- Mortgagee gets possession and income
- No court process needed for possession

**Disadvantages:**
- Mortgagor loses possession and use
- Recovery slow (depends on rental income)

---

#### Type 4: ENGLISH MORTGAGE (Section 58(e))

**Definition**: Mortgagor binds himself to repay debt on certain date and transfers property absolutely to mortgagee, subject to proviso that mortgagee will re-transfer on payment.

**Essential Features:**
1. ABSOLUTE transfer of property to mortgagee
2. Condition of re-transfer on payment by certain date
3. Personal liability to repay
4. Mortgagee becomes owner (subject to redemption)
5. Possession may or may not be delivered

**Example**: A borrows Rs. 50 lakhs from Bank B, executes mortgage deed transferring ownership absolutely to Bank, with condition that on repayment within 15 years, Bank will re-transfer.

**Distinguishing Features:**
- Similar to mortgage by conditional sale
- BUT English mortgage has absolute transfer FIRST, then condition of re-transfer
- Conditional sale has apparent sale with condition making it void

**Remedy for Mortgagee:**
- On default: Mortgagee is already owner
- File suit for foreclosure (to extinguish redemption right)
- OR sell property and recover debt

**Advantages:**
- Strong security (mortgagee is owner)
- Easy enforcement

**Disadvantages:**
- Mortgagor loses ownership (only equity of redemption remains)
- Requires careful drafting to distinguish from sale

---

#### Type 5: MORTGAGE BY DEPOSIT OF TITLE DEEDS / EQUITABLE MORTGAGE (Section 58(f))

**Definition**: In certain towns (notified), delivery of title documents to creditor with intent to create security, without written mortgage deed.

**Essential Features:**
1. DEPOSIT of title deeds (original documents)
2. INTENT to create security (proved by circumstances)
3. NO written mortgage deed required
4. Applicable ONLY in notified towns (Presidency towns + notified areas)
5. Debt may be existing or future

**Notified Towns (Examples):**
- Calcutta (Kolkata)
- Madras (Chennai)
- Bombay (Mumbai)
- Delhi
- Pune
- Bangalore
- Hyderabad
- Ahmedabad
- + many others (check state notifications)

**Example**: A wants loan of Rs. 15 lakhs from Bank B in Mumbai. Deposits original sale deed, previous title documents with bank. Bank disburses loan. No mortgage deed executed.

**Proof of Intent:**
- Loan agreement mentioning deposit as security
- Receipt of title documents acknowledging security purpose
- Correspondence showing intent

**Registration:**
- NOT required (no written instrument)
- But bank may register memorandum of deposit

**Advantages:**
- Simple, quick, cost-effective
- No stamp duty on mortgage deed
- No registration fee
- Popular with banks

**Disadvantages:**
- Available only in notified towns
- Difficult to prove intent if documents missing
- Priority issues with registered mortgages

**Remedy for Mortgagee:**
- Same as simple mortgage: File suit for SALE (Section 67)
- Cannot take possession without court order

---

#### Type 6: ANOMALOUS MORTGAGE (Section 58(g))

**Definition**: Mortgage combining features of two or more types, OR not falling under any of the above five types.

**Essential Feature:**
- Hybrid nature (combination of features)

**Example 1**: Simple mortgage + Delivery of possession = Anomalous (combines simple + usufructuary features)

**Example 2**: Conditional sale + Power of sale without court = Anomalous

**Remedy:**
- Determined by mortgage deed terms
- Courts apply principles of all types involved

### 3. Rights and Duties of Mortgagor

#### A. Rights of Mortgagor

**Right 1: RIGHT TO REDEEM (Section 60) - EQUITY OF REDEMPTION**

Most Fundamental Right - CANNOT BE EXCLUDED by contract

```
Section 60: "At any time after the principal money has become due, 
the mortgagor has right to redeem the mortgage"
```

**Essential Features:**
1. Right arises when debt becomes due
2. Cannot be excluded by clog/fetter on equity
3. Exercisable by payment of entire debt + interest + costs
4. Available to mortgagor, his heirs, assignees

**Clog on Equity of Redemption (VOID):**
- Stipulation making redemption impossible/difficult
- Irredeemable mortgage (void after debt due)
- Unconscionable terms preventing redemption

**Example of Clog (VOID)**: Mortgage deed says "Redemption allowed only if paid in gold coins" - VOID

**Method of Redemption:**
- Tender of entire debt + interest + costs
- File suit for redemption (if mortgagee refuses)
- Obtain redemption decree and pay into court

**Right 2: RIGHT TO LEASE (Section 65A)**

During subsistence of mortgage:
- Simple mortgage: Mortgagor can lease (possession with him)
- Usufructuary/English mortgage: Mortgagee can lease (possession with mortgagee)

Restrictions:
- Lease should not prejudice mortgagee's interest
- Lease beyond mortgage term may be invalid

**Right 3: RIGHT TO ACCESSIONS (Section 63)**

Mortgagor entitled to:
- Accretions to property (alluvion, new land)
- Improvements made by mortgagor

**Right 4: RIGHT TO INSPECT DOCUMENTS (Section 60(c))**

Mortgagor can:
- Inspect title documents in mortgagee's custody
- Obtain certified copies at his cost

**Right 5: RIGHT TO REDEEM LATER MORTGAGES (Section 92)**

If property has multiple mortgages:
- Mortgagor can redeem later mortgage without redeeming earlier
- Prevents junior mortgagee from consolidating position

#### B. Duties of Mortgagor

**Duty 1: PAY DEBT (Primary Obligation)**

Mortgagor must:
- Pay principal amount on due date
- Pay interest as agreed
- Pay costs of mortgage

**Duty 2: NOT COMMIT WASTE (Section 65)**

Mortgagor cannot:
- Destroy/damage property
- Alter property substantially
- Commit acts reducing property value

**Duty 3: INSURE PROPERTY (If Agreed)**

If mortgage deed requires:
- Insure property against fire, earthquake, etc.
- Premium paid by mortgagor
- Policy assigned to mortgagee

**Duty 4: PAY TAXES (Unless Otherwise Agreed)**

Mortgagor responsible for:
- Property tax
- Land revenue
- Other statutory dues

**Duty 5: DEFEND TITLE (Section 62)**

If third party claims property:
- Mortgagor must defend title
- Indemnify mortgagee if title defeated

### 4. Rights and Duties of Mortgagee

#### A. Rights of Mortgagee

**Right 1: RIGHT TO SUE FOR DEBT (Section 67)**

If mortgagor defaults:
- File suit for recovery of debt (personal liability)
- Without disturbing mortgage security
- Limitation: 12 years from due date (Article 132)

**Right 2: RIGHT TO SUE FOR SALE (Section 69)**

Applicable to:
- Simple mortgage
- Equitable mortgage (deposit of title deeds)

Procedure:
1. File suit for sale (Section 67 TPA + Order XXXIV CPC)
2. Preliminary decree (declaring debt, ordering redemption in 6 months)
3. If not redeemed: Final decree for sale
4. Court-conducted sale
5. Sale proceeds applied to debt

**Right 3: RIGHT TO FORECLOSURE (Section 67)**

Applicable to:
- English mortgage
- Mortgage by conditional sale

**Foreclosure**: Extinguishing mortgagor's right to redeem

Procedure:
1. File suit for foreclosure
2. Preliminary decree (declaring debt, ordering redemption in 6 months)
3. If not redeemed: Final decree of foreclosure
4. Mortgagor's equity of redemption extinguished
5. Mortgagee becomes absolute owner

**Strict Foreclosure**: Property value > Debt
- Mortgagee keeps entire property (no refund of excess)
- Harsh remedy (courts reluctant)

**Right 4: RIGHT TO POSSESSION**

Depends on mortgage type:
- Usufructuary/English mortgage: Mortgagee entitled to possession
- Simple/Equitable mortgage: NO right to possession without court order

**Right 5: RIGHT TO SUBSEQUENT MORTGAGEE'S SALE PROCEEDS (Section 92)**

If property sold by subsequent mortgagee:
- Prior mortgagee entitled to payment from sale proceeds (priority)

**Right 6: RIGHT TO ACCESSIONS (Section 63)**

Mortgagee's security extends to:
- Accretions to property
- Improvements made by mortgagee

**Right 7: RIGHT TO INSURANCE MONEY**

If property destroyed and insured:
- Insurance proceeds applied to mortgage debt
- Mortgagee's security transfers to insurance money

**Right 8: RIGHT TO SALE OF MORTGAGED PROPERTY**

If mortgage deed confers power of sale:
- Sell property WITHOUT court intervention
- Common in bank mortgages, institutional loans

Requirements:
- Default by mortgagor
- Notice to mortgagor (30-60 days)
- Sell at fair market value
- Account for sale proceeds

**Right 9: RIGHT TO APPOINT RECEIVER**

If property income at risk:
- Court may appoint receiver to collect rents
- Receiver protects property and income for mortgagee

#### B. Duties of Mortgagee

**Duty 1: RETURN TITLE DOCUMENTS (Section 60(c))**

On redemption:
- Return all title documents to mortgagor
- Provide receipts, accounts

**Duty 2: RE-TRANSFER PROPERTY (Section 60(b))**

On redemption of English mortgage/conditional sale:
- Execute re-transfer deed (at mortgagor's cost)
- Deliver possession if taken

**Duty 3: ACCOUNT FOR RENTS/PROFITS**

In usufructuary mortgage:
- Account for rents and profits received
- Apply to debt reduction

**Duty 4: MANAGE PROPERTY PROPERLY**

If in possession:
- Maintain property
- Not commit waste
- Not make unauthorized alterations

**Duty 5: SELL AT FAIR VALUE (If Power of Sale)**

If selling under power of sale:
- Advertise properly
- Sell at fair market value
- Account for sale proceeds
- Return surplus to mortgagor

## Framework

### Phase 1: Creating Mortgage

**Step 1: Determine Mortgage Type**

Based on requirements:
- Need loan + retain possession? → Simple mortgage OR Equitable mortgage
- Lender wants possession + income? → Usufructuary mortgage
- Bank loan secured by title deposit? → Equitable mortgage
- Absolute security to lender? → English mortgage

**Step 2: Draft Mortgage Deed**

**Mandatory Contents:**

1. **PARTIES**
   - Mortgagor (borrower) - Full name, address, age
   - Mortgagee (lender) - Full name, address

2. **RECITALS**
   - Purpose of loan
   - Mortgagor's ownership of property

3. **PROPERTY DESCRIPTION**
   - Schedule with survey numbers, boundaries, area

4. **DEBT DETAILS**
   - Principal amount
   - Interest rate (simple/compound)
   - Repayment schedule
   - Due date

5. **MORTGAGE CLAUSE**
   - Type of mortgage (simple, English, etc.)
   - Security interest created
   - Personal liability (if applicable)

6. **COVENANTS**
   - Mortgagor's covenants (pay debt, maintain property, insure)
   - Mortgagee's covenants (return documents on redemption)

7. **DEFAULT PROVISIONS**
   - Events of default
   - Remedies on default (sale, foreclosure)
   - Notice period

8. **POWER OF SALE** (If Applicable)
   - Mortgagee's right to sell without court
   - Conditions for exercise

9. **INSURANCE**
   - Requirement to insure
   - Policy assignment

10. **ATTESTATION**
    - Two witnesses

**Step 3: Valuation and Loan-to-Value (LTV) Ratio**

Standard LTV:
- Residential property: 75-90% of market value
- Commercial property: 60-75%
- Agricultural land: 50-70%

Example:
- Property value: Rs. 1 crore
- LTV: 80%
- Maximum loan: Rs. 80 lakhs

**Step 4: Stamp Duty and Registration**

**Stamp Duty on Mortgage:**
- Varies by state (usually 0.1% to 1% of debt amount)
- Much lower than sale deed stamp duty

Examples:
- Maharashtra: 0.3% of debt
- Karnataka: 0.1% of debt
- Delhi: Rs. 5,000 fixed + 0.2% of debt

**Registration:**
- Section 17 Registration Act: Mortgage deed MUST be registered
- Unregistered mortgage void (Section 49 Registration Act)

Procedure:
- Execute mortgage deed
- Pay stamp duty
- Register at Sub-Registrar within 4 months
- Both parties + witnesses appear

**Step 5: Creation of Equitable Mortgage (Deposit of Title Deeds)**

Applicable: Notified towns only

Procedure:
1. **Loan Agreement** - Document loan terms, refer to deposit
2. **Deposit Title Deeds** - Hand over original sale deed, previous chain documents
3. **Receipt** - Mortgagee gives receipt acknowledging deposit for security
4. **Memorandum** - Draft memorandum of deposit (optional but advisable)
5. **Registration** - Not mandatory, but memorandum can be registered

No Stamp Duty on Deposit (Big Advantage)

**Step 6: Disbursement**

After mortgage created:
- Mortgagee disburses loan amount
- Usually to mortgagor's bank account
- Receipt obtained

### Phase 2: During Subsistence of Mortgage

**Step 1: Repayment**

Mortgagor makes:
- EMI payments (if amortized loan)
- Interest payments (if interest-only loan)
- Bullet payment at end (if structured)

**Step 2: Monitoring**

Mortgagee monitors:
- Timely payments
- Property condition (if possession with mortgagee)
- Tax payments
- Insurance renewal

**Step 3: Further Advances (Section 94 - Tacking)**

If mortgagee makes additional loans:
- Can "tack" to original mortgage (same priority)
- If no notice of intervening mortgage

**Tacking Conditions:**
1. Original mortgage reserves right to further advances, OR
2. No notice of subsequent mortgage at time of further advance

**Step 3: Second/Third Mortgage (Subsequent Mortgages)**

Mortgagor can create multiple mortgages:
- Must disclose prior mortgages
- Second mortgagee gets second priority
- Third mortgagee gets third priority

**Priority Rule**: First in time, first in right (if all registered)

### Phase 3: Redemption (Paying Off Mortgage)

**Step 1: Payment Tender**

Mortgagor:
- Calculates total dues (principal + interest + costs)
- Tenders payment to mortgagee
- Demands return of documents

**Step 2: Redemption Deed Execution**

For English mortgage/Conditional sale:
- Mortgagee executes reconveyance/re-transfer deed
- Property transferred back to mortgagor
- Registered at Sub-Registrar

**Step 3: Return of Documents**

Mortgagee returns:
- Original mortgage deed
- Title documents (if deposited)
- Sale deed, previous chain documents

**Step 4: Discharge Entry**

In Sub-Registrar records:
- Endorsement of satisfaction of mortgage
- Updates property records (mortgage discharged)

**Step 5: Mutation**

Revenue records updated:
- Mortgage charge removed
- Clear title restored

### Phase 4: Enforcement (Mortgagor Defaulted)

**Step 1: Notice of Default**

Mortgagee sends:
- Notice to mortgagor specifying default
- Demand for payment within 30-60 days
- Warning of enforcement action

**Step 2A: Suit for Sale (Simple/Equitable Mortgage)**

Procedure (Order XXXIV CPC):

1. **File Suit** - In court having jurisdiction (where property situated)
   - Plaint contents: Debt details, mortgage deed, default, prayer for sale
   
2. **Preliminary Decree** (Section 67 TPA)
   - Court declares debt due
   - Orders mortgagor to pay within 6 months (or court-fixed period)
   - If paid: Mortgage redeemed
   - If not paid: Proceed to final decree

3. **Final Decree for Sale**
   - Court orders sale of mortgaged property
   - Sale conducted by court-appointed officer

4. **Court Sale**
   - Advertisement (3 notices: 30 days, 15 days, 7 days before sale)
   - Auction on specified date
   - Sale to highest bidder
   - Sale requires court confirmation

5. **Application of Sale Proceeds:**
   ```
   Priority:
   1. Court costs and expenses
   2. Mortgagee's debt (principal + interest + costs)
   3. Subsequent mortgagees (in order of priority)
   4. Surplus to mortgagor
   ```

6. **Deficiency**
   - If sale proceeds < debt: Mortgagor liable for deficit
   - Mortgagee can file execution petition for deficit

**Limitation**: 12 years from due date (Article 132 Limitation Act)

**Step 2B: Foreclosure Suit (English Mortgage)**

Procedure:

1. **File Suit for Foreclosure**
   - Claim: Extinguish mortgagor's equity of redemption

2. **Preliminary Decree**
   - Court declares debt
   - Orders mortgagor to redeem within 6 months

3. **Final Decree of Foreclosure**
   - If not redeemed: Mortgagor's right extinguished
   - Mortgagee becomes ABSOLUTE OWNER
   - No surplus returned (even if property value > debt)

**Strict Foreclosure Problem:**
- Harsh remedy
- Mortgagee gains windfall if property valuable
- Courts may order sale instead (Section 67 TPA)

**Step 2C: Sale Under Power of Sale (If Mortgage Deed Confers)**

Common in bank mortgages, SARFAESI Act loans

Procedure:

1. **Notice** - 60 days notice to mortgagor (demand payment)

2. **Valuation** - Obtain property valuation

3. **Advertisement** - Public notice in newspapers (2 newspapers, 30 days)

4. **Auction** - Public auction OR private sale

5. **Sale Deed** - Execute sale deed to purchaser

6. **Account** - Apply proceeds to debt, return surplus

**Advantages:**
- Quick (no court process)
- Cost-effective

**Disadvantages:**
- Risk of challenge (if procedure not followed)
- Mortgagor can file suit to restrain sale

**SARFAESI Act 2002 (Special Statute):**
- Banks/Financial Institutions can enforce security under SARFAESI
- Section 13(2): Notice to borrower
- Section 13(4): Take possession after 60 days
- Sale under Section 13(4) without court intervention

## Special Doctrines in Mortgage Law

### 1. SUBROGATION (Section 92)

**Principle**: One who pays off mortgage steps into shoes of mortgagee

**Application:**

**Scenario 1**: Subsequent mortgagee pays off prior mortgage
- Second mortgagee pays first mortgagee's debt
- Second mortgagee gets first mortgagee's priority (subrogation)

**Scenario 2**: Buyer of mortgaged property pays mortgage debt
- Buyer pays mortgagee to get clear title
- Buyer subrogated to mortgagee's rights (can recover from mortgagor)

**Scenario 3**: Surety pays mortgage debt
- Surety who guaranteed loan pays mortgagee
- Surety subrogated to mortgagee's position

**Conditions for Subrogation:**
1. Payment made to protect own interest
2. Payment discharges debt
3. Not merely voluntary payment

**Effect:**
- Subrogated person gets all rights of original mortgagee
- Can enforce mortgage against mortgagor
- Gets same priority

---

### 2. MARSHALLING (Section 81)

**Principle**: Adjustment of rights where one creditor has multiple securities and another has single security in one of those properties

**Scenario:**
- Mortgagee A has mortgage on Property X + Property Y
- Mortgagee B has mortgage on Property X ONLY (subsequent)
- If A enforces against Property X, B's security destroyed

**Marshalling Rule:**
- B can compel A to enforce against Property Y first (where B has no interest)
- Preserves B's security in Property X

**Conditions:**
1. A must have TWO OR MORE securities
2. B must have security in ONE of those properties (junior to A)
3. B's security must be at risk
4. No prejudice to A

**Example:**
- Bank A: Mortgage on House + Factory (debt Rs. 50 lakhs)
- Bank B: Mortgage on House only (debt Rs. 20 lakhs, second mortgage)
- House value: Rs. 40 lakhs; Factory value: Rs. 30 lakhs

Without Marshalling:
- A sells House (Rs. 40 lakhs) + Factory (Rs. 30 lakhs) = Rs. 70 lakhs
- A recovers Rs. 50 lakhs (full debt)
- B gets Rs. 20 lakhs from surplus (full recovery)

If A Sells House First:
- A recovers Rs. 40 lakhs from House sale
- B gets nothing (House sold)
- A then sells Factory for Rs. 30 lakhs
- Total: A gets Rs. 70 lakhs (Rs. 20 lakhs surplus)
- B gets nothing

Marshalling Application:
- B can compel A to sell Factory FIRST (Rs. 30 lakhs)
- A gets Rs. 30 lakhs
- A then sells House for Rs. 40 lakhs (Rs. 10 lakhs more needed)
- A gets full Rs. 50 lakhs total
- House sale surplus: Rs. 30 lakhs (Rs. 40 lakhs - Rs. 10 lakhs to A)
- B recovers Rs. 20 lakhs from surplus

**Effect**: Protects junior mortgagee where senior has multiple securities

---

### 3. CONTRIBUTION (Section 82)

**Principle**: Where multiple properties mortgaged for single debt, each property contributes proportionately

**Scenario:**
- Single mortgage deed covering Property A + Property B + Property C
- Total debt: Rs. 60 lakhs
- Property A value: Rs. 30 lakhs
- Property B value: Rs. 20 lakhs
- Property C value: Rs. 10 lakhs
- Total value: Rs. 60 lakhs

**Contribution Rule:**
- Each property contributes in proportion to its value

Calculation:
- Property A: (30/60) × Rs. 60 lakhs = Rs. 30 lakhs liability
- Property B: (20/60) × Rs. 60 lakhs = Rs. 20 lakhs liability
- Property C: (10/60) × Rs. 60 lakhs = Rs. 10 lakhs liability

**Application:**
If mortgagee sells Property A for Rs. 30 lakhs and recovers full Rs. 30 lakhs:
- Property A owner can claim contribution from Property B and C owners
- B and C must contribute proportionately

**Conditions:**
1. Multiple properties mortgaged for SINGLE debt (not separate debts)
2. Properties owned by different persons OR different interests
3. Debt satisfied from one property disproportionately

---

### 4. CONSOLIDATION (Section 61)

**Principle**: Where same mortgagor and mortgagee in multiple mortgages, mortgagee can insist on redemption of ALL or NONE

**Scenario:**
- A mortgaged Property X to B (debt Rs. 10 lakhs)
- Later, A mortgaged Property Y to B (debt Rs. 5 lakhs)
- Now Property X value increased to Rs. 30 lakhs
- Property Y value declined to Rs. 3 lakhs
- A wants to redeem Property X only (valuable)

**Consolidation Right:**
- B can insist: "Redeem BOTH or NONE"
- Prevents cherry-picking by mortgagor

**Conditions (All Must Be Satisfied):**
1. Same mortgagor and mortgagee in both mortgages
2. Dates of mortgage different (not same transaction)
3. Legal redemption date passed for both
4. No contract excluding consolidation
5. Equities equal on both sides

**Effect:**
- Mortgagor must redeem all mortgages to B
- Cannot redeem selectively

**Modern Relevance:** Rarely applied (courts reluctant)

## Priority Between Mortgages

### General Rule: First in Time, First in Right

If Property has multiple mortgages:
1. First mortgage: First priority
2. Second mortgage: Second priority
3. Third mortgage: Third priority

**Priority Order for Sale Proceeds:**

Property sold for Rs. 80 lakhs:
- Court costs: Rs. 5 lakhs
- First mortgagee (debt Rs. 50 lakhs): Rs. 50 lakhs (full recovery)
- Second mortgagee (debt Rs. 30 lakhs): Rs. 25 lakhs (partial recovery)
- Third mortgagee (debt Rs. 10 lakhs): Rs. 0 (nothing)
- Mortgagor: Rs. 0 (nothing)

Second mortgagee can claim Rs. 5 lakhs deficit from mortgagor (personal liability)

### Exception 1: Registered vs Unregistered

**Section 27 Registration Act:**
- Registered document gets priority over unregistered
- Even if unregistered is earlier in time

**Example:**
- 1st June: Mortgage to A (NOT registered)
- 1st July: Mortgage to B (REGISTERED)
- Priority: B > A (registration prevails)

### Exception 2: Actual Notice vs Constructive Notice

**Constructive Notice:**
- Registration creates constructive notice to world
- Subsequent mortgagee deemed to have notice

**Actual Notice:**
- If subsequent mortgagee has actual knowledge of prior unregistered mortgage
- Cannot claim bona fide purchaser protection

### Exception 3: Tacking of Further Advances (Section 94)

**Scenario:**
- 1st January: Mortgage to Bank A (Rs. 20 lakhs)
- 1st June: Mortgage to Bank B (Rs. 10 lakhs)
- 1st December: Bank A gives further advance (Rs. 10 lakhs)

**Question**: Is Bank A's further Rs. 10 lakhs prior to Bank B OR subsequent?

**Tacking Rule (Section 94):**
Bank A's further advance gets priority over B IF:
1. Original mortgage to A reserved right to further advances, OR
2. A had NO NOTICE of B's mortgage when making further advance

If either condition satisfied:
- Bank A's total debt (Rs. 30 lakhs) ranks ahead of Bank B

If neither condition satisfied:
- Bank A's original Rs. 20 lakhs ranks first
- Bank B's Rs. 10 lakhs ranks second
- Bank A's further Rs. 10 lakhs ranks THIRD

## Common Scenarios and Solutions

### Scenario 1: Mortgagor Defaults - Simple Mortgage

**Facts:**
- A borrowed Rs. 50 lakhs from B
- Simple mortgage on property
- A defaulted after paying Rs. 10 lakhs
- Outstanding: Rs. 40 lakhs + Rs. 5 lakhs interest
- Property value: Rs. 60 lakhs

**B's Remedies:**

Option 1: **Sue for Debt** (Section 67 - Personal Action)
- File suit for Rs. 45 lakhs debt
- Decree against A personally
- Execute against A's ALL assets (not just mortgaged property)
- Limitation: 12 years from default

Option 2: **Sue for Sale** (Section 69 - Action Against Security)
- File suit for sale of mortgaged property
- Preliminary decree declaring debt
- Final decree for sale
- Property sold through court
- Limitation: 12 years from default

Option 3: **Both**
- Sue for debt + sale simultaneously
- Recover from property sale
- If deficit, recover from A's other assets

**Procedure:**
1. Send legal notice (demand payment within 30 days)
2. File suit in court (where property situated)
3. Obtain preliminary decree (usually 6-12 months)
4. Obtain final decree (6 months after preliminary decree)
5. Court sale conducted
6. Sale proceeds applied to debt

---

### Scenario 2: Equitable Mortgage - Title Deeds Deposited

**Facts:**
- A deposited title deeds with Bank B in Mumbai
- Loan Rs. 30 lakhs
- No written mortgage deed
- A defaulted

**Legal Position:**
- Valid equitable mortgage (Section 58(f))
- Mumbai is notified town
- Deposit with intent to secure = Mortgage

**Bank B's Rights:**
- Same as simple mortgage
- Sue for sale (Section 67)
- Cannot take possession without court order
- Cannot sell under power of sale (unless separate agreement)

**Bank B's Procedure:**
1. File suit for sale (Order XXXIV CPC)
2. Preliminary decree
3. Final decree
4. Court sale

**Defense by A:**
- Challenge intent (argue deposit not for security)
- Difficult if loan documents mention deposit

---

### Scenario 3: English Mortgage - Foreclosure vs Sale

**Facts:**
- A executed English mortgage to B
- Debt Rs. 40 lakhs
- Property value Rs. 80 lakhs
- A defaulted

**B's Options:**

Option 1: **Foreclosure**
- File suit for foreclosure
- Preliminary decree (6 months to redeem)
- Final decree: B becomes absolute owner
- B gets property worth Rs. 80 lakhs for Rs. 40 lakhs debt (windfall)
- A loses property + Rs. 40 lakhs equity

Option 2: **Sale**
- File suit for sale (Section 67)
- Property sold for Rs. 80 lakhs
- B recovers Rs. 40 lakhs
- A gets Rs. 40 lakhs surplus

**Court's Approach:**
- Courts prefer Sale over Foreclosure (Section 67 proviso)
- Foreclosure granted only if:
  - Property value = Debt (no surplus)
  - Special circumstances
  - Mortgagor consents

**A's Defense:**
- File redemption suit before foreclosure decree
- Deposit Rs. 40 lakhs + interest in court
- Redeem mortgage

---

### Scenario 4: Multiple Mortgages - Priority Dispute

**Facts:**
- Property value: Rs. 1 crore
- 1st Mortgage to Bank A: Rs. 60 lakhs (registered)
- 2nd Mortgage to Bank B: Rs. 30 lakhs (registered)
- 3rd Mortgage to C: Rs. 20 lakhs (registered)
- Total debt: Rs. 1.10 crores
- Property sold for Rs. 1 crore

**Distribution:**
1. Court costs: Rs. 2 lakhs
2. Bank A (1st mortgagee): Rs. 60 lakhs (FULL)
3. Bank B (2nd mortgagee): Rs. 30 lakhs (FULL)
4. C (3rd mortgagee): Rs. 8 lakhs (PARTIAL)
5. Mortgagor: Rs. 0

**C's Position:**
- Shortfall: Rs. 12 lakhs
- Can sue mortgagor for deficit (personal liability)
- Limitation: 12 years from sale

**Prevention for C:**
- Before taking 3rd mortgage, verify prior mortgages
- Ensure adequate security margin
- Demand mortgagor to partially discharge prior mortgages

---

### Scenario 5: Usufructuary Mortgage - Possession Issue

**Facts:**
- A executed usufructuary mortgage to B
- Debt Rs. 20 lakhs
- B entitled to possession and rents (Rs. 1 lakh/month)
- 10 years period
- After 3 years, A obstructs B's possession

**B's Remedies:**

Remedy 1: **Sue for Possession**
- File suit under Order XXI Rule 35 CPC
- Claim possession based on mortgage deed
- Obtain possession decree

Remedy 2: **Sue for Injunction**
- Restrain A from obstructing possession
- Claim damages for lost rents

**Calculation:**
- Debt: Rs. 20 lakhs
- Rent received (3 years): Rs. 36 lakhs
- Debt satisfied (Rs. 36 lakhs > Rs. 20 lakhs)
- Mortgage redeemed automatically

**If Debt Satisfied:**
- A can claim return of possession
- B must vacate and return possession
- File redemption suit if B refuses

---

### Scenario 6: Second Mortgage Without Notice of First

**Facts:**
- 1st March: A mortgaged property to Bank B (Rs. 30 lakhs) - NOT registered
- 1st June: A mortgaged same property to Bank C (Rs. 20 lakhs) - REGISTERED
- A did not disclose B's mortgage to C
- C had no notice of B's mortgage

**Priority:**
- Section 27 Registration Act: Registered prevails over unregistered
- C (registered) > B (unregistered)

**C's Position:**
- First priority despite being later in time
- If property sold, C gets Rs. 20 lakhs first

**B's Position:**
- Second priority
- Can challenge if proves C had actual notice

**B's Remedies Against A:**
- Sue A for fraud/misrepresentation
- Section 420 IPC (cheating)
- Claim damages

**Prevention:**
- B should have registered mortgage (priority protection)
- C should have verified encumbrances (due diligence)

## Integration with Other Laws

### SARFAESI Act 2002 (Securitisation and Reconstruction of Financial Assets and Enforcement of Security Interest Act)

**Applicability:**
- Banks, Financial Institutions, Asset Reconstruction Companies
- Debt Rs. 1 lakh or more (earlier Rs. 1 lakh, now increased periodically)

**Section 13 Enforcement:**

**Step 1: Notice** - Bank sends notice under Section 13(2)
- Demand full payment within 60 days

**Step 2: Possession** - If not paid, bank can take possession (Section 13(4))
- Without court intervention
- Take symbolic/actual possession

**Step 3: Sale** - Bank can sell property
- After taking possession
- Public auction or private sale
- Apply proceeds to debt

**Borrower's Remedies:**
- File application before Debt Recovery Tribunal (DRT) within 45 days
- Section 17: DRT can stay SARFAESI action if prima facie case
- Appeal to Debt Recovery Appellate Tribunal (DRAT)

**Advantages of SARFAESI:**
- Quick enforcement (no court suit needed)
- Cost-effective
- 60-90 days timeline

**Limitations:**
- Not applicable to agricultural land
- Not applicable if debt < threshold
- DRT can stay action

### Code of Civil Procedure 1908 - Order XXXIV

**Special Procedure for Mortgage Suits:**

Order XXXIV Rules:
- Suit for sale/foreclosure follows special procedure
- Preliminary decree mandatory (declaring debt + redemption period)
- Final decree after redemption period expires
- Mortgagor can redeem even after preliminary decree (before final decree)

**Valuation for Court Fees:**
- Based on debt amount (not property value)
- Court fees on Rs. 50 lakhs debt: Calculate as per state rules

**Jurisdiction:**
- Where property situated (Section 16 CPC)
- Cannot be waived

### Registration Act 1908

**Section 17:** Mortgage deed must be registered
**Section 49:** Unregistered mortgage void
**Section 27:** Registered document prevails over unregistered

**Exception:** Equitable mortgage (deposit of title deeds)
- No written instrument
- Registration not required
- But memorandum of deposit CAN be registered (optional)

### Limitation Act 1963

**Article 132:** Suit on mortgage
- **Limitation:** 12 years from date when money sued for becomes due
- For sale suit: 12 years from due date
- For foreclosure: 12 years from due date
- For redemption: 30 years from accrual of right

**Redemption Limitation:**
- Equity of redemption continues for 30 years (Article 61)
- After 30 years, mortgagor's right barred

### Income Tax Act 1961

**Interest Deduction:**
- Section 24(b): Interest on home loan deductible (up to Rs. 2 lakhs for self-occupied)
- Section 80EE: Additional deduction for first-time home buyers

**Capital Gains on Sale:**
- Section 50C: Sale value = Higher of actual or stamp duty value
- Mortgagee's sale: Capital gains implications for mortgagor

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### 1. Not Registering Mortgage Deed
**Mistake:** Execute mortgage deed but don't register (thinking possession/payment sufficient)
**Consequence:** Mortgage void (Section 49 Registration Act); no security created
**Solution:** ALWAYS register mortgage deed within 4 months

### 2. Equitable Mortgage Outside Notified Towns
**Mistake:** Deposit title deeds in non-notified town thinking equitable mortgage created
**Consequence:** No mortgage (Section 58(f) limited to notified towns); only personal liability
**Solution:** Verify town is notified; if not, execute registered mortgage deed

### 3. Ignoring Prior Mortgages
**Mistake:** Take second mortgage without verifying encumbrances
**Consequence:** Discover first mortgage later; become junior mortgagee with second priority
**Solution:** Obtain encumbrance certificate; verify all prior mortgages before lending

### 4. Over-Valuation of Property
**Mistake:** Lend Rs. 80 lakhs on property worth Rs. 60 lakhs (thinking value will appreciate)
**Consequence:** On default, sale proceeds insufficient to recover debt
**Solution:** Maintain LTV ratio (75-90%); obtain professional valuation

### 5. Not Taking Title Insurance
**Mistake:** Lend large amount without title insurance/verification
**Consequence:** Discover title defect later; mortgagor's title invalid; cannot enforce mortgage
**Solution:** Conduct thorough title verification (30-40 years chain); take title insurance for high-value properties

### 6. Foreclosure Without Court Approval
**Mistake:** Mortgagee (English mortgage) takes possession and claims ownership without court decree
**Consequence:** Mortgagor files suit; court orders sale instead; mortgagee loses possession
**Solution:** Always obtain court decree for foreclosure (even if English mortgage)

### 7. Sale Under Power Without Notice
**Mistake:** Bank sells property under power of sale without proper notice/advertisement
**Consequence:** Sale voidable; mortgagor challenges sale; court sets aside
**Solution:** Follow procedure strictly: Notice (60 days) → Advertisement (30 days) → Auction → Account

### 8. Accepting Mortgaged Property Without Redemption
**Mistake:** Buyer purchases property without checking encumbrances; discovers mortgage later
**Consequence:** Mortgagee enforces security; buyer loses property
**Solution:** Obtain encumbrance certificate; insist on mortgage discharge before purchase

### 9. Mortgaging Agricultural Land
**Mistake:** Bank takes mortgage on agricultural land without verifying restrictions
**Consequence:** Cannot enforce under SARFAESI (agricultural land excluded); lengthy court process
**Solution:** Verify land classification; avoid agricultural land OR convert to non-agricultural first

### 10. Not Updating Mortgage Records
**Mistake:** Mortgage redeemed but not discharged in Sub-Registrar records
**Consequence:** Encumbrance certificate shows subsisting mortgage; buyer refuses to purchase
**Solution:** After redemption, obtain discharge certificate; update Sub-Registrar records

```
MORTGAGE TRANSACTION
│
├─ PHASE 1: MORTGAGE CREATION
│  ├─ Determine Type (Simple/English/Usufructuary/Equitable/Conditional Sale/Anomalous)
│  ├─ Property Valuation (LTV ratio 75-90%)
│  ├─ Draft Mortgage Deed
│  ├─ Pay Stamp Duty (0.1-1% of debt)
│  ├─ Register (within 4 months)
│  │  └─ Exception: Equitable mortgage (deposit title deeds - no registration)
│  └─ Disburse Loan
│
├─ PHASE 2: SUBSISTENCE
│  ├─ Repayment (EMI/Bullet/Interest-only)
│  ├─ Monitoring (payments, property condition, insurance)
│  └─ Further Advances (Section 94 - Tacking)
│
├─ PHASE 3: REDEMPTION (Mortgagor Pays Debt)
│  ├─ Tender Payment (Principal + Interest + Costs)
│  ├─ Return of Documents (Title deeds)
│  ├─ Re-transfer Deed (English mortgage)
│  ├─ Discharge Certificate
│  └─ Mutation (Remove mortgage charge)
│
└─ PHASE 4: ENFORCEMENT (Mortgagor Defaults)
   ├─ Notice of Default (30-60 days demand)
   │
   ├─ OPTION A: Suit for Sale (Simple/Equitable Mortgage)
   │  ├─ Preliminary Decree (6 months redemption period)
   │  ├─ Final Decree (if not redeemed)
   │  ├─ Court Sale (Auction)
   │  └─ Application of Proceeds (Priority order)
   │
   ├─ OPTION B: Foreclosure (English/Conditional Sale Mortgage)
   │  ├─ Preliminary Decree (6 months redemption period)
   │  ├─ Final Decree of Foreclosure
   │  └─ Mortgagee Becomes Absolute Owner
   │
   ├─ OPTION C: Sale Under Power of Sale (If Deed Confers)
   │  ├─ Notice (60 days)
   │  ├─ Advertisement (30 days, 2 newspapers)
   │  ├─ Auction (Public/Private)
   │  └─ Sale Deed to Purchaser
   │
   └─ OPTION D: SARFAESI (Banks/FIs only)
      ├─ Section 13(2) Notice (60 days)
      ├─ Section 13(4) Possession
      ├─ Sale
      └─ DRT Appeal (Borrower - 45 days)
```
