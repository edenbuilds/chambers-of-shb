# Five Types of Writs - Applications and Distinctions

## Purpose
Detailed understanding of five constitutional writs (habeas corpus, mandamus, prohibition, certiorari, quo warranto), when each is appropriate, and procedural requirements.

---

## 1. Habeas Corpus ("Produce the Body")

### Meaning
Latin: "You may have the body"
**Purpose**: Secure release of person illegally detained.

### When to Use
- Illegal arrest/detention
- Detention beyond legal period
- Detention without following procedure established by law
- Missing person (if suspected illegal detention by state)

### Against Whom
- Police (illegal detention)
- Jail authorities (detention beyond sentence period)
- Government (preventive detention under invalid order)
- Private person (if detaining in collusion with state authority)

### Procedure
**Petition Contents**:
1. Detainee name and location
2. Authority detaining
3. Grounds of detention (if known)
4. Why detention illegal

**Court's Power**:
- Summon detaining authority
- Examine detention order/warrant
- If illegal → Order immediate release
- If legal → Dismiss petition

### Examples

**Example 1**: Police arrested A without FIR, holding beyond 24 hours (Code of Criminal Procedure mandates production before magistrate within 24 hours).
→ **Habeas corpus**: Detention illegal (procedural violation).

**Example 2**: Person detained under National Security Act. Detention order doesn't disclose grounds.
→ **Habeas corpus**: Detention violates Article 22(5) (right to know grounds).

**Example 3**: Convicted person served full sentence, but jail authorities haven't released.
→ **Habeas corpus**: Detention beyond sentence period illegal.

### Landmark Case
**ADM Jabalpur v. Shivakant Shukla** (1976) - Emergency Case:
During 1975 Emergency, SC held habeas corpus suspended (controversial decision, later overruled in *Maneka Gandhi*).

**Post-Maneka Gandhi**: Even during Emergency, habeas corpus maintainable if detention violates procedure.

## 2. Mandamus ("We Command")

### Meaning
Latin: "We command"
**Purpose**: Compel public authority to perform legal duty.

### When to Use
- Authority refuses to perform statutory/constitutional duty
- Authority delays performing duty unreasonably
- Authority performs duty inadequately

### Conditions for Mandamus

**1. Legal Duty** (Not Discretion):
Mandamus compels **mandatory** duty, not **discretionary** action.

**Example**:
- ✓ Registrar of Companies **must** register company if documents comply (mandatory duty) → Mandamus lies
- ✗ Governor **may** grant pardon (discretionary power) → Mandamus doesn't lie

**2. Duty Must Be Public**:
Cannot compel performance of private contract (remedy: suit for specific performance).

**3. Petitioner Must Have Legal Right**:
Corresponding to duty, petitioner must have enforceable legal right.

**Example**: Citizen has right to pollution-free environment (Article 21) → Pollution Board has duty to act → Mandamus lies.

**4. No Alternative Equally Efficacious Remedy**:
If statutory appeal available, mandamus may be refused (unless appeal inadequate).

**5. Demand and Refusal**:
Petitioner should have demanded performance and been refused (except where futile).

### Against Whom
- Government departments
- Statutory corporations (if performing public duty)
- Local authorities (municipalities, panchayats)
- Universities, professional bodies (if statutory)

**Cannot Issue Mandamus Against**:
- Private companies (unless "State" under Article 12)
- President / Governor (Article 361 immunity)
- Judges (in judicial capacity - alternative: appeal)

### Examples

**Example 1**: Municipality refuses to issue trade license despite application complying with all requirements.
→ **Mandamus**: Municipality has statutory duty to issue license if conditions met.

**Example 2**: Pollution Control Board doesn't act on complaint of factory pollution despite evidence.
→ **Mandamus**: Board has duty under Environmental Protection Act to enforce standards.

**Example 3**: University doesn't declare exam results for 2 years.
→ **Mandamus**: University has duty to declare results within reasonable time.

### Prohibition vs. Mandamus
- **Mandamus**: "Do this" (positive command)
- **Prohibition**: "Don't do this" (negative command - see below)

## 3. Prohibition ("Forbid")

### Meaning
**Purpose**: Prevent inferior court/tribunal from exceeding jurisdiction or acting contrary to natural justice.

### When to Use
- Tribunal/authority about to act beyond jurisdiction
- Proceeding vitiated by bias/violation of natural justice
- Prevent action before it's taken (preventive)

### Key Requirement: "Inferior Court/Tribunal"
Prohibition issues only against **judicial/quasi-judicial** authorities, not administrative authorities.

**Judicial/Quasi-Judicial**: Body deciding rights between parties after hearing (tribunal characteristics).

### Examples

**Example 1**: District Court (civil) about to try criminal case (no criminal jurisdiction).
→ **Prohibition**: Court exceeding jurisdiction.

**Example 2**: Tribunal member has financial interest in subject matter (bias).
→ **Prohibition**: Proceeding violates natural justice (nemo judex principle).

**Example 3**: Family Court about to decide corporate dispute (no jurisdiction over companies).
→ **Prohibition**: Court acting beyond subject-matter jurisdiction.

### Distinction from Certiorari
- **Prohibition**: **Preventive** (before final order, during proceedings)
- **Certiorari**: **Curative** (after final order passed)

**Timing**:
File prohibition **while proceedings pending**.
File certiorari **after order passed**.

---

## 4. Certiorari ("To Be Certified")

### Meaning
Latin: "To be more fully informed"
**Purpose**: Quash order of inferior court/tribunal passed without/in excess of jurisdiction.

### When to Use
- Final order already passed by tribunal/authority
- Order suffers from:
  - Jurisdictional error
  - Violation of natural justice
  - Error of law apparent on face of record
  - Perversity

### Grounds for Certiorari

**1. Lack of Jurisdiction**:
Authority had no power to decide the matter.

**Example**: Labour Court decides consumer dispute (no jurisdiction under Consumer Protection Act).

**2. Excess of Jurisdiction**:
Authority had jurisdiction but exceeded it in exercise.

**Example**: Tribunal imposed penalty of ₹1 lakh when statute caps penalty at ₹50,000.

**3. Violation of Natural Justice**:
- No opportunity of hearing (audi alteram partem)
- Decision by biased authority (nemo judex)
- Non-application of mind

**Example**: University expels student without inquiry/hearing.

**4. Error of Law Apparent on Face of Record**:
Legal error obvious from order itself (no deep investigation needed).

**Example**: Tribunal applies repealed law to decide case.

**5. Perversity / No Evidence**:
Finding based on no evidence or contrary to all evidence.

**Example**: Tribunal finds accused guilty when prosecution led no evidence.

### Against Whom
- Tribunals (CAT, NCLT, Debt Recovery Tribunal, etc.)
- Quasi-judicial authorities (Collector in land acquisition, Pollution Board, etc.)
- Inferior courts (Magistrate, District Judge if SC/HC issuing writ)

**Cannot Issue Against**:
- Administrative actions (unless quasi-judicial)
- Legislative actions
- Private persons/bodies

### Examples

**Example 1**: Income Tax Appellate Tribunal dismisses appeal without hearing taxpayer.
→ **Certiorari**: Violation of natural justice.

**Example 2**: Building approval authority approves construction violating height restrictions in law.
→ **Certiorari**: Decision contrary to statute (error of law).

**Example 3**: Consumer Forum awards damages for deficiency in service that doesn't exist under Consumer Protection Act definition.
→ **Certiorari**: Jurisdictional error (no deficiency as per law).

## 5. Quo Warranto ("By What Authority")

### Meaning
Latin: "By what authority/warrant"
**Purpose**: Challenge person's right to hold public office.

### When to Use
- Person holding public office without legal authority
- Appointment violates constitutional/statutory requirements
- Usurpation of public office

### Conditions for Quo Warranto

**1. Public Office**:
Must be **public office** of substantive nature (not private employment).

**Public Office**: Created by Constitution/statute, involves public duties, remuneration from public funds.

**Examples**:
- ✓ Chairperson of PSU
- ✓ Vice Chancellor of University
- ✓ Member of Statutory Commission
- ✗ Employee of private company (not public office)

**2. Actual Holding of Office**:
Person must be presently holding office (not merely appointed, must have assumed charge).

**3. Appointment Invalid**:
Appointment violates:
- Eligibility criteria (age, qualification, experience)
- Selection procedure (no advertisement, no interview)
- Disqualification (convicted person appointed)

### Locus Standi
**Any person** can file quo warranto (public interest involved - office is public property).

No need to show personal interest.

### Against Whom
Person holding public office + Appointing authority

### Remedy
If court finds appointment illegal → Order:
1. Declare office vacant
2. Restrain person from acting as office holder
3. Recover salary/perquisites drawn (in some cases)

### Examples

**Example 1**: Vice Chancellor appointed without following UGC regulations (no search committee).
→ **Quo warranto**: Appointment violates statutory procedure.

**Example 2**: Chairperson of Regulatory Commission is 72 years old (statute requires below 65).
→ **Quo warranto**: Appointee ineligible (age disqualification).

**Example 3**: Member of Service Commission has criminal conviction (disqualifying under statute).
→ **Quo warranto**: Disqualified person holding office.

### Cannot Issue Quo Warranto Against
- Purely private employment
- Ministerial offices (elected representatives - remedy is election petition, not quo warranto)
- De facto doctrine: If person held office for long time, performed public acts (those acts valid even if appointment questioned)

## Comparison Table

| Writ | Purpose | Timing | Against | Key Requirement |
|------|---------|--------|---------|-----------------|
| **Habeas Corpus** | Secure release | During detention | Detaining authority | Illegal detention |
| **Mandamus** | Compel duty | Before/after refusal | Public authority | Legal duty exists |
| **Prohibition** | Prevent action | **Before** final order | Judicial/quasi-judicial | Excess jurisdiction / natural justice |
| **Certiorari** | Quash order | **After** final order | Judicial/quasi-judicial | Jurisdictional error |
| **Quo Warranto** | Challenge office | During tenure | Office holder | Public office, invalid appointment |

## Strategic Selection

**Scenario 1: Illegal Police Detention**
→ **Habeas Corpus** (secure release)

**Scenario 2: Passport Office Refuses Passport Without Reason**
→ **Mandamus** (compel issuance if eligible)

**Scenario 3: Tribunal Hearing Case Beyond Its Jurisdiction (Proceedings Ongoing)**
→ **Prohibition** (prevent tribunal from proceeding)

**Scenario 4: Tribunal Passed Order Violating Natural Justice**
→ **Certiorari** (quash order)

**Scenario 5: Unqualified Person Appointed as University VC**
→ **Quo Warranto** (challenge appointment)

**Scenario 6: Government Demolishing House Without Notice**
→ **Prohibition** (if demolition not yet done) OR **Certiorari** (if demolition order passed) + **Mandamus** (compel compensation if already demolished)

**Multiple Writs in Single Petition**: Possible if facts support (e.g., certiorari + mandamus: quash illegal order + compel correct decision).
