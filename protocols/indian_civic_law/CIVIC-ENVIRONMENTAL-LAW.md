# Environmental Law - Pollution Control and Conservation

## Purpose
Understand environmental protection framework in India, enforcement mechanisms under Environment Protection Act and allied laws, constitutional basis for environmental rights, and citizen remedies for pollution/environmental degradation.

## Activation Triggers
- Air/water/noise pollution affecting community
- Illegal construction in forest/wetland/protected area
- Industrial effluent discharge
- Illegal mining/quarrying
- Waste management violations
- Wildlife protection violations
- Need to challenge environment clearance

---

## Constitutional Framework

### Article 21: Right to Clean Environment

**Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India** (1978):
Article 21 expanded to include **right to wholesome environment**.

**Subhash Kumar v. State of Bihar** (1991):
> "Right to life includes right to enjoy pollution-free water and air for full enjoyment of life."

**M.C. Mehta v. Union of India** (1987):
> "Article 21 protects right to wholesome environment. Polluting environment violates fundamental right."

**Implication**:
- Environmental pollution = Violation of Article 21 (fundamental right)
- Writ petition maintainable against polluters (if "State" under Article 12)
- PIL maintainable for environmental protection

---

### Directive Principles (Part IV)

**Article 48A**:
> "The State shall endeavour to protect and improve the environment and to safeguard the forests and wildlife of the country."

**Article 51A(g)** (Fundamental Duty):
> "It shall be the duty of every citizen of India to protect and improve the natural environment including forests, lakes, rivers and wildlife, and to have compassion for living creatures."

**Constitutional Status**: Environment protection is both state's duty (Article 48A) and citizen's duty (Article 51A).

## Environmental Laws - Framework

### 1. Environment (Protection) Act, 1986 (EPA)

**Umbrella Legislation**: Enacted after Bhopal Gas Tragedy (1984).

**Key Features**:
- Central Government empowered to take measures for environmental protection
- Set standards for emissions, discharge
- Regulate industries, processes
- Prohibit/restrict location of industries in certain areas
- Penalties for violations

**Section 3** (Central Government's Powers):
```
Central Government may take measures for:
(i) Coordination of actions by State Governments
(ii) Planning and execution of nationwide programme for environmental protection
(iii) Laying down standards for quality of environment
(iv) Laying down standards for emission/discharge of pollutants
(v) Restriction of areas for industries/operations
```

**Section 15** (Penalties):
- Imprisonment up to 5 years
- Fine up to ₹1 lakh
- Both
- **Continuing offense**: Additional fine up to ₹5,000/day for continued violation

---

### 2. Water (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1974

**Regulatory Bodies**:
- **Central Pollution Control Board (CPCB)**: National level
- **State Pollution Control Boards (SPCBs)**: State level

**Key Provisions**:
- Industries must obtain **Consent to Establish** (CTE) and **Consent to Operate** (CTO) from SPCB
- No discharge of effluent beyond prescribed standards
- SPCBs can take samples, inspect, close non-compliant industries

**Section 24** (Prohibition on stream pollution):
> "No person shall knowingly cause or permit any poisonous, noxious or polluting matter... to enter into any stream or well."

**Penalty** (Section 41, 43):
- Imprisonment up to 6 years
- Fine

---

### 3. Air (Prevention and Control of Pollution) Act, 1981

**Purpose**: Control and abatement of air pollution.

**Key Provisions**:
- **Air Pollution Control Areas** declared by State Government
- Industries in such areas need consent from SPCB
- Standards for emission from automobiles, industries
- SPCBs can give directions to close/regulate industries

**Section 21** (Prohibition):
> "No person shall, without the previous consent of the State Board, establish or operate any industrial plant in an air pollution control area."

**Penalty** (Section 37):
- Imprisonment up to 6 years
- Fine

---

### 4. Noise Pollution (Regulation and Control) Rules, 2000

**Standards**:
| Area Type | Daytime (6 AM - 10 PM) | Nighttime (10 PM - 6 AM) |
|-----------|------------------------|--------------------------|
| Industrial | 75 dB | 70 dB |
| Commercial | 65 dB | 55 dB |
| Residential | 55 dB | 45 dB |
| Silence Zone | 50 dB | 40 dB |

**Silence Zones**: Areas within 100m of hospitals, educational institutions, courts.

**Enforcement**: State Pollution Control Board, Police.

**Penalty**: Under EPA Section 15 (up to 5 years imprisonment).

---

### 5. Solid Waste Management Rules, 2016

**Municipal Duty**:
- Municipalities responsible for collection, segregation, disposal
- Door-to-door collection mandatory
- Waste processing and disposal facilities

**Generator Duty** (Households, businesses):
- Segregate waste into wet, dry, hazardous
- No littering, burning
- Pay user charges

**Penalty**: Under EPA (if rules violated).

---

### 6. Plastic Waste Management Rules, 2016

**Prohibition**:
- Single-use plastic banned (carry bags <50 microns)
- Multi-layered plastic banned

**Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR)**:
- Manufacturers responsible for collection and recycling

**Penalty**: Under EPA.

---

### 7. Hazardous Waste Management Rules, 2016

**Authorization Required**:
Industries generating hazardous waste must obtain authorization from SPCB.

**Manifest System**:
Track movement of hazardous waste from generator → transporter → disposer.

**Penalty**: Imprisonment up to 5 years (EPA).

---

### 8. Forest (Conservation) Act, 1980

**Restriction on Diversion of Forest Land**:
No state government can permit use of forest land for non-forest purpose without Central Government approval.

**Non-Forest Purpose**: Mining, cultivation, construction, dam, etc.

**Section 2** (Prior approval mandatory):
> "No State Government shall make... any order directing that any forest land... shall cease to be reserved... except with the prior approval of the Central Government."

**Penalty** (Section 3A):
- Imprisonment up to 15 days
- Fine (inadequate - often not deterrent)

**Compensatory Afforestation**:
If forest diverted, equivalent non-forest land must be afforested OR payment to Compensatory Afforestation Fund.

---

### 9. Wildlife (Protection) Act, 1972

**Protected Species**:
- Schedule I: Most endangered (tiger, elephant, etc.) - Absolute protection
- Schedule II-IV: Varying levels of protection
- Schedule V: Vermin (can be hunted)

**Prohibition**:
- Hunting of protected species
- Cutting trees/vegetation in protected areas without permission

**Protected Areas**:
- National Parks
- Wildlife Sanctuaries
- Conservation Reserves
- Community Reserves

**Penalty** (Section 51):
- Imprisonment up to 7 years
- Fine minimum ₹25,000

## Environmental Principles (Judicial Development)

### 1. Polluter Pays Principle

**Vellore Citizens Welfare Forum v. Union of India** (1996):
SC held: "Polluter must pay for damage caused to ecology. Cost of reversing damage is to be borne by enterprise."

**Application**:
- Industry pollutes river → Court orders industry to pay cost of cleaning river
- Exemplary damages imposed on polluters

**Example**: *Indian Council for Enviro-Legal Action v. Union of India* (1996) - Chemical industries polluted land in Rajasthan. SC ordered industries to pay compensation for restoration.

---

### 2. Precautionary Principle

**Vellore Citizens** (1996):
> "Where there are threats of serious or irreversible damage, lack of full scientific certainty shall not be used as a reason for postponing measures to prevent environmental degradation."

**Application**:
- Don't wait for 100% proof of harm
- If activity likely to cause serious harm, prevent it (even if scientific uncertainty)

**Example**: Genetically Modified Organisms (GMOs) - SC allowed cautious introduction only after extensive trials (despite industry claiming "no proof of harm").

---

### 3. Sustainable Development

**Definition**: Development that meets needs of present without compromising ability of future generations to meet their needs.

**Application**:
- Courts balance development vs. environment
- Not absolute ban on development, but sustainable development

**Example**: Mining allowed in some areas BUT with strict conditions (afforestation, pollution control, restoration).

---

### 4. Public Trust Doctrine

**M.C. Mehta v. Kamal Nath** (1997):
> "Natural resources (rivers, seashores, forests, air) are held by government as trustee for public. Government cannot alienate these resources for private profit."

**Application**:
- Government cannot privatize rivers, beaches, forests
- Rivers, air, seashore are public property

**Example**: Encroachment on river bank for resort - SC held government breached public trust, ordered removal.

---

### 5. Intergenerational Equity

**Future generations have right to clean environment.**

Courts consider impact on future generations when deciding environmental cases.

**Example**: Groundwater depletion - SC restricts extraction to preserve for future.

## Regulatory Mechanisms

### 1. Environment Impact Assessment (EIA)

**EIA Notification, 2006**:
Projects likely to have significant environmental impact must obtain **Environment Clearance (EC)** from:
- **Ministry of Environment, Forest & Climate Change (MoEFCC)**: Category A projects (large, e.g., major mining, thermal power >500 MW)
- **State Environment Impact Assessment Authority (SEIAA)**: Category B projects (smaller, e.g., mining <50 hectares)

**Process**:
1. **Screening**: Is EIA required?
2. **Scoping**: What aspects to study?
3. **EIA Study**: Assess environmental impact
4. **Public Consultation**: Hear affected persons' objections
5. **Appraisal**: Expert committee reviews
6. **Decision**: Grant / Deny / Grant with conditions

**Validity**: EC valid for specified period (5 years for mining, etc.). Must be renewed.

**Violation**: Starting project without EC → EPA offense (imprisonment + fine).

---

### 2. Consents from Pollution Control Boards

**Required for Industries**:

**Consent to Establish (CTE)**:
Before establishing industry, obtain CTE from SPCB.

**Consent to Operate (CTO)**:
Before starting operations, obtain CTO from SPCB (after construction complete).

**Validity**: 5-10 years (varies). Must be renewed.

**Conditions**: SPCBs impose conditions (effluent treatment, stack height, monitoring).

**Violation**: Operating without CTO → Water Act / Air Act offense.

---

### 3. Closure Directions

**SPCBs Power** (Water Act Section 33A, Air Act Section 31A):
If industry violates standards, SPCB can issue **closure direction** (stop operations till compliance).

**Industry's Remedy**:
Appeal to **State Pollution Control Board** → Appellate Authority (if provided in state law) → **National Green Tribunal** (NGT).

---

## Citizen Remedies for Environmental Violations

### 1. Complaint to Pollution Control Board

**Process**:
1. File written complaint to **SPCB** citing violation (pollution, no consent, etc.)
2. Provide evidence (photos, videos, pollution data if available)
3. SPCB must inspect, take action

**Timeline**: No statutory timeline (varies, often 1-3 months).

**SPCB Action**:
- Inspection
- Sampling (water, air, soil)
- Show-cause notice to industry
- Closure direction (if serious violation)

**Citizen Follow-Up**: Use RTI to check status of complaint.

---

### 2. Police Complaint (FIR)

**EPA Section 15, Water Act Section 43, Air Act Section 37**:
Environmental offenses are cognizable (police can register FIR without magistrate's order).

**When to File FIR**:
- Serious pollution causing health hazard
- Illegal dumping of hazardous waste
- Wildlife killing

**Jurisdiction**: Police station where offense committed.

**Investigation**: Police investigate → Submit charge-sheet to court → Trial.

**Practical Reality**: Police often reluctant to register FIR against industries (economic pressure). May require pressure (media, NGO, senior officers).

---

### 3. Public Interest Litigation (PIL)

**Article 21 Violation**:
Pollution = Violation of right to clean environment → PIL maintainable.

**Who Can File**: Any citizen, NGO (bonafide interest).

**Forum**:
- **High Court** (Article 226): State-level pollution issue
- **Supreme Court** (Article 32): National importance

**Relief Sought**:
- Direct SPCB to take action
- Order closure of polluting industry
- Appoint monitoring committee
- Compensatory damages (on Polluter Pays Principle)

**Example**: *M.C. Mehta v. Union of India* (Ganga pollution) - SC issued directions to close polluting industries, set up effluent treatment plants.

---

### 4. National Green Tribunal (NGT)

**Established**: 2010 (National Green Tribunal Act, 2010)

**Jurisdiction**: Environmental disputes under:
- EPA, 1986
- Water Act, 1974
- Air Act, 1981
- Forest Conservation Act, 1980
- Wildlife Protection Act, 1972
- Biodiversity Act, 2002
- + 6 more laws

**Who Can File**:
- Aggrieved person
- Any person for substantial question relating to environment (PIL-type jurisdiction)

**Bench Structure**:
- **Principal Bench**: Delhi
- **Regional Benches**: Bhopal, Pune, Kolkata, Chennai

**Procedure**:
- File application with prescribed fee (₹1,000 for individuals)
- No lawyer mandatory (can file yourself)
- NGT hears matter summarily (faster than regular courts)
- Can award compensation, issue directions

**Appeal**: To Supreme Court (within 90 days).

**Advantage Over Regular Courts**:
- **Faster**: Supposed to decide within 6 months
- **Expert Members**: Judicial + environmental scientists
- **Simplified Procedure**: Less formal than courts

**See**: **CIVIC-NGT** protocol for detailed NGT procedure.

---

### 5. Criminal Prosecution (Private Complaint)

**If Police Don't Register FIR**:
File **private complaint** before Magistrate under CrPC Section 200.

**Process**:
1. Draft complaint alleging offense under EPA/Water Act/Air Act
2. File before **Judicial Magistrate First Class (JMFC)**
3. Magistrate examines complainant on oath
4. If prima facie case → Issue process (summon accused)
5. Trial proceeds

**Disadvantage**: Lengthy process. Criminal prosecution takes years.

## Common Environmental Violations & Remedies

### Problem 1: Industrial Air Pollution

**Violation**: Factory emitting smoke/particulates beyond prescribed standards.

**Remedy**:
1. **Complaint to SPCB**: Most effective first step
2. **RTI to SPCB**: Check if factory has valid CTO, last inspection date, violation status
3. **PIL to HC/SC**: If SPCB inactive
4. **NGT Application**: For closure direction + compensation

**Legal Ground**: Air Act violation, Article 21 violation.

---

### Problem 2: River/Water Pollution

**Violation**: Industry discharging untreated effluent into river.

**Remedy**:
1. **SPCB Complaint** with photos, water samples (if possible)
2. **PIL**: Seeking closure + pollution abatement
3. **NGT**: Compensation on Polluter Pays Principle

**Legal Ground**: Water Act Section 24, Article 21 violation.

**Case Law**: *M.C. Mehta (Ganga)* - SC ordered closure of polluting tanneries.

---

### Problem 3: Illegal Construction in Forest/Protected Area

**Violation**: Construction/mining in forest without Forest Clearance.

**Remedy**:
1. **Complaint to Forest Department** (District Forest Officer)
2. **PIL to HC** seeking demolition, restoration
3. **NGT Application**

**Legal Ground**: Forest Conservation Act Section 2 violation.

**Case Law**: *T.N. Godavarman* - SC banned all non-forest activity in forests without Central Government approval.

---

### Problem 4: Noise Pollution

**Violation**: Loudspeakers beyond permissible decibel limits, especially at night.

**Remedy**:
1. **Police Complaint**: Police can seize loudspeakers (Noise Pollution Rules empower police)
2. **SPCB Complaint**
3. **Writ Petition** (if systematic violation, e.g., religious place using loudspeakers daily)

**Legal Ground**: Noise Pollution Rules, 2000.

**Case Law**: *Church of God v. KKR Majestic Colony* (2000) - SC restricted use of loudspeakers.

---

### Problem 5: Garbage Burning / Poor Waste Management

**Violation**: Municipality not collecting garbage, or citizens burning garbage in open.

**Remedy**:
1. **Complaint to Municipality** (Health Officer)
2. **Grievance Portal** (CPGRAMS / State portal)
3. **PIL**: Mandamus to municipality to comply with Solid Waste Management Rules

**Legal Ground**: Municipal duty under Solid Waste Management Rules, 2016.

**Case Law**: *Almitra Patel v. Union of India* (2000) - SC issued comprehensive directions on solid waste management.

---

### Problem 6: Illegal Mining/Quarrying

**Violation**: Mining without Environment Clearance, or mining in prohibited area.

**Remedy**:
1. **Complaint to District Collector / Mining Department**
2. **PIL to HC** seeking stoppage, restoration, penalty
3. **NGT**

**Legal Ground**: Mines and Minerals Act, EIA Notification violation, Forest Act (if forest area).

**Case Law**: *Samaj Parivartana Samudaya v. State of Karnataka* (2013) - SC banned mining in Western Ghats eco-sensitive zones.

## Strategic Tips

### 1. Use RTI for Evidence

**Before Filing PIL/NGT Application**:
File RTI to:
- SPCB: Industry's consent status, inspection reports, violation notices
- Forest Department: Clearances granted, encroachment details
- Municipality: Waste management contracts, sanitation records

**RTI Responses = Strong Evidence in Court.**

---

### 2. Document Violations

- **Photos/Videos**: Pollution, illegal activity (timestamped)
- **Media Reports**: News coverage of issue
- **Independent Tests**: Water/soil/air samples (if affordable)
- **Affidavits**: From affected persons

**Better Evidence = Higher Success Chance.**

---

### 3. Explore NGT First (Before HC)

**Why NGT**:
- Faster than HC
- Lower court fee (₹1,000 vs. ₹5,000+ in HC)
- Expert members (understand technical environmental issues)

**When HC Instead of NGT**:
- Issue involves constitutional questions (Articles 14, 21)
- Need interim stay urgently (NGT sometimes slower in granting stay)

---

### 4. Seek Compensation (Not Just Injunction)

**On Polluter Pays Principle**:
Ask for:
- Cost of ecological restoration
- Compensation to affected persons
- Exemplary damages (to deter future violations)

**Example**: NGT ordered mining company to pay ₹100 crore for illegal mining + ecological damage.

---

### 5. Collaborate with Environmental NGOs

**NGOs Specializing in Environmental Law**:
- Centre for Science and Environment (CSE)
- Legal Initiative for Forest and Environment (LIFE)
- Environics Trust
- Bombay Environmental Action Group (BEAG)

**Benefits**: Technical expertise, legal support, credibility with courts.

## Disclaimer

**This protocol provides information about environmental law. It does NOT constitute legal advice.**

**Key Points**:
- Environmental violations are criminal offenses (imprisonment + fine)
- Pollution Control Boards are first authority (exhaust before courts)
- NGT is specialized forum for environmental disputes
- PIL powerful tool but requires bonafide intent + strong evidence
- Compensatory damages available on Polluter Pays Principle

**Environmental protection is both citizen's duty (Article 51A(g)) and right (Article 21). Exercise it responsibly.**
