# Administrative Tribunals - Specialized Adjudicatory Forums

## Purpose
Understand administrative tribunal system in India, jurisdiction of major tribunals (CAT, NCLT, ITAT, DRT), when to approach tribunals vs. courts, procedure, appeals, and judicial review limitations.

## Activation Triggers
- Government service matter (transfer, promotion, disciplinary action)
- Company law dispute (oppression, winding up, insolvency)
- Tax dispute (income tax, GST, customs)
- Debt recovery matter (bank loan default)
- Intellectual property dispute (patent, trademark)
- Consumer complaint (NCDRC)
- Need specialized, faster, cheaper forum than regular courts

---

## Constitutional Framework

### Article 323A: Administrative Tribunals

**Text** (Article 323A(1)):
> "Parliament may, by law, provide for the adjudication or trial by administrative tribunals of disputes and complaints with respect to recruitment and conditions of service of persons appointed to public services..."

**Administrative Tribunals Act, 1985** enacted under this.

**Tribunals Established**:
- **Central Administrative Tribunal (CAT)**: Central Government employees
- **State Administrative Tribunals (SATs)**: State Government employees (in some states)

---

### Article 323B: Tribunals for Other Matters

**Text** (323B(2)):
Parliament/State Legislature may provide for tribunals for:
- Taxation
- Foreign exchange
- Industrial and labour disputes
- Land reforms
- Ceilings on urban property
- Elections
- Food
- **And other matters**

**Tribunals Established Under This**:
- Income Tax Appellate Tribunal (ITAT)
- Customs, Excise and Service Tax Appellate Tribunal (CESTAT)
- National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT)
- Debt Recovery Tribunal (DRT)
- Securities Appellate Tribunal (SAT)
- Telecom Disputes Settlement and Appellate Tribunal (TDSAT)
- Competition Appellate Tribunal (COMPAT)
- Intellectual Property Appellate Board (IPAB)
- National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (NCDRC)
- National Green Tribunal (NGT)
- Armed Forces Tribunal (AFT)
- Railway Claims Tribunal

## Rationale for Tribunals

### Why Tribunals (Not Regular Courts)?

**1. Specialized Expertise**:
Technical matters (tax, company law, IP) need expert adjudicators, not general civil judges.

**2. Faster Disposal**:
Courts overburdened (3+ crore pending cases). Tribunals handle specific domains, faster.

**3. Reduced Costs**:
Lower court fees, simplified procedure, less lawyer dependence (in some tribunals).

**4. Accessibility**:
Multiple benches across country (e.g., CAT has 17 benches vs. 25 HCs).

**5. Less Formalistic**:
Not strictly bound by Evidence Act/CPC (though follow principles of natural justice).

---

### Constitutional Validity

**S.P. Sampath Kumar v. Union of India** (1987):
SC upheld validity of tribunals under Articles 323A, 323B.

**BUT**: **L. Chandra Kumar v. Union of India** (1997) - Landmark Case:
SC held:
1. ✓ Tribunals valid
2. ✗ **Cannot exclude HC's judicial review** (Article 226) - Unconstitutional to bar writ jurisdiction completely
3. ✓ Direct appeals to HC can be barred (i.e., must first appeal to tribunal's appellate authority)
4. ✗ Writ jurisdiction of HC under Article 226 **cannot be ousted**

**Implication**:
- Must exhaust tribunal remedy first
- But if tribunal violates jurisdiction/natural justice, can file writ in HC

## Major Tribunals - Overview

### 1. Central Administrative Tribunal (CAT)

**Jurisdiction**: Service matters of Central Government employees (recruitment, promotion, disciplinary action, pension, etc.)

**Statute**: Administrative Tribunals Act, 1985

**Benches**: 17 across India (Principal Bench: Delhi)

**Who Can File**:
- Central Government employee
- Retired employee (pension matters)

**Timeline**: No strict timeline, but faster than civil courts (typically 1-2 years vs. 5-10 in HC)

**Appeal**: High Court (under Article 226) OR directly to Supreme Court (Article 136 SLP)

**Details**: See sub-section below.

---

### 2. National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT)

**Jurisdiction**:
- Company law matters (under Companies Act, 2013)
- Insolvency & bankruptcy (under Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code, 2016)
- Oppression and mismanagement
- Merger, amalgamation
- Winding up

**Statute**: Companies Act, 2013

**Benches**: 15+ (Principal Bench: Delhi)

**Who Can File**:
- Shareholders (oppression/mismanagement)
- Creditors (insolvency)
- Company (mergers, schemes)

**Appeal**: National Company Law Appellate Tribunal (NCLAT) → Supreme Court

**Timeline**: IBC cases must be resolved within 180 days (extendable to 330 days).

---

### 3. Income Tax Appellate Tribunal (ITAT)

**Jurisdiction**: Income tax disputes

**Statute**: Income Tax Act, 1961

**Benches**: 60+ (all major cities)

**Who Can File**:
- Taxpayer (appeal against income tax order)
- Income Tax Department (appeal against CIT(A) order)

**Procedure**:
1. File return
2. Assessment by Assessing Officer
3. Appeal to Commissioner (Appeals) - **CIT(A)**
4. Appeal to **ITAT**
5. Appeal to HC (only on substantial question of law)
6. Appeal to SC (if high-value / important legal question)

**Timeline**: 1-3 years (varies by bench backlog)

---

### 4. Debt Recovery Tribunal (DRT)

**Jurisdiction**: Recovery of debts due to banks and financial institutions (loans above ₹20 lakh - earlier ₹10 lakh)

**Statute**: Recovery of Debts Due to Banks and Financial Institutions Act, 1993 (RDDBFI Act)

**Benches**: 39 (across India)

**Who Can File**:
- Banks / Financial Institutions (for loan recovery)

**Who Defends**: Borrower

**Remedy**: DRT can attach property, order sale, recovery.

**Appeal**: Debt Recovery Appellate Tribunal (DRAT) → High Court (Article 227) / Supreme Court (Article 136)

**Timeline**: Supposed to be fast (within 180 days), but backlog delays.

---

### 5. Customs, Excise and Service Tax Appellate Tribunal (CESTAT)

**Jurisdiction**: Customs, central excise, service tax disputes

**Statute**: Customs Act, 1962; Central Excise Act, 1944

**Benches**: 8 (major cities)

**Who Can File**:
- Importer/Exporter (customs dispute)
- Manufacturer (excise dispute)
- Service provider (service tax - pre-GST)

**Appeal**: High Court (substantial question of law)

**Post-GST**: For GST disputes, appellate structure different (GST Appellate Tribunal - not yet fully operational as of 2024; appeals currently to HC).

---

### 6. Securities Appellate Tribunal (SAT)

**Jurisdiction**: Appeals against SEBI orders (securities market violations)

**Statute**: Securities and Exchange Board of India Act, 1992

**Bench**: Mumbai

**Who Can File**: Any person aggrieved by SEBI order

**Appeal**: Supreme Court

**Timeline**: Supposed to dispose within 6 months.

---

### 7. National Consumer Disputes Redressal Commission (NCDRC)

**Jurisdiction**: Consumer complaints (value >₹10 crore - earlier ₹1 crore)

**Statute**: Consumer Protection Act, 2019

**Hierarchy**:
1. **District Consumer Forum**: Up to ₹1 crore
2. **State Consumer Commission**: ₹1 crore - ₹10 crore (+ appeals from District Forum)
3. **National Commission (NCDRC)**: >₹10 crore (+ appeals from State Commission)

**Location**: NCDRC in Delhi

**Who Can File**: Consumer (buyer of goods/services)

**Against**: Seller, manufacturer, service provider

**Relief**: Replacement, refund, compensation

**Appeal**: Supreme Court

**Simplified Procedure**: Can file yourself, no advocate mandatory, low court fee.

---

### 8. Intellectual Property Appellate Board (IPAB)

**Status**: **Abolished in 2021** (Tribunals Reforms Act, 2021)

**Current Position**: IP appeals now go directly to **High Courts** (patent, trademark, copyright appeals).

---

### 9. Competition Appellate Tribunal (COMPAT)

**Status**: **Abolished in 2017**

**Current Position**: Appeals from Competition Commission of India (CCI) go to **Supreme Court** directly.

---

### 10. Armed Forces Tribunal (AFT)

**Jurisdiction**: Service matters of armed forces personnel (army, navy, air force)

**Statute**: Armed Forces Tribunal Act, 2007

**Benches**: 17 (Principal Bench: Delhi)

**Who Can File**: Armed forces personnel (serving or retired)

**Appeal**: Supreme Court (under Article 136)

---

### 11. Railway Claims Tribunal

**Jurisdiction**: Claims against railways (compensation for death/injury to passengers, loss of goods, accidents at unmanned railway crossings)

**Statute**: Railway Claims Tribunal Act, 1987

**Benches**: 17

**Who Can File**: Passengers, goods consignors, victims of railway accidents

**Advantage**: Simpler than civil suit, faster.

**Appeal**: High Court (Article 227) / Supreme Court (Article 136)

---

## CAT - Detailed Procedure

### Central Administrative Tribunal (CAT)

**Who Can Approach CAT**:
- **Central Government employees** (civilian): Ministries, departments, PSUs (if "State" under Article 12)
- **Retired employees**: Pension disputes

**Matters Covered** (Section 14, Administrative Tribunals Act):
- Recruitment
- Promotion
- Seniority
- Transfer
- Disciplinary action (suspension, dismissal, removal)
- Pension
- Allowances, pay scales
- Service conditions

**Matters NOT Covered**:
- Armed forces personnel (have Armed Forces Tribunal)
- Members of All India Services (can approach CAT OR HC - choice)

---

### CAT Benches

**Principal Bench**: Delhi

**16 Regular Benches**:
Ahmedabad, Allahabad, Bangalore, Chandigarh, Chennai, Cuttack, Ernakulam, Guwahati, Hyderabad, Jabalpur, Jaipur, Jammu, Jodhpur, Kolkata, Lucknow, Mumbai

**Circuit Benches** (sit periodically): Madurai, Patna, Port Blair, Ranchi, Srinagar

**Territorial Jurisdiction**: Determined by place of posting / cause of action.

---

### Procedure in CAT

**Step 1: Draft Original Application (OA)**

**Format**:
```
BEFORE THE CENTRAL ADMINISTRATIVE TRIBUNAL
[BENCH NAME]

ORIGINAL APPLICATION NO. _____ OF 2024

[Applicant Name, Designation, Office] ... Applicant

Versus

[Union of India / Authority Name] ... Respondent(s)
```

**Contents**:
1. Applicant's details (name, service, posting)
2. Respondent(s)
3. Facts (chronological)
4. Grounds (why impugned order illegal - violation of rules, natural justice, arbitrary, etc.)
5. Relief sought
6. Affidavit verifying facts
7. Annexures (impugned order, service records, correspondence)

---

**Step 2: Filing Fee**

- ₹50 (individuals)
- ₹500 (associations/unions)

**Fee Exemption**: Some categories (SC/ST, ex-servicemen, widows) may be exempt.

---

**Step 3: File Application**

- **In person**: At CAT Registry (bench having jurisdiction)
- **By post**: Registered post to Registrar, CAT [Bench]

**Documents Required**:
- Application (4 copies)
- Annexures (4 sets)
- Affidavit
- Vakalatnama (if filing through advocate)
- Demand draft (filing fee)

---

**Step 4: Admission & Notice**

- Registry scrutinizes application
- If complete → Registers, issues notice to respondents
- Respondents file reply (usually within 4 weeks)

---

**Step 5: Interim Relief (If Sought)**

If applicant seeks stay of impugned order (e.g., suspension revoked, transfer stayed):
- File **interim application (IA)**
- CAT may grant interim stay if prima facie case + balance of convenience

**Example**: Employee suspended → Files CAT application + IA for stay of suspension → CAT may stay suspension till final hearing.

---

**Step 6: Hearing**

- **Simplified Procedure**: Not strictly bound by CPC/Evidence Act
- **Proceeds on affidavits** (usually no oral evidence)
- Arguments by applicant's advocate and respondent's advocate (usually Government Counsel)
- CAT Bench (1 Judicial Member + 1 Administrative Member) hears

---

**Step 7: Order**

**CAT can**:
- Quash impugned order (if illegal)
- Direct respondent to take specific action (promote, reinstate, grant pension)
- Award costs
- Dismiss application (if no merit)

**Timeline**: No strict timeline, but typically 1-2 years (faster than HC).

---

**Step 8: Execution**

If respondent doesn't comply with CAT order:
- File **execution application** (EA)
- CAT can issue contempt notice

---

### Appeal from CAT

**Direct Appeal to High Court**: Abolished (after L. Chandra Kumar case).

**Current Position**:
1. **Writ to HC** (Article 226): On limited grounds (jurisdictional error, violation of natural justice)
2. **Direct to SC** (Article 136 SLP): Rare (SC selectively entertains)

**Preferred Route**: File writ in HC → Appeal to SC (if HC dismisses).

## NCLT - Detailed Procedure

### National Company Law Tribunal (NCLT)

**Jurisdiction**:
1. **Company Law Matters** (Companies Act, 2013)
2. **Insolvency & Bankruptcy** (IBC, 2016)

**Common Cases**:
- Oppression and mismanagement (shareholders' rights violated)
- Mergers, amalgamations, schemes of arrangement
- Reduction of capital
- Winding up
- Corporate insolvency resolution

---

### Procedure for Insolvency (IBC)

**When**: Creditor cannot recover debt (default ≥₹1 lakh - earlier ₹1 lakh, now as low as ₹1,000 for certain cases).

**Step 1: File Application**

**Who Can File**:
- **Financial Creditor** (banks, debenture holders)
- **Operational Creditor** (suppliers, employees, service providers)
- **Corporate Debtor** itself (voluntary insolvency)

**To**: NCLT bench having jurisdiction over corporate debtor's registered office.

**Documents**:
- Application
- Record of default (bank statement showing NPA, invoice unpaid, etc.)
- Financial statements of corporate debtor

---

**Step 2: Admission**

**Timeline**: NCLT must admit/reject within **14 days**.

If admitted:
- **Moratorium** declared (all legal proceedings against corporate debtor stayed)
- **Interim Resolution Professional (IRP)** appointed
- IRP takes over management from existing directors

---

**Step 3: Corporate Insolvency Resolution Process (CIRP)**

**Timeline**: **180 days** (extendable by 90 days, max 330 days total - strict).

**IRP Tasks**:
- Constitute Committee of Creditors (CoC)
- Invite resolution plans
- CoC evaluates plans, votes (75% majority needed)

---

**Step 4: Approval of Resolution Plan**

**If Resolution Plan Approved** by CoC (75% vote):
- NCLT approves plan
- Debtor company revived under new management/ownership
- Creditors recover partial dues as per plan

**If No Resolution Plan / Plan Rejected**:
- NCLT orders **Liquidation**
- Company assets sold, proceeds distributed to creditors

---

**Step 5: Appeal**

**From NCLT**: Appeal to **NCLAT** (within 30 days)

**From NCLAT**: Appeal to **Supreme Court** (within 45 days)

---

### Procedure for Oppression & Mismanagement

**When**:
- Majority shareholders/directors misuse powers
- Minority shareholders' rights violated
- Company's affairs conducted in oppressive manner

**Who Can File** (Section 241, Companies Act):
- Members (minimum 10% shareholding OR 100 members, whichever is less)
- Central Government

**Relief**:
- Regulate company's conduct
- Remove directors
- Terminate agreements
- In extreme cases: Winding up

**Timeline**: No strict timeline (can take 2-5 years).

## Strategic Considerations

### Tribunal vs. Court - Choice

**Prefer Tribunal When**:
- Matter falls under tribunal's jurisdiction (e.g., service matter → CAT, tax → ITAT)
- Seek specialized expertise (company law, tax, etc.)
- Cost-effective (lower fees, sometimes can self-represent)
- Faster disposal (relatively - though tribunals also have backlogs)

**Prefer Court (HC/SC) When**:
- Constitutional question (Articles 14, 21)
- Challenge law's validity (tribunals can't declare law unconstitutional)
- Tribunal doesn't have jurisdiction
- Need writ powers (mandamus, certiorari)

---

### Exhausting Tribunal Remedy

**General Rule** (L. Chandra Kumar):
Must exhaust tribunal remedy before approaching HC in writ.

**Exception** (HC Will Entertain Writ Despite Tribunal Remedy Available):
1. **Jurisdictional Error**: Tribunal itself lacks jurisdiction
2. **Violation of Natural Justice**: Tribunal violated fair hearing principles
3. **Perversity**: Tribunal order perverse, against evidence
4. **Delay**: Tribunal takes inordinately long (remedy ineffective)

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**1. Filing in Wrong Forum**:
Check jurisdiction carefully (e.g., Central Govt employee → CAT, State Govt employee → SAT/HC).

**2. Missing Limitation Period**:
Each tribunal has limitation (CAT: 1 year from cause of action; NCLT-IBC: within limitation of suit). **Late filing = Rejection.**

**3. Incomplete Application**:
Tribunals reject if documents incomplete. Attach all annexures.

**4. Not Seeking Interim Relief**:
If order causes immediate harm (suspension, dismissal), file interim application along with main application.

**5. Ignoring Alternative Remedy**:
If departmental appeal available (e.g., appeal to higher officer), exhaust first (unless futile).
