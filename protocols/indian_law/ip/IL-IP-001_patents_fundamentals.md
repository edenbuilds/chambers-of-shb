## Purpose
Comprehensive coverage of Indian patent law under the Patents Act 1970 (as amended 2005, 2016, 2020), including patentability criteria (Sections 2(1)(j), 3, 4), registration procedure, patent rights, duration, and recent developments including compulsory licensing and pharmaceutical patents post-TRIPS compliance.

---

## 1. LEGISLATIVE FRAMEWORK

### 1.1 Constitutional and International Basis

**Constitution**: Patents fall under **Entry 49 List I (Union List)** - Union has exclusive legislative power

**TRIPS Agreement 1995** (Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights):
- India WTO member since 1995
- Obligation to comply with TRIPS minimum standards
- **2005 Amendment**: Introduced product patents for pharmaceuticals/chemicals (previously only process patents)

**Paris Convention 1883**: Priority rights, national treatment

### 1.2 Patents Act 1970 - Major Amendments

**2005 Amendment** (TRIPS Compliance):
- Section 3(d): Added to prevent evergreening (minor modifications)
- Product patents for pharmaceuticals/chemicals introduced
- Extended patent term to **20 years** from filing date

**2016 Amendment**:
- Expedited examination (Section 11-B)
- Startup fee benefits
- Working statement simplified (Form 27)

**2020 Amendment** (Patent Rules):
- Video conferencing for hearings
- Reduced timelines for opposition/responses

## 2. PATENTABILITY CRITERIA (SECTION 2(1)(j))

### 2.1 Definition of "Invention"

**Section 2(1)(j)**: "Invention" means a new product or process involving an **inventive step** and capable of **industrial application**.

**Three Requirements (Cumulative)**:
1. **Novelty** (New)
2. **Inventive Step** (Non-obvious)
3. **Industrial Application** (Useful)

### 2.2 Novelty (Sections 2(1)(l), 13)

**Section 2(1)(l) - "New Invention"**:
Invention NOT anticipated by **prior publication** or **prior public use** in India or elsewhere.

**Prior Art** (Section 13):
- Any knowledge, publication, use **anywhere in the world**
- Before **priority date** or filing date of patent application

**Global Novelty Standard**:
- Publication in any country (not just India) destroys novelty
- Example: Patent granted in USA for invention X → Same invention NOT novel in India

**Grace Period**: **None** (unlike USA 12-month grace period)
- Disclosure by inventor before filing → Destroys novelty
- **Best Practice**: File patent application BEFORE any public disclosure

**Exceptions to Novelty Objection**:
- Disclosure at **recognized international exhibition** (Section 30) - if filed within 12 months
- Disclosure before **learned society** OR published in transactions (Section 30) - if filed within 12 months

### 2.3 Inventive Step / Non-Obviousness (Section 2(1)(ja))

**Section 2(1)(ja)**: Feature that involves **technical advance** compared to existing knowledge or having **economic significance** or both, making invention **not obvious** to person skilled in art.

**Test**:
1. **Technical Advance**: Improvement over prior art (better efficiency, new function, superior performance)
2. **Economic Significance**: Commercial value, cost reduction
3. **Non-Obvious**: Person having **ordinary skill in art** would NOT have arrived at invention as obvious next step

**"Person Skilled in Art"**: Hypothetical person with average knowledge/skill in relevant technical field

**Example**:
- Prior Art: Drug X treats disease Y
- Invention: Combining Drug X + Drug Z treats Y more effectively
- Question: Would skilled person obviously try this combination?
  - If YES → NOT inventive ❌
  - If NO (unexpected synergistic effect) → Inventive ✅

**Evergreening Prevention** (Section 3(d) - Pharmaceutical Focus):
"Mere discovery of new form of known substance NOT patentable UNLESS it results in **enhancement of known efficacy**."

**Application**: Prevents patenting of:
- New salt/ester/polymorph of known drug (unless enhanced efficacy proved)
- New dosage form (sustained release) of known drug (unless enhanced efficacy)

**Landmark Case - *Novartis v. Union of India* (2013 SC)**:
- Novartis sought patent for beta-crystalline form of imatinib mesylate (Gleevec)
- Supreme Court: Section 3(d) violated (no enhanced **therapeutic** efficacy over known imatinib)
- Improved bioavailability alone ≠ enhanced efficacy (must show better clinical outcome)

### 2.4 Industrial Application (Section 2(1)(ac))

**Section 2(1)(ac)**: Invention capable of being **made or used in industry**.

**Broad Interpretation**: "Industry" includes agriculture, commerce, services (not just manufacturing)

**Requirement**:
- Invention must be **useful** (have practical utility)
- NOT purely theoretical/abstract

**Example**:
- Chemical compound with no known use → NOT capable of industrial application ❌
- Chemical compound as pharmaceutical ingredient → Industrial application ✅

## 3. NON-PATENTABLE SUBJECT MATTER (SECTIONS 3, 4)

### 3.1 Section 3 - Excluded Inventions

**Section 3(a) - Frivolous/Contrary to Law**:
Invention frivolous or contrary to natural laws

**Section 3(b) - Harmful to Public/Immoral**:
Invention causing serious prejudice to human/animal/plant life, health, or environment

**Section 3(c) - Scientific Principles/Abstract Theories**:
- Mathematical methods, algorithms
- Business methods, computer programs **per se**
- **Note**: Computer programs **with technical application** MAY be patentable (*Ferid Allani v. Union of India* 2019)

**Section 3(d) - New Form of Known Substance** (Evergreening):
Mere discovery of new form of known substance UNLESS enhanced efficacy

**Section 3(e) - Admixtures**:
Mere admixture without synergistic effect (combining ingredients with no new property)

**Section 3(f) - Known Process/Machine/Apparatus**:
Mere rearrangement/duplication

**Section 3(g) - Agricultural/Horticultural Methods**:
Methods of agriculture/horticulture

**Section 3(h) - Medical Treatment Methods**:
- Method of treatment of humans/animals
- **BUT**: Pharmaceutical products (drugs) ARE patentable (only method of treatment excluded)

**Section 3(i) - Plants/Animals**:
Plants, animals in whole or part (seeds, varieties, species)
- **Exceptions**: Microorganisms, non-biological/microbiological processes

**Section 3(j) - Traditional Knowledge**:
Invention being traditional knowledge (oral or otherwise)

**Section 3(k) - Computer Programs Per Se**:
Computer programs per se (algorithms, software as such)
- **But**: Software with **technical contribution/industrial application** patentable (inventions "involving" computer programs)

**Section 3(m) - Atomic Energy**:
Inventions related to atomic energy (Section 20 Atomic Energy Act jurisdiction)

**Section 3(n) - Biological Process**:
Traditional/biological process for propagation/multiplication of plants

**Section 3(p) - Aggregation/Presentation of Information**:
Mere scheme, rule, method of performing mental act, playing game

### 3.2 Section 4 - Plant Varieties and Atomic Energy

**Section 4**: Plant varieties NOT patentable (governed by Protection of Plant Varieties and Farmers' Rights Act 2001)

## 4. PATENT REGISTRATION PROCEDURE

### 4.1 Patent Application (Sections 7-11)

**Who Can Apply** (Section 6):
- True and first inventor (or legal representative)
- Assignee of invention
- Legal representative of deceased inventor

**Forms**:
- **Form 1**: Application for Patent
- **Form 2**: Provisional/Complete Specification
- **Form 3**: Statement and Undertaking (Section 8 - foreign applications)
- **Form 5**: Declaration of Inventorship

**Provisional vs. Complete Specification**:
- **Provisional** (Section 9(1)): Filed to secure priority date, broad description (no claims)
  - Must file complete within **12 months** or application deemed withdrawn
- **Complete** (Section 9(3)): Detailed description + claims defining invention scope

**Priority Date** (Section 11):
- **Indian application**: Filing date (provisional OR complete)
- **Convention application**: Foreign filing date (if filed in India within 12 months under Paris Convention)

### 4.2 Examination (Sections 11-B, 12-14)

**Publication** (Section 11-A):
- Application published **18 months** after priority date (in patent journal)
- Early publication possible on request (Form 9)

**Request for Examination** (Section 11-B):
- **Mandatory** within **48 months** of priority date (or application deemed withdrawn)
- Examination fee payable

**Examination Report** (Section 14):
- Patent Office issues First Examination Report (FER) identifying objections
- Applicant must respond within **6 months** (or 12 months from filing)

**Common Objections**:
- Lack of novelty, inventive step, industrial application
- Non-patentable subject matter (Section 3)
- Insufficient disclosure (Section 10)
- Claims not clear/supported by description

**Response Strategy**:
- **Amendments** to claims/description (narrow scope, clarify, add technical details)
- **Arguments** distinguishing prior art, demonstrating inventive step
- **Evidence** (affidavits, technical reports, comparative data)

### 4.3 Grant or Refusal (Sections 15, 25)

**Grant** (Section 43):
If objections satisfied → Patent granted
- Patent Certificate issued (Form 7)
- Patent details published in journal

**Refusal** (Section 15):
If objections not satisfied → Application refused

**Pre-Grant Opposition** (Section 25(1)):
- **Any person** may file opposition **after publication** (18 months) but before grant
- Grounds: Non-patentability (Section 3), lack of novelty/inventive step, insufficiency, non-disclosure of foreign applications

**Hearing** (Section 14):
Applicant and opponent given oral hearing opportunity

## 5. PATENT RIGHTS AND DURATION

### 5.1 Exclusive Rights (Section 48)

**Patent grants exclusive right** to:
1. **Make** the patented product
2. **Use** the patented product
3. **Offer for sale** the patented product
4. **Sell/import** the patented product
5. **License** others (grant permission for above acts)

**For Process Patents**:
- Right to use process
- Right to prevent use of process
- Right to prevent use/sale of product **directly obtained by patented process**

### 5.2 Duration (Section 53)

**20 years** from **filing date** (priority date for convention applications)

**NOT extendable** (unlike trademarks which are renewable indefinitely)

**Calculation Example**:
- Filing date: January 1, 2020
- Patent expires: December 31, 2039 (20 years from filing)

### 5.3 Annual Renewal Fees (Section 53)

**Payment Required**: Annually from **3rd year** onwards

**Consequences of Non-Payment**:
- **6-month grace period** with additional fee
- If not paid within grace period → Patent **lapses**
- Restoration possible within 18 months (Section 60 - if failure unintentional)

## 6. WORKING OF PATENTS (SECTION 84-92)

### 6.1 Section 146 Working Statement

**Obligation** (Section 146):
Patentee must file annual statement of **working of patent** in India (Form 27)

**Due Date**: Within 6 months of end of each calendar year (by June 30 each year)

**Purpose**: Monitor whether patent worked in India (compulsory licensing ground)

**Content**:
- Quantity of patented product manufactured in India
- Imports (if any)
- Licenses granted

### 6.2 Compulsory Licensing (Sections 84-92)

**Grounds** (Section 84(1)):
After **3 years** from grant, compulsory license may be granted if:
1. Reasonable requirements of public **not satisfied** (product not available/affordable), OR
2. Patented invention **not worked in India** (no manufacture), OR
3. Patented product **not available at reasonably affordable price**

**Procedure**:
- Application to Patent Controller
- Patentee given hearing opportunity
- Controller may grant license with conditions (royalty rate, territory, duration)

**Landmark Case - *Natco v. Bayer* (2013)**:
- First compulsory license granted post-2005 Amendment
- Bayer's kidney/liver cancer drug Nexavar (sorafenib tosylate)
- Grounds: Not available at reasonably affordable price (₹2.8 lakhs/month), not worked in India
- Compulsory license granted to Natco at **6% royalty**

**Government Use** (Section 100):
- Government may use patented invention for its own purposes
- Royalty paid to patentee (negotiated or determined by Government)

## 7. POST-GRANT OPPOSITION (SECTION 25(2))

**Any Person** may file opposition **after grant** within **12 months** of grant

**Grounds**: Same as pre-grant opposition (Section 25(1))
- Non-patentability
- Lack of novelty, inventive step
- Wrongful obtainment
- Insufficiency/non-disclosure

**Hearing**: Both parties heard

**Revocation Possible**: Patent may be revoked if grounds proved

## 8. PHARMACEUTICAL PATENTS - SPECIAL CONSIDERATIONS

### 8.1 Section 3(d) Application

**Purpose**: Prevent evergreening (extending patent life through minor modifications)

**Test**: New form must show **enhanced known efficacy**
- For drugs: Enhanced **therapeutic** efficacy (better clinical outcome)
- Improved bioavailability alone ≠ enhanced efficacy (*Novartis* 2013)

**Examples**:
- New salt of known drug → NOT patentable (unless enhanced efficacy) ❌
- New polymorph (crystalline form) → NOT patentable (unless enhanced efficacy) ❌
- New combination of known drugs → Patentable if synergistic effect (enhanced efficacy) ✅

### 8.2 TRIPS Flexibilities

**Bolar Provision** (Section 107-A):
- Exceptions to patent infringement for **regulatory approval purposes**
- Generic manufacturers can conduct studies/trials before patent expiry (for marketing authorization)

**Parallel Imports** (Section 107-A(b)):
- Import of patented product from abroad (where lawfully sold) NOT infringement
- Exhaustion of rights doctrine

## 9. PRACTICAL GUIDANCE

### 9.1 Patent Search Strategy

**Before Filing**:
1. **Prior Art Search**: Databases (Indian Patent Office, USPTO, EPO, Google Patents)
2. **Freedom to Operate (FTO) Search**: Ensure commercialization doesn't infringe others' patents
3. **Patentability Opinion**: From patent agent/attorney

**Databases**:
- Indian Patent Office (ipindiaservices.gov.in)
- USPTO (patents.google.com)
- Espacenet (European Patent Office)

### 9.2 Drafting Claims

**Independent Claims**: Broad scope (cover invention essence)
**Dependent Claims**: Narrow scope (specific embodiments, fall-back positions)

**Best Practices**:
- Draft **multiple independent claims** (different aspects of invention)
- Use functional language where possible (broader protection)
- Include fall-back positions (if broad claims rejected)

### 9.3 Timeline Management

| Milestone | Deadline | Consequence |
|-----------|----------|-------------|
| Complete specification after provisional | 12 months | Application deemed withdrawn |
| Request for examination | 48 months from priority date | Application deemed withdrawn |
| Response to FER | 6 months (or 12 months from filing) | Application deemed abandoned |
| Pre-grant opposition | After publication, before grant | Patent may not be granted |
| Post-grant opposition | 12 months from grant | Patent may be revoked |
| Annual renewal fees | From 3rd year, 6-month grace period | Patent lapses |
