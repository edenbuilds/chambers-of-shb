## Purpose
Comprehensive coverage of Indian trademark law under Trademarks Act 1999 (as amended 2010, 2017, 2021), including trademark registrability, registration procedure, rights, infringement, passing off remedy, and well-known marks protection.

---

## 1. LEGISLATIVE FRAMEWORK

### 1.1 Definition of Trademark (Section 2(1)(zb))

**"Trademark"** means a mark capable of:
1. Being represented **graphically** (visually perceptible)
2. Capable of **distinguishing** goods/services of one person from others

**Includes**:
- Shape of goods
- Packaging
- Combination of colors

**"Mark"** (Section 2(1)(m)): Device, brand, heading, label, ticket, name, signature, word, letter, numeral, shape of goods, packaging, combination of colors, or any combination thereof

### 1.2 Types of Marks

**Word Marks**: Text-based (brand names, slogans)
- Example: "TATA", "COCA-COLA"

**Device/Logo Marks**: Graphic symbols, logos
- Example: Nike swoosh, Apple bitten apple

**Combined Marks**: Word + device
- Example: "McDonald's" text + golden arches

**Shape Marks**: 3D shapes (product shape, packaging)
- Example: Coca-Cola bottle shape

**Sound Marks**: Audio (jingles, tunes)
- Registrable if graphically represented (notation/sonogram)
- Example: Yahoo yodel, Intel chime

**Smell/Scent Marks**: Fragrances (limited acceptance in India)

**Certification Marks** (Section 2(1)(e)): Certify origin, quality, accuracy
- Example: ISI mark, Woolmark

**Collective Marks** (Section 2(1)(g)): Used by members of association
- Example: CPA (Chartered Accountants association mark)

**Well-Known Marks** (Section 2(1)(zg)): High reputation, extensive use
- Protection beyond registered classes

## 2. REGISTRABILITY CRITERIA

### 2.1 Absolute Grounds for Refusal (Section 9)

**Section 9(1) - Inherently Non-Distinctive Marks**:

**(a) Not Capable of Distinguishing**:
Mark lacks distinctiveness (cannot identify source)

**(b) Descriptive Marks**:
Marks designating kind, quality, quantity, intended purpose, value, geographical origin, time of production

**Examples**:
- "COLD" for refrigerators ❌ (descriptive of quality)
- "BEST" for any product ❌ (laudatory/non-distinctive)
- "MANGALORE" for mangoes from Mangalore ❌ (geographical origin)

**(c) Generic/Common Marks**:
Marks consisting exclusively of marks/indications that have become **customary in current language/trade**

**Example**: "Escalator" (originally TM, now generic)

**(d) Shapes**:
- Shape resulting from **nature of goods** (e.g., football shape for football)
- Shape necessary to obtain **technical result** (functional shape)
- Shape giving **substantial value** to goods (ornamental shape)

**Proviso - Acquired Distinctiveness**:
Marks under Section 9(1)(b), (c) **MAY be registered** if acquired **distinctiveness through use** (secondary meaning)

**Test for Acquired Distinctiveness**:
- Extensive use (duration, volume)
- Advertising expenditure
- Market surveys showing public association
- Registration in other countries

**Example**: "TATA" (surname, descriptive) acquired distinctiveness through 150+ years use

### 2.2 Relative Grounds for Refusal (Section 11)

**Section 11(1) - Identical/Deceptively Similar**:
Mark shall NOT be registered if:
- **Identical** with earlier trademark (same class), OR
- **Deceptively similar** (likely to confuse/deceive)

**Deceptive Similarity Test** (*Amritdhara Pharmacy v. Satya Deo Gupta* 1963 SC):
Marks must be compared as **wholes** (not dissected)
- Visual similarity (appearance)
- Phonetic similarity (sound when spoken)
- Conceptual similarity (idea suggested)

**Average Consumer Test**:
- Person of **average intelligence and imperfect recollection**
- First impression (not side-by-side comparison)

**Examples**:
- COLGATE vs. COALGATE → Deceptively similar ✅ (phonetically similar)
- PARLE vs. PARLE-G → NOT deceptively similar ❌ (different visual, established co-existence)

**Section 11(2) - Similarity in Related Goods**:
Mark may be refused if similar to earlier mark in **related goods/services** (not necessarily same class)

**Section 11(6) - Identical/Similar to Well-Known Mark**:
Mark identical/similar to **well-known mark** (even different class) → Refusal
- Protection **beyond registered classes**
- Prevents dilution of well-known marks

**Examples of Well-Known Marks** (recognized by Indian courts):
- GOOGLE, MICROSOFT, COCA-COLA, BMW, ROLEX

### 2.3 Other Grounds for Refusal

**Section 9(2) - Prohibited Marks**:
- **(a)** Contains scandalous/obscene matter
- **(b)** Prohibited under **Emblems and Names (Prevention of Improper Use) Act 1950** (government emblems, UN flag, Red Cross)

**Section 9(3) - Geographical Indications**:
Marks consisting of geographical indications (protected under GI Act 1999)

**Example**: "DARJEELING" for tea (protected GI)

## 3. NICE CLASSIFICATION (SECTION 9-A)

### 3.1 Classes of Goods and Services

**45 Classes Total**:
- **Classes 1-34**: Goods
- **Classes 35-45**: Services

**Common Classes**:
- Class 5: Pharmaceutical products
- Class 9: Electronics, software
- Class 25: Clothing
- Class 35: Advertising, business management services
- Class 42: Scientific/technological services, IT services

**One Application Per Class**:
Separate applications for each class (separate fees)

**Cross-Class Protection**:
Registration in Class A does NOT automatically prevent registration of similar mark in Class B (unless well-known mark)

## 4. TRADEMARK REGISTRATION PROCEDURE

### 4.1 Application (Section 18)

**Form TM-A**: Application for Trademark Registration

**Information Required**:
- Applicant details (name, address, nationality)
- Trademark representation (image/text)
- **Class** of goods/services
- **Date of first use** (if claiming prior use)
- Priority claim (if Convention application)

**Fees**: Based on class and applicant type
- Individuals/startups: Lower fees
- Others: Higher fees

### 4.2 Examination (Section 18(3))

**Trademark Registry** examines application for:
- **Absolute grounds** (Section 9) - Distinctiveness, descriptiveness
- **Relative grounds** (Section 11) - Similarity to earlier marks
- **Formal requirements** - Proper classification, representation

**Examination Report**:
If objections → Issued to applicant

**Response** (Section 18(4)):
Within **1 month** (extendable)
- Arguments addressing objections
- Evidence of acquired distinctiveness (if Section 9(1) objection)
- Amendments to specification of goods/services

**Hearing**: If objections persist, oral hearing granted

### 4.3 Publication and Opposition (Sections 20-21)

**Advertisement** (Section 20):
If accepted → Published in **Trademark Journal** (weekly)

**Opposition** (Section 21):
**Any person** may file opposition within **4 months** of publication (extendable 1 month)

**Grounds for Opposition**:
- Non-registrability (Sections 9, 11)
- Prior use by opponent
- Passing off likelihood

**Counter-Statement**: Applicant files counter-statement (within 2 months)

**Evidence**: Both parties file evidence

**Hearing**: Registrar hears both sides

**Decision**:
- Opposition upheld → Application **refused**
- Opposition dismissed → Application proceeds to **registration**

### 4.4 Registration (Section 23)

**Certificate of Registration** issued

**Rights Effective**: From **date of application** (priority given from filing date)

**Validity**: **10 years** from date of registration

**Renewal**: Indefinitely renewable every 10 years (unlike patents with fixed 20-year term)

## 5. TRADEMARK RIGHTS (SECTION 28)

### 5.1 Exclusive Rights

**Registered Proprietor** has **exclusive right**:
- Use trademark in relation to registered goods/services
- **Prevent unauthorized use** by others (infringement action)
- **License** trademark to others (Section 48)
- **Assign/sell** trademark (Section 37)

**Infringement** (Section 29):
Unauthorized use of **identical/deceptively similar** mark by third party

### 5.2 Permitted Uses (No Infringement)

**Section 30 - Defenses to Infringement**:

**(1) Registered Proprietor's Use**:
Use of registered trademark by proprietor or licensee

**(2)(a) Own Name/Address**:
Use of person's **own name/address** (bona fide)
- Example: Mahendra Singh Dhoni uses "MS Dhoni" (his name) → Not infringement of MS Dhoni trademark

**(2)(b) Descriptive Use**:
Use indicating **kind, quality, quantity, intended purpose** (not as trademark)
- Example: "Extra Strong Coffee" (descriptive, not trademark use)

**(2)(c) Comparative Advertising**:
Use for **honest** comparison (not disparaging)

**Section 33 - Prior Use Defense**:
User who used mark **continuously from before registration** may continue (limited to extent of prior use)

---

## 6. INFRINGEMENT (SECTION 29)

### 6.1 Types of Infringement

**Section 29(1) - Identical Mark, Identical Goods**:
Use of **identical mark** for **identical goods/services** → Infringement (strict liability)

**Section 29(2) - Similarity Causing Confusion**:
Use of **similar mark** for **similar goods/services** if likely to cause **confusion** (including association)

**Test**:
- Similarity of marks (visual, phonetic, conceptual)
- Similarity of goods/services
- Likelihood of confusion among average consumers

**Section 29(4) - Well-Known Marks**:
Use of identical/similar mark (even different goods) to take **unfair advantage** or cause **detriment** to well-known mark

**Section 29(9) - Spoken Use**:
Oral reference to trademark (phonetic infringement)

### 6.2 Remedies (Sections 134-135)

**Civil Remedies** (Section 134):
- **Injunction** (interim and permanent) - Prevent further infringement
- **Damages** - Compensatory damages OR account of profits (disgorgement)
- **Delivery up** - Surrender infringing goods for destruction

**Criminal Remedies** (Section 103):
- Imprisonment up to **3 years**
- Fine up to ₹**2 lakhs**
- Both (for serious/repeat offenses)

**Border Measures** (Sections 139-141):
- Customs authorities may **seize** infringing goods at border (upon notice from trademark owner)

## 7. PASSING OFF (COMMON LAW REMEDY)

### 7.1 Basis and Requirements

**NOT Statutory** (common law tort remedy)

**Available to**:
- Unregistered trademark users
- Registered trademark owners (concurrent remedy with infringement)

**Test** (*Reckitt & Colman v. Borden Inc.* "Jif Lemon" case):
Three requirements:
1. **Goodwill/Reputation** in the mark
2. **Misrepresentation** by defendant (likely to deceive)
3. **Damage** (actual or likely) to plaintiff's goodwill

**Goodwill**: Acquired through use (not registration)
- Extensive use in India
- Public association with plaintiff

**Misrepresentation**:
- Use of similar mark/trade dress/packaging
- Creating impression of connection with plaintiff

**Damage**:
- Loss of sales, confusion, dilution of distinctiveness

### 7.2 Passing Off vs. Infringement

| Feature | Passing Off | Trademark Infringement |
|---------|-------------|------------------------|
| **Basis** | Common law (tort) | Statute (Trademarks Act) |
| **Registration Required** | NO | YES (for infringement suit) |
| **Proof** | Goodwill + Misrepresentation + Damage | Use of registered mark without authorization |
| **Burden** | Plaintiff must prove all three | Lighter burden (registration = prima facie validity) |
| **Scope** | Unlimited (any goods/services if confusion) | Limited to registered classes (except well-known marks) |

## 8. WELL-KNOWN MARKS (SECTION 11(6)-(9))

### 8.1 Protection

**Enhanced Protection**:
- Protection **beyond registered classes**
- NO need to prove use/registration in all classes

**Example**: GOOGLE (registered for IT services) protected against GOOGLE shoes, GOOGLE cars (not IT services)

**Rationale**: Prevent **dilution**, **tarnishment**, **unfair advantage** of well-known marks' reputation

### 8.2 Determining Well-Known Status (Section 11(6))

**Factors** (non-exhaustive):
- Knowledge/recognition among **relevant public**
- Duration, extent, geographical area of **use**
- Duration, extent of **promotion/advertising**
- Records of **successful enforcement** (litigation success)
- **Registrations** in multiple jurisdictions

**Procedure**:
- Proprietor may claim well-known status in application/opposition
- Registrar/court determines based on evidence

**Central List**: Trademark Registry maintains list of recognized well-known marks (updated periodically)

---

## 9. RENEWAL AND RESTORATION (SECTIONS 25, 60)

### 9.1 Renewal (Section 25)

**Validity**: **10 years** from registration date

**Renewal**: Indefinitely renewable every 10 years

**Renewal Application**:
- File within **6 months** before expiry (early renewal)
- **6-month grace period** after expiry (with additional fee)

**Failure to Renew**: Trademark removed from register

**Effects of Removal**:
- Rights cease
- Mark available for third-party registration (after 1 year - Section 25(3))

### 9.2 Restoration (Section 60)

**If removed for non-renewal**:
- Restoration application within **1 year** of removal
- Discretionary relief (if Registrar satisfied non-renewal unintentional/reasonable)

## 10. ASSIGNMENT AND LICENSING

### 10.1 Assignment (Section 37)

**Trademark assignable** (registered or unregistered)

**With or Without Goodwill**:
- **With goodwill**: All rights transferred (new owner exclusive user)
- **Without goodwill**: Rare (prior owner may continue use)

**Recordation** (Section 45):
- Assignment must be **recorded** with Trademark Registry (within 6 months)
- Unrecorded assignment → Assignee cannot sue for infringement

**Formalities**:
- Written deed
- Stamp duty payable (state-specific)

### 10.2 Licensing (Section 48)

**Registered User** (now called "Permitted User"):
Licensee using trademark under license from proprietor

**Recording**:
- License recorded with Registry
- Protects licensee's rights (can sue infringers if registered)

**Quality Control**:
- Proprietor must exercise **quality control** over licensee's use (prevent abandonment)
- Naked licensing (no control) → Trademark may be cancelled (Section 47)

## 11. PRACTICAL GUIDANCE

### 11.1 Trademark Selection Strategy

**Strong Marks** (Highly Distinctive):
- **Fanciful**: Invented words (KODAK, XEROX)
- **Arbitrary**: Common words unrelated to product (APPLE for computers)

**Weaker Marks** (May face objections):
- **Suggestive**: Hint at product nature (COPPERTONE for suntan lotion)
- **Descriptive**: Describe product (COLD for refrigerators) - Need acquired distinctiveness
- **Generic**: Common name (cannot be trademark)

**Best Practice**: Choose fanciful/arbitrary marks for easier registration and stronger protection

### 11.2 Clearance Search

**Before Adoption**:
- **Trademark search**: Indian Trademark Registry database, pending applications
- **Domain name search**: Availability of .in, .com domains
- **Business name search**: ROC company name database
- **Common law search**: Google, industry databases (unregistered marks)

**Clearance Opinion**: From trademark attorney assessing registration/infringement risks

### 11.3 Use Requirements

**Actual Use**:
- Trademark must be **used** (not merely registered warehoused)
- **Non-use for 5 years** → Vulnerable to removal for non-use (Section 47)

**Bona Fide Use**:
- Commercial use in ordinary course of trade
- Token use NOT sufficient

**Maintaining Rights**:
- Continuous use
- Quality control (if licensed)
- Timely renewal
- Enforcement against infringers (prevent genericization)

## 12. DOMAIN NAMES AND TRADEMARKS

### 12.1 Cybersquatting

**Definition**: Registering domain name identical/similar to another's trademark to profit

**Remedies**:
1. **Trademark infringement** (if domain use amounts to trademark use)
2. **Passing off** (if goodwill + misrepresentation + damage)
3. **UDRP** (Uniform Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy) - ICANN mechanism
   - For .com, .net, .org (international TLDs)
4. **INDRP** (Indian Domain Name Dispute Resolution Policy)
   - For .in domains (administered by NIXI)

**Test** (*Yahoo Inc. v. Akash Arora* 1999 Delhi HC - first Indian cybersquatting case):
- Plaintiff has trademark rights
- Defendant's domain identical/deceptively similar
- Defendant registered in bad faith (to profit/prevent trademark owner)

---

## 13. LIMITATIONS AND EXCEPTIONS

**Concurrent Use** (Section 12):
If two parties using identical/similar marks in different geographical areas **bona fide** before registration → Both may register (with territorial limitations)

**Honest Concurrent Use**: Long, honest, simultaneous use by multiple parties

**Defensive Trademark** (Section 2(1)(ea)):
Well-known trademark registered for non-used classes (to prevent third-party use)
