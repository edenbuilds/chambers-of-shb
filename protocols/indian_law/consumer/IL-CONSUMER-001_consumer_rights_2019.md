## Purpose
Comprehensive coverage of Consumer Protection Act 2019 which replaced 1986 Act, including consumer rights, definitions, Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA), product liability, e-commerce regulations, unfair trade practices, and regulatory framework.

---

## 1. LEGISLATIVE FRAMEWORK

### 1.1 Evolution

**Consumer Protection Act 1986**: Pioneering consumer rights legislation (repealed)

**Consumer Protection Act 2019** (effective July 20, 2020):
- Modernized framework for digital economy
- Established Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA)
- Introduced product liability provisions
- E-commerce protections
- Enhanced penalties
- Streamlined dispute redressal

**Constitutional Basis**: Entry 7 List III (Concurrent List) - Contracts including sale of goods, partnerships

### 1.2 Key Definitions (Section 2)

**"Consumer"** (Section 2(7)):
Person who:
1. **Buys goods** for consideration (paid or deferred/installments), OR
2. **Hires/avails services** for consideration

**AND** uses goods/services for **self-consumption** (NOT for:
- ❌ Commercial resale
- ❌ Commercial purpose/earning livelihood)

**Includes**:
- Actual buyer/user
- Beneficiary (gift recipient if goods bought for gift)
- Online purchasers

**Excludes**:
- Buyers for resale (traders, retailers)
- Buyers for commercial manufacturing (raw materials)

**"Goods"** (Section 2(19)): Every kind of movable property (exclud ing actionable claims, money, stocks/shares)

**"Service"** (Section 2(42)): Any service made available to potential users (excluding free services, personal services under contract of employment)

**"Defect"** (Section 2(10)): Fault, imperfection, shortcoming in quality, quantity, potency, purity, standard

**"Deficiency"** (Section 2(11)): Inadequacy in quality, nature, manner of service performance

**"Unfair Trade Practice"** (Section 2(47)): Trade practice that adopts unfair method/deceptive practice (false representation, misleading advertisements)

## 2. CONSUMER RIGHTS (SECTION 2(9))

### 2.1 Six Fundamental Rights

**1. Right to Safety** (Section 2(9)(a)):
Protection against goods/services hazardous to life/property

**2. Right to Information** (Section 2(9)(b)):
Information about quality, quantity, potency, purity, standard, price (for informed choice)

**3. Right to Choose** (Section 2(9)(c)):
Access to variety of goods/services at competitive prices (no forced purchase)

**4. Right to be Heard** (Section 2(9)(d)):
Representation in consumer forums/bodies

**5. Right to Seek Redressal** (Section 2(9)(e)):
Redress against unfair trade practices/exploitation (through consumer forums)

**6. Right to Consumer Education** (Section 2(9)(f)):
Awareness of rights and remedies

---

## 3. CENTRAL CONSUMER PROTECTION AUTHORITY (CCPA)

### 3.1 Establishment and Powers (Sections 10-32)

**Section 10**: Central Government shall establish CCPA

**Composition**:
- Chief Commissioner (head)
- Additional, Joint, Deputy, Assistant Commissioners

**Functions** (Section 18):
1. **Inquiry** into violations of consumer rights (suo motu or on complaint)
2. **Recall products** (if unsafe)
3. **Refund orders** (if defective goods)
4. **Penalty** (if unfair trade practices, false advertisements)
5. **Product safety standards** enforcement
6. **File complaints** with consumer commissions on behalf of consumers

**Powers**:
- Issue **safety notices** (warn public)
- Order **discontinuance of unfair trade practices**
- Impose **penalties** (up to ₹10 lakhs for false advertisements)
- **Recall/refund/replacement** orders

**False/Misleading Advertisements** (Section 21):
- Manufacturer/endorser penalty: Up to ₹**10 lakhs** (first offense), ₹**50 lakhs** (subsequent)
- **Ban** on endorsing product for up to 1 year (repeat offenses 3 years)

**Endorser Liability**: Celebrity endorsers liable for false/misleading advertisements (unless due diligence proved)

## 4. UNFAIR TRADE PRACTICES (SECTION 2(47))

### 4.1 Definition and Types

**Includes**:
1. **False representation** about goods/services (quality, grade, composition, standard)
2. **False representation** about sponsorship, approval, affiliation
3. **False/misleading facts** disparaging competitors' goods/services
4. **Bait advertising** (advertise goods at low price without intent to sell)
5. **Offering gifts/prizes** without intent to provide
6. **Withholding information** about goods/services (quality, fitness)

**Example Scenarios**:
- "Pure honey" containing added sugar ❌
- "Approved by XYZ authority" (false claim) ❌
- "50% off" without actual discount (misleading price) ❌
- Celebrity endorsing product making false health claims ❌

### 4.2 Remedies

**Consumer can**:
1. File complaint with **Consumer Commission** (District/State/National)
2. Complain to **CCPA** (for suo motu action)

**Reliefs**:
- Removal of defect/deficiency
- Replacement of goods
- Refund of price paid
- Compensation for loss/injury
- Discontinuance of unfair practice
- Penalty/punishment

## 5. PRODUCT LIABILITY (SECTIONS 82-87)

### 5.1 Strict Liability Framework

**New Provision** (NOT in 1986 Act):
Manufacturer, service provider, seller **strictly liable** for harm caused by defective product/deficiency in service

**"Product Liability"** (Section 2(34)): Liability of manufacturer, service provider, seller for damages for harm caused by defective product/deficiency

**Liable Parties**:
1. **Product manufacturer**
2. **Product service provider** (installer, assembler, repairer)
3. **Product seller** (retailer, distributor)

**Harm** (Section 2(21)): Personal injury, death, damage to property (excluding defective product itself)

**Example**:
- Defective pressure cooker explodes → Causes injury → Manufacturer liable for personal injury + property damage
- Defective car → Accident → Manufacturer liable

### 5.2 Grounds for Product Liability (Section 84)

**Manufacturer/Service Provider/Seller liable if**:
1. Product contains **manufacturing defect**
2. Product **defective in design**
3. Product deviates from **express warranty/representations**
4. Product does NOT contain **adequate instructions** for safe use
5. Product does NOT conform to **mandatory safety standards**

**No-Fault Liability**: Consumer NOT required to prove negligence (strict liability)

### 5.3 Defenses (Section 85)

**Product manufacturer/seller NOT liable if proves**:
1. Did NOT place product in **stream of commerce**
2. Defect did NOT exist when placed in stream of commerce
3. **Compliance with mandatory government standards** (does not negate other defenses)
4. **Express written** warning of danger (and consumer voluntarily accepted risk)
5. **State of scientific/technical knowledge** at time of supply did not permit discovery of defect

## 6. E-COMMERCE PROTECTIONS (SECTIONS 88-94)

### 6.1 E-Commerce Entity Obligations

**"E-Commerce"** (Section 2(16)): Buying/selling goods/services through digital/electronic network

**"E-Commerce Entity"** (Section 2(17)): Company owning, operating, managing digital/electronic facility/platform for e-commerce

**Mandatory Requirements** (Section 88):
1. **Legal name**, principal office address, website
2. **General terms and conditions** (prominently displayed)
3. **Grievance redressal mechanism** (details of grievance officer)
4. **Return/refund/exchange** policies
5. **Payment methods** (security details)
6. **Delivery/shipment** timelines
7. **Seller information** (for marketplace models - name, address, contact)

**Consumer Protection (E-Commerce) Rules 2020**:
- **Country of origin** marking (products)
- **Total price** display (including all charges)
- **No false reviews** (fake ratings/testimonials prohibited)
- **Warranties/guarantees** honoured
- **Grievance officer** appointment (respond within 48 hours)

### 6.2 Prohibited Practices

**Direct Selling** (Section 2(13)): Multi-level marketing, network marketing

**Pyramid Schemes** (illegal under Prize Chits and Money Circulation Schemes (Banning) Act 1978):
- Promise of quick returns through recruiting others
- Unsustainable business model
- CCPA can act against pyramid schemes disguised as direct selling

**Flash Sales**: Permissible if genuine (limited inventory), but NOT if artificial scarcity created to manipulate pricing

## 7. EXEMPTIONS AND LIMITATIONS

### 7.1 Services NOT Covered

**Free Services**: Services provided **free of charge** (no consideration)
- Example: Free education, free healthcare (government hospitals)

**Employment Contracts**: Personal services under employer-employee relationship

**BUT**:
- Government services for which **fee paid** (passport services, vehicle registration) → Covered ✅
- Educational institutions charging fees → Covered ✅ (*Maharshi Dayanand University v. Surjeet Kaur* 2010 SC)

### 7.2 Commercial Purpose Exclusion

**Buyer for commercial purpose** NOT consumer

**Test** (*Laxmi Engineering Works v. P.S.G. Industrial Institute* 1995 SC):
- If goods/services bought for **self-consumption** (personal/household use) → Consumer ✅
- If bought for **earning livelihood** by means of self-employment → Consumer ✅ (farmer buying tractor, professional buying computer)
- If bought for **commercial resale/manufacturing** → NOT consumer ❌

**Examples**:
- Doctor buying stethoscope for medical practice → Consumer ✅ (earning livelihood through self-employment)
- Shopkeeper buying goods for resale → NOT consumer ❌
- Farmer buying tractor for agriculture → Consumer ✅

## 8. COMPARISON: 2019 ACT VS. 1986 ACT

| Feature | 1986 Act | 2019 Act |
|---------|----------|----------|
| **CCPA** | No central authority | Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA) established |
| **Product Liability** | No specific provisions | Strict liability framework (Sections 82-87) |
| **E-Commerce** | No provisions | Comprehensive e-commerce protections (Sections 88-94) |
| **Pecuniary Jurisdiction** | Fixed (₹20 lakhs District, ₹1 crore State) | Increased (₹1 crore District, ₹10 crore State, beyond ₹10 crore National) |
| **Mediation** | No provision | Mediation as first step encouraged (Section 37) |
| **Penalties** | Lower | Enhanced (up to ₹50 lakhs for misleading ads) |
| **Endorser Liability** | No provision | Celebrities/endorsers liable for false ads |
| **Appeals** | To higher commission then National Commission | To State Commission, National Commission, then Supreme Court |

## 9. PRACTICAL GUIDANCE

### 9.1 Filing Consumer Complaint - Checklist

**Determine Jurisdiction**:
- Value ≤ ₹1 crore → **District Commission**
- Value > ₹1 crore but ≤ ₹10 crore → **State Commission**
- Value > ₹10 crore → **National Commission**

**Documents Required**:
- Complaint in prescribed form
- **Purchase receipt/invoice** (proof of transaction)
- **Warranty/guarantee card** (if applicable)
- **Correspondence** with seller/manufacturer (complaint emails, letters)
- Evidence of defect/deficiency (photos, expert reports)

**Complaint must state**:
- Consumer's name, address, contact
- Opposite party's (seller/manufacturer) details
- Facts of complaint (what, when, where)
- Relief sought (replacement, refund, compensation)
- Court fee (nominal - ₹100-500 depending on value)

### 9.2 Avoiding Unfair Trade Practices - Business Compliance

**For Businesses**:
1. **Accurate advertising**: No false/misleading claims
2. **Country of origin**: Mandatory marking (e-commerce)
3. **Total price transparency**: All charges disclosed upfront
4. **Warranty/guarantee**: Honour commitments
5. **Grievance redressal**: Functional grievance officer (48-hour response)
6. **Product safety**: Comply with BIS/statutory standards
7. **Endorsements**: Ensure celebrity endorsers have conducted due diligence
8. **Return/refund**: Clear policies, honor commitments

**CCPA Enforcement**: Non-compliance → Investigation → Penalty → Recall/refund orders
