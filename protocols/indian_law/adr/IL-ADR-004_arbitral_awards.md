## 1. MAKING OF ARBITRAL AWARD (SECTION 31)

### 1.1 Form and Content Requirements

**Section 31(1) - Written and Reasoned**:
Award must be:
- **In writing** (typed/printed, signed)
- **Reasoned** (unless parties agreed otherwise or interim award)
- **Signed by arbitrators** (majority signature sufficient for panel)

**Section 31(3) - Essential Elements**:
1. **Date** of award
2. **Place (seat)** of arbitration
3. **Names of parties**
4. **Statement of issues/disputes**
5. **Findings** on each issue
6. **Reasons** supporting findings (unless parties waived reasoning)
7. **Decision/relief** granted
8. **Costs** determination (Section 31(8))

**Language**: Same as arbitration proceedings (or as agreed by parties)

**Signature** (Section 31(4)):
- All arbitrators in tribunal shall sign
- If minority refuses to sign: Majority arbitrators note dissent, award valid with majority signatures

### 1.2 Types of Awards

**Final Award** (Section 32(1)):
- Conclusively determines all disputes submitted to arbitration
- Terminates arbitration
- Immediately enforceable (subject to challenge)

**Interim Award** (Section 31(6)):
- Decides preliminary issues (jurisdiction, admissibility, liability)
- Arbitration continues for remaining issues
- Example: Liability award (defendant liable for breach) → Quantum award later

**Partial Award** (Section 31(6)):
- Decides part of claims/disputes
- Example: Three disputes submitted → Award on two disputes → Final award on third later

**Award on Agreed Terms** (Section 30):
- Parties settle during arbitration
- Tribunal records settlement as consent award
- Same status as arbitral award (enforceable under Section 36)

**Difference**: Interim/partial vs. final:
- Final award terminates arbitration
- Interim/partial awards → Arbitration continues
- All types challengeable under Section 34 after final award

### 1.3 Timeline for Award (Section 29-A)

**12-Month Deadline**:
- From date of **completion of pleadings** (statement of claim + statement of defense)
- Extendable by **6 months** by consent of parties
- Beyond 18 months: **Court may extend** on sufficient cause

**Consequences of Delay**:
- Arbitrator's mandate may be **terminated** (Section 14(1)(a)) for unreasonable delay
- Arbitrator's **fees cease** after 12 months (unless extended)

**Time NOT Counted** (Exclusions):
- Period of Section 17 interim measure proceedings
- Delays attributable to parties (failure to submit documents, adjournment requests)

### 1.4 Costs (Section 31(8))

**Tribunal Shall Fix Costs** (mandatory):
- Arbitrator fees and expenses
- Legal fees of parties
- Administrative fees (institutional arbitration)
- Expert witness fees, travel costs
- Venue costs

**Costs Follow the Event** (general rule):
- Losing party pays costs
- Tribunal has discretion to apportion

**Frivolous Claims/Defense** (Section 31-A added 2015):
If claim or defense found to be **frivolous or vexatious**:
- Tribunal may impose **exemplary costs** (punitive element)
- Deters bad faith litigation tactics

**Fourth Schedule** (2015 Amendment):
- Model fee schedule (not binding, but guidance)
- Based on sum in dispute (₹1 crore dispute → ₹3-5 lakhs arbitrator fee approx.)

## 2. CHALLENGE TO ARBITRAL AWARD (SECTION 34)

### 2.1 Statutory Framework

**Section 34** - **ONLY** remedy against domestic arbitral award (no appeal)

**Nature of Review**:
- **Limited judicial review** (NOT appeal on merits)
- Narrow grounds (public policy, procedural fairness)
- NOT re-hearing of evidence

**Timeline**:
- Application within **3 months** of receipt of award
- Extendable by **30 days** (sufficient cause)
- **Total maximum: 3 months + 30 days** (no further extension per *Simplex Infrastructure v. UOI* 2019)

**Forum**:
- **Principal Civil Court** where arbitration seated
- Example: Delhi-seated arbitration → Delhi High Court (original jurisdiction)

### 2.2 Grounds for Setting Aside (Section 34(2))

**Two Categories**:
1. **Section 34(2)(a)**: Party must prove
2. **Section 34(2)(b)**: Court must find (suo motu)

**Section 34(2)(a) - Proving Grounds**:

**(i) Incapacity or Invalid Agreement**:
- Party under **incapacity** (minor, unsound mind) at time of arbitration agreement
- Arbitration agreement **not valid** under applicable law
  - Void (Section 10 ICA): coercion, fraud, illegal object
  - NOT stamped (inadmissible but curable by paying stamp duty)

**(ii) Lack of Notice or Inability to Present Case**:
- Party **not given proper notice** of:
  - Arbitrator appointment
  - Arbitration proceedings
  - Hearing dates
- Party **unable to present case** due to procedural unfairness
  - Example: Evidence excluded unfairly, hearing truncated

**(iii) Award Beyond Scope of Submission**:
- Award deals with dispute **not submitted to arbitration**
- Award deals with dispute **outside arbitration clause** scope
- **If separable**: Court may set aside only excess part (Section 34(4))

**(iv) Composition/Procedure Not Per Agreement**:
- Tribunal **composition** not as per parties' agreement or Act
  - Example: Sole arbitrator appointed when parties agreed on three
- **Arbitral procedure** violated parties' agreement or Act provisions
  - Example: Oral hearings denied when parties expected them

**Section 34(2)(b) - Court Finding Grounds**:

**(i) Subject Matter Not Arbitrable**:
- Dispute falls in **non-arbitrable** category (see IL-ADR-002 arbitrability analysis)
  - Criminal prosecution, matrimonial status, insolvency status, testamentary matters

**(ii) Award in Conflict with Public Policy of India**:

**"Public Policy" Defined** (Explanation 1 to Section 34(2)(b)(ii) added 2015):
- **(a) Induced by fraud or corruption**
- **(b) In contravention with fundamental policy of Indian law**
- **(c) In conflict with basic notions of morality or justice**
- **(d) Patent illegality appearing on face of award** (domestic awards ONLY)

**Patent Illegality** (Explanation 1 proviso):
- **Contravention of substantive law** of India (e.g., award violates ICA provisions)
- **Contravention of Limitation Act** 1963 (time-barred claim awarded)
- Award is **perverse or so irrational** that no reasonable person would have made it

**IMPORTANT LIMITATION** (2015 Amendment):
Patent illegality ground applies to:
- ✅ **Domestic arbitral awards** (purely Indian parties, Indian seat)
- ❌ **International commercial arbitrations** seated in India (conflicting views - *Ssangyong* 2019 vs. *Delhi Airport Metro* 2022)

**Narrow Interpretation** (*Ssangyong Engineering v. NHAI* 2019 SC):
- Public policy narrowly construed
- Not every error of law = patent illegality
- Must be **fundamental and palpable** error going to root of matter

### 2.3 Standard of Review

**Limited Review** (*Associate Builders v. DDA* 2015):
- Court does NOT re-appreciate evidence
- Court does NOT correct errors of fact or law (unless patent illegality for domestic awards)
- Court examines only **procedural fairness** and **public policy**

**Perversity Test** (*Ssangyong* 2019):
Award perverse ONLY if:
- Findings contrary to evidence (no evidence supporting)
- Award violates statutory provisions
- Award based on no legal principle

**Impossibility of Performance**:
If award directs performance physically/legally impossible → May be set aside (fundamental error)

## 3. ENFORCEMENT OF ARBITRAL AWARD (SECTION 36)

### 3.1 Award as Decree (Section 36(1))

**Section 36(1)**: "Where the time for making an application to set aside the arbitral award under section 34 has expired... the award shall be enforced under the Code of Civil Procedure, 1908 in the same manner as if it were a decree of the Court."

**Effect**:
- Award becomes **deemed decree** of court
- Executable under **CPC Order XXI** (execution procedures)
  - Attachment of property
  - Arrest and detention (rare, money decrees)
  - Appointment of receiver

### 3.2 Automatic Stay Removed (2015 Amendment)

**Pre-2015 Position**:
- Filing Section 34 application **automatically stayed** award enforcement
- Delay tactic (award creditors waited years)

**Post-2015 Position** (Section 36(2)):
- Filing Section 34 does **NOT automatically stay** enforcement
- Separate application for stay required

**Section 36(3) - Conditions for Stay**:
Court **may** grant stay if:
1. **Prima facie case** for setting aside award
2. **Balance of convenience** favors stay
3. **Irreparable injury** to applicant if stay not granted

**Court may impose conditions**:
- Deposit of **part/whole** award amount
- Furnish security/bank guarantee

**2021 Amendment - Exception** (Section 36(3) proviso):
If court is satisfied that deposit would cause **undue hardship**, may grant unconditional stay for:
- Micro/small enterprises (MSMEs)
- Startups
- "Reasons to be recorded"

**Practical Effect**:
- Award creditors can execute immediately after 3-month Section 34 period (unless stay obtained)
- Award debtors must show strong prima facie case and provide security to get stay

## 4. APPEAL (SECTION 37)

### 4.1 Appealable Orders

**Section 37(1)** - Appeal lies from:

**(a) Section 11(6) order** (refusing to appoint arbitrator)

**(b) Section 11(7) order** (deciding on arbitrator's jurisdiction under Section 16 at appointment stage)

**(c) Section 34 order**:
- Granting/refusing to set aside arbitral award
- **Most common appeal**

**(d) Section 36 order** (refusing to enforce award or granting/refusing stay)

**No Other Appeal**:
- No appeal from interim orders of tribunal (Section 17)
- No appeal from tribunal's preliminary jurisdiction ruling (Section 16) - wait until final award

### 4.2 Appellate Forum

**Domestic Arbitration**:
- Section 34 order by District Court → Appeal to **High Court**
- Section 34 order by High Court (if HC has original jurisdiction) → Appeal to **Division Bench of same HC**

**International Commercial Arbitration**:
- Section 34 order → Appeal to **High Court**

**Timeline**:
- Appeal within **90 days** (some HCs have 60 days under local rules)
- Condonation possible under Limitation Act Section 5

### 4.3 No Second Appeal (Section 50)

**Section 50**: "Notwithstanding anything contained in any other law for the time being in force, no second appeal shall lie from an order passed in appeal under this section, but nothing in this section shall affect or take away any right to appeal to the Supreme Court."

**Effect**:
- Appeal from District Court → High Court → **NO second appeal** to Supreme Court under CPC Section 100
- **Only SLP** (Special Leave Petition) under Article 136 Constitution to Supreme Court (discretionary)

**Finality**: Arbitral awards have greater finality than civil court decrees (which allow second appeals on substantial questions of law)

## 5. FOREIGN AWARDS ENFORCEMENT (PART II)

### 5.1 New York Convention Awards (Sections 44-52)

**Convention on Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards 1958** (New York Convention):
- India party since 1960
- 172 contracting states (as of 2024)

**"Foreign Award"** (Section 44):
Award made in territory of:
- New York Convention country, OR
- Country notified by India as reciprocating territory

**Enforcement Procedure** (Section 47):
- Application to competent court (where award debtor has assets)
- Produce:
  - **Original award** or certified copy
  - **Original arbitration agreement** or certified copy
  - Certified translation (if not in English)

**Conditions for Enforcement** (Section 48):
Award enforced **UNLESS** award debtor proves:

**(a) Party-Proving Grounds**:
1. **Incapacity** or **arbitration agreement invalid** under applicable law
2. **Lack of notice** or unable to present case
3. **Award beyond scope** of submission
4. **Composition/procedure not per agreement** or law of arbitral country
5. **Award not binding** or set aside/suspended by competent authority of award country

**(b) Court-Finding Grounds**:
1. Subject matter **not arbitrable** under Indian law
2. Enforcement **contrary to public policy** of India

**NO Patent Illegality** for Foreign Awards:
- Public policy limited to:
  - Fundamental policy of Indian law
  - Interests of India
  - Justice/morality
- **Renusagar Power* (1994) and *Shri Lal Mahal* (2014)

**Procedure**: Foreign award treated as decree of Indian court (enforceable under CPC)

### 5.2 Geneva Convention Awards (Sections 53-60)

**Geneva Convention 1927** (Protocol and Convention on Arbitration Clauses):
- Pre-dates New York Convention
- Limited applicability now (most countries adopted NYC)

**Fewer Countries**: ~40 countries (many also NYC parties, NYC prevails)

**Enforcement**: Similar procedure, slightly different grounds

**Practical Note**: New York Convention awards overwhelmingly more common in practice

## 6. SPECIFIC SCENARIOS

### 6.1 Consent Awards (Section 30)

**Parties Settle During Arbitration**:
- Request tribunal to record settlement as consent award
- Tribunal shall terminate proceedings and record settlement as award

**Effect**:
- Same status as regular arbitral award
- Enforceable under Section 36
- Challengeable under Section 34 (limited grounds - fraud, incapacity, non-arbitrability)

**Advantage Over Settlement Agreement**:
- Award immediately executable as decree
- Settlement agreement requires separate suit for enforcement (unless filed under Mediation Act 2023 Section 29)

### 6.2 Rectification/Interpretation/Additional Award (Section 33)

**Section 33(1) - Correction/Interpretation**:
Within **30 days** of award receipt, party may request tribunal to:
- **Correct** computation errors, clerical/typographical errors
- **Interpret** specific point/part of award (clarification)

**Section 33(3) - Additional Award**:
- If claims presented but **not decided** in award → Party may request additional award
- Within **60 days** of award receipt

**Tribunal Action**:
- Decide within **30 days** (extendable 30 days)
- Correction/interpretation/additional award has same status as original award

**Example**:
- Award grants ₹50 lakhs but computation error (should be ₹55 lakhs based on findings) → Correction
- Award silent on interest from award date → Request additional award on interest

### 6.3 Termination Without Award (Section 32(2))

**Arbitration Terminates Without Award**:
1. Parties agree to terminate
2. Claimant withdraws claim (unless respondent has legitimate interest in final settlement)
3. Tribunal finds continuation unnecessary/impossible

**Effect**: Arbitration ends, no award issued

**Costs**: Tribunal may still determine costs (Section 32(3))

## 7. CRITICAL CASE LAW

**Associate Builders v. DDA (2015) 3 SCC 49**:
- Limited scope of Section 34 review
- Not re-appreciation of evidence
- Public policy narrowly construed

**Ssangyong Engineering v. NHAI (2019) 15 SCC 131**:
- Patent illegality NOT ground for international commercial arbitrations seated in India
- Public policy: fundamental policy, justice, morality only
- Perversity test

**Project Director, NHAI v. M. Hakeem (2021) 9 SCC 1**:
- Section 11 orders (appointment) NOT appealable to Supreme Court (no substantial question of law)
- Reduces delays in arbitrator appointments

**N.N. Global Mercantile v. Indo Unique Flame (2021) 4 SCC 379**:
- Unstamped arbitration agreement valid but inadmissible
- Can be stamped later for enforcement

**Simplex Infrastructure v. Union of India (2019) 2 SCC 455**:
- 3 months + 30 days maximum for Section 34 application
- No further extension

**Vijay Karia v. Prysmian Cavi (2020) 11 SCC 1**:
- Emergency arbitrator orders enforceable under Section 17(2)

## 8. PRACTICAL GUIDANCE

### 8.1 Award Debtor Strategy (Resisting Enforcement)

**Immediate Actions After Award Receipt**:
1. **Analyze award** for Section 34 grounds (consult arbitration counsel)
2. **File Section 34** within 3 months (or 3 months + 30 days with extension application)
3. **Simultaneously apply for stay** (Section 36(3)) with:
   - Prima facie case demonstration
   - Offer security/deposit (if financially viable)

**Strategic Considerations**:
- **Frivolous challenges deter**: If award on strong legal/factual basis, challenge unlikely to succeed and incurs costs
- **Deposit requirement**: Be prepared to deposit substantial portion (courts often require 50-75% deposit for stay)
- **Time cost**: Section 34 + appeal takes 2-5 years; during this time, interest accrues on award amount

### 8.2 Award Creditor Strategy (Enforcing Award)

**Post-Award Actions**:
1. Wait for **3-month Section 34 period** to expire (if no challenge filed)
2. If Section 34 filed:
   - **Oppose stay application** vigorously (demonstrate no prima facie case, no irreparable injury)
   - Seek **security/deposit order** (if stay granted)
   - **Execute award** if stay not granted (initiate execution under CPC Order XXI)

**Execution Steps**:
1. File execution petition in court where award seated
2. Attach debtor's assets (bank accounts, immovable property)
3. If debtor absconding/diverting assets → Seek orders under Section 9 (preservation measures)

### 8.3 Drafting Award - Arbitrator's Perspective

**Best Practices for Arbitrators**:
1. **Detailed Reasoning**: Reduces Section 34 challenges (no "non-reasoned award" ground)
2. **Address All Issues**: Ensure no claim left undecided (avoid Section 33 additional award applications)
3. **Jurisdiction Findings**: Explicitly state tribunal's jurisdiction determination (Section 16 compliance)
4. **Costs Breakdown**: Itemize costs (arbitrator fees, party costs) clearly
5. **Compliance with Section 31**: Date, place, signatures, language
6. **Timely Award**: Meet 12-month deadline (avoid mandate termination risk)
