## Purpose
Comprehensive coverage of the Arbitration & Conciliation Act 1996 (A&C Act) framework, constitutional validity, amendments (2015, 2019, 2021), and foundational principles governing domestic and international commercial arbitration in India.

---

## 1. LEGISLATIVE FRAMEWORK

### 1.1 Constitutional Basis
**Article 246(2) - State List Entry 3**: Administration of justice, constitution and organization of courts

**Article 227**: High Court supervisory jurisdiction over arbitral tribunals (limited post-2015 Amendment)

**Judicial Pronouncements**:
- **Renusagar Power Co. v. General Electric Co. (1994)**: Arbitration as alternative adjudicatory mechanism
- **BALCO v. Kaiser Aluminum (2012)**: Part I applies to all arbitrations seated in India
- **Bharat Aluminium Co. v. Kaiser Aluminium (BALCO)**: Territorial nexus of seat determines Indian law applicability

### 1.2 Model Law Adoption
Based on **UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Arbitration 1985** (as amended 2006)

**Key Adaptations for Indian Context**:
- Part I (Sections 1-43): Domestic and international arbitrations seated in India
- Part II (Sections 44-60): Enforcement of foreign awards (New York Convention 1958, Geneva Convention 1927)
- Part III (Sections 61-66): Conciliation procedures

### 1.3 Major Amendments

**2015 Amendment (246th Law Commission Report)**:
- Section 2(2): Excluded Part I applicability to international commercial arbitrations seated outside India
- Section 11(6): Time limit for appointing arbitrator (30/60 days)
- Section 12: Mandatory disclosure of conflicts, Schedule V/VII grounds
- Section 17: Interim measures by tribunal enforceable as court orders
- Section 29-A: 12-month timeline for award (extendable 6 months)
- Section 34: Narrowed scope of challenge (patent illegality limited to domestic awards)
- Section 36: Automatic stay on award removed (need to apply separately)

**2019 Amendment**:
- Section 11(6-A): Arbitral institutions for appointment
- Section 42-A: Confidentiality obligation
- Section 43-J: Arbitration Council of India (ACI) establishment
- Eighth Schedule: Quality requirements for arbitral institutions

**2021 Amendment**:
- Section 36(3): Automatic stay if deposit waived for small value claims
- Section 43-J: ACI constitution and powers clarified
- Schedule: Qualifications and accreditation of arbitrators

## 2. SCOPE AND APPLICATION

### 2.1 Part I Application (Section 2(2))

**Applies to**:
- ✅ Domestic arbitrations (Indian parties, Indian seat)
- ✅ International commercial arbitrations **seated in India**
- ✅ Interim measures by Indian courts for arbitrations seated abroad (Section 9, if parties agree)

**Does NOT apply to**:
- ❌ International commercial arbitrations **seated outside India** (BALCO doctrine)
- ❌ Statutory arbitrations (unless parties agree - Section 2(3))

**Seat vs. Venue Distinction**:
- **Seat**: Legal/juridical home of arbitration, determines curial law (Part I applicable)
- **Venue**: Geographic location of hearings (may differ from seat)

**Determining Seat** (*BGS SGS SOMA JV v. NHPC Ltd.* 2020):
1. Express designation in agreement
2. Rules of arbitral institution chosen
3. Place of arbitration clause (rebuttable presumption)
4. Circumstances (location of cause of action, subject matter)

### 2.2 International Commercial Arbitration (Section 2(1)(f))

**Definition**: At least one party is:
- Individual who is national/resident of country other than India, OR
- Body corporate incorporated in country other than India, OR
- Company/association/body managed from outside India, OR
- Government of foreign country

**AND** dispute arises out of **commercial relationship** (Section 2(1)(c))

**Commercial Relationship** (broadly defined):
- Trade, commerce, supply of goods/services
- Investment, financing, banking
- Joint ventures, licensing
- Intellectual property
- Construction contracts
- Professional services
- Exploration/extraction of natural resources

### 2.3 Exclusions and Carve-Outs

**Non-Arbitrable Disputes** (Evolving Jurisprudence):

**Traditionally Non-Arbitrable**:
- ❌ **Criminal offenses** (prosecution, punishment)
- ❌ **Matrimonial disputes** (divorce, custody) - *A. Ayyasamy v. A. Paramasivam* (2016)
- ❌ **Guardianship** under Guardians and Wards Act
- ❌ **Insolvency and winding up** (corporate status questions) - BUT moratorium disputes arbitrable
- ❌ **Testamentary matters** (will validity, probate)
- ❌ **Tenancy governed by rent control laws** (eviction)

**Now Arbitrable (Expanded Scope)**:
- ✅ **Fraud allegations** if incidental to contract dispute (*Avitel Post Studioz v. HSBC PI Holdings* 2021 SC)
- ✅ **IP disputes** (infringement, licensing) - *Eros International v. Telemax* (2016)
- ✅ **Competition Act violations** if civil consequences of contract (*Vimal Kishor Shah v. Jayesh Dinesh Shah* 2016)
- ✅ **Shareholder disputes** (except oppression/mismanagement under Companies Act)
- ✅ **FEMA violations** (civil/contractual aspects)
- ✅ **Consumer disputes** (if bilateral arbitration clause, not adhesion contract)

**Test for Arbitrability** (*Booz Allen & Hamilton v. SBI Home Finance* 2011):
1. Does dispute involve **rights in personam** (between parties)? ✅ Arbitrable
2. Does dispute involve **rights in rem** (status/third-party rights)? ❌ Non-arbitrable

## 3. FOUNDATIONAL PRINCIPLES

### 3.1 Party Autonomy (Section 19(1))

**Freedom to determine**:
- Procedural rules (subject to Part I mandatory provisions)
- Rules of evidence
- Language of proceedings
- Seat and venue

**Mandatory Provisions (Cannot Be Contracted Out)**:
- Section 9: Interim measures by court
- Section 12: Independence and impartiality disclosure
- Section 16: Kompetenz-kompetenz (tribunal decides own jurisdiction)
- Section 17: Interim measures by tribunal
- Section 28: Tribunal to decide on equity if parties agree
- Section 29-A: 12-month timeline (extendable)
- Section 34: Challenge to award
- Section 36: Enforcement of award

### 3.2 Minimal Judicial Intervention (Section 5)

**"Notwithstanding anything contained in any other law for the time being in force, in matters governed by this Part, no judicial authority shall intervene except where so provided in this Part."**

**Limited Grounds for Court Intervention**:
1. **Section 8**: Refer parties to arbitration if valid agreement exists
2. **Section 9**: Interim measures before/during arbitration
3. **Section 11**: Appointment of arbitrator if mechanism fails
4. **Section 14**: Termination of arbitrator mandate
5. **Section 27**: Court assistance in taking evidence
6. **Section 34**: Challenge to award (narrow grounds)
7. **Section 37**: Appeal from Section 34 order

**Landmark Case - *SBP & Co. v. Patel Engineering Ltd.* (2005)**:
- Chief Justice of India (CJI) not bound by Section 5 (inherent administrative powers)
- Judicial authority = courts, NOT tribunals
- Arbitration inherently involves "judicial authority" acting under statute

### 3.3 Kompetenz-Kompetenz (Section 16)

**Principle**: Arbitral tribunal can rule on its own jurisdiction, including objections to:
- Existence or validity of arbitration agreement
- Scope of arbitrable disputes

**Section 16(1)**: Tribunal may rule on own jurisdiction

**Section 16(2)**: Plea of no jurisdiction must be raised **no later than submission of statement of defence**

**Section 16(3)**: Tribunal decides jurisdiction plea as preliminary question OR in final award

**Section 16(5)**: Tribunal decision on jurisdiction **NOT appealable** (challenge only under Section 34 after final award)

**Severability/Separability Doctrine (Section 16(1)(a))**:
- Arbitration clause treated as **independent agreement**
- Invalidity of main contract does NOT automatically invalidate arbitration clause
- Tribunal can adjudicate validity of main contract

**Example**: Contract void for fraud. Arbitration clause survives—tribunal can decide if fraud vitiates contract.

### 3.4 Seat-Centric Framework (BALCO Doctrine)

**BALCO v. Kaiser Aluminum (2012) - Paradigm Shift**:

**Pre-BALCO (Bhatia International Regime)**:
- Part I applied to ALL arbitrations involving Indian parties (even if seated abroad)
- Indian courts had jurisdiction for interim measures, appointment, challenge

**Post-BALCO (Current Law)**:
- Part I applies ONLY if seat is in India
- Seat determines curial law (law governing arbitration)
- Foreign-seated arbitrations: Part II only (enforcement of foreign awards)

**Determining Factor**: **Seat, not nationality of parties**

**Practical Implication**:
- Singapore-seated arbitration (Indian parties): Singapore Arbitration Act governs, Indian courts NO jurisdiction under Part I
- Mumbai-seated arbitration (foreign parties): Indian A&C Act Part I governs, Indian courts have jurisdiction

## 4. KEY STATUTORY PROVISIONS

### 4.1 Section 7: Arbitration Agreement

**Definition (Section 7(1))**: Agreement to submit present or future disputes to arbitration

**Forms (Section 7(3))**:
- In writing (document signed by parties)
- Exchange of letters, fax, email, electronic communication
- Exchange of pleadings in which arbitration clause alleged and not denied
- Reference in contract to document containing arbitration clause

**Incorporation by Reference (Section 7(5))**:
- Valid if main contract refers to arbitration clause in another document (e.g., charter party incorporating bill of lading terms)

**Pathological Clauses**: Defective arbitration clauses (vague institutions, contradictory rules)
- Courts adopt **principle of effectiveness** (give effect to arbitration intent if possible)
- *Inox Renewables v. Jayesh Electricals* (2021): Court-assisted interpretation to save agreement

### 4.2 Section 8: Power to Refer Parties to Arbitration

**Mandatory Reference**:
"A judicial authority, before which an action is brought in a matter which is the subject of an arbitration agreement shall, if a party to the arbitration agreement or any person claiming through or under him, so applies not later than the date of submitting his first statement on the substance of the dispute, then, notwithstanding any judgment, decree or order of the Supreme Court or any Court, refer the parties to arbitration unless it finds that prima facie no valid arbitration agreement exists."

**Stage for Application**: **Before submitting first statement on substance of dispute** (first written statement in suit)

**Standard of Review**: **Prima facie** existence of arbitration agreement (NOT detailed inquiry)

**Effect**: Judicial proceedings stayed/terminated, parties referred to arbitration

**Non-Signatory Binding** (*Chloro Controls (I) P. Ltd. v. Severn Trent Water Purification* 2013):
- Group of companies doctrine
- Conduct implying consent
- Assignment/succession

### 4.3 Section 9: Interim Measures by Court

**Before or During Arbitral Proceedings**:
Court may grant interim measures:
- Preservation, interim custody, sale of goods
- Securing amount in dispute
- Detention, preservation, inspection of property/documents
- Interim injunction
- Appointment of receiver
- Other interim measures (Section 9(1)(ii)(e))

**Post-Award**: Section 9 NOT available after award made (use Section 36 enforcement)

**Conditions**:
- Prima facie arbitration agreement exists
- Urgency/balance of convenience
- Court satisfied measure necessary

**Enforcement**: Section 9 order executable as court decree

**Alternative**: Section 17 interim measures by tribunal (enforceable as court order under Section 17(2))

### 4.4 Section 11: Appointment of Arbitrators

**Appointment Mechanism**:
1. **Parties' agreement** (Section 11(2)): Follow agreed procedure
2. **Default procedure** (Section 11(3)):
   - Sole arbitrator: Joint appointment by parties (30 days)
   - Three arbitrators: Each party appoints one, two appoint third (presiding)
3. **Failure of mechanism** (Section 11(4)-(6)): Court/designated arbitral institution appoints

**Designated Authority** (Pre-2015: Chief Justice; Post-2015: Designated Judge/Arbitral Institution):
- Supreme Court: Justice nominated by CJI
- High Court: Justice nominated by Chief Justice of HC
- Arbitral institution (if parties agree under Section 11(6-A))

**Timelines** (Section 11(13)):
- Application for appointment: Dispose within **60 days**

**Qualifications** (Section 11(8)):
- Person of any nationality (unless parties agree otherwise)
- For international commercial arbitration: Parties free to determine qualifications

**Ineligibility** (Schedule VII added by 2015 Amendment):
- Employee, consultant, advisor to party
- Manager, director, shareholder of party (substantial interest)
- Relationship that creates reasonable doubts about independence

### 4.5 Section 12: Disclosure and Challenge

**Mandatory Disclosure (Section 12(1))**:
Arbitrator must disclose in writing:
- Any circumstances likely to give rise to **justifiable doubts** as to independence or impartiality
- Disclosure continues throughout arbitration

**Challenge Grounds (Section 12(3) + Schedule V/VII)**:

**Schedule V (Grounds for Ineligibility)**:
1. Relationship/interest (employee, shareholder, advisor to party)
2. Prior involvement as representative, expert in same dispute
3. Currently advising party on dispute

**Schedule VII (Grounds for Reasonable Doubts)**:
1. Financial interest in outcome
2. Business relationship with party
3. Regular advice to party (though not in this dispute)
4. Previous expression of views on merits
5. Arbitrator appointed unilaterally by one party if institution not involved

**Procedure for Challenge (Section 13)**:
- Written statement of reasons within **15 days** of becoming aware
- If arbitrator doesn't withdraw or other party doesn't agree, arbitral tribunal decides
- If challenge rejected, party can apply to court within **30 days** of tribunal decision

### 4.6 Section 29-A: Time Limit for Award

**12-Month Deadline** (from date of completion of pleadings):
- Arbitral tribunal shall make award within 12 months
- Extendable by **6 months** by consent of parties
- Beyond 18 months: Court may extend (sufficient cause)

**Consequences of Delay**:
- Arbitrator's mandate may be terminated (Section 14(1)(a)) if unreasonable delay
- Arbitrator's fees cease after 12 months (unless extended)

**Exclusions** (Time not counted):
- Proceedings under Section 17 (interim measures by tribunal)

**Rationale**: Prevent indefinite arbitration, maintain arbitration as speedy alternative

### 4.7 Section 34: Application for Setting Aside Award

**Grounds for Challenge (Section 34(2))**:

**Sub-Section (a) - Party Proving**:
1. **Incapacity** (Section 34(2)(a)(i))
2. **Arbitration agreement invalid** under applicable law (Section 34(2)(a)(i))
3. **Lack of notice** or unable to present case (Section 34(2)(a)(ii))
4. **Award beyond scope** of submission to arbitration (Section 34(2)(a)(iii))
5. **Composition or procedure not per agreement** (Section 34(2)(a)(iv))

**Sub-Section (b) - Court Finding**:
1. **Subject matter not arbitrable** (Section 34(2)(b)(i))
2. **Award in conflict with public policy of India** (Section 34(2)(b)(ii)):
   - **Patent illegality** appearing on face of award (domestic awards only - post-2015)
   - Award induced by fraud/corruption
   - Award in contravention with fundamental policy of Indian law
   - Award in conflict with basic notions of morality/justice

**Patent Illegality (Explanation 1 to Section 34(2)(b)(ii))**:
- Contravention of substantive law OR
- Contravention of Limitation Act 1963 OR
- Award is perverse/irrational

**Clarification**: Patent illegality NOT grounds for foreign awards (Section 48) or international commercial arbitrations seated in India (conflicting precedents)

**Procedure**:
- Application within **3 months** of receipt of award (extendable 30 days)
- Court may remand to tribunal OR set aside award

**Standard**: **Narrow judicial review** (not appeal on merits)

### 4.8 Section 36: Enforcement of Award

**Post-2015 Amendment**: Automatic stay removed

**Current Position**:
- Award enforceable as decree (Section 36(1))
- If Section 34 application filed: Automatic stay **removed**
- Need to apply separately for stay under Section 36(2)

**Conditions for Stay (Section 36(3))**:
- Prima facie case for setting aside award
- Balance of convenience/irreparable injury
- May require deposit/security

**Exception (2021 Amendment - Section 36(3))**:
- Automatic stay if court waives deposit for "reasons to be recorded"

**Effect**: Award creditor can execute during pendency of Section 34 (unless stay granted)

## 5. INSTITUTIONAL VS. AD HOC ARBITRATION

### 5.1 Institutional Arbitration

**Arbitral Institutions**:
- MCIA (Mumbai Centre for International Arbitration)
- DIAC (Delhi International Arbitration Centre)
- ICA (International Centre for ADR, ICADR)
- SIAC (Singapore International Arbitration Centre) - foreign, often chosen
- ICC (International Chamber of Commerce) - foreign
- LCIA (London Court of International Arbitration) - foreign

**Advantages**:
- Administered by institution (case management, timelines)
- Pre-established rules (MCIA Rules, SIAC Rules, ICC Rules)
- Panel of arbitrators (expertise, vetted)
- Scrutiny of awards (institutional review)
- Logistical support (hearing venues, secretariat)

**Costs**: Higher administrative fees + arbitrator fees

### 5.2 Ad Hoc Arbitration

**Definition**: Parties create own procedure, appoint arbitrators directly

**Applicable Rules** (if parties choose):
- UNCITRAL Arbitration Rules 2013 (commonly adopted)
- Bespoke rules agreed by parties

**Advantages**:
- Flexibility (tailor procedure to dispute)
- Lower administrative costs (no institutional fees)
- Direct control by parties

**Disadvantages**:
- No institutional support (parties manage logistics)
- Risk of procedural deadlock
- Arbitrator appointment delays if mechanism fails

**Hybrid**: Ad hoc with institutional appointment (e.g., ad hoc arbitration, MCIA appoints arbitrator under Section 11(6-A))

## 6. COSTS AND FEES

### 6.1 Arbitrator's Fees

**No Statutory Schedule** (unlike court fees)

**Determination**:
- Agreement between parties and arbitrator
- Institutional rules (if institutional arbitration)
- Court determination (if dispute arises)

**Section 31(8)**: Award to fix costs (including arbitrator fees)

**Section 31-A**: Tribunal may order costs based on frivolous/vexatious claims/defenses

**Fourth Schedule (2015 Amendment)**: Model fee schedule (not binding)
- Based on sum in dispute (sliding scale)
- Example: ₹1 crore dispute → ₹3-5 lakhs arbitrator fee (approx.)

### 6.2 Costs of Arbitration

**Recoverable Costs** (Section 31(8)):
- Arbitrator fees and expenses
- Legal fees and expenses
- Administrative fees (institutional arbitration)
- Expert witness fees
- Venue costs

**Costs Follow the Event**: Generally, losing party pays costs (tribunal has discretion)

**Frivolous Claims** (Section 31-A): Costs may include exemplary costs if claim/defense frivolous

## 7. ARBITRATION COUNCIL OF INDIA (ACI)

**Section 43-J (2019 Amendment)**: Establishment of ACI

**Functions**:
- Grade arbitral institutions (Section 43-K)
- Maintain panel of accredited arbitrators
- Establish norms for costs/fees
- Promote/facilitate ADR
- Maintain depository of arbitral awards (Section 42-B)

**Constitution** (2021 Amendment):
- Chairperson, 2 academics, 1 High Court judge (retired), 5 ex-officio members

**Status**: Notified in 2023, operational from 2024 (gradual rollout)

## 8. CRITICAL CASE LAW

### 8.1 Seat and Jurisdiction

**BALCO v. Kaiser Aluminum (2012) 9 SCC 552**:
- Overruled Bhatia International regime
- Part I applies ONLY to India-seated arbitrations
- Prospective overruling (contracts post-06.09.2012)

**BGS SGS SOMA JV v. NHPC Ltd. (2020) 4 SCC 234**:
- Seat vs. venue distinction
- Seat determines curial law
- Four-factor test for determining seat

### 8.2 Arbitrability

**Booz Allen & Hamilton v. SBI Home Finance (2011) 5 SCC 532**:
- Rights in personam arbitrable, rights in rem non-arbitrable

**Avitel Post Studioz v. HSBC PI Holdings (2021) 4 SCC 713**:
- Fraud allegations arbitrable if incidental to contract

**A. Ayyasamy v. A. Paramasivam (2016) 10 SCC 386**:
- Matrimonial disputes non-arbitrable
- Serious fraud allegations may oust arbitration (high threshold)

### 8.3 Judicial Intervention

**SBP & Co. v. Patel Engineering (2005) 8 SCC 618**:
- Section 11 appointment is administrative (not judicial), but post-2015 involves prima facie review

**Duro Felguera v. Gangavaram Port (2017) 9 SCC 729**:
- Limited review under Section 11 (existence of arbitration agreement, not detailed merits)

**Ssangyong Engineering v. NHAI (2019) 15 SCC 131**:
- Public policy confined to fundamental policy, justice, morality
- Patent illegality for domestic awards only
- Narrow scope of Section 34

### 8.4 Interim Measures

**Alka Chandewar v. Shamshul Ishrar Khan (2017) 16 SCC 119**:
- Section 9 not available after award made (pre-Section 36 enforcement)

**Board of Control for Cricket in India v. Kochhar (2018) 10 SCC 337**:
- Section 17 interim order enforceable as court order under Section 17(2)

## 9. MODERN DEVELOPMENTS

### 9.1 Emergency Arbitration

**Mechanism**: Urgent interim relief before tribunal constituted

**Institutional Rules**:
- SIAC, ICC, LCIA, MCIA have emergency arbitrator provisions
- Emergency arbitrator appointed within 24-48 hours
- Interim order within 7-14 days

**Indian Law Position**:
- **Amazon v. Future Retail (2020)**: Emergency arbitrator order enforceable under Section 17(2) (Delhi HC)
- **PASL v. GE Power (2021)**: Emergency arbitrator order enforceable (Supreme Court affirmed)

**Controversy**: Whether emergency arbitrator is "arbitral tribunal" under Section 2(1)(d) - generally accepted post-Amazon

### 9.2 Virtual Hearings (COVID-19 Impact)

**Section 19(2)** (amended 2020): Arbitral tribunal may hold oral hearings or conduct proceedings on basis of documents/written submissions

**Interpretation**: Includes video conferencing

**Supreme Court Guidelines** (Dr. Sharma v. Sri Sandeep (2020)):
- Consent of parties required for virtual cross-examination
- Technical safeguards necessary

**Institutional Rules**: MCIA, DIAC adopted specific virtual hearing protocols (2020-2021)

### 9.3 Third-Party Funding

**Definition**: Non-party finances arbitration in exchange for share of recovery

**Indian Position**:
- **Bar Council of India** rules prohibit champerty/maintenance by advocates
- **Third-party funders** (non-lawyers, international funders) - NO explicit prohibition
- Disclosure required under Section 12 (if creates reasonable doubt about arbitrator impartiality)

**Growing Practice**: Increased third-party funding in high-value international commercial arbitrations (India-seated)

## 10. PRACTICAL GUIDANCE

### 10.1 Drafting Arbitration Clauses

**Essential Elements**:
1. **Scope**: "All disputes arising out of or in connection with this contract"
2. **Number of arbitrators**: Sole OR panel of three (avoid even numbers)
3. **Seat**: "seated in Mumbai, India" (determines curial law)
4. **Governing law**: "governed by Indian law" (substantive law)
5. **Language**: "English" (if international parties)
6. **Institution/Rules** (optional): "administered by MCIA under MCIA Rules 2024"

**Example - Model Clause (Institutional)**:
"Any dispute arising out of or in connection with this contract, including any question regarding its existence, validity or termination, shall be referred to and finally resolved by arbitration administered by the Mumbai Centre for International Arbitration (MCIA) in accordance with the MCIA Arbitration Rules 2024. The seat of arbitration shall be Mumbai, India. The tribunal shall consist of three arbitrators. The language of arbitration shall be English."

**Example - Model Clause (Ad Hoc)**:
"Any dispute arising out of or in connection with this contract shall be referred to arbitration by a sole arbitrator appointed by mutual agreement of the parties. The arbitration shall be seated in Delhi, India and conducted in accordance with the UNCITRAL Arbitration Rules 2013. The language of arbitration shall be English. The substantive law governing the contract shall be Indian law."

### 10.2 Selecting Arbitrators

**Criteria**:
- Subject-matter expertise (construction, IP, banking, etc.)
- Experience (number of arbitrations conducted)
- Availability (time commitment)
- Independence (no conflicts under Schedule V/VII)
- Track record (awards upheld/set aside)

**Sources**:
- Arbitral institution panels (MCIA, DIAC)
- Chartered Institute of Arbitrators (CIArb)
- Professional bodies (ICAI for accounting disputes, Bar associations)

### 10.3 Choosing Between Court and Arbitration

**Prefer Arbitration When**:
- Confidentiality critical (arbitration confidential under Section 42-A)
- International parties (neutrality of forum)
- Technical expertise required (choose expert arbitrator)
- Speed important (12-month timeline under Section 29-A)

**Prefer Litigation When**:
- Need for precedent (arbitration awards not binding precedent)
- Interim relief critical and urgent (court Section 9 may be faster than constituting tribunal)
- Multiple parties/third-party implication (arbitration limited to signatories)
- Low-value disputes (arbitration costs may exceed court fees)

## 11. LIMITATIONS AND EXCEPTIONS

### 11.1 Non-Arbitrable Disputes
See Section 2.3 above (criminal, matrimonial, insolvency status, testamentary)

### 11.2 Statutory Arbitrations
**Section 2(3)**: A&C Act provisions apply to statutory arbitrations ONLY if parties agree

**Examples of Statutory Arbitrations**:
- Industrial Disputes Act 1947
- Payment of Gratuity Act 1972
- Micro, Small and Medium Enterprises Development Act 2006 (MSMED Act)

**MSMED Act Arbitration**: Mandatory reference to MSMED Facilitation Council (Section 18) - deemed arbitration, A&C Act applies with modifications

### 11.3 Public Procurement Contracts

**Arbitration Clause Validity**: Generally valid

**Challenges**:
- Government privilege clauses (unilateral termination)
- Sovereign immunity issues (enforcement against State)

**Board of Control for Cricket in India v. Kochhar (2018)**: Public bodies/PSUs can arbitrate; public policy not violated
