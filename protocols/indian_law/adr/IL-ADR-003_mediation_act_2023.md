# IL-ADR-003: Mediation Act 2023

## Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-ADR-003
- **Domain**: Indian Law - Alternative Dispute Resolution
- **Category**: Mediation
- **Version**: 1.0
- **Last Updated**: December 2024
- **Scope**: Mediation Act 2023 framework, pre-litigation mediation, enforceability, mediation settlement agreements
- **Prerequisites**: IL-ADR-001 (ADR fundamentals)
- **Related Protocols**: IL-ADR-006 (Conciliation), IL-COMMERCIAL-001 (Pre-institution mediation Section 12-A)

## Purpose
Comprehensive coverage of the Mediation Act 2023 which came into force on September 15, 2023, providing statutory framework for mediation in India, including pre-litigation mediation, institutional mediation, online mediation, enforceability of mediated settlement agreements, and integration with existing dispute resolution mechanisms.

---

## 1. LEGISLATIVE FRAMEWORK

### 1.1 Historical Context

**Pre-2023 Position**:
- No standalone mediation legislation in India
- Mediation conducted under:
  - **Part III A&C Act 1996** (Conciliation - limited scope)
  - **Section 89 CPC 1908** (Court-referred ADR)
  - **Legal Services Authorities Act 1987** (Lok Adalat)
  - **Commercial Courts Act Section 12-A** (Pre-institution mediation for commercial disputes)

**Need for Legislation**:
- Lack of statutory enforceability of mediated settlements
- No institutional framework for mediation
- Absence of mediator accreditation standards
- Limited awareness and adoption

**Mediation Act 2023**:
- Enacted: September 15, 2023
- Based on **UNCITRAL Model Law on International Commercial Mediation 2018** (Singapore Convention framework)
- Comprehensive framework for domestic and international mediation

### 1.2 Structure of the Act

**Five Parts, 62 Sections**:

**Part I (Sections 1-2)**: Preliminary (definitions, application)
**Part II (Sections 3-43)**: Mediation (domestic and international, seated in India)
**Part III (Sections 44-47)**: Enforcement of international mediated settlement agreements (Singapore Convention)
**Part IV (Sections 48-56)**: Mediation Council of India (MCI)
**Part V (Sections 57-62)**: Miscellaneous (rules, repeal of Part III A&C Act)

### 1.3 Scope and Application

**Applies to** (Section 3(1)):
- ✅ Mediation conducted in India (domestic or international)
- ✅ Pre-litigation, online, and community mediation
- ✅ Mediation agreements to mediate (Section 4)

**Does NOT apply to** (Section 3(2)):
- ❌ Mediation under Legal Services Authorities Act 1987 (Lok Adalat)
- ❌ Section 89 CPC court-referred mediation (unless parties agree to apply Mediation Act)
- ❌ Mediation conducted by Tribunals/Statutory Authorities under their own rules

**International Mediation** (Section 2(g)):
At least one party is:
- Individual resident/national of country other than India, OR
- Entity incorporated/managed from outside India, OR
- Government of foreign country

**AND** dispute arises from **commercial relationship**

## 2. KEY DEFINITIONS

**Mediation** (Section 2(h)): Process, whether referred to by any expression, whereby parties attempt to reach an amicable settlement of their dispute with assistance of a third person ("mediator") lacking authority to impose solution

**Mediator** (Section 2(i)): Person appointed to mediate

**Mediated Settlement Agreement** (Section 2(j)): Written agreement resulting from mediation

**Mediation Service Provider** (Section 2(k)): Institution/body providing mediation services

**Pre-Litigation Mediation** (Section 2(n)): Mediation before institution of suit/proceeding

**Online Mediation** (Section 2(m)): Mediation conducted partially/wholly using audiovisual electronic means

**Community Mediation** (Section 2(c)): Mediation of disputes involving parties residing in same geographical area (village, locality) for social harmony

## 3. PRE-LITIGATION MEDIATION (Section 4)

### 3.1 Mandatory Consideration

**Section 4(1)**: "Where parties to a dispute, which is **not excluded** under sub-section (2), agree to settle such dispute through mediation, such party or parties shall make an application in such form and in such manner as may be prescribed to the Court, tribunal or other judicial authority before which such dispute is pending or is likely to be instituted."

**Interpretation**:
- NOT mandatory pre-litigation mediation for ALL disputes
- Mandatory to **consider** mediation if parties agree
- Encouragement to mediate before litigation

**Section 4(2) - Excluded Disputes** (No Pre-Litigation Mediation):
- Affecting rights of third parties
- Involving criminal prosecution
- Affecting public interest/policy
- Impacting rights of vulnerable persons (minors, persons of unsound mind)

### 3.2 Court's Power (Section 4(3))

**Court may**:
- Refer parties to mediation at any stage of proceedings
- Adjourn proceedings for mediation (up to **180 days** - 6 months)

**No Adverse Costs** (Section 4(4)):
- Party not liable for costs merely for initiating pre-litigation mediation (even if unsuccessful)

**Effect of Pre-Litigation Mediation**:
- **Limitation Period Extended** (Section 20): Period of mediation (max 180 days) excluded from Limitation Act calculation
- Encourages mediation without fear of limitation expiry

## 4. MEDIATION PROCESS

### 4.1 Commencement (Sections 5-7)

**Invitation to Mediate** (Section 5):
- Party may invite other party to mediate (written communication)
- Acceptance creates **agreement to mediate** (binding on parties)
- Rejection does NOT preclude future invitation

**Mediation Agreement** (Section 6):
- Written agreement between parties to submit dispute to mediation
- May be standalone OR clause in contract

**Appointment of Mediator** (Section 7):
- Parties free to appoint mediator (by agreement)
- May appoint single mediator OR panel
- If parties cannot agree: Mediation Service Provider appoints

### 4.2 Conduct of Mediation (Sections 8-16)

**Party Autonomy** (Section 8):
- Parties determine procedure (subject to Act)
- Language, location, documents
- Mediator may suggest procedure if parties agree

**Commencement Date** (Section 9):
- Date when mediator communicates acceptance to parties
- OR date parties agree to mediate

**Mediator's Role** (Section 10):
- **Facilitate** communication between parties
- **Assist** parties in identifying issues, exploring options
- **NO power to impose settlement** (unlike arbitrator)
- May meet parties separately (caucus) with consent

**Confidentiality** (Section 22-23):
- Mediator, parties, mediation service provider bound by confidentiality
- Communications, documents, settlement proposals NOT admissible in court/arbitration
- **Exceptions**: Fraud, corruption, threat to life/safety, court order

**Multiple Disputes (Section 11)**:
- Mediation may involve multiple parties/contracts if all consent

**Participation** (Section 14):
- Parties may participate personally OR through authorized representative
- Legal representation permitted (but discouraged unless complex)

**Timeline** (Section 18):
- Mediation concluded within **180 days** (extendable 180 days by consent)
- Beyond 360 days: Mediation terminates unless parties agree to continue

### 4.3 Termination of Mediation (Section 19)

**Mediation Terminates When**:
1. Parties execute **mediated settlement agreement** (successful)
2. Mediator declares no settlement possible (deadlock)
3. Parties jointly declare termination
4. Party unilaterally withdraws (written notice)
5. **180 days** elapsed (unless extended)

## 5. MEDIATED SETTLEMENT AGREEMENT

### 5.1 Form and Validity (Section 28)

**Requirements**:
- **In writing** (Section 28(1))
- **Signed by parties** (or authorized representatives)
- **Dated**
- **Signed by mediator** (authenticates mediation occurred)

**Content**:
- Terms of settlement
- Obligations of each party
- Timeline for performance

**Validity**:
- Agreement is **contract** (enforceable under ICA 1872)
- Has same status and effect as **arbitral award** under Section 30 A&C Act (Section 29(2))

### 5.2 Enforceability (Section 29)

**Section 29(1)**: Mediated settlement agreement is **final and binding** on parties

**Section 29(2)**: Mediated settlement agreement has **same status and effect as arbitral award on agreed terms** under Section 30 A&C Act 1996

**Implications**:
- Executable as **decree** under Section 36 A&C Act
- Challengeable only on grounds in Section 34 A&C Act (very narrow):
  - Fraud, corruption
  - Incapacity, lack of free consent
  - Not arbitrable/against public policy

**Procedure for Enforcement** (Section 30):
1. Application to competent court (jurisdiction where settlement executed or where property situated)
2. Court shall **make settlement rule of court** (no discretion if valid settlement)
3. Executable as court decree

**No Automatic Stay on Challenge**: If challenge filed under Section 34 A&C Act analogy, settlement remains enforceable unless court orders stay

### 5.3 Stamping

**Stamp Duty Applicable**:
- Mediated settlement agreement is "instrument" under Indian Stamp Act 1899
- Stamp duty payable based on state law (value of property/amount involved)
- Non-stamped agreement NOT void but inadmissible until stamped

**Best Practice**: Stamp settlement agreement before seeking enforcement to avoid delays

## 6. MEDIATOR QUALIFICATIONS AND REGISTRATION

### 6.1 Mediator Standards (Section 42)

**Mediation Council of India (MCI)** to specify:
- Educational qualifications
- Experience requirements
- Training and certification standards
- Code of conduct

**Current Position** (Rules under formulation as of Dec 2024):
- No mandatory registration yet
- MCI issuing guidelines for mediator accreditation

**Expected Standards** (Draft Rules):
- Graduate degree
- **40 hours** mediation training (from recognized institution)
- Practical experience (observed/conducted mediations)
- Continuing education requirements

### 6.2 Mediator's Duties (Sections 10, 21, 22)

**Independence and Impartiality** (Section 21):
- Disclose any circumstances likely to affect impartiality
- Withdraw if unable to conduct mediation impartially

**Confidentiality** (Section 22):
- Not disclose information received in mediation
- Not testify in proceedings regarding mediation

**Facilitate Settlement** (Section 10):
- Assist parties in identifying issues
- Explore settlement options
- NOT impose settlement

### 6.3 Immunity (Section 43)

**Mediator and Mediation Service Provider NOT liable** for:
- Actions/omissions in good faith during mediation
- Settlement terms agreed by parties

**Exception**: Liability for fraud, corruption, gross negligence

## 7. MEDIATION SERVICE PROVIDERS

### 7.1 Registration (Section 40)

**Mediation Service Providers may register** with Mediation Council of India (voluntary as of Dec 2024)

**Benefits of Registration**:
- Listed on MCI website (visibility)
- Quality certification by MCI
- Eligibility for government-referred mediations

**Requirements** (under formulation):
- Adequate infrastructure (mediation rooms, technology)
- Panel of trained mediators
- Administrative support
- Adherence to MCI code of conduct

### 7.2 Functions

**Mediation Service Providers**:
- Maintain panel of mediators
- Appoint mediators when parties cannot agree
- Provide mediation venue/facilities
- Administer mediation process (scheduling, documents)
- Charge fees (mediator fees + administrative fees)

**Existing Institutions**:
- CAMP (Centre for Advanced Mediation Practice) - Delhi HC
- Mediation centres under High Courts
- MCIA, DIAC (arbitration institutions also offering mediation)
- Private mediation centres

## 8. ONLINE AND COMMUNITY MEDIATION

### 8.1 Online Mediation (Section 26)

**Definition** (Section 2(m)): Mediation conducted partially/wholly using audiovisual electronic means

**Advantages**:
- Cost-effective (no travel)
- Accessibility (parties in different locations/countries)
- Flexibility in scheduling

**Requirements**:
- Consent of parties for online mode
- Secure platform (confidentiality, data protection)
- Authentication of identity
- Electronic signatures for settlement (IT Act 2000)

**Challenges**:
- Digital divide (limited access in rural areas)
- Technical issues (connectivity, platform failures)
- Non-verbal communication cues lost

### 8.2 Community Mediation (Section 27)

**Definition** (Section 2(c)): Mediation for disputes involving parties in same geographical area

**Purpose**:
- Resolve community disputes (neighbors, local businesses)
- Preserve social harmony
- Accessible, low-cost dispute resolution

**Types of Disputes**:
- Property boundary disputes (non-title)
- Noise, nuisance complaints
- Family disputes (property division, not matrimonial status)
- Business disputes (local vendors)

**Community Mediators**:
- Trained volunteers from local community
- Respected community members (elders, retired professionals)

**Integration**: May be conducted through Legal Services Authorities (Lok Adalat) or standalone mediation centers

## 9. MEDIATION COUNCIL OF INDIA (MCI)

### 9.1 Establishment (Section 48)

**Composition**:
- Chairperson: Supreme Court judge (sitting or retired) nominated by CJI
- Two members: High Court judges
- Two members: Senior advocates with mediation expertise
- Two members: Academicians (law or conflict resolution)
- Secretary: Government appointee

**Functions** (Section 50):
1. **Registration** of mediators and mediation service providers
2. **Recognition** of mediation institutes for training
3. **Standards**: Develop code of conduct, ethical standards
4. **Accreditation**: Certify mediators, mediators' training courses
5. **Promotion**: Promote mediation awareness, research
6. **Repository**: Maintain database of mediation settlements (anonymized)

### 9.2 Current Status (as of December 2024)

**MCI Establishment**: Notified in 2023, operationalization ongoing

**Key Initiatives**:
- Drafting mediator registration rules
- Developing online registration portal
- Creating mediation training curriculum
- Establishing empanelment guidelines for mediation institutes

## 10. INTERNATIONAL MEDIATION AND SINGAPORE CONVENTION

### 10.1 Singapore Convention on Mediation (Part III)

**UN Convention on International Settlement Agreements Resulting from Mediation** (Singapore Convention 2018):
- India **signed** on August 7, 2019
- NOT yet **ratified** (as of December 2024)

**Mediation Act 2023 Part III** (Sections 44-47): Gives statutory effect to Singapore Convention

**Scope**: Enforcement of **international mediated settlement agreements** in India

**Requirements for Enforcement** (Section 45):
1. Settlement agreement in **writing**
2. Signed by parties
3. Evidences settlement from **mediation** (mediator signature OR document evidencing mediation)
4. **Commercial** dispute
5. At least one party from different country than India (international element)

**Procedure** (Section 46):
- Application to competent court with:
  - Signed settlement agreement
  - Evidence of mediation (mediator signature/document)
- Court shall grant relief **unless grounds for refusal** exist

**Grounds for Refusal** (Section 46(5)):
1. Party under **incapacity**
2. Settlement agreement **void, inoperative, incapable of performance** under applicable law
3. Settlement **not binding/final** under its terms
4. Settlement **modified/amended** subsequently
5. Obligations already **performed**
6. Settlement agreement **unclear or incomplete** (relief cannot be granted)
7. Mediator **breach of standards** applicable (affected settlement)
8. **Fraud, corruption** in mediation process
9. Settlement **contrary to public policy** of India
10. Subject matter **not arbitrable** (using arbitrability standards)

### 10.2 Practical Implications

**Once India Ratifies Singapore Convention**:
- Mediated settlement agreements from Singapore Convention countries enforceable in India (reciprocal enforcement)
- Indian mediated settlement agreements enforceable abroad (55+ signatories including US, China, Singapore)
- Boost to cross-border mediation

**Current Position** (Pre-Ratification):
- Part III provisions on hold until ratification
- International mediated settlements enforceable only under domestic contract law (less certain)

## 11. INTEGRATION WITH COMMERCIAL COURTS ACT

### 11.1 Section 12-A Pre-Institution Mediation (Commercial Courts)

**Commercial Courts Act Section 12-A** (2018 Amendment):
- **Mandatory pre-institution mediation** for all commercial disputes (≥₹3 lakhs)
- Exceptions: Urgent interim relief, summary suits, execution, admiralty

**Relationship with Mediation Act 2023**:
- Commercial Courts Act Section 12-A **continues to apply** (not repealed)
- Mediation Act 2023 provides **statutory framework** for conducting Section 12-A mediation

**Procedure**:
1. Commercial dispute (≥₹3 lakhs) → File application with Mediation Centre (Section 12-A)
2. Mediation conducted under **Mediation Act 2023** framework
3. If settled → Mediated settlement agreement under Section 29 Mediation Act
4. If not settled → Certificate of non-settlement → File suit in Commercial Court

**Timeline**: 3-5 months (Section 12-A) - aligns with 180-day Mediation Act timeline

## 12. COMPARISON: MEDIATION VS. CONCILIATION VS. ARBITRATION

| Feature | Mediation (Mediation Act 2023) | Conciliation (Part III A&C Act - Repealed) | Arbitration (A&C Act 1996) |
|---------|-------------|-------------|------------|
| **Facilitator's Role** | Facilitate (no power to impose) | Facilitate + propose solutions | Adjudicate (power to impose award) |
| **Outcome** | Settlement agreement (consensual) | Settlement agreement (consensual) | Award (binding) |
| **Enforceability** | Same as arbitral award (Section 29) | Same as arbitral award (Section 73 A&C Act - repealed) | Enforceable as decree |
| **Confidentiality** | Strict (Section 22-23) | Strict (Section 75 A&C Act) | Confidential (Section 42-A A&C Act) |
| **Timeline** | 180 days (extendable 180 days) | No statutory limit (Part III repealed) | 12 months (Section 29-A) |
| **Cost** | Lower (no adjudication) | Lower | Higher (arbitrator fees, lawyer fees) |
| **Relationship Preservation** | High (collaborative) | High | Low (adversarial) |

## 13. PRACTICAL GUIDANCE

### 13.1 Mediation Clause Drafting

**Model Mediation Clause (Standalone)**:
"Any dispute arising out of or in connection with this contract shall be referred to mediation under the Mediation Act 2023. The parties shall appoint a mediator by mutual agreement within 15 days of written notice by either party. If the parties fail to agree on a mediator, the mediator shall be appointed by [Mediation Service Provider Name]. The mediation shall be conducted in [City]. If the dispute is not resolved through mediation within 180 days, either party may initiate arbitration/litigation in accordance with Clause [X]."

**Model Mediation-Arbitration Clause (Med-Arb)**:
"In the event of any dispute, the parties shall first attempt to resolve the dispute through mediation under the Mediation Act 2023 for a period of 90 days. If the dispute is not resolved through mediation, the dispute shall be referred to arbitration in accordance with the Arbitration and Conciliation Act 1996. [Arbitration clause details]."

### 13.2 When to Choose Mediation

**Prefer Mediation When**:
- Parties wish to preserve relationship (ongoing business, family)
- Confidentiality critical (commercial reputation)
- Creative solution desired (beyond money damages)
- Speed and cost important
- Dispute involves multiple interests (not zero-sum)

**Avoid Mediation When**:
- Need for precedent (legal clarification)
- Power imbalance between parties (mediation may not be fair)
- Bad faith party (unwilling to negotiate genuinely)
- Urgent interim relief needed (mediation takes time)
- Complex legal/technical issues requiring adjudication

### 13.3 Mediator Selection Criteria

**Experience**: Number of mediations conducted, success rate
**Subject-Matter Expertise**: Relevant industry/legal background
**Training**: Certification from recognized institutes (40-hour courses)
**Style**: Facilitative vs. evaluative (choose based on dispute nature)
**Availability**: Timeline commitment
**Fees**: Transparent fee structure

## 14. LIMITATIONS AND CHALLENGES

### 14.1 Statutory Limitations

**Non-Applicability to**:
- Lok Adalat mediations (Legal Services Authorities Act)
- Court-referred Section 89 CPC mediation (unless parties opt-in to Mediation Act)
- Tribunal mediations (unless Tribunal rules adopt Mediation Act)

**Pre-Litigation Mediation**: Not truly mandatory (only if parties agree - Section 4(1))

**Singapore Convention**: Not yet ratified (Part III dormant)

### 14.2 Practical Challenges

**Awareness**: Low awareness among public and legal practitioners (new legislation)

**Infrastructure**: Limited mediation centers, especially in tier-2/3 cities

**Mediator Supply**: Shortage of trained, certified mediators

**Enforcement Delays**: Despite statutory enforceability, court application for enforcement may still take time

**Cultural Resistance**: Adversarial litigation culture, preference for adjudication over negotiation
