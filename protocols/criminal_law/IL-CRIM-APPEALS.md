## Overview and Purpose

### What is a Criminal Appeal?

A **criminal appeal** is a **legal proceeding** where a higher court **reviews the decision** of a lower court in a criminal case, to determine if errors of law or fact occurred that warrant reversal or modification of the judgment.

### Purpose of Criminal Appeals

1. **Correct Errors**: Rectify mistakes of law or fact made by trial court
2. **Ensure Justice**: Second opinion on conviction/acquittal/sentence
3. **Protect Rights**: Safeguard constitutional and legal rights
4. **Consistency**: Ensure uniform application of law
5. **Public Confidence**: Demonstrate fairness through multi-tier review

### Types of Appellate Remedies

| Remedy | Nature | Forum | Against |
|--------|--------|-------|---------|
| **Appeal** | As of right OR with leave | Sessions Court, High Court, Supreme Court | Conviction, acquittal, sentence |
| **Revision** | Discretionary | High Court, Sessions Court | Any order (not just conviction/acquittal) |
| **Reference** | By lower court seeking guidance | High Court, Supreme Court | Difficult questions of law |
| **Review** | Reconsideration by same court | High Court, Supreme Court | Own judgment (limited grounds) |
| **Writ** | Constitutional remedy | High Court (226), Supreme Court (32) | Illegal orders, violations of rights |

## Legal Framework and Statutory Basis

### Constitutional Foundation

#### Article 134: Appellate Jurisdiction of Supreme Court (Criminal Matters)
- Appeal to Supreme Court if High Court:
  - Reverses acquittal and sentences to death, OR
  - Withdraws case for trial before itself and convicts with death/life/≥10 years, OR
  - Certifies case fit for appeal to Supreme Court

#### Article 136: Special Leave Petition (SLP)
- Supreme Court may grant **special leave to appeal** from any judgment/order of any court/tribunal
- **Discretionary** power

### Primary Legislation

#### Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC) 1973
- **Chapter XXIX**: Appeals (Sections 372-394)
- **Chapter XXX**: Reference and Revision (Sections 395-405)
- **Chapter XXXI**: Transfer of Criminal Cases (Sections 406-412)
- **Chapter XXXII**: Execution, Suspension, Remission, Commutation (Sections 413-435)
- **Section 482**: Inherent Powers of High Court

#### Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS) 2023
- **Chapters corresponding to above** (Sections 431-528)
- Effective from **July 1, 2024**
- Substantively similar to CrPC with procedural enhancements

## Types of Appeals

### 1. Appeal to Sessions Court

#### A. Appeal by Convicted Person (Section 374 CrPC / 432 BNSS)

**Who Can Appeal**: Any person convicted by Magistrate

**Against**:
- Conviction
- Sentence
- Both

**Appealable Orders**:
- Conviction by Magistrate
- Sentence imposed by Magistrate

**Limitation**: **30 days** from date of judgment (Section 389 / 445 BNSS)
- Court can condone delay (up to 90 days with sufficient cause)

**Contents of Appeal**:
- Grounds of appeal
- Copy of judgment appealed against
- Memorandum of appeal

**Powers of Sessions Court** (Section 386 CrPC / 442 BNSS):
1. **Reverse** finding and acquit
2. **Alter** finding (convict of lesser offence)
3. **Reduce** sentence
4. **Enhance** sentence (with notice to accused)
5. **Remit** case to Magistrate for retrial
6. **Dismiss** appeal

**Important**: Sessions Court can **enhance sentence** (Section 386(b)(ii))
- But must give notice to accused (opportunity to be heard)

#### B. Appeal by Acquitted Person: NOT PERMITTED
- Acquitted person cannot appeal against acquittal (no relief needed)
- But prosecution can appeal against acquittal (to High Court)

### 2. Appeal to High Court

#### A. Appeal by Convicted Person (Section 374 CrPC / 432 BNSS)

**Who Can Appeal**: Person convicted on trial by:
- **Sessions Court**, OR
- **High Court** (in exercise of original jurisdiction)

**Against**:
- Conviction
- Sentence
- Both

**Appealable Convictions**:
- Any conviction by Sessions Court
- Conviction by High Court in original jurisdiction

**Limitation**: **30 days** from date of judgment (Section 389 / 445 BNSS)
- Condonation possible (90 days with sufficient cause)

#### B. Appeal by Prosecution Against Acquittal (Section 378 CrPC / 436 BNSS)

**Who Can Appeal**: **State Government** (through Public Prosecutor)

**Against**: Order of acquittal by:
- **Magistrate** (if Sessions Court confirms or High Court certifies), OR
- **Sessions Court**

**Procedure**:
- State Government **directs** Public Prosecutor to appeal
- High Court hears appeal

**Important**: Not automatic; State Government's sanction required

**Landmark Case**: **State of U.P. v. Shambhu Nath Singh (2001)**
- Prosecution's right to appeal against acquittal is statutory
- High Court can reverse acquittal if finding perverse or illegal
- But acquittal orders require **greater scrutiny** (presumption of double innocence)

#### C. Appeal Against Sentence (Section 377 CrPC / 435 BNSS)

**Who Can Appeal**:
- **Convicted person**: Against sentence (if no appeal against conviction)
- **State Government**: Against **inadequate sentence** (seeking enhancement)

**When State Appeals for Enhancement**:
- If sentence is **inadequate**
- Public Prosecutor argues for higher sentence
- High Court can enhance sentence
- But must give notice to accused and opportunity to be heard

**Landmark Case**: **Deo Narain v. State of U.P. (1973)**
- Prosecution can appeal for enhancement of sentence
- High Court has power to enhance under Section 377
- Notice to accused mandatory before enhancement

#### D. Appeal by Complainant/Victim (Section 301, 372 CrPC / 355, 431 BNSS)

**Limitation**: Complainant/victim **cannot directly appeal** against acquittal

**Procedure**:
- Can **file application** to State Government requesting appeal
- State Government decides whether to appeal
- If State Government refuses, victim can file **protest petition** before High Court (Section 301)
- High Court can **direct** State to file appeal OR **entertain** victim's plea

**Landmark Case**: **Santosh Kumar v. State of Haryana (2012)**
- Victim has right to file protest petition against acquittal
- High Court can entertain victim's complaint even without State's appeal
- But victim has no **independent right** to appeal

### 3. Appeal to Supreme Court

#### A. As of Right (Article 134)

**When Available** (Section 379 CrPC / 437 BNSS + Article 134):

1. **High Court Reverses Acquittal** and sentences to **death**
2. **High Court Withdraws Case** to itself and convicts with **death, life, or ≥10 years**
3. **High Court Certifies** case fit for appeal to Supreme Court (on **substantial question of law** of general importance)

**Automatic Right**: If High Court reverses acquittal and gives death sentence
- No leave required
- Accused has **absolute right** to appeal

**Limitation**: **60 days** from date of High Court judgment (can be condoned)

#### B. Special Leave Petition (SLP) - Article 136

**Nature**: **Discretionary** remedy

**When**: When no appeal as of right exists

**Examples**:
- High Court confirms conviction by Sessions Court
- High Court confirms acquittal
- High Court reduces sentence (no acquittal)
- Any other case where Supreme Court deems fit

**Procedure**:
- File **Special Leave Petition** under Article 136
- Supreme Court may grant leave or dismiss
- If leave granted, becomes regular appeal

**Landmark Case**: **Pritam Singh v. State (1950)**
- Article 136 is **discretionary** power
- Supreme Court not bound to entertain every SLP
- Exercises discretion where:
  - Substantial question of law
  - Grave miscarriage of justice
  - Violation of fundamental rights
  - High Court judgment perverse

**Statistics**: Supreme Court admits only ~1-2% of SLPs (very selective)

## Revision (Sections 397-401 CrPC / 456-460 BNSS)

### What is Revision?

**Revision** is a **supervisory jurisdiction** where higher court can call for records of subordinate court and examine legality/propriety of any order (not limited to conviction/acquittal).

### Difference Between Appeal and Revision

| Aspect | Appeal | Revision |
|--------|--------|----------|
| **Nature** | Right (in most cases) | Discretionary |
| **Scope** | Re-hearing on merits | Supervisory (legality/propriety) |
| **Orders** | Conviction, acquittal, sentence | Any order (including interlocutory) |
| **Forum** | Sessions Court, High Court, Supreme Court | Sessions Court (limited), High Court |
| **Fresh Evidence** | Can be taken | Generally not taken |
| **Limitation** | 30/60 days | **No limitation** prescribed (but should be within reasonable time) |

### Revision to High Court (Section 397 CrPC / 456 BNSS)

**Who Can File**: Any person aggrieved by any order passed by Magistrate or Sessions Court

**Against What Orders**:
- Any order passed in course of proceeding
- Includes:
  - Bail orders
  - Discharge/acquittal orders
  - Conviction/sentence orders
  - Interlocutory orders (e.g., framing of charges, summoning witnesses)

**Grounds**:
- **Illegality** in order
- **Impropriety** in order
- **Material irregularity**

**Exclusions** (Section 397(2)):

High Court **cannot** revise:
1. Order of acquittal if **appeal lies** and not filed
2. Conviction if appeal lies and not filed
3. Order where **alternative remedy** (appeal) exists and not availed

**Principle**: Revision is **not substitute for appeal**
- If appeal available and not filed (or filed but dismissed), revision barred
- Revision only for orders where no appeal lies OR appeal filed but on limited grounds

**Powers of High Court** (Section 401 CrPC / 460 BNSS):
1. **Set aside** order
2. **Modify** order
3. **Remit** case for retrial
4. **Dismiss** revision

**Landmark Case**: **Akilu v. State of Rajasthan (1997)**
- Revision is supervisory jurisdiction
- Not re-hearing on merits
- High Court interferes only if illegality or material irregularity

### Revision to Sessions Court (Section 397(3) CrPC / 456(3) BNSS)

**Limited Power**: Sessions Judge can revise orders of **Magistrates subordinate to him**

**Same Restrictions**: Cannot revise if appeal lies and not filed

**Less Common**: Revision to Sessions Court rarely used (most go to High Court)

## Reference (Sections 395-396 CrPC / 454-455 BNSS)

### Reference to High Court (Section 395 CrPC / 454 BNSS)

**What is Reference?**
When **Sessions Court** has doubt on a question of law, it can **refer** the question to High Court for opinion

**Procedure**:
1. Sessions Court frames **question of law**
2. Submits reference to High Court
3. Parties heard in High Court
4. High Court gives **opinion**
5. Sessions Court decides case based on High Court's opinion

**Not Binding**: High Court's opinion is persuasive, but Sessions Court not absolutely bound (rare to deviate)

### Reference to Supreme Court (Article 143)

**Presidential Reference**: President can refer questions of law of public importance to Supreme Court

**Advisory Opinion**: Supreme Court gives advisory opinion

**Rare**: Used very rarely (e.g., Ayodhya reference, constitutional interpretation)

## Review (Order XLVII Rule 1 CPC, applied to criminal cases)

### What is Review?

**Review** is **reconsideration** by the **same court** that passed the judgment/order

### Grounds for Review (Very Limited)

**Only Three Grounds**:
1. **Discovery of new and important matter or evidence** which, after exercise of due diligence, was not within knowledge or could not be produced at time of judgment
2. **Mistake or error apparent on face of record**
3. **Any other sufficient reason** (interpreted very restrictively)

**Error Apparent**: Obvious, self-evident error; not requiring long-drawn reasoning to establish

**NOT Grounds for Review**:
- Re-appreciation of evidence
- Different interpretation of law
- Change of opinion

**Limitation**: **30 days** from date of judgment (can be extended)

**Landmark Case**: **Thungabhadra Industries v. Govt. of A.P. (1964)**
- Review is **not re-hearing**
- Only on limited grounds
- No re-appreciation of evidence
- Error must be **glaring and obvious**

**Criminal Cases**: Review rarely granted in criminal cases (finality important)

## Inherent Powers of High Court (Section 482 CrPC / 528 BNSS)

### What are Inherent Powers?

**Section 482 CrPC / 528 BNSS**: High Court has **inherent powers** to make such orders as necessary to:
1. Give effect to any order under CrPC/BNSS
2. Prevent **abuse of process** of court
3. Secure **ends of justice**

**Nature**: **Residuary and plenary** powers

**Not Codified**: Cannot be exhaustively listed; used in exceptional circumstances

### When Invoked?

**Common Situations**:
1. **Quashing of FIR**: If no cognizable offence disclosed or frivolous/vexatious complaint
2. **Quashing of charge sheet**: If no prima facie case
3. **Quashing of proceedings**: If continuation would be abuse of process
4. **Compounding of offences**: In certain cases
5. **Stay of proceedings**: Pending investigation/inquiry

**Landmark Case**: **State of Haryana v. Bhajan Lal (1992)**

**Bhajan Lal Guidelines** (When High Court can quash proceedings):
1. FIR/complaint does not disclose cognizable offence
2. Allegations, even if true, do not make out offence
3. Criminal proceedings are abuse of process (e.g., to harass, settle civil dispute)
4. Proceedings initiated with mala fide intent
5. Allegations are absurd and inherently improbable
6. No legal evidence to support allegations
7. Complaint not made in good faith
8. Proceedings would be futile (e.g., statutory bar, limitation)

**Important**: Inherent powers used **sparingly**
- Not to be used as substitute for appellate/revisional powers
- Interference only in **exceptional cases**

## Suspension of Sentence Pending Appeal (Section 389 CrPC / 445 BNSS)

### Meaning

**Suspension of Sentence**: Temporary stay of execution of sentence while appeal is pending

**Effect**: Convicted person released from jail during pendency of appeal

### Who Can Grant?

**Appellate Court** (Sessions Court, High Court, Supreme Court) hearing the appeal

### When Granted?

**Discretionary**: Court considers:
1. **Prima facie merit** in appeal
2. **Nature of offence** (serious or not)
3. **Severity of sentence**
4. **Period already undergone**
5. **Chances of accused absconding**
6. **Possibility of tampering** with evidence/witnesses

**General Principle**: Suspension granted liberally if:
- Short sentence
- Non-serious offence
- Prima facie strong grounds of appeal
- Substantial period already served

**Restrictive**: If serious offence (murder, rape, terrorism), suspension rarely granted

### Conditions

**Section 389(1)**: Appellate court may:
- **Suspend execution** of sentence
- **Release accused on bail**
- Impose **conditions** (surety, reporting, surrender passport)

**Duration**: Until appeal disposed of (or until court revokes)

**Landmark Case**: **Gurbaksh Singh Sibbia v. State of Punjab (1980)**
- Suspension of sentence is different from bail
- Once convicted, accused loses presumption of innocence
- Suspension granted only if **prima facie case** in appeal

## Powers of Appellate Court (Section 386 CrPC / 442 BNSS)

### Disposal of Appeal

**Appellate Court may**:

#### 1. Reverse Finding and Acquit (Section 386(a))
- If prosecution failed to prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt
- Trial court erred in convicting
- Accused acquitted

#### 2. Alter Finding (Section 386(b))

**Three Sub-Options**:

##### (i) Convict of Lesser Offence
- Trial court convicted of major offence, but evidence proves only minor offence
- Example: Conviction under Section 302 (murder) altered to Section 304 Part II (culpable homicide not amounting to murder)

##### (ii) Reduce Sentence
- Finding of conviction upheld
- But sentence reduced
- Example: Death sentence reduced to life imprisonment

##### (iii) Enhance Sentence (With Notice)
- If prosecution appeals
- Sentence increased
- **Mandatory**: Notice to accused and opportunity to be heard before enhancement

#### 3. Remit Case for Retrial (Section 386(c))
- If trial vitiated due to irregularity/illegality
- Fresh trial ordered
- Example: Denial of cross-examination, violation of Section 313

#### 4. Dismiss Appeal (Section 386(d))
- If no merit
- Conviction and sentence confirmed

**Landmark Case**: **Jameel v. State of U.P. (2010)**
- Appellate court has wide powers under Section 386
- Can alter finding and convict of lesser offence if evidence supports
- But cannot convict of different offence not charged at trial

## Appreciation of Evidence in Appeal

### Principles for Appellate Court

**General Rule**: Appellate court can **re-appreciate evidence** and come to its own conclusion

**Unlike Trial Court**: Appellate court does not have advantage of seeing witnesses (demeanor, credibility)

### Guidelines for Interfering with Acquittal

**Stricter Standard**: Acquittal orders have **presumption of double innocence**

**Landmark Case**: **Chandrappa v. State of Karnataka (2007)** (Leading case on appeals against acquittal)

**Principles**:
1. **Double Presumption**: Accused has (i) presumption of innocence, (ii) presumption that trial court acquitted him correctly
2. **Not Re-Trial**: Appellate court should not re-try case
3. **Perversity Test**: Interfere only if findings are **perverse, illegal, or suffer from material irregularity**
4. **Two Views**: If two views possible (one leading to conviction, other to acquittal), view favorable to accused should prevail
5. **Reasons Required**: If reversing acquittal, detailed reasons mandatory
6. **Benefit of Doubt**: Continued application of "benefit of doubt" principle

**Effect**: Reversing acquittal more difficult than confirming conviction

### Guidelines for Confirming Conviction

**Less Restrictive**: Appellate court can more easily uphold conviction if satisfied of guilt beyond reasonable doubt

**Fresh Evidence**: Can take fresh evidence (Section 391 CrPC / 447 BNSS) if necessary

## Compounding of Offences (Section 320 CrPC / 380 BNSS)

### What is Compounding?

**Compounding** means **settlement/compromise** of criminal case between accused and victim, leading to **termination of prosecution**

### Types of Compoundable Offences

**Section 320 CrPC / 380 BNSS** lists compoundable offences in **two categories**:

#### Category 1: Compoundable by Victim Without Court Permission
**Examples**:
- Assault (Section 323 IPC / 115 BNS)
- Defamation (Section 500 IPC / 356 BNS)
- Theft (certain circumstances)

**Procedure**: Parties settle; inform court; case closed

#### Category 2: Compoundable by Victim With Court Permission
**Examples**:
- Voluntarily causing hurt (Section 325 IPC / 117 BNS)
- Wrongful restraint/confinement
- House-trespass

**Procedure**: Parties settle; apply to court; court grants permission; case closed

### Non-Compoundable Offences

**Serious Offences NOT Compoundable**:
- Murder (Section 302 IPC / 103 BNS)
- Rape (Section 376 IPC / 64 BNS)
- Dacoity
- Terrorism
- Corruption

**Rationale**: These offences affect society at large, not just victim

### Inherent Powers for Compounding

**Section 482 CrPC / 528 BNSS**: High Court can quash proceedings to give effect to compromise **even in non-compoundable offences** (in exceptional circumstances)

**Landmark Case**: **Gian Singh v. State of Punjab (2012)**
- Supreme Court held High Court can quash even non-compoundable offences under Section 482 in exceptional cases
- Factors: Nature of offence, whether private/public injury, settlement genuine
- But offences affecting society (murder, rape, corruption) generally NOT quashed even if compromise

## Sentencing Appeals and Modification

### Appeal on Sentence Alone (Section 377 CrPC / 435 BNSS)

**Convicted Person**:
- Can appeal against **sentence** even if not appealing conviction
- Example: Accepts conviction but argues sentence too harsh

**Prosecution**:
- Can appeal for **enhancement** of sentence if inadequate
- High Court can enhance (with notice to accused)

### Principles of Sentencing on Appeal

**Appellate Court Considers**:
1. **Mitigating factors**:
   - First-time offender
   - Young age
   - Provocation
   - Socio-economic background
   - Period already undergone
   - Remorse
2. **Aggravating factors**:
   - Heinous manner of commission
   - No remorse
   - Danger to society
   - Previous convictions

**Death Sentence Appeals**:
- **Automatic Reference** to High Court for confirmation (Section 366 CrPC / 420 BNSS)
- High Court must confirm or commute
- Supreme Court reviews under **"Rarest of Rare"** doctrine (Bachan Singh)

**Landmark Case**: **Swamy Shraddananda v. State of Karnataka (2008)**
- Supreme Court introduced **"Special Category"** sentence
- Life imprisonment for **minimum 30 years** without remission (for offences falling short of "rarest of rare" but still very serious)
- Now applied in several cases

## Limitation Periods for Appeals and Revisions

### Appeals

| Appeal Type | Limitation Period | Condonation Possible? |
|-------------|-------------------|----------------------|
| **To Sessions Court** | 30 days | Yes (up to 90 days total) |
| **To High Court** | 30 days | Yes (up to 90 days total) |
| **To Supreme Court** (as of right) | 60 days | Yes (Supreme Court has liberal power) |
| **SLP to Supreme Court** | Reasonable time | Supreme Court discretion |

**Section 389 CrPC / 445 BNSS**: Limitation periods

**Condonation**: If sufficient cause shown, delay can be condoned

**Landmark Case**: **N. Balakrishnan v. M. Krishnamurthy (1998)**
- Sufficient cause for condonation to be decided on facts
- Bona fide reasons, not deliberate delay
- Each day of delay must be explained

### Revision

**No Specific Limitation**: Section 397 CrPC / 456 BNSS does not prescribe limitation

**But**: Should be filed within **reasonable time**
- Inordinate delay can be ground for dismissal
- Laches (delay and acquiescence) is relevant

## Bail Pending Appeal

### After Conviction

**Section 389 CrPC / 445 BNSS**: Appellate court can suspend sentence and release on bail

**Factors**:
- Prima facie merit in appeal
- Nature of offence
- Severity of sentence
- Period already undergone

**Restrictive for Serious Offences**: Murder, rape, terrorism - bail rarely granted

### After Acquittal (Prosecution Appeal)

**If Prosecution Appeals Against Acquittal**:
- Accused already acquitted at trial
- Can be arrested pending appeal **only if High Court directs**
- Generally, accused not arrested pending prosecution's appeal
- Arrested only if strong case and flight risk

**Landmark Case**: **Ranjit Singh v. State of Punjab (1994)**
- Acquitted person has additional protection
- Arrest pending prosecution's appeal only in exceptional cases

| Aspect | CrPC 1973 | BNSS 2023 | Change |
|--------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Appeals to Sessions Court** | Section 374 | Section 432 | Section number changed; **substance same** |
| **Appeals to High Court** | Sections 374, 377, 378 | Sections 432, 435, 436 | Section numbers changed; **substance same** |
| **Appeals to Supreme Court** | Section 379 | Section 437 | Section number changed |
| **Powers of appellate court** | Section 386 | Section 442 | Same powers |
| **Revision** | Sections 397-401 | Sections 456-460 | Section numbers changed; **procedure same** |
| **Reference** | Sections 395-396 | Sections 454-455 | Same |
| **Suspension of sentence** | Section 389 | Section 445 | Same |
| **Inherent powers** | Section 482 | Section 528 | **Section number changed; scope same** |
| **Compounding** | Section 320 | Section 380 | Same offences compoundable |
| **Limitation** | 30/60 days | 30/60 days | **Same** |

### Key Observations

**No Material Change**: BNSS 2023 **retains** CrPC appellate provisions with minimal changes
- Section numbers updated
- Substance remains same
- Judicial precedents continue to apply

**Victim Rights**: BNSS enhances victim participation, but appeal rights remain same (victim cannot independently appeal; must through State)

## Practical Compliance Checklist

### For Convicted Accused (Filing Appeal)

#### Pre-Filing
- [ ] Obtain **certified copy** of judgment (apply immediately after conviction)
- [ ] Consult **appellate lawyer** (trial lawyer may not be expert in appeals)
- [ ] Evaluate **grounds of appeal**:
  - Errors of law
  - Errors of fact
  - Improper appreciation of evidence
  - Illegal/improper procedure
  - Excessive sentence
- [ ] Check **limitation period** (30 days to Sessions/High Court, 60 days to Supreme Court)

#### Filing Appeal
- [ ] Prepare **memorandum of appeal** with grounds
- [ ] Attach **certified copy** of judgment
- [ ] If delay, file **condonation application** with affidavit explaining delay
- [ ] Pay **court fees**
- [ ] Apply for **suspension of sentence** (Section 389 / 445):
  - Prepare separate application
  - Show prima facie merit
  - Undertake to abide by conditions

#### After Filing
- [ ] Attend **hearings** regularly
- [ ] Comply with **bail conditions** (if sentence suspended)
- [ ] If sentence not suspended, file **bail application**
- [ ] Prepare **written arguments** (helpful for court)
- [ ] If fresh evidence needed, apply under Section 391 / 447

### For Prosecution (Appeal Against Acquittal)

#### Decision to Appeal
- [ ] Evaluate **merits**: Whether trial court judgment perverse/illegal
- [ ] Obtain **State Government sanction** (Section 378 / 436)
- [ ] File within **30 days**

#### Filing Appeal
- [ ] Prepare detailed grounds showing:
  - Trial court's findings are perverse
  - Material evidence ignored
  - Improper appreciation
  - Legal errors
- [ ] Attach certified copy of acquittal order
- [ ] If seeking arrest of acquitted person, file separate application (generally not granted)

#### Hearing
- [ ] Present arguments showing **perversity** in acquittal
- [ ] Respect **double presumption** of innocence (Chandrappa principle)
- [ ] Provide **detailed reasons** why acquittal should be reversed
- [ ] If successful and conviction ordered, argue for appropriate sentence

### For Appellate Courts

#### Hearing Appeals

**Against Conviction**:
- [ ] Re-appreciate evidence
- [ ] Consider whether guilt proved beyond reasonable doubt
- [ ] Check if trial suffered from material irregularity
- [ ] If acquitting, provide detailed reasons
- [ ] If confirming conviction, briefly explain agreement with trial court

**Against Acquittal** (Chandrappa Principles):
- [ ] Apply **double presumption** of innocence
- [ ] Interfere only if findings **perverse, illegal, or materially irregular**
- [ ] If two views possible, prefer view favorable to accused
- [ ] If reversing acquittal, provide **detailed, cogent reasons**
- [ ] Consider whether fresh trial needed (if irregularity vitiated trial)

**Against Sentence**:
- [ ] Consider mitigating and aggravating factors
- [ ] Proportionality of sentence
- [ ] Period already undergone
- [ ] If enhancing sentence, **give notice** to accused and hear them (mandatory)

#### Suspension of Sentence Applications
- [ ] Consider prima facie merit
- [ ] Nature of offence (serious or not)
- [ ] Likelihood of absconding
- [ ] Period already undergone
- [ ] Impose appropriate conditions (surety, reporting, surrender passport)

## Common Errors and Pitfalls

### For Accused

1. **Missing Limitation Period**: Not filing within 30/60 days (condonation possible but not guaranteed)
2. **Not Obtaining Certified Copy Promptly**: Delay in obtaining judgment copy wastes time
3. **Filing Appeal Without Merits**: Frivolous appeals waste time and money
4. **Not Applying for Suspension of Sentence**: Serving full sentence before appeal heard
5. **Violating Bail Conditions**: Leads to cancellation and arrest

### For Prosecution

1. **Filing Appeal Against Every Acquittal**: Not evaluating merits; should appeal only if perversity
2. **Not Obtaining State Sanction**: Appeal dismissed for lack of sanction (Section 378 / 436)
3. **Weak Grounds**: Not showing perversity; appellate court dismisses
4. **Not Preparing Detailed Arguments**: Acquittal has double presumption; weak arguments fail

### For Courts

1. **Not Giving Notice Before Enhancing Sentence**: Violates natural justice; enhancement set aside
2. **Reversing Acquittal Without Detailed Reasons**: Supreme Court sets aside (Chandrappa violation)
3. **Re-Trying Case Instead of Reviewing**: Appellate court should not act as trial court
4. **Not Applying Correct Standard**: "Beyond reasonable doubt" for conviction, "Perversity" for acquittal

## Conclusion

Criminal appeals are **essential safeguards** ensuring:
- **Errors corrected**: Trial court mistakes rectified
- **Justice served**: Second opinion on conviction/acquittal/sentence
- **Rights protected**: Fair trial, due process
- **Finality with fairness**: Balance between finality and correctness

**Key Principles**:
1. **Appeal is right** in most convictions (not discretionary)
2. **Appellate court can re-appreciate evidence**
3. **Acquittal has double presumption** (Chandrappa principle)
4. **Reversal of acquittal requires detailed reasons**
5. **Sentence can be enhanced** (with notice to accused)
6. **Suspension of sentence discretionary** (based on merit and nature of offence)
7. **Compounding possible** in specified offences
8. **Inherent powers used sparingly** (Section 482 / 528 - Bhajan Lal guidelines)
9. **Limitation: 30/60 days** (condonable with sufficient cause)
10. **Supreme Court SLP discretionary** (Article 136)

**Special Considerations**:
- **Death Penalty**: Automatic reference to High Court (Section 366 / 420); Supreme Court applies "rarest of rare" (Bachan Singh)
- **Acquittal Appeals**: Stricter standard (perversity, illegality, material irregularity)
- **Victim Rights**: Cannot independently appeal; must through State (Section 378 / 436)

The transition from CrPC to BNSS **retains appellate framework** with section number changes but **same substantive provisions** - ensuring continuity of jurisprudence and procedure.

**Ultimate Goal**: **Correct errors** while ensuring **finality** - balance between justice and closure.
