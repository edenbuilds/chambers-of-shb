# IL-CRIM-DEFAMATION

**Protocol ID**: IL-CRIM-DEFAMATION
**Domain**: Criminal Law > Crimes Against Person
**Version**: 1.0.0
**Last Updated**: 2025-12-06
**Statutory Basis**: Indian Penal Code (IPC) Sections 499-502 | Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023 Sections 356-359

---

## Overview

**Defamation** under Indian law protects an individual's reputation from false and malicious attacks. Uniquely, India recognizes **both civil and criminal defamation**, with criminal defamation serving as deterrent against deliberate reputation damage. This protocol covers:

- Definition of defamation (spoken, written, visual representations)
- Ten exceptions (truth, good faith, public interest)
- Distinction between civil vs. criminal defamation
- Constitutional validity (challenged but upheld in 2016)
- Punishment provisions and landmark cases

---

## Statutory Framework

### IPC (Pre-July 1, 2024)

**Section 499**: Definition of defamation (with 10 exceptions)
**Section 500**: Punishment for defamation
**Section 501**: Printing or engraving defamatory matter
**Section 502**: Sale of printed/engraved defamatory matter

### BNS (Post-July 1, 2024)

**Sections 356-359**: Corresponds to IPC defamation provisions (definition and punishment largely unchanged)

## Definition of Defamation (IPC Section 499 / BNS Section 356)

### Statutory Text (Simplified)

**Section 499**: "Whoever, by words either spoken or intended to be read, or by signs or by visible representations, makes or publishes any imputation concerning any person intending to harm, or knowing or having reason to believe that such imputation will harm, the reputation of such person, is said to defame that person."

---

### Essential Elements

1. **Imputation**: Statement/allegation made about person
2. **Concerning Person**: About specific person (living or dead, if family harmed)
3. **Publication**: Communicated to third party (not just to person defamed)
4. **Intention OR Knowledge**: Intent to harm reputation OR knowledge/reason to believe it will harm reputation
5. **Reputation Harmed**: Lowering of estimation in eyes of society
6. **No Exception Applies**: None of 10 exceptions in Section 499 apply

---

### Element Analysis

#### 1. Imputation (Statement/Allegation)

**Forms of Imputation**:
- **Spoken Words**: Slander (oral defamation - "A told B that C is a thief")
- **Written Words**: Libel (written defamation - article, email, social media post)
- **Signs**: Gestures, drawings, cartoons
- **Visual Representations**: Photographs, videos, memes

**Direct vs. Indirect**:
- **Direct**: "A is a criminal" (explicit statement)
- **Indirect**: "A was seen leaving the crime scene at 2am" (innuendo suggesting A committed crime)

**Innuendo**: Implied meaning understood by reasonable person with knowledge of circumstances.

---

#### 2. Concerning Person

**Living Person**: Primary protection

**Dead Person**: Defamation actionable if:
- Imputation harms reputation of **living family members** (e.g., "X's father was murderer" harms X's reputation)
- **Not actionable** if only dead person's reputation harmed (dead have no right to reputation)

**Legal Entity**: Companies, corporations can be defamed (reputation in business context)

**Group**: Defamation of group actionable only if:
- Group is **small and identifiable** (e.g., "All 5 partners of XYZ Law Firm are fraudsters")
- **Not actionable** for large groups (e.g., "All lawyers are liars" - too broad, no specific person defamed)

---

#### 3. Publication (Third Party Communication)

**Published**: Communicated to at least **one person other than defamed person**

**Examples**:
- A tells B that C is thief → Published (B is third party)
- A writes letter to C calling C a thief (only A and C read it) → **NOT published** (no third party)
- A posts on Facebook "C is corrupt" (500 friends see it) → Published (500 third parties)

**Internet Publication**: Social media posts, emails forwarded, blog articles = published (potentially millions of third parties).

---

#### 4. Intention OR Knowledge

**Two Mental States (Alternative)**:

**Intending to Harm**: Direct purpose to damage reputation

**OR**

**Knowing/Having Reason to Believe**: Aware that statement will harm reputation (even if not direct intention)

**Strict Liability**: No need to prove malice (ill-will); knowledge suffices.

**Example**: Newspaper publishes false accusation about politician. Editor did not intend harm but knew publication would harm reputation → Defamation proven.

---

#### 5. Reputation Harmed

**Reputation**: Estimation in which person held by society

**Test**: Would imputation lower person's reputation in eyes of **reasonable person**?

**Subjective vs. Objective**:
- **Objective Test**: Would reasonable person think less of defamed person after hearing imputation? (Not defamed person's own feelings)
- **Hurt Feelings ≠ Defamation**: Person may be offended, but if reputation not lowered → Not defamation

**Example**: "A is ugly" → Hurtful but not defamatory (does not lower moral reputation)
**Example**: "A is dishonest" → Defamatory (lowers moral reputation)

## Ten Exceptions to Defamation (Section 499 Exceptions)

**Effect**: If imputation falls under exception → **NOT defamation** (even if reputation harmed).

---

### Exception 1: Imputation of Truth for Public Good

**Requirements**:
1. Imputation is **TRUE**
2. Made for **public good** (not personal vendetta)

**Example**: Journalist publishes article exposing politician's corruption (with evidence) → Exception 1 applies (truth + public good).

**Burden**: Accused must prove (i) truth of statement, (ii) public good purpose.

---

### Exception 2: Public Conduct of Public Servants

**Requirements**:
1. Imputation concerning **public servant**'s discharge of public functions
2. Made in **good faith** (honest belief in truth)

**Example**: Citizen criticizes government official's handling of public project (honest belief) → Exception 2 applies.

**Not Exception**: Attack on private character of public servant (e.g., family life, personal habits) unrelated to official duties.

---

### Exception 3: Conduct of Person Touching Public Question

**Requirements**:
1. Imputation concerning person's **conduct in public matter**
2. Made in **good faith**

**Example**: Activist criticizes corporate CEO's environmental policies affecting public health → Exception 3 applies.

---

### Exception 4: Publication of Reports of Court Proceedings

**Requirements**:
1. Substantially true report of **judicial proceedings** (trial, judgment, court orders)
2. Made in **good faith**

**Example**: Newspaper reports accurately on court's conviction of accused in murder trial → Exception 4 applies.

**Rationale**: Open justice principle (public has right to know court proceedings).

---

### Exception 5: Merits of Cases Decided or Conduct of Judges

**Requirements**:
1. Opinion on **merits of case** decided by court OR **conduct of judge** in official capacity
2. Expressed in **good faith**

**Example**: Legal scholar writes article criticizing Supreme Court judgment on constitutional law → Exception 5 applies (good faith critique of judicial decision).

**Not Exception**: Attack on judge's personal character unrelated to official conduct.

---

### Exception 6: Merits of Public Performance

**Requirements**:
1. Opinion on **public performance** (literary, artistic, scientific work)
2. Expressed in **good faith**

**Example**: Film critic writes negative review of movie ("acting was poor, script weak") → Exception 6 applies.

**Not Exception**: Attack on artist's personal character ("actor is alcoholic") unrelated to performance.

---

### Exception 7: Censure by Person Having Authority

**Requirements**:
1. Censure passed by person having **lawful authority** over person censured
2. Made in **good faith**

**Example**: Employer issues warning letter to employee for poor performance (factual basis) → Exception 7 applies.

**Not Exception**: Employer publishes defamatory statement without factual basis or authority.

---

### Exception 8: Accusation Preferred in Good Faith

**Requirements**:
1. Accusation made to **competent authority** (police, court, investigating agency)
2. Made in **good faith** (honest belief in truth)

**Example**: Citizen files police complaint accusing neighbor of theft (honest belief + evidence) → Exception 8 applies.

**Rationale**: Encourage reporting of crimes without fear of defamation suits.

---

### Exception 9: Imputation Made in Good Faith for Protection of Interests

**Requirements**:
1. Imputation made in **good faith** for protection of:
   - Accuser's own interests, OR
   - Interests of person on whose behalf accusation made, OR
   - Public interest

**Example**: Employee warns employer that colleague is embezzling company funds (good faith + protecting employer's interests) → Exception 9 applies.

---

### Exception 10: Caution Intended for Good of Person or Public

**Requirements**:
1. Caution intended for **good of person** cautioned OR **public**
2. Made in **good faith**

**Example**: A warns B not to employ C as accountant because C has history of financial misconduct (good faith warning) → Exception 10 applies.

## Punishment for Defamation

### IPC Section 500 / BNS Section 357

> "Whoever defames another shall be punished with simple imprisonment up to **2 years** OR fine OR both."

**Nature**: Non-cognizable (police cannot arrest without warrant), bailable, compoundable (parties can settle), triable by Magistrate.

---

### Aggravated Form: Printing/Engraving Defamatory Matter (IPC Section 501 / BNS Section 358)

**Elements**:
1. Prints or engraves defamatory matter
2. **Knowing or having good reason to believe** it is defamatory

**Punishment**: Simple imprisonment up to **2 years** OR fine OR both

**Example**: Newspaper publisher prints defamatory article (knowing it is false) → Section 500 + Section 501.

---

### Sale of Defamatory Matter (IPC Section 502 / BNS Section 359)

**Elements**:
1. Sells or offers to sell printed/engraved defamatory matter
2. **Knowing** it contains defamatory matter

**Punishment**: Simple imprisonment up to **2 years** OR fine OR both

**Example**: Bookstore sells defamatory pamphlets (knowing contents) → Section 502.

## Criminal vs. Civil Defamation

| Aspect | **Criminal Defamation (IPC 499-500)** | **Civil Defamation (Tort)** |
|--------|---------------------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Purpose** | Punishment + deterrence (criminal sanction) | Compensation (monetary damages) |
| **Punishment** | Imprisonment up to 2 years OR fine | **No imprisonment** (only damages) |
| **Burden of Proof** | Beyond reasonable doubt (criminal standard) | Balance of probabilities (civil standard) |
| **Initiated By** | Defamed person files **criminal complaint** (Magistrate court) | Defamed person files **civil suit** (damages claim) |
| **Compoundable?** | **YES** (parties can settle, withdraw complaint) | YES (parties can settle out of court) |
| **Procedure** | CrPC (Magistrate trial, 6-12 months) | CPC (civil suit, 2-5 years typically) |
| **Remedy** | Punishment (deterrence) | Damages (typically ₹1 lakh - ₹50 lakh, depending on severity + status) |
| **Parallel Proceedings?** | **YES** (criminal + civil can run simultaneously) | YES (victim can file both criminal complaint + civil suit) |

## Constitutional Validity: Freedom of Speech vs. Reputation

### Article 19(1)(a) (Freedom of Speech) vs. Article 21 (Right to Reputation)

**Conflict**:
- **Article 19(1)(a)**: Citizens have right to freedom of speech and expression
- **Article 21**: Reputation is integral to right to life and personal liberty (right to reputation)

**Challenge**: Criminal defamation restricts free speech (chilling effect on criticism of government/powerful individuals).

---

### Landmark Case: **Subramanian Swamy v. Union of India (2016)**

**Facts**: Petitioners (Arvind Kejriwal, Rahul Gandhi, Subramanian Swamy) challenged constitutional validity of IPC Sections 499-500, arguing:
1. Criminal defamation violates Article 19(1)(a) (freedom of speech)
2. Disproportionate punishment (imprisonment) for speech offense
3. Chilling effect on political discourse and investigative journalism

**Issues**:
1. Is criminal defamation constitutional?
2. Does it violate freedom of speech under Article 19(1)(a)?

---

**Holding**: Supreme Court (2-1 majority) **upheld constitutional validity** of criminal defamation.

**Reasoning**:

**1. Reputation is Constitutional Right** (Article 21):
- Reputation integral to life and personal liberty (Article 21)
- Cannot be sacrificed at altar of free speech
- Balance required between free speech (Article 19) and reputation (Article 21)

**2. Reasonable Restriction** (Article 19(2)):
- Article 19(2) allows restrictions on free speech for "defamation"
- Criminal sanction (imprisonment) is reasonable restriction (deterrent against malicious defamation)

**3. Civil Remedy Insufficient**:
- Civil damages inadequate deterrent (rich/powerful can afford to defame, pay damages)
- Criminal punishment (imprisonment) serves deterrent purpose
- Complainant can choose civil OR criminal OR both (freedom to elect remedy)

**4. International Comparison**:
- Many democracies (UK, Australia, Canada - until recently) had criminal defamation
- India's criminal defamation not uniquely restrictive

**5. Public Good Exception**:
- Section 499 Exception 1 (truth + public good) protects genuine public interest reporting
- Good faith criticism of public figures protected (Exception 2-3)
- Chilling effect argument overstated (10 exceptions provide sufficient breathing space)

**Dissent** (Justice Nariman):
- Criminal defamation disproportionate (civil damages sufficient)
- Chilling effect on free speech real (journalists, activists fear imprisonment)
- Should decriminalize defamation (follow global trend - UK abolished criminal defamation in 2010)

**Aftermath**: Criminal defamation remains valid law (post-2016), but debate continues on reform.

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Criminal Defamation (IPC Section 500)

**Scenario**: A (politician) posts on Twitter: "B (rival politician) has embezzled ₹10 crore from government funds." Statement is false (no evidence). Post seen by 100,000 followers. B files criminal complaint.

**Analysis**:
- **Imputation**: Allegation of embezzlement (criminal conduct)
- **Publication**: 100,000 followers (third parties)
- **Intention**: A intended to harm B's reputation (political rivalry)
- **Reputation Harmed**: Accusation of corruption lowers B's reputation
- **No Exception**: No evidence (not truth - Exception 1 fails), not good faith (malicious political attack - Exceptions 2-3 fail)

**Charge**: **IPC Section 500** (defamation)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to 2 years OR fine OR both.

**Civil Parallel**: B can also file civil defamation suit claiming damages (₹10 lakh-₹50 lakh).

---

### Example 2: Exception 1 (Truth + Public Good)

**Scenario**: Investigative journalist publishes article: "Corporate CEO X evaded ₹50 crore in taxes." Journalist has income tax documents, whistleblower testimony (evidence). CEO files criminal complaint.

**Analysis**:
- **Imputation**: Tax evasion allegation (criminal conduct)
- **Publication**: Newspaper (thousands of readers)
- **Reputation Harmed**: Lowers CEO's reputation
- **Exception 1**: (i) **True** (documentary evidence), (ii) **Public good** (exposing tax evasion serves public interest)

**Charge**: **NO defamation** (Exception 1 applies - truth + public good defense)

**Journalist's Burden**: Prove truth (produce tax documents, witness testimony) + public good purpose (not personal vendetta).

---

### Example 3: Exception 6 (Critique of Public Performance)

**Scenario**: Film critic writes review: "Actor A's performance in Movie X was terrible - worst acting I've seen." A files criminal complaint.

**Analysis**:
- **Imputation**: Criticism of acting performance
- **Publication**: Newspaper/online review (public)
- **Reputation Harmed**: May lower A's professional reputation
- **Exception 6**: (i) **Public performance** (movie released in theaters), (ii) **Good faith** (critic's honest opinion on performance)

**Charge**: **NO defamation** (Exception 6 applies - bona fide critique of public performance)

**Note**: If critic attacked A's personal character ("A is drunk on set, unprofessional") unrelated to performance → Exception 6 fails, defamation stands.

---

### Example 4: Exception 8 (Accusation to Competent Authority)

**Scenario**: A files police complaint: "My neighbor B stole my bicycle." A provides description, timeline. Police investigate, find no evidence, B innocent. B files defamation complaint against A.

**Analysis**:
- **Imputation**: Accusation of theft (criminal conduct)
- **Publication**: Complaint to police (third party)
- **Reputation Harmed**: Accusation lowers B's reputation
- **Exception 8**: (i) **Competent authority** (police), (ii) **Good faith** (A honestly believed B stole bicycle, had some basis - saw B near bicycle)

**Charge**: **NO defamation** (Exception 8 applies - good faith accusation to police)

**Rationale**: Law encourages crime reporting; citizens should not fear defamation suits for honest police complaints.

---

### Example 5: No Exception (Malicious Statement)

**Scenario**: A posts on Facebook: "B (business competitor) sells substandard products, his company is fraud." Statement is false, A has no evidence. B's sales drop 30% after post. B files criminal + civil complaints.

**Analysis**:
- **Imputation**: Fraud allegation (business reputation harmed)
- **Publication**: Facebook post (hundreds of friends/customers)
- **Intention**: A intended to harm B's business reputation (commercial rivalry)
- **Reputation Harmed**: B's business reputation lowered, sales dropped
- **No Exception**: (i) Not true (false statement), (ii) Not good faith (malicious), (iii) Not public good (personal vendetta)

**Charge**: **IPC Section 500** (criminal defamation) + **Civil suit** (damages ₹10 lakh - loss of business)

**Punishment**: Criminal: 2 years imprisonment OR fine; Civil: ₹10 lakh damages + injunction (remove post).

## Compliance Checklist: Investigation & Prosecution

### For Complainant (Defamed Person)

**Filing Criminal Complaint**:
- [ ] Draft complaint (Magistrate court under CrPC Section 200) specifying:
  - Exact defamatory statement (words, publication, date)
  - How reputation harmed (lowered estimation in society)
  - Publication details (who saw/heard, number of third parties)
  - No exception applies (address potential defenses)
- [ ] Evidence: Screenshot (social media), newspaper clipping, witness affidavits (third parties who saw publication)
- [ ] Limitation: File within **1 year** of publication (CrPC Section 468 - limitation for offenses punishable <3 years)

**Parallel Civil Suit**:
- [ ] File civil defamation suit claiming damages (₹1 lakh-₹50 lakh depending on severity + status)
- [ ] Seek injunction (remove defamatory content from internet/publication)

---

### For Accused (Defamation Charge)

**Defense Strategy**:
- [ ] **Exception Defense**: Identify applicable exception (truth + public good, good faith criticism, competent authority accusation)
- [ ] **Burden on Accused**: Prove exception applies (provide evidence - documents, witness testimony)
- [ ] **Challenge Publication**: Argue no third party saw/heard statement (private communication only)
- [ ] **Challenge Reputation Harm**: Argue statement did not lower reputation (opinion, not fact; truthful statement)

**Settlement (Compounding)**:
- [ ] Negotiate settlement with complainant (apology + compensation)
- [ ] File compounding application (CrPC Section 320 - defamation is compoundable offense)
- [ ] Magistrate approval required (case dismissed upon settlement)

---

### For Magistrate (Adjudication)

**Preliminary Inquiry** (CrPC Section 202):
- [ ] Examine complainant on oath
- [ ] Review evidence (screenshots, publications, witness statements)
- [ ] Determine: Prima facie case for defamation? (if no → dismiss complaint; if yes → issue process/summons to accused)

**Trial**:
- [ ] Prosecution proves: (i) Imputation made, (ii) Publication to third party, (iii) Reputation harmed, (iv) Intent/knowledge
- [ ] Accused proves: Exception applies (burden shifts to accused per Section 499 exceptions)
- [ ] Consider: Good faith, public interest, truth of statement

**Compounding** (if parties settle):
- [ ] Verify settlement voluntary (no coercion)
- [ ] Approve compounding application → Dismiss case

### Section Mapping

| IPC Section | BNS Section | Change |
|-------------|-------------|--------|
| IPC 499 (Definition) | BNS 356 | Definition unchanged (10 exceptions retained) |
