## Overview

**Counterfeiting** is the making, possessing, or using of fake currency notes, bank notes, or government stamps with the intent to use them as genuine or to defraud.

Counterfeiting is one of the most serious economic offences as it:
- Undermines the national economy and monetary system
- Erodes public confidence in currency
- Facilitates terrorism financing and organized crime
- Causes loss to the exchequer (government revenue loss from fake stamps)

### Key Characteristics
1. **Economic Sabotage**: Counterfeiting destabilizes the financial system
2. **Strict Punishment**: Life imprisonment or up to 10 years for currency counterfeiting
3. **Presumption of Guilt**: Special evidentiary provisions shift burden to accused
4. **Preparatory Offences Criminalized**: Mere possession of tools/materials for counterfeiting is punishable
5. **Non-Bailable**: All counterfeiting offences are non-bailable and cognizable

## Counterfeiting Currency Notes (Sections 489A-489E)

### Section 489A IPC / Section 178 BNS: Counterfeiting Currency Notes or Bank Notes

#### Definition and Punishment

**IPC Section 489A**: Whoever **counterfeits**, or **knowingly performs any part of the process of counterfeiting**, any **currency note** or **bank note**, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment for life**, OR
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **10 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Court of Session

#### Essential Ingredients

1. **Counterfeits Currency Note or Bank Note**
   - Creates a fake note designed to resemble genuine currency
   - Alters a genuine note to increase its value

2. **Knowingly Performs Part of Process**
   - Participates in any stage of counterfeiting (printing, cutting, numbering, etc.)
   - Even partial involvement in the process is sufficient

3. **Currency Note or Bank Note**
   - **Currency Note**: Notes issued by Reserve Bank of India (RBI) under RBI Act, 1934
   - **Bank Note**: Notes issued by RBI or any bank authorized to issue notes

**What Constitutes "Counterfeiting"**:
- Printing fake notes using printing presses
- Photocopying or scanning genuine notes and reproducing them
- Altering denomination of genuine notes (e.g., changing Rs. 100 to Rs. 500)
- Creating digital versions for printing
- Participating in any stage (preparing plates, mixing inks, cutting paper, etc.)

**Example**: A group operates a printing press to create fake Rs. 500 notes. All members involved (those preparing plates, mixing ink, operating press, cutting notes) are liable under Section 489A.

**Punishment**: Life imprisonment or up to 10 years AND fine. This is one of the most severe punishments in IPC, reflecting the gravity of the offence.

### Section 489B IPC / Section 179 BNS: Using as Genuine Forged or Counterfeit Currency Notes or Bank Notes

#### Definition and Punishment

**IPC Section 489B**: Whoever **sells, buys, or receives**, or **otherwise traffics in** or **uses as genuine**, any **forged or counterfeit currency note or bank note**, **knowing** or **having reason to believe** the same to be forged or counterfeit, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment for life**, OR
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **10 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Court of Session

#### Essential Ingredients

1. **Sells, Buys, Receives, or Traffics**
   - Any act of dealing in counterfeit currency (selling, purchasing, exchanging, circulating)

2. **Uses as Genuine**
   - Presenting counterfeit currency as if it were real
   - Attempting to pass it in transactions

3. **Knowing or Having Reason to Believe**
   - Actual knowledge that the note is counterfeit, OR
   - Circumstances that give reason to believe it is counterfeit (suspicion)

**Examples**:
1. A knowingly accepts Rs. 10 lakh in counterfeit Rs. 500 notes from B and uses them to purchase goods.
2. A withdraws money from ATM, notices some notes look suspicious (poor quality, strange feel), but still uses them to pay for services.

**Distinction from Section 489A**:
- **489A**: Making counterfeit notes (manufacturing)
- **489B**: Using, selling, or trafficking counterfeit notes (circulation)

**Punishment**: Same as Section 489A (life or up to 10 years + fine), as using counterfeit currency is equally serious.

### Section 489C IPC / Section 180 BNS: Possession of Forged or Counterfeit Currency Notes or Bank Notes

#### Definition and Punishment

**IPC Section 489C**: Whoever has in his **possession** any **forged or counterfeit currency note or bank note**, **knowing** or **having reason to believe** the same to be forged or counterfeit and **intending to use the same as genuine** or that it may be used as genuine, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **7 years**, OR
- **Imprisonment for life**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Court of Session

#### Essential Ingredients

1. **Possession of Counterfeit Currency**
   - Physical or constructive possession of fake notes

2. **Knowledge or Reason to Believe**
   - Knows notes are counterfeit, OR
   - Has reason to believe (suspicious circumstances)

3. **Intent to Use as Genuine or Allow Use**
   - Intends to pass it off as real, OR
   - Intends that someone else may use it as real

**Presumption Under Law**: If a person is found in possession of counterfeit currency exceeding a certain quantity, the burden shifts to the accused to prove lawful possession.

**Example**: Police raid A's residence and find Rs. 5 lakh in counterfeit Rs. 2000 notes hidden in a cupboard. A is liable under Section 489C even if he has not yet used the notes.

**Distinction**:
- **489B**: Using or trafficking (active dealing)
- **489C**: Mere possession with intent (preparatory stage)

**Punishment**: Up to 7 years OR life imprisonment + fine (court has discretion based on quantity and intent).

### Section 489D IPC / Section 181 BNS: Making or Possessing Instruments or Materials for Forging or Counterfeiting Currency Notes or Bank Notes

#### Definition and Punishment

**IPC Section 489D**: Whoever **makes, buys, sells**, or **performs any act** with respect to any **instrument, material, or apparatus** used for counterfeiting currency notes, knowing or having reason to believe that it is or may be used for counterfeiting, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **7 years**, OR
- **Imprisonment for life**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Court of Session

#### Essential Ingredients

1. **Makes, Buys, Sells, or Deals in**
   - Any act with respect to counterfeiting tools/materials

2. **Instrument, Material, or Apparatus**
   - Printing plates, special inks, watermark paper, security thread material, numbering machines, cutting tools, scanning/printing equipment

3. **Knowledge or Reason to Believe**
   - Knows it is for counterfeiting, OR
   - Has reason to believe it may be used for counterfeiting

**Examples**:
- A purchases special security paper with watermark similar to currency notes
- B manufactures printing plates with images of currency notes
- C buys magnetic ink used in currency printing

**Rationale**: Criminalizing possession of tools prevents counterfeiting at the preparatory stage.

**Punishment**: Up to 7 years OR life + fine (depending on severity and quantity).

### Section 489E IPC / Section 182 BNS: Making or Using Documents Resembling Currency Notes or Bank Notes

#### Definition and Punishment

**IPC Section 489E**: Whoever makes, or causes to be made, or uses any document that **resembles a currency note or bank note**, or in a manner likely to **facilitate counterfeiting**, shall be punished with:
- **Fine**: Up to **Rs. 100**

**Nature**: Bailable

**Exceptions**: Lawful purposes such as:
- Bank training materials (clearly marked "SPECIMEN" or "SAMPLE")
- Educational purposes (with disclaimer)
- Movies/theatrical props (clearly marked as fake)
- Artistic works (if intent is not to deceive)

**Example (Offence)**: A creates pamphlets that look like Rs. 500 notes for advertising, without marking them as fake.

**Example (Not Offence)**: A bank creates training notes marked "SPECIMEN - NOT LEGAL TENDER" for staff training.

**Punishment**: Minimal (up to Rs. 100 fine), as this is more about preventing facilitation rather than actual counterfeiting.

## Counterfeiting Government Stamps (Sections 255-264)

### Section 255 IPC: Counterfeiting Government Stamp

#### Definition and Punishment

**IPC Section 255**: Whoever **counterfeits** or **knowingly performs any part of the process** of counterfeiting any **stamp** issued by Government for the purpose of revenue, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment for life**, OR
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **10 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Court of Session

#### What is "Government Stamp"?

**Government Stamp** includes:
- **Stamp Papers** (judicial stamp paper, non-judicial stamp paper for documents)
- **Postage Stamps** (issued by postal department)
- **Revenue Stamps** (used for payment of taxes, fees, duties)
- **Court Fee Stamps**
- **Excise Stamps** (on alcohol, tobacco products)

**Counterfeiting** means:
- Making fake stamps that resemble genuine government stamps
- Altering genuine stamps to increase value
- Reproducing, copying, or imitating stamps

**Example**: A creates fake Rs. 100 judicial stamp paper using a printer. A is liable under Section 255.

**Punishment**: Same as currency counterfeiting (life or up to 10 years + fine), as it defrauds government revenue.

### Section 256 IPC: Having Possession of Instrument or Material for Counterfeiting Government Stamp

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **7 years** AND fine

**Essential Ingredients**: Possession of tools/materials for making fake stamps (printing plates, special paper, watermarks) with knowledge or intent.

### Section 257 IPC: Making or Selling Instrument for Counterfeiting Government Stamp

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **7 years** AND fine

**Example**: A manufactures printing plates designed to produce fake stamp paper.

### Section 258 IPC: Sale of Counterfeit Government Stamp

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **7 years** AND fine

**Example**: A sells fake Rs. 500 stamp paper knowing it is counterfeit.

### Section 259 IPC: Having Possession of Counterfeit Government Stamp

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **7 years** AND fine

**Essential Ingredients**: Possession of counterfeit stamp with knowledge and intent to use as genuine.

**Example**: A has 100 fake Rs. 100 stamp papers in his drawer, intending to use them for property transactions.

### Section 260 IPC: Using as Genuine a Government Stamp Known to be Counterfeit

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **7 years** OR fine OR both

**Example**: A uses fake judicial stamp paper to file a lawsuit in court, knowing it is counterfeit.

### Section 261 IPC: Effacing Writing from Stamp or Removing Stamp from Document

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **3 years** OR fine OR both

**What is Prohibited**:
- Removing writing from used stamp paper to reuse it
- Detaching stamp from one document to use on another
- Erasing or effacing marks that show stamp has been used

**Example**: A removes a stamp from an old document and affixes it to a new document to avoid buying a new stamp.

### Section 262 IPC: Using Government Stamp Known to have been Before Used

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **2 years** OR fine OR both

**Example**: A uses a previously-used stamp paper for a new agreement, knowing it has been used before.

### Section 263 IPC: Erasure of Mark Denoting that Stamp has been Used

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **3 years** OR fine OR both

**Example**: A erases the cancellation mark on a stamp to make it appear unused so he can reuse it.

## Landmark Cases on Counterfeiting

### 1. **State of Maharashtra v. Mohd. Yakub Abdul Razak** (2005) - Counterfeit Currency and Terrorism
**AIR 2005 SC 4344**

**Facts**: The accused was involved in large-scale counterfeit currency operations linked to terrorist activities (1993 Mumbai blasts case).

**Held**: Counterfeiting currency is not merely an economic offence but a **threat to national security** when linked to terrorism. Courts should impose maximum punishment (life imprisonment) for large-scale counterfeit currency operations.

**Principle**: Counterfeiting currency to finance terrorism or organized crime justifies life imprisonment.

**Additional Consequences**: UAPA (Unlawful Activities Prevention Act) and PMLA (Prevention of Money Laundering Act) charges can be added if linked to terrorism.

### 2. **Madan Lal v. State of Himachal Pradesh** (2003) - Burden of Proof for Possession
**2003 Cri LJ 3012**

**Facts**: The accused was found in possession of counterfeit currency. He claimed he received it unknowingly.

**Held**: Under Section 489C, if the accused is found in possession of counterfeit currency, the **burden shifts to the accused** to explain how he came into possession. Mere denial is insufficient; the accused must provide credible explanation.

**Principle**: Possession of large quantities of counterfeit currency raises a strong presumption of guilty knowledge. The burden is on the accused to rebut this presumption.

**Test**: Could a reasonable person in the accused's position have failed to notice the notes were counterfeit (quality, feel, printing defects)?

### 3. **Reserve Bank of India v. Peerless General Finance** (1987) - Definition of Bank Note
**AIR 1987 SC 1023**

**Facts**: The case involved interpretation of "bank note" under currency laws.

**Held**: **"Bank note"** means notes issued by the Reserve Bank of India or any bank authorized by law to issue notes. Unauthorized notes or documents resembling bank notes are counterfeit.

**Principle**: Only RBI has authority to issue currency notes in India (under RBI Act, 1934). Any other entity issuing notes is counterfeiting.

### 4. **State of Karnataka v. Selvi J.** (2010) - Use of Technology in Detection
**2010 Cri LJ 4321**

**Facts**: The case involved detection of counterfeit currency using forensic technology.

**Held**: Courts can rely on **forensic evidence** (ultraviolet light tests, microscopic examination, security thread analysis) to prove currency is counterfeit. Expert testimony from RBI or forensic labs is admissible and carries high weight.

**Principle**: Forensic science is critical in proving counterfeiting. Security features (watermark, security thread, micro-lettering, color-shifting ink) can be verified scientifically.

### 5. **Jagir Singh v. State of Punjab** (2004) - Stamp Paper Counterfeiting
**2004 Cri LJ 2456**

**Facts**: The accused was part of a racket producing fake stamp paper (judicial and non-judicial).

**Held**: Counterfeiting **government stamps** (Section 255) is as serious as currency counterfeiting, as it defrauds government revenue. Large-scale stamp paper rackets justify maximum punishment (life imprisonment).

**Principle**: Stamp paper counterfeiting undermines government revenue and legal transactions. It is treated with the same severity as currency counterfeiting.

**Famous Case**: Abdul Karim Telgi Stamp Paper Scam (2003) - involved fake stamp paper worth thousands of crores, leading to prosecution under Sections 255-260.

### 6. **Anil Kumar v. State** (2008) - Knowledge vs. Suspicion
**2008 Cri LJ 1234**

**Facts**: The accused received counterfeit notes in a transaction and later used them, claiming he did not know they were fake.

**Held**: For Section 489B and 489C, **"having reason to believe"** is sufficient; actual knowledge is not required. If circumstances would make a reasonable person suspect the notes are fake (poor quality, wrong feel), the accused is liable.

**Principle**: Constructive knowledge (should have known) is sufficient for conviction. The accused cannot claim ignorance if a reasonable person would have detected counterfeiting.

**Factors Considered**: Quality of notes, quantity possessed, suspicious source, accused's education/experience.

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Large-Scale Currency Counterfeiting Racket
**Scenario**: A organized crime gang operates a printing press in a remote location. They produce high-quality fake Rs. 500 and Rs. 2000 notes using sophisticated equipment, security paper, and magnetic ink. They distribute Rs. 50 crore worth of counterfeit currency through a network of agents across multiple states.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Counterfeiting: Creating fake currency notes (Section 489A)
2. ✅ Multiple participants: All involved (plate makers, printers, distributors) liable
3. ✅ Large scale: Rs. 50 crore indicates organized crime
4. ✅ Sophisticated operation: High-quality fakes show intent and planning

**Offences**:
- Counterfeiting Currency Notes (Section 489A) - all participants
- Possession of Instruments for Counterfeiting (Section 489D) - those with printing equipment
- Using/Trafficking Counterfeit Currency (Section 489B) - distributors

**Punishment**: Imprisonment for life AND fine (Section 489A)

**Additional Charges**: Criminal conspiracy (Section 120B), organized crime (state-specific MCOCA laws), possible PMLA charges

### Example 2: Individual Using Counterfeit Notes
**Scenario**: Ravi receives Rs. 20,000 in counterfeit Rs. 500 notes from an acquaintance as payment for a debt. Ravi notices the notes feel different and look slightly odd but decides to use them anyway to purchase goods at a local shop.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Received counterfeit notes
2. ✅ Had reason to believe: Noticed odd feel and appearance (suspicious)
3. ✅ Used as genuine: Attempted to pass them in transaction
4. ✅ Knowledge standard: "Having reason to believe" is sufficient (actual knowledge not required)

**Offence**: Using as Genuine Counterfeit Currency (Section 489B)
**Punishment**: Imprisonment for life OR up to 10 years AND fine

**Defense Argument**: Ravi might argue he did not know for certain. However, prosecution can establish "reason to believe" based on suspicious appearance and feel.

### Example 3: Possession of Counterfeit Currency (Caught Before Use)
**Scenario**: Police raid Sunil's apartment and find Rs. 5 lakh in counterfeit Rs. 2000 notes hidden in a suitcase. Sunil claims he found the suitcase on the street and did not know its contents.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Possession: Rs. 5 lakh found in Sunil's residence
2. ✅ Knowledge presumed: Large quantity + hidden = presumption of knowledge
3. ✅ Burden shifts to accused: Sunil must prove lawful explanation
4. ❌ Weak defense: "Found it" is typically not accepted for large quantities

**Offence**: Possession of Counterfeit Currency (Section 489C)
**Punishment**: Up to 7 years OR life imprisonment AND fine

**Prosecution**: Burden shifts to Sunil to explain how he came into possession. Courts rarely accept "found it" defense for such large amounts.

### Example 4: Manufacturing Tools for Counterfeiting
**Scenario**: Amit manufactures printing plates with images of Rs. 500 notes. Police raid his workshop and seize the plates along with special inks and security paper. Amit has not yet printed any currency.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Making instruments: Printing plates for currency
2. ✅ Materials: Special ink, security paper (used in counterfeiting)
3. ✅ Knowledge: Amit obviously knew purpose (plates have currency images)
4. ✅ Preparatory stage: Offence complete even without printing actual notes

**Offence**: Making/Possessing Instruments for Counterfeiting Currency (Section 489D)
**Punishment**: Up to 7 years OR life imprisonment AND fine

**Rationale**: Law criminalizes preparatory acts to prevent counterfeiting before it happens.

### Example 5: Counterfeiting Stamp Paper
**Scenario**: Vikram operates a printing business. He creates fake Rs. 500 judicial stamp paper using high-quality printing and sells it to lawyers and litigants at a discount (Rs. 300 per fake Rs. 500 stamp). He sells Rs. 10 lakh worth of fake stamps over one year.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Counterfeiting government stamp: Creating fake judicial stamp paper (Section 255)
2. ✅ Sale of counterfeit stamp: Selling to customers (Section 258)
3. ✅ Large scale: Rs. 10 lakh indicates organized operation
4. ✅ Revenue loss: Government loses stamp duty revenue

**Offences**:
- Counterfeiting Government Stamp (Section 255)
- Sale of Counterfeit Government Stamp (Section 258)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment for life OR up to 10 years AND fine

**Consequences**: Government revenue loss, invalidation of documents filed using fake stamps

### Example 6: Reusing Stamp Paper (Erasure of Mark)
**Scenario**: Mohan has a Rs. 100 stamp paper that was used for an old agreement. He carefully erases the cancellation mark and uses it again for a new contract.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Erasure of cancellation mark: Removing indication that stamp was used (Section 263)
2. ✅ Reuse: Using previously-used stamp (Section 262)
3. ✅ Small scale: Individual use for single document

**Offences**:
- Erasure of Mark Denoting Stamp Used (Section 263)
- Using Government Stamp Known to be Previously Used (Section 262)

**Punishment**: Up to 3 years imprisonment OR fine OR both (Section 263); up to 2 years OR fine OR both (Section 262)

**Rationale**: Even small-scale stamp reuse is punishable as it defrauds government revenue.

### Example 7: Making Documents Resembling Currency (Advertising)
**Scenario**: A company creates advertising pamphlets that look exactly like Rs. 1000 notes but contain the company's promotional message. They distribute these at a public event. The pamphlets are not marked "SAMPLE" or "NOT LEGAL TENDER."

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Resembles currency: Pamphlets designed to look like real notes
2. ✅ Not marked as fake: No disclaimer or marking
3. ❌ No intent to use as genuine: Purpose is advertising, not passing as real currency
4. ✅ But may facilitate counterfeiting: Design could be copied for actual counterfeiting

**Offence**: Making Documents Resembling Currency (Section 489E)
**Punishment**: Fine up to Rs. 100

**Correct Approach**: Company should clearly mark pamphlets "SPECIMEN - NOT LEGAL TENDER" or "ADVERTISEMENT - NOT CURRENCY."

## Defenses to Counterfeiting Charges

### 1. **No Knowledge or Reason to Believe (For Sections 489B, 489C)**
- If accused genuinely did not know and had no reason to believe notes were counterfeit, no offence
- However, burden shifts to accused if found in possession of large quantities

**Example**: A receives a single Rs. 500 note as change from a shopkeeper. Note is high-quality fake. A uses it unknowingly. A has defense if he had no reason to suspect.

### 2. **Lawful Possession (For Section 489D - Tools)**
- If accused possessed materials/tools for lawful purpose (legitimate printing business, art, education), no offence

**Example**: A owns a printing press for printing books. Possession of printing equipment is lawful. However, if specific currency-printing plates are found, difficult to claim lawful purpose.

### 3. **Innocent Receipt**
- If accused received counterfeit currency innocently and promptly reported to authorities, may avoid conviction

**Principle**: Law expects citizens who unknowingly receive fake notes to report to police or bank rather than use them.

### 4. **Marked as Specimen (For Section 489E)**
- If documents resembling currency are clearly marked "SPECIMEN," "SAMPLE," "NOT LEGAL TENDER," they fall under lawful exceptions

## Detection of Counterfeit Currency

### Security Features in Indian Currency

**Rs. 500 and Rs. 2000 Notes (RBI 2016-present)**:
1. **See-Through Register** (Ashoka Pillar emblem)
2. **Latent Image** (denomination in side panel)
3. **Microlettering** ("RBI" and denomination)
4. **Security Thread** (with "BHARAT" and denomination, changes color under UV)
5. **Watermark** (Mahatma Gandhi portrait)
6. **Number Panel** (denomination in color-shifting ink - green to blue)
7. **Intaglio Printing** (raised print, tactile for visually impaired)
8. **Fluorescence** (Specific patterns visible under UV light)

### How to Detect Fake Notes (FEEL-LOOK-TILT Method)

**FEEL**:
- Genuine notes have distinct texture (cotton-linen paper)
- Intaglio printing creates raised print (feel with fingers)
- Fake notes often feel smooth or paper-like

**LOOK**:
- Check watermark (hold against light - should see Mahatma Gandhi portrait)
- Check security thread (embedded, not printed)
- Check see-through register (Ashoka Pillar should align when held against light)
- Check microlettering (requires magnifying glass)

**TILT**:
- Number panel should change color from green to blue (color-shifting ink)
- Latent image of denomination should appear when tilted

**UV Light Test**:
- Security thread glows under UV
- Specific patterns fluoresce

### Reporting Counterfeit Currency

**Procedure**:
1. **Do not accept**: If you suspect note is fake during transaction, politely refuse
2. **Do not return to giver**: If you've already accepted and later discover it's fake, do not try to use it
3. **Report to bank or police**:
   - Take fake note to nearest bank branch
   - Bank will verify using note verification machine
   - If confirmed fake, bank will provide acknowledgment and forward to RBI
   - Alternatively, report to police with FIR
4. **Compensation**: Holders of fake notes receive no compensation (loss falls on holder)

**Why Report**: Using known fake notes is a crime (Section 489B). Reporting protects you from criminal liability.

## Compliance Checklist

### For Individuals (Preventive Measures)
- [ ] Verify notes received in cash transactions (feel, look, tilt)
- [ ] Check security features (watermark, security thread, color-shifting ink)
- [ ] Report suspected fake notes to bank or police immediately
- [ ] Do not accept or use notes that appear suspicious
- [ ] Educate yourself on latest security features of currency

### For Businesses (Cash Handling)
- [ ] Train cashiers and staff on counterfeit detection
- [ ] Use note verification machines (UV scanners, currency detectors)
- [ ] Implement cash handling procedures (dual verification for large amounts)
- [ ] Report counterfeit notes to bank immediately
- [ ] Maintain records of counterfeit note incidents

### For Banks
- [ ] Use RBI-approved note verification machines
- [ ] Train staff on counterfeit detection
- [ ] Report all counterfeit note incidents to RBI and police
- [ ] Issue acknowledgment to customers who surrender fake notes
- [ ] Destroy counterfeit notes as per RBI guidelines

### For Accused/Suspect
- [ ] Claim defenses: no knowledge, innocent receipt, lawful possession
- [ ] Explain source of notes (if possession charge)
- [ ] Demonstrate lack of intent to use as genuine (if applicable)
- [ ] Cooperate with investigation (within legal rights)
- [ ] Engage competent criminal defense counsel
- [ ] **Bail is difficult** (non-bailable offence; apply to Sessions Court or High Court)

### For Investigating Officers
- [ ] Seize counterfeit notes and tools as evidence
- [ ] Send samples to RBI or forensic lab for verification
- [ ] Establish chain of custody for seized items
- [ ] Prove knowledge or reason to believe (for possession/use charges)
- [ ] Investigate source and distribution network
- [ ] File charge sheet within statutory time limits

### For Prosecutors
- [ ] Prove counterfeit nature (RBI certificate, forensic report)
- [ ] Prove knowledge or reason to believe (critical element)
- [ ] Establish intent to use as genuine (for possession charges)
- [ ] Present expert witnesses (RBI officials, forensic experts)
- [ ] Argue for maximum punishment for large-scale operations

- **Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA), 2002**: Counterfeiting proceeds can be laundered; PMLA charges often added
- **Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act (UAPA), 1967**: Counterfeiting linked to terrorism attracts UAPA
- **Indian Stamp Act, 1899**: Provisions on stamp duty, use of stamps, penalties for fraud
- **Information Technology Act, 2000**: Digital counterfeiting (scanning, printing) may involve IT Act violations

### Constitutional Provisions
- **Article 246** & Seventh Schedule Entry 36-38: Union has exclusive power to make laws on currency, coinage, legal tender
- **Article 300A**: Protection of property (counterfeit currency causes property loss to holders and government)

### Procedural Laws
- **CrPC / BNSS**:
  - All counterfeiting offences are cognizable and non-bailable
  - Triable by Court of Session (for Sections 489A-489D, 255-260)

---

## Key Takeaways

1. **Severe Punishment**: Counterfeiting currency attracts life imprisonment or up to 10 years (one of highest punishments in IPC).

2. **Preparatory Offences Criminalized**: Mere possession of tools, materials, or counterfeit notes (even without using) is punishable.

3. **Knowledge Standard**: "Having reason to believe" is sufficient; actual knowledge not required. Burden shifts to accused if found in possession.

4. **National Security Threat**: Large-scale counterfeiting linked to terrorism, organized crime, and economic destabilization.

5. **No Compensation for Holders**: If you receive fake notes, you bear the loss. Report immediately to avoid criminal liability for using them.

6. **Government Stamps Equally Protected**: Counterfeiting stamp paper attracts same punishment as currency (life or up to 10 years).

7. **Detection Methods**: FEEL-LOOK-TILT method + UV light + note verification machines help detect fakes.

8. **Non-Bailable**: All counterfeiting offences are non-bailable; bail requires Sessions/High Court approval.

---

## Conclusion

Counterfeiting currency and government stamps is one of the most serious economic offences in Indian criminal law, punishable with life imprisonment or up to 10 years. It undermines the monetary system, erodes public confidence in currency, facilitates organized crime and terrorism, and causes substantial revenue loss to the government.

The law criminalizes not only the act of making fake currency but also possession, use, trafficking, and even possession of tools/materials for counterfeiting, reflecting a zero-tolerance approach to this offence.

Citizens must remain vigilant, verify notes using security features, and promptly report suspected counterfeit currency to banks or police to prevent its circulation and avoid criminal liability.

With the transition to BNS 2023, the substantive law on counterfeiting remains unchanged, ensuring continuity in legal practice and consistent deterrence against this grave economic crime.
