# India Criminal Law: Extortion (Jabar-Vusuli / Bhatta)

## Protocol ID
**IL-CRIM-EXTORTION**

## Domain
Criminal Law > Crimes Against Property

## Jurisdiction
India (All States and Union Territories)

## Overview

**Extortion** is the act of intentionally putting a person in fear of injury to himself or any other person, or to the reputation of himself or any other person, in order to dishonestly induce that person to deliver any property or valuable security.

Extortion is a property offence characterized by the use of **threat** or **fear** to obtain property. Unlike theft (where property is taken without consent) or robbery (where instant violence is used), extortion involves **obtaining property with consent, but that consent is procured by fear**.

### Key Characteristics
1. **Consent Under Fear**: Victim delivers property voluntarily but under fear
2. **Threat Element**: Fear is induced by threat of injury (physical, reputational, or to property)
3. **Broad Scope of Injury**: Injury includes harm to person, reputation, or property
4. **Future or Immediate**: Threat may be of immediate or future harm (unlike robbery which requires instant harm)
5. **Delivery Induced**: Victim must deliver or transfer property due to the fear

## Definition of Extortion (IPC Section 383 / BNS Section 308)

### Full Legal Definition

**IPC Section 383**: Whoever intentionally puts any person in fear of any injury to that person, or to any other person, or to the reputation of that person or of any other person, and thereby dishonestly induces the person so put in fear to deliver to any person any property or valuable security, or anything signed or sealed which may be converted into a valuable security, commits **extortion**.

### Seven Essential Ingredients of Extortion

#### 1. **Intentionally Putting in Fear**
- The accused must **intentionally** create fear in the victim's mind
- Accidental or unintentional fear does not constitute extortion
- The act must be deliberate and purposeful

**Example**: A tells B, "If you don't pay me Rs. 10 lakh, I will publish this compromising video of you." A intentionally puts B in fear of reputational injury.

#### 2. **Fear of Injury**
The fear must be of **injury** to:
- **The person himself** (physical harm to victim)
- **Any other person** (physical harm to victim's family, friends, etc.)
- **The reputation of the person** (defamation, dishonor of victim)
- **The reputation of any other person** (defamation of victim's family, etc.)

**Meaning of "Injury"** (IPC Section 44):
- Any harm whatever illegally caused to any person
- In body, mind, reputation, or property

**Types of Injury**:
1. **Physical Injury**: Threat of death, hurt, wrongful restraint, wrongful confinement
2. **Reputational Injury**: Threat of defamation, exposure of secrets, false accusations
3. **Property Injury**: Threat of damage to property, destruction of business

**Example 1 (Physical)**: A threatens to kill B's son unless B pays Rs. 5 lakh. Fear of physical injury to another person.

**Example 2 (Reputational)**: A threatens to expose B's extramarital affair unless B transfers property. Fear of reputational injury to B.

**Example 3 (Property)**: A threatens to burn down B's shop unless B pays monthly protection money. Fear of property injury.

#### 3. **Dishonestly Inducing Delivery**
- The fear must **induce** the victim to deliver property
- The induction must be **dishonest** (with intention of wrongful gain or wrongful loss)
- There must be a causal link between the fear and the delivery

**Dishonest** (IPC Section 24): Intention to cause wrongful gain to one person or wrongful loss to another

**Example**: If A threatens B and B pays money due to that threat, the delivery is induced by fear. But if B pays for some other reason (e.g., genuine debt repayment), no extortion.

#### 4. **Delivery of Property or Valuable Security**
The victim must deliver:
- **Property** (movable or immovable)
- **Valuable security** (documents of title, promissory notes, cheques, etc.)
- **Anything signed or sealed** that may be converted into a valuable security (blank cheques, signed documents)

**Note**: Unlike theft (which applies only to movable property), extortion can apply to immovable property as well (e.g., forcing someone to transfer land by threat).

**Example**: A threatens B and induces B to sign a deed transferring B's house to A. This is extortion of immovable property.

#### 5. **Delivery to Any Person**
- The property need not be delivered to the accused himself
- It may be delivered to any person designated by the accused

**Example**: A threatens B and induces B to transfer money to C's account (C is A's associate). A commits extortion even though property was delivered to C.

#### 6. **Actual Delivery Required**
- For completed extortion, the victim must actually deliver the property
- If property is not delivered (victim refuses or police intervene), it is **attempt to commit extortion** (Section 385 or 387 or 389)

#### 7. **No Consent Defense**
- The victim's consent to deliver property is **irrelevant** if it is obtained by fear
- Consent procured by threat is not free consent

## Punishment for Extortion

### Section 384 IPC / Section 309 BNS: Simple Extortion
**Punishment**:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **3 years**, OR
- **Fine**, OR
- **Both**

**Nature**: Non-cognizable, Bailable, Triable by Any Magistrate

**Example**: A threatens to report B to the police for a minor traffic violation unless B pays Rs. 5,000. B pays. A commits extortion.

### Section 385 IPC / Section 310 BNS: Putting Person in Fear of Injury to Commit Extortion
Whoever, in order to commit extortion, puts any person in fear of any injury, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **2 years**, OR
- **Fine**, OR
- **Both**

**Nature**: Non-cognizable, Bailable, Triable by Any Magistrate

**This is ATTEMPT to commit extortion**: The threat is made, but property is not delivered.

**Example**: A sends a threatening letter to B demanding money, but B refuses to pay and reports to police. A is liable under Section 385 (attempt).

### Section 386 IPC / Section 311 BNS: Extortion by Putting Person in Fear of Death or Grievous Hurt
Whoever commits extortion by putting any person in fear of **death** or **grievous hurt** to that person or any other, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **10 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Magistrate of First Class

**Aggravating Circumstance**: Threat of death or grievous hurt (most serious form of physical harm)

**Example**: A threatens to kill B's daughter unless B pays Rs. 50 lakh. B pays. A commits aggravated extortion under Section 386.

### Section 387 IPC / Section 312 BNS: Putting Person in Fear of Death or Grievous Hurt to Commit Extortion
Whoever, in order to commit extortion, puts any person in fear of **death** or **grievous hurt**, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **7 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Magistrate of First Class

**This is ATTEMPT to commit aggravated extortion**: Threat of death/GH is made, but property not delivered.

**Example**: A threatens B with death unless B transfers property. B refuses and reports to police. A is liable under Section 387 (attempt).

### Section 388 IPC / Section 313 BNS: Extortion by Threat of Accusation of an Offence Punishable with Death or Imprisonment for Life
Whoever commits extortion by putting any person in fear of an **accusation** against that person or any other person of having committed or attempted to commit:
- An offence **punishable with death**, OR
- An offence **punishable with imprisonment for life**, OR
- An offence **punishable with imprisonment for 10 years**, OR
- Of having **attempted to commit suicide** (before IPC amendment decriminalizing suicide attempt)

shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **10 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Magistrate of First Class

**Aggravating Circumstance**: Threat of serious criminal accusation (blackmail)

**Example**: A threatens to file a false case of rape (punishable with life imprisonment) against B unless B pays Rs. 10 lakh. B pays. A commits extortion under Section 388.

### Section 389 IPC / Section 314 BNS: Putting Person in Fear of Accusation of Offence to Commit Extortion
Whoever, in order to commit extortion, puts any person in fear of an accusation against that person or any other of having committed or attempted to commit an offence punishable with death, life, or 10 years imprisonment, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **10 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Magistrate of First Class

**This is ATTEMPT to commit extortion by accusation**: Threat made, property not delivered.

**Example**: A threatens to accuse B of murder unless B pays money. B refuses and reports to police. A is liable under Section 389 (attempt).

## Distinction Between Theft, Robbery, and Extortion

| **Element** | **Theft (378)** | **Robbery (390)** | **Extortion (383)** |
|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|---------------------|
| **Consent** | Without consent | Without consent (or consent under instant fear) | With consent (but obtained by fear) |
| **Taking vs. Delivery** | Offender takes property | Offender takes property (theft-robbery) OR victim delivers under instant fear (extortion-robbery) | Victim delivers property |
| **Violence/Threat** | No violence or threat | Violence OR threat of **instant** death/hurt/restraint | Threat of injury (may be **future** or immediate) |
| **Timing** | No time restriction | Instant harm required | Harm may be future or immediate |
| **Presence** | Offender need not be present | For extortion-robbery: offender must be present | Offender need not be present (e.g., threatening letter) |
| **Type of Property** | Movable property only | Movable property only | Movable **or immovable** property |
| **Punishment (simple)** | Up to 3 years | Minimum 1 year, up to 10 years (RI) | Up to 3 years (simple extortion) |
| **Nature** | Cognizable, Non-bailable (simple) | Cognizable, Non-bailable | Non-cognizable (simple), Cognizable (aggravated) |

### Key Distinction: Robbery vs. Extortion

**When Extortion Becomes Robbery** (Section 390 Explanation):
1. Offender must be **in the presence** of the victim
2. Fear must be of **instant** death, hurt, or wrongful restraint
3. Victim delivers property **then and there** due to instant fear

**Example 1 (Extortion, not Robbery)**: A sends a letter to B threatening to kill B's son if B does not pay Rs. 10 lakh within a week. B pays. This is **extortion** (threat of future harm, offender not present).

**Example 2 (Robbery by Extortion)**: A points a gun at B and says, "Give me your wallet now or I will shoot you." B hands over the wallet. This is **robbery** (threat of instant death, offender present, immediate delivery).

**Example 3 (Extortion, not Robbery)**: A calls B on the phone and threatens to kill B if B does not transfer Rs. 5 lakh to A's account by tomorrow. B transfers the money. This is **extortion** (threat of future harm, not instant).

## Landmark Cases on Extortion

### 1. **Romesh Chandra Mehta v. State of West Bengal** (1969) - Fear of Injury
**AIR 1970 SC 940**

**Facts**: The accused threatened to initiate false criminal proceedings against the victim unless the victim paid money.

**Held**: The Supreme Court held that **fear of injury to reputation** (including fear of false accusation) is sufficient for extortion under Section 383. The injury need not be physical; reputational harm qualifies as "injury" within the meaning of Section 44.

**Principle**: Injury includes harm to reputation. Threatening to file false cases or defame a person to extract money is extortion.

### 2. **State of M.P. v. Dhani Ram** (2004) - Dishonest Intention
**2004 Cri LJ 4816**

**Facts**: The accused threatened the victim to pay money, but the prosecution could not prove that the accused had dishonest intention (wrongful gain).

**Held**: For extortion, **dishonest intention** (intention to cause wrongful gain or wrongful loss) is an essential ingredient. If the accused had a bona fide claim of right (e.g., recovering a legitimate debt), dishonest intention may be absent.

**Principle**: Extortion requires dishonest intention. If the accused genuinely believes he has a right to the money (e.g., debt recovery), and the means used are not illegal, it may not be extortion.

**Caution**: However, using threats to recover even a legitimate debt may still be illegal (e.g., criminal intimidation under Section 503).

### 3. **Bhuvan Mohan Singh v. State of U.P.** (1963) - Threat of Accusation
**AIR 1963 All 101**

**Facts**: The accused threatened to file a false rape complaint against the victim unless the victim paid money.

**Held**: Threatening to make a false accusation of a serious offence (rape, punishable with life imprisonment) to extract money constitutes extortion under **Section 388** (extortion by threat of accusation of offence punishable with death/life/10 years).

**Principle**: False accusations of serious crimes used as leverage for extortion fall under the aggravated form of extortion (Section 388), attracting up to 10 years imprisonment.

### 4. **Harman Singh Sidhu v. State of Punjab** (2007) - Inducement and Delivery
**2007 Cri LJ 2873**

**Facts**: The accused threatened the victim, but the victim did not deliver any property. The prosecution charged the accused with extortion (Section 384).

**Held**: For **completed extortion** (Section 384), there must be actual delivery of property induced by the threat. If no property is delivered, the offence is **attempt to commit extortion** (Section 385, 387, or 389), not completed extortion.

**Principle**: Extortion (Section 384) requires actual delivery. If threat is made but property not delivered, charge Section 385/387/389 (attempt).

### 5. **Ram Narain v. State of Delhi** (1953) - Delivery to Any Person
**AIR 1953 SC 277**

**Facts**: The accused threatened the victim and induced the victim to deliver money to a third person (the accused's associate).

**Held**: Extortion is complete even if the property is delivered to a person other than the accused, as long as the delivery is induced by the accused's threat. Section 383 explicitly states "deliver to **any person**."

**Principle**: The property need not be delivered to the accused personally. Delivery to any person at the accused's behest completes the offence.

### 6. **Abdul Sattar v. State of Mysore** (1956) - Fear of Future vs. Instant Harm
**AIR 1956 SC 168**

**Facts**: The accused threatened the victim with harm at a future time unless money was paid. The prosecution charged robbery.

**Held**: For **robbery**, the fear must be of **instant** death, hurt, or wrongful restraint. If the threat is of harm at a future time, it is **extortion**, not robbery.

**Principle**: The critical distinction between robbery and extortion is the **timing** of the threatened harm. Instant harm = robbery; future harm = extortion.

**Test**: Was the victim in fear of harm "right now" or "then and there"? If yes, robbery (if other conditions met). Was the fear of harm "later" or "if you don't pay within X days"? If yes, extortion.

### 7. **State of Bihar v. Rajendra Prasad** (2000) - Attempt to Extort
**2000 Cri LJ 3421**

**Facts**: The accused sent threatening letters demanding money, but the victim refused to pay and reported to police.

**Held**: The offence is **attempt to commit extortion** (Section 385) because the threat was made (putting in fear) but no property was delivered.

**Principle**: Sections 385, 387, and 389 criminalize the **attempt** to extort (making threats without successful delivery of property).

**Sentencing**: Attempt carries lower punishment (2-7 years depending on type) compared to completed extortion (3-10 years).

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Simple Extortion by Physical Threat
**Scenario**: Ravi owes Amit Rs. 50,000 (a legitimate debt). Amit sends Ravi a message: "Pay me the Rs. 50,000 within 7 days or I will break your legs." Ravi, in fear, pays the money.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Intentionally putting in fear: Amit threatens physical harm
2. ✅ Fear of injury: Threat of physical injury to Ravi
3. ✅ Dishonestly inducing delivery: Even though the debt is legitimate, using threats to recover it is dishonest (wrongful means)
4. ✅ Delivery of property: Ravi pays money
5. ✅ Causal link: Ravi paid due to fear of threat

**Offence**: Extortion under Section 384 IPC / Section 309 BNS
**Punishment**: Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine or both

**Note**: Even if Amit had a legitimate claim (debt recovery), using threats of violence is illegal and constitutes extortion. Legal remedies (civil suit) should be used.

### Example 2: Extortion by Threat to Reputation (Blackmail)
**Scenario**: Sneha has compromising photographs of Vikram. She threatens to publish them on social media unless Vikram pays her Rs. 10 lakh. Vikram, fearing reputational damage, pays.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Intentionally putting in fear: Sneha threatens to publish photos
2. ✅ Fear of injury to reputation: Vikram fears defamation and dishonor
3. ✅ Dishonestly inducing delivery: Sneha has no right to the money; wrongful gain
4. ✅ Delivery of property: Vikram pays Rs. 10 lakh
5. ✅ Causal link: Payment induced by fear of reputational harm

**Offence**: Extortion under Section 384 IPC / Section 309 BNS
**Punishment**: Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine or both

**Additional Charges**: Criminal intimidation (Section 503-506 IPC), possibly defamation or IT Act violations

### Example 3: Aggravated Extortion by Threat of Death
**Scenario**: A gang threatens a businessman: "Pay us Rs. 50 lakh protection money or we will kill you and your family." The businessman, in fear of his life, pays.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Intentionally putting in fear: Threat of death to victim and family
2. ✅ Fear of death (injury to person): Most serious form of physical harm
3. ✅ Dishonestly inducing delivery: Gang has no right to money
4. ✅ Delivery of property: Businessman pays Rs. 50 lakh

**Offence**: Extortion by Putting in Fear of Death under Section 386 IPC / Section 311 BNS (aggravated form)
**Punishment**: Up to 10 years imprisonment AND fine (cognizable, non-bailable)

**Additional Charges**: Criminal conspiracy (Section 120B), organized crime (MCOCA, if applicable)

### Example 4: Extortion by Threat of False Criminal Accusation
**Scenario**: Priya threatens Rohit: "Transfer your property to me or I will file a false rape case against you (punishable with life imprisonment)." Rohit, fearing the accusation and social stigma, transfers the property.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Intentionally putting in fear: Threat of serious criminal accusation
2. ✅ Fear of accusation of offence punishable with life: Rape is punishable with life imprisonment
3. ✅ Fear of injury to reputation: False accusation would destroy reputation
4. ✅ Dishonestly inducing delivery: Priya has no right to property
5. ✅ Delivery: Rohit transfers property

**Offence**: Extortion by Threat of Accusation under Section 388 IPC / Section 313 BNS (aggravated form)
**Punishment**: Up to 10 years imprisonment AND fine (cognizable, non-bailable)

**Additional Charges**: Criminal intimidation (Section 506), defamation (if accusation is published), falsely charging with intent to injure (Section 211)

### Example 5: Attempt to Commit Extortion (No Delivery)
**Scenario**: Ajay sends a threatening email to a company demanding Rs. 1 crore, threatening to leak confidential data. The company refuses and reports to police. No money is paid.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Intentionally putting in fear: Threat to leak confidential data
2. ✅ Fear of injury: Reputational and business injury
3. ❌ No delivery: Company refused to pay

**Offence**: Putting Person in Fear to Commit Extortion (Attempt) under Section 385 IPC / Section 310 BNS
**Punishment**: Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine or both

**Additional Charges**: IT Act violations (unauthorized access, data theft), criminal intimidation

### Example 6: Not Extortion (Lack of Dishonest Intention)
**Scenario**: Mohan genuinely believes that Suresh owes him Rs. 1 lakh for a business transaction. Mohan tells Suresh, "Pay me my Rs. 1 lakh or I will file a civil suit against you." Suresh pays.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Putting in fear: Threat of civil litigation
2. ✅ Delivery: Suresh pays
3. ❌ No dishonest intention: If Mohan has a bona fide belief that the money is owed to him, and he is merely asserting his legal right through lawful means (civil suit), there is no dishonest intention

**Offence**: Likely no offence (bona fide claim of right, lawful means)

**Caution**: However, if Mohan threatened criminal prosecution or violence (unlawful means) to recover even a legitimate debt, it could still be extortion or criminal intimidation.

**Principle**: Dishonest intention is essential. Bona fide assertion of legal rights through lawful means is not extortion.

### Example 7: Extortion of Immovable Property
**Scenario**: A threatens to burn down B's factory unless B transfers ownership of a plot of land to A. B, fearing destruction of his business, executes a sale deed transferring the land to A.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Intentionally putting in fear: Threat to destroy factory (property injury)
2. ✅ Dishonestly inducing delivery: A has no right to the land
3. ✅ Delivery of immovable property: B transfers land ownership
4. ✅ Note: Extortion applies to immovable property (unlike theft which applies only to movable property)

**Offence**: Extortion under Section 384 IPC / Section 309 BNS
**Punishment**: Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine or both

**Additional Charges**: Criminal intimidation (Section 506)

**Civil Remedy**: B can also file a civil suit to set aside the sale deed on grounds of coercion (Section 15, Indian Contract Act).

## Defenses to Extortion

### 1. **Absence of Dishonest Intention**
- If the accused had a bona fide claim of right to the property (e.g., recovering a legitimate debt), dishonest intention may be absent
- The means used must also be lawful; threatening violence or false accusations is still illegal even for debt recovery

**Example**: If A genuinely believes B owes him money and threatens to file a civil suit (lawful means), it may not be extortion.

### 2. **No Fear Induced**
- If the victim was not actually put in fear, or the so-called threat was not perceived as a threat, no extortion
- The fear must be subjectively felt by the victim and objectively reasonable

**Example**: If A jokingly says to B, "Give me Rs. 100 or I'll complain to your wife," and B laughs and refuses, no fear was induced.

### 3. **Voluntary Payment (No Causal Link)**
- If the victim paid voluntarily for reasons other than the threat, no extortion
- The delivery must be causally linked to the fear

**Example**: If A threatens B, but B pays because he genuinely owes the money (not due to the threat), no extortion.

### 4. **Lawful Threat**
- If the threat is of lawful action (e.g., "Pay your debt or I will sue you in court"), it may not constitute extortion if the claim is bona fide
- However, threatening criminal prosecution to recover civil debt is generally not lawful

**Example**: A tells B, "Pay the rent you owe or I will file an eviction suit." If the rent is genuinely due, this is a lawful threat, not extortion.

### 5. **No Delivery**
- If no property was delivered, the offence is attempt to extort (Sections 385/387/389), not completed extortion (Section 384)
- Defense may argue for lesser charge

### 6. **Entrapment**
- If the alleged extortion was induced by police or undercover agents (entrapment), it may be a defense, though Indian law has limited recognition of entrapment

## Procedural Aspects

### Cognizance and Complaint
- **Simple Extortion (Section 384)**: Non-cognizable, Bailable, Triable by Any Magistrate
  - Police cannot arrest without warrant
  - Complaint to magistrate or police with warrant required
- **Aggravated Extortion (Sections 386, 388)**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Magistrate of First Class
  - Police can arrest without warrant
  - Bail requires court approval

### Investigation
- For cognizable offences: FIR registration, arrest without warrant, investigation
- For non-cognizable offences: Complaint to magistrate, magistrate may direct investigation
- Evidence: threatening letters/emails/messages, witness testimony, evidence of delivery (bank transfers, property documents)

### Trial
- Burden of proof on prosecution to establish all ingredients: intentional fear, dishonest intention, delivery induced by fear
- Defense may challenge dishonest intention, claim of right, or causal link

### Compounding
- **Simple Extortion (Section 384)**: Compoundable with permission of court (Section 320 CrPC)
- **Aggravated Extortion (Sections 386, 388)**: Non-compoundable

## Compliance Checklist

### For Potential Victims (Preventive Measures)
- [ ] Do not yield to threats; report immediately to police
- [ ] Preserve evidence: threatening letters, emails, messages, call recordings (where lawful)
- [ ] Document all communications and demands
- [ ] Seek legal advice before making any payments or transfers
- [ ] File FIR or complaint promptly (for aggravated extortion, file FIR)
- [ ] Consider civil remedies: set aside contracts obtained by coercion (Section 15, Contract Act)

### For Accused/Suspect (Legal Safeguards)
- [ ] Claim defenses: bona fide claim of right, no dishonest intention, lawful threat
- [ ] Demonstrate that any payment was voluntary (not induced by threat)
- [ ] Show lawful means were used (e.g., civil suit, not threats)
- [ ] Seek bail (for simple extortion, bail is available as of right; for aggravated, apply to court)
- [ ] Engage competent defense counsel
- [ ] Explore compounding (for simple extortion only)

### For Investigating Officer
- [ ] Register FIR or complaint
- [ ] Preserve evidence: threatening communications, evidence of delivery
- [ ] Record statements of victim and witnesses
- [ ] Prove all ingredients: intentional fear, dishonest intention, delivery
- [ ] Differentiate from theft, robbery, or criminal intimidation
- [ ] File charge sheet with appropriate section (384, 386, or 388 based on facts)

### For Prosecutors
- [ ] Frame appropriate charge: Section 384, 386, or 388 based on type of threat
- [ ] Prove dishonest intention (critical element)
- [ ] Prove causal link between threat and delivery
- [ ] Establish fear was induced (victim's testimony)
- [ ] Anticipate defenses (claim of right, voluntary payment)

### For Defense Counsel
- [ ] Challenge dishonest intention: establish bona fide claim of right
- [ ] Challenge causal link: payment was voluntary, not due to threat
- [ ] Argue lawful means: threat of lawful action (civil suit) is not extortion
- [ ] Challenge delivery: if no property delivered, reduce to attempt charge
- [ ] Seek acquittal if prosecution fails to prove all ingredients beyond reasonable doubt
- [ ] If conviction likely, argue for leniency: first-time offender, return of property, compromise

## Cross-References

### Related Criminal Offences
- **Theft (IPC 378-382)**: Taking without consent (no threat element)
- **Robbery (IPC 390-402)**: Theft/extortion with instant violence or fear
- **Criminal Intimidation (IPC 503-506)**: Threat to cause injury (may or may not involve property)
- **Cheating (IPC 415-420)**: Obtaining property by deception (not threat)
- **Criminal Breach of Trust (IPC 405-409)**: Dishonest misappropriation of entrusted property
- **Abduction (IPC 362-369)**: May be used in conjunction with extortion (kidnapping for ransom)

### Constitutional Provisions
- **Article 21**: Right to Life and Personal Liberty (protection from threats and coercion)
- **Article 300A**: Right to Property (protection from unlawful deprivation)

### Contract Law
- **Section 15, Indian Contract Act, 1872**: Coercion - A contract induced by coercion is voidable at the option of the party whose consent was coerced
- Victim can seek to set aside any contract or transfer obtained by extortion

### Procedural Laws
- **CrPC / BNSS**:
  - Section 154: FIR registration (for cognizable offences)
  - Section 155: Non-cognizable offences (for simple extortion)
  - Section 320: Compounding (simple extortion is compoundable with court permission)
  - Section 437: Bail for bailable offences (simple extortion)

### Evidence Law
- **Indian Evidence Act / BSA**:
  - Section 8: Motive, preparation, and conduct (relevant for proving dishonest intention)
  - Electronic evidence: Emails, messages, call recordings (subject to IT Act compliance)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Consent Under Fear**: Extortion involves obtaining property with the victim's consent, but that consent is procured by fear of injury

2. **Broad Scope of Injury**: Injury includes physical harm, reputational harm (defamation), and property harm

3. **Future or Immediate Threat**: Unlike robbery (which requires instant fear), extortion can involve threats of future harm

4. **Offender Need Not Be Present**: Extortion can be committed remotely (letters, emails, phone calls), unlike robbery which requires presence

5. **Immovable Property**: Extortion applies to both movable and immovable property, unlike theft (movable only)

6. **Aggravated Forms**: Higher punishment (up to 10 years) for extortion involving death/grievous hurt threats or threats of serious criminal accusations

7. **Dishonest Intention Essential**: Bona fide claim of right (even if asserted forcefully) may negate dishonest intention, but using unlawful means (threats) is still illegal

8. **Attempt Criminalized**: Even if property is not delivered, putting in fear to commit extortion is punishable (Sections 385, 387, 389)

---

## Conclusion

Extortion is a serious property offence that violates both property rights and personal liberty (freedom from fear and coercion). The law distinguishes between simple extortion (threats of injury) and aggravated forms (threats of death, grievous hurt, or serious criminal accusations), with correspondingly higher punishments for the latter.

Understanding the distinction between extortion, robbery, theft, and criminal intimidation is critical for proper charge framing and legal remedies. While extortion involves obtaining property through threats, robbery involves instant violence, theft involves taking without consent, and criminal intimidation may involve threats without property transfer.

With the transition to BNS 2023, the substantive law on extortion remains unchanged, ensuring continuity in legal practice and precedent.
