## Overview

**Money laundering** is the process of making illegally obtained money (from crime) appear legal by disguising its true source. It involves converting "dirty money" (proceeds of crime) into "clean money" through a series of transactions designed to obscure the origin.

Money laundering facilitates and perpetuates serious crimes by allowing criminals to enjoy the fruits of their illegal activities without detection. It undermines financial system integrity, distorts economic decisions, and poses risks to financial institutions.

### Key Characteristics
1. **Predicate Offence Requirement**: Money laundering requires an underlying "scheduled offence" (crime that generated illegal proceeds)
2. **Three-Stage Process**: Placement → Layering → Integration
3. **Severe Punishment**: Imprisonment from 3 to 7 years (may extend to 10 years for certain offences)
4. **Asset Attachment and Confiscation**: Properties derived from proceeds of crime can be attached and confiscated
5. **Burden of Proof Reversed**: Once prosecution proves involvement in proceeds of crime, burden shifts to accused to prove property is legitimate
6. **Non-Bailable**: Money laundering is a cognizable, non-bailable offence

## Definition of Money Laundering (Section 3 PMLA)

### Section 3: Offence of Money Laundering

**Whoever directly or indirectly attempts to indulge or knowingly assists or knowingly is a party or is actually involved in any process or activity connected with the proceeds of crime including its concealment, possession, acquisition or use and projecting or claiming it as untainted property shall be guilty of offence of money-laundering.**

### Essential Ingredients

#### 1. **Proceeds of Crime**

**Definition (Section 2(1)(u) PMLA)**: "Proceeds of crime" means any property derived or obtained, directly or indirectly, by any person as a result of **criminal activity relating to a scheduled offence**, or the value of any such property.

**"Property"** includes:
- Movable or immovable property
- Assets of any kind (tangible or intangible)
- Deeds, documents of title
- Money, bank credits, deposits
- Securities, stocks, shares, bonds
- Rights, claims, or interests (whether legal or equitable)

**Key Principle**: There must be proceeds derived from a **scheduled offence** (predicate crime). No proceeds of crime = no money laundering.

#### 2. **Scheduled Offence** (Predicate Crime)

**Definition (Section 2(1)(y) PMLA)**: "Scheduled offence" means offences listed in the **Schedule to PMLA** (Parts A, B, C).

**Part A (Indian Penal Code Offences)**:
- All offences punishable with **imprisonment of 3 years or more** under IPC
- Examples: Murder, kidnapping, robbery, dacoity, cheating, criminal breach of trust, forgery, etc.

**Part B (Special Acts)**:
- Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act, 1985 (drug trafficking)
- Unlawful Activities (Prevention) Act, 1967 (terrorism)
- Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 (corruption)
- Explosives Act, 1884
- Wildlife Protection Act, 1972 (illegal wildlife trade)
- Arms Act, 1959 (illegal arms trade)
- Immoral Traffic (Prevention) Act, 1956 (human trafficking)
- Companies Act, 2013 (fraud)
- SEBI Act, 1992 (securities fraud)
- Customs Act, 1962 (smuggling)
- Income Tax Act, 1961 (tax evasion above threshold)
- Indian Stamp Act, 1899
- Information Technology Act, 2000 (cyber crimes)

**Part C (Cross-Border Offences)**:
- Offences under laws of other countries corresponding to scheduled offences in India

**Broad Coverage**: PMLA Schedule is very broad, covering virtually all serious economic crimes, organized crime, terrorism, corruption, and trafficking.

#### 3. **Process or Activity Connected with Proceeds of Crime**

The accused must be involved in any **process or activity** connected with proceeds of crime, including:

**A. Concealment**
- Hiding the true nature, source, location, disposition, or ownership of proceeds of crime

**Example**: A (drug trafficker) deposits drug money in bank accounts in fictitious names, concealing the source.

**B. Possession**
- Having control or custody of proceeds of crime

**Example**: B holds Rs. 10 crore cash derived from corruption in a locker, knowing it is proceeds of crime.

**C. Acquisition**
- Obtaining or coming to possess proceeds of crime

**Example**: C (a money changer) exchanges drug money for clean currency, acquiring proceeds of crime.

**D. Use**
- Utilizing proceeds of crime for any purpose

**Example**: D purchases property using money obtained from fraud.

**E. Projecting or Claiming as Untainted Property**
- Representing proceeds of crime as legitimate money

**Example**: E invests bribe money in a business, claiming it as legitimate business income.

#### 4. **Knowledge or Involvement**

The accused must:
- **Knowingly assist**, OR
- **Knowingly be a party**, OR
- **Actually be involved**

in the process/activity connected with proceeds of crime.

**Mens Rea**: Knowledge (actual or constructive) that the property is proceeds of crime is essential.

**"Knowingly"**: The person knows or has reason to believe that the property is proceeds of crime.

**Example**: Bank employee helps client transfer Rs. 50 crore to offshore accounts knowing the client is under investigation for corruption. Employee commits money laundering by knowingly assisting.

#### 5. **Directly or Indirectly, or Attempts**

Even **indirect involvement** or **attempt** to launder money is punishable.

**Example**: A asks B (a hawala operator) to transfer drug money abroad. B transfers money through multiple intermediaries. All persons in the chain (directly or indirectly involved) are liable for money laundering.

## Three Stages of Money Laundering

### Stage 1: **Placement**
- **Introducing** illegal money into the financial system
- Converting cash into other forms (bank deposits, money orders, traveler's cheques, etc.)

**Methods**:
- Structuring (smurfing): Breaking large amounts into smaller deposits to avoid reporting thresholds
- Cash-intensive businesses: Mixing illegal cash with legitimate business revenues (restaurants, casinos, retail shops)
- Currency smuggling: Moving cash across borders

**Example**: A drug dealer has Rs. 1 crore in cash. He deposits Rs. 10,000 per day in 100 different bank accounts over several months (structuring).

### Stage 2: **Layering**
- **Distancing** the money from its illegal source through complex layers of financial transactions
- Obscuring the audit trail to hide the source

**Methods**:
- Multiple wire transfers (domestic and international)
- Buying and selling assets (real estate, stocks, gold, art)
- Using shell companies (companies with no real business, used only to move money)
- Round-tripping: Moving money out of country and bringing it back as foreign investment
- Trade-based money laundering: Over-invoicing or under-invoicing in international trade

**Example**: After depositing drug money in banks (placement), the launderer wires funds to offshore accounts, then invests in real estate in a different country, then sells the property and brings money back as "property sale proceeds" (layering).

### Stage 3: **Integration**
- **Reintroducing** laundered money into the legitimate economy
- Money now appears to have a legal source and can be freely used

**Methods**:
- Investing in legitimate businesses
- Purchasing luxury assets (cars, yachts, art)
- Using laundered money for personal expenses
- Declaring it as income from legitimate sources

**Example**: The launderer uses the "property sale proceeds" to purchase a luxury apartment in Mumbai, start a restaurant business, and lead a lavish lifestyle. The money now appears legitimate.

## Punishment for Money Laundering (Section 4 PMLA)

### Imprisonment and Fine

**Whoever commits the offence of money-laundering shall be punishable with:**
- **Rigorous Imprisonment**: Minimum **3 years**, may extend to **7 years**, AND
- **Fine**: May extend to **Rs. 5 lakh**

**For offences under Paragraph 2 of Part A of the Schedule (Drug Trafficking - NDPS Act)**:
- **Rigorous Imprisonment**: Minimum **3 years**, may extend to **10 years**, AND
- **Fine**: May extend to **Rs. 5 lakh**

**Nature**: Cognizable, Non-bailable

### Additional Consequences

1. **Attachment of Property (Section 5)**:
   - Authorities can attach (freeze) property involved in or derived from money laundering during investigation
   - Attachment can last up to 365 days (may be extended by court)

2. **Confiscation of Property (Section 8)**:
   - Upon conviction, court orders confiscation (forfeiture to government) of property involved in money laundering
   - Even if conviction doesn't happen, property may be confiscated if proven to be proceeds of crime

3. **Freezing of Bank Accounts**:
   - Bank accounts and financial assets can be frozen

4. **Travel Ban**:
   - Passport can be impounded; accused cannot leave country during investigation/trial

5. **Publication of Identity**:
   - Names of convicted money launderers may be published by Enforcement Directorate (ED)

## Investigating Authority: Enforcement Directorate (ED)

### Powers of ED

**Enforcement Directorate** is the primary agency for investigating money laundering offences under PMLA.

**Section 17: Power to Call for Information**
- ED can summon any person to furnish information, documents, or records
- Failure to comply is punishable with imprisonment up to 2 years + fine

**Section 19: Power to Arrest**
- ED officers can arrest any person if they have **reason to believe** (not mere suspicion, but reasonable grounds) that the person has committed money laundering
- Arrest without warrant (cognizable offence)

**Section 5: Power to Attach Property**
- ED can attach property involved in money laundering
- Attachment order can be challenged in Adjudicating Authority (later in Tribunal)

**Section 50: Search and Seizure**
- ED can search premises and seize records, documents, cash, assets

**Special Powers**:
- ED officers have powers similar to police (under CrPC) for investigation
- Can record statements (which are admissible in court)
- Can arrest during investigation or trial

## Burden of Proof (Section 24 PMLA)

### Reverse Burden of Proof

**Section 24**: In any proceedings relating to proceeds of crime under PMLA, the **burden of proving** that alleged proceeds of crime are **untainted property** shall be on the **accused**.

**How It Works**:
1. **Prosecution proves**:
   - Scheduled offence occurred (predicate crime)
   - Accused was involved in process/activity with property derived from crime
   - Prima facie case that property is proceeds of crime

2. **Burden shifts to Accused**:
   - Accused must prove property is from legitimate sources (salary, lawful business, inheritance, gifts, etc.)
   - If accused cannot satisfactorily explain, conviction follows

**Rationale**: Money laundering involves complex financial transactions designed to hide sources. It's difficult for prosecution to trace every transaction. Shifting burden to accused (who has better knowledge of his own financial affairs) aids conviction.

**Constitutional Validity**: Upheld by Supreme Court in *Vijay Madanlal Choudhary v. Union of India* (2022) - Section 24 is constitutional; does not violate presumption of innocence as accused gets opportunity to rebut.

**Example**: ED proves that A (government clerk with salary of Rs. 30,000/month) has Rs. 5 crore in bank accounts and properties. Burden shifts to A to explain source. If A cannot provide satisfactory explanation (legitimate income, lottery, inheritance, etc.), conviction for money laundering.

## Special Features of PMLA

### 1. **Twin Conditions for Bail (Section 45)**

**Section 45**: No person accused of money laundering shall be released on bail **unless**:
1. **Public Prosecutor** has been given opportunity to oppose bail application, AND
2. Court is satisfied that there are **reasonable grounds for believing** that the accused is **not guilty** of such offence and he is **not likely to commit any offence while on bail**

**Effect**: Very difficult to get bail in money laundering cases (stricter than regular bail provisions).

**Constitutionality**: Upheld by Supreme Court in *Vijay Madanlal Choudhary* (2022) - Section 45 is constitutional for serious economic offences.

**Practical Impact**: Accused in high-profile money laundering cases (politicians, businessmen) often spend years in custody during trial.

### 2. **No Requirement of Conviction in Scheduled Offence**

**PMLA does NOT require conviction in the predicate (scheduled) offence** for money laundering prosecution.

**Meaning**: Even if the accused is not convicted (or even not prosecuted) for the underlying crime (e.g., corruption, fraud), he can still be prosecuted for laundering the proceeds.

**Rationale**: Money laundering is an independent offence. The focus is on the money trail, not the predicate crime.

**Example**: A is alleged to have obtained Rs. 100 crore through corruption. Corruption case is pending/delayed/dismissed. But ED can prosecute A for money laundering if A cannot explain the source of Rs. 100 crore.

**Supreme Court Clarification** (*Vijay Madanlal Choudhary*, 2022): Scheduled offence need not result in conviction, but must be shown to have been committed (evidence of criminal activity).

### 3. **Statements to ED Officers Admissible (Section 50)**

**Statements made to ED officers** during investigation are **admissible as evidence** in court (unlike statements to police under Section 161 CrPC, which are not admissible).

**Safeguard**: Person being questioned must be informed that he is bound to answer truthfully (but he cannot be compelled to incriminate himself - Article 20(3) protection applies).

**Practical Impact**: ED statements carry high evidentiary value; accused's own statements can be used against him.

### 4. **Property Can Be Confiscated Even Without Conviction**

**Section 8(3)**: In certain circumstances, property can be confiscated even if the accused is not convicted, if the property is proven to be proceeds of crime.

**Example**: Accused dies during trial, or absconds (fugitive), or is acquitted on technical grounds but property is clearly proceeds of crime - property can still be confiscated.

## Obligations on Financial Institutions (Sections 12-13)

### Section 12: Reporting Obligations

**Banking companies, financial institutions, and intermediaries** must:
- **Furnish information** to authorities about transactions (especially suspicious transactions)
- **Maintain records** of all transactions
- **Report cash transactions** above threshold (Rs. 10 lakh)
- **Report suspicious transactions** (transactions that appear unusual or lack economic rationale)

**Financial Intelligence Unit - India (FIU-IND)**: Nodal agency to receive, process, analyze, and disseminate information on suspicious financial transactions.

### Section 13: Know Your Customer (KYC) Norms

**Financial institutions must**:
- **Verify identity** of customers (KYC - Know Your Customer)
- **Maintain records** of identity, addresses, transactions for at least 5 years
- **Conduct due diligence** for high-risk customers (Politically Exposed Persons - PEPs, high-value clients)

**Failure to comply**: Penalty up to Rs. 10 lakh per violation (Section 13(2))

## Landmark Cases on Money Laundering

### 1. **Vijay Madanlal Choudhary v. Union of India** (2022) - Constitutional Validity of PMLA
**AIR 2022 SC 4482**

**Issues**:
- Constitutional validity of PMLA provisions (arrest powers, bail conditions, burden of proof, attachment/confiscation)
- Whether PMLA violates rights of accused

**Held** (3-Judge Bench):
1. **PMLA is constitutional**: Parliament has legislative competence to enact PMLA
2. **Arrest powers (Section 19) valid**: ED can arrest based on "reason to believe" (objective standard)
3. **Bail conditions (Section 45) valid**: Twin conditions for bail are constitutionally permissible for serious economic offences
4. **Burden of proof (Section 24) valid**: Reverse burden does not violate presumption of innocence; accused has opportunity to rebut
5. **No conviction in scheduled offence required**: Money laundering is independent offence; conviction in predicate crime not mandatory
6. **Statements to ED admissible**: Subject to safeguards (Article 20(3) protection)

**Significance**: Landmark judgment settling constitutional challenges to PMLA; strengthened ED's powers.

### 2. **P. Chidambaram Case** (INX Media Money Laundering) - 2019-ongoing
**Amount**: Rs. 305 crore (alleged)

**Facts**:
- P. Chidambaram (former Finance Minister) and son Karti Chidambaram accused of receiving bribes in INX Media case
- Alleged FIPB (Foreign Investment Promotion Board) approval was granted illegally in exchange for bribes
- Proceeds allegedly laundered through shell companies and foreign accounts

**Charges**:
- Prevention of Corruption Act (bribery)
- PMLA (money laundering)

**Outcome**:
- P. Chidambaram arrested by ED (2019); spent months in custody
- Bail granted by Supreme Court after stringent conditions
- Trial ongoing

**Significance**: High-profile case demonstrating ED's powers to arrest and prosecute even senior politicians.

### 3. **Dawood Ibrahim and D-Company** - Organized Crime Money Laundering
**Context**: Dawood Ibrahim's criminal empire (drug trafficking, extortion, terrorism financing)

**ED Actions**:
- Properties worth thousands of crores attached/confiscated across India and abroad
- Associates prosecuted for money laundering
- India designated Dawood as "global terrorist" under UAPA

**Methods Used by D-Company**:
- Hawala networks for moving money
- Real estate investments (benami properties)
- Trade-based money laundering (hawala through import-export)
- Shell companies in tax havens

**Significance**: Demonstrates transnational nature of money laundering and challenges in enforcement.

### 4. **2G Spectrum Scam Money Laundering** (2011-2017)
**Amount**: Rs. 1.76 lakh crore (alleged loss to exchequer)

**Facts**:
- Spectrum licenses allocated at undervalued prices; alleged bribes and kickbacks
- ED investigated money laundering aspects (proceeds routed through companies, offshore accounts)

**Accused**: A. Raja (Telecom Minister), corporate executives

**Outcome**:
- ED attached properties worth hundreds of crores
- Trial concluded in 2017; accused acquitted in main case (lack of evidence of conspiracy)
- ED appeals pending

**Significance**: Highlighted challenges in proving money laundering when underlying scheduled offence conviction fails.

### 5. **Nirav Modi-PNB Fraud Money Laundering** (2018-ongoing)
**Amount**: Rs. 14,000 crore

**Facts**:
- Nirav Modi and associates defrauded PNB through fake Letters of Undertaking
- Proceeds laundered through shell companies, offshore accounts, purchase of assets abroad

**ED Actions**:
- Properties worth Rs. 5,000+ crore attached in India and abroad (Hong Kong, Belgium, US)
- Nirav Modi arrested in London (2019); extradition proceedings
- Declared fugitive under Fugitive Economic Offenders Act, 2018
- Assets confiscated

**Significance**: India's largest banking fraud and money laundering case; demonstrated cross-border enforcement challenges.

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Drug Money Laundering (Placement → Layering → Integration)
**Scenario**: A is a drug trafficker with Rs. 50 crore cash from drug sales (scheduled offence: NDPS Act). He launders money as follows:

**Stage 1 - Placement**:
- A deposits cash in small amounts (Rs. 50,000-1 lakh) in 500 different bank accounts opened in fake names (structuring/smurfing)

**Stage 2 - Layering**:
- A wires funds from these accounts to offshore shell companies in tax havens (Cayman Islands, Panama)
- Shell companies invest in stocks, bonds, real estate in multiple countries
- Properties sold; proceeds routed back to India as "foreign investment"

**Stage 3 - Integration**:
- A uses "foreign investment" to purchase luxury apartments, start a restaurant chain, buy cars
- Money now appears legitimate (claimed as "business income" and "returns on foreign investments")

**PMLA Charges**:
- Scheduled offence: Drug trafficking (NDPS Act)
- Money laundering: Concealment (fake accounts), possession (cash), use (purchasing properties), projecting as untainted (claiming as foreign investment)

**Punishment**: 3-10 years imprisonment (NDPS-related) + fine + confiscation of all properties (Rs. 50 crore)

### Example 2: Corruption Proceeds Laundering
**Scenario**: B is a government officer who received Rs. 10 crore in bribes (scheduled offence: Prevention of Corruption Act). He launders as follows:

- B invests bribe money in benami properties (properties in names of family members, associates)
- Purchases gold, jewelry, and stores in bank lockers
- Invests in shell companies and claims income as "business profits"

**ED Investigation**:
- ED finds B's assets are disproportionate to known income
- ED attaches properties, gold, bank accounts
- Burden shifts to B to explain source of Rs. 10 crore

**Outcome**: B cannot explain. Convicted for money laundering (3-7 years) + confiscation of Rs. 10 crore assets.

### Example 3: Trade-Based Money Laundering
**Scenario**: C wants to launder Rs. 100 crore obtained from fraud. He uses trade-based method:

- C exports goods worth Rs. 50 crore to a shell company in Dubai, but **over-invoices** as Rs. 100 crore
- Dubai shell company "pays" Rs. 100 crore to C's Indian company
- C now has Rs. 100 crore as "legitimate export proceeds"

**Alternative (Under-Invoicing for Import)**:
- C imports goods worth Rs. 100 crore but **under-invoices** as Rs. 50 crore
- Pays only Rs. 50 crore officially (lower customs duty)
- Remaining Rs. 50 crore paid through hawala (unrecorded)
- Goods sold in India at actual value (Rs. 100 crore profit)

**PMLA Charges**: Projecting proceeds of crime as untainted property (export/import proceeds)

## Compliance and Prevention

### For Individuals
- [ ] Maintain records of sources of wealth (salary slips, business records, inheritance documents, gift deeds)
- [ ] File income tax returns regularly
- [ ] Avoid cash transactions above reporting thresholds (Rs. 2 lakh for jewelry, Rs. 10 lakh general)
- [ ] Do not participate in structuring (breaking large amounts into small deposits to avoid reporting)
- [ ] Verify legitimacy of business partners and sources of funds

### For Financial Institutions
- [ ] Implement KYC (Know Your Customer) norms rigorously
- [ ] Report suspicious transactions to FIU-IND
- [ ] Report cash transactions above Rs. 10 lakh
- [ ] Conduct enhanced due diligence for high-risk customers (PEPs, high-value clients)
- [ ] Train employees on money laundering indicators (red flags)
- [ ] Maintain transaction records for at least 5 years

### For Businesses
- [ ] Verify identity and sources of funds of investors, partners
- [ ] Avoid shell companies or entities in tax havens unless legitimate business purpose
- [ ] Maintain proper accounting and audit trail
- [ ] Comply with tax and regulatory filings
- [ ] Report suspicious activities to authorities

## Key Takeaways

1. **Predicate Offence Required**: Money laundering requires proceeds from a "scheduled offence" (serious crime listed in PMLA Schedule).

2. **Broad Definition**: Any process/activity involving proceeds of crime - concealment, possession, acquisition, use, or projecting as untainted - is money laundering.

3. **Reverse Burden of Proof**: Once ED proves property is proceeds of crime, burden shifts to accused to prove it's legitimate.

4. **Stringent Bail Conditions**: Bail very difficult under Section 45 (twin conditions); accused must prove innocence and unlikelihood of committing offence on bail.

5. **No Conviction in Scheduled Offence Required**: Money laundering prosecution can proceed even if underlying crime conviction hasn't happened.

6. **Severe Consequences**: 3-10 years imprisonment + confiscation of all proceeds + travel ban + freezing of accounts.

7. **Enforcement Directorate (ED)**: Powerful investigation agency with arrest, search, seizure, and attachment powers.

8. **International Cooperation**: PMLA enables cross-border enforcement (mutual legal assistance treaties, extradition).

---

## Conclusion

Money laundering is a serious economic offence that enables criminals to enjoy proceeds of crime and perpetuates organized crime, corruption, terrorism, and drug trafficking. India's Prevention of Money Laundering Act, 2002 provides a robust framework with stringent provisions: reverse burden of proof, difficult bail, property confiscation, and powerful investigative agency (ED).

Recent Supreme Court validation of PMLA's constitutional validity (*Vijay Madanlal Choudhary*, 2022) has strengthened enforcement. High-profile prosecutions (Chidambaram, Nirav Modi, Vijay Mallya) demonstrate ED's reach.

However, challenges persist: lengthy trials, complexity of financial transactions, cross-border enforcement, and balancing effective prosecution with rights of accused. As financial systems become more sophisticated and globalized, combating money laundering requires international cooperation, technological tools (AI for transaction monitoring), and a skilled enforcement apparatus.
