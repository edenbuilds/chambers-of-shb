# India Criminal Law: Theft (Chori)

## Protocol ID
**IL-CRIM-THEFT**

## Domain
Criminal Law > Crimes Against Property

India (All States and Union Territories)

## Overview

**Theft (Chori)** is the dishonest taking of movable property out of the possession of another person without that person's consent, with the intention of taking it permanently or for keeping it for a gain.

Theft is the foundational property offence in Indian criminal law. It is distinguished from other property offences by the element of **dishonest intention** and the act of **taking movable property** without consent.

### Key Characteristics
1. **Movable Property**: Only movable property can be subject of theft (not immovable property like land)
2. **Dishonest Intention**: The act must be done dishonestly (IPC Section 24: wrongful gain or wrongful loss)
3. **Without Consent**: Taking must be without the consent of the person in possession
4. **Out of Possession**: Property must be moved out of another's possession
5. **Permanently or for Gain**: Intention to keep permanently or for unlawful gain

## Definition of Theft (IPC Section 378 / BNS Section 303)

### Full Legal Definition

**IPC Section 378**: Whoever, intending to take dishonestly any movable property out of the possession of any person without that person's consent, moves that property in order to such taking, is said to commit **theft**.

### Seven Essential Ingredients of Theft

#### 1. **Dishonest Intention**
- The taking must be "dishonest" as defined in IPC Section 24
- **IPC Section 24**: A person is said to do a thing dishonestly if he does that thing with the intention of causing wrongful gain to one person or wrongful loss to another person
- **Wrongful Gain**: Gain by unlawful means of property to which the person gaining is not legally entitled
- **Wrongful Loss**: Loss by unlawful means of property to which the person losing is legally entitled

**Example**: A picks up B's watch from a table intending to keep it. A has dishonest intention. But if A picks up B's watch thinking it is his own, there is no dishonest intention, hence no theft.

#### 2. **Movable Property**
- Only movable property can be the subject of theft
- **IPC Section 22**: "Movable property" means corporeal property of every description, except land and things attached to the earth or permanently fastened to anything which is attached to the earth
- Includes: money, goods, animals, documents, electricity (by legal fiction)

**Examples of Movable Property**:
- Cash, jewelry, vehicles, livestock, crops after severance from land
- Electricity (Supreme Court held electricity is "movable property" for theft purposes)
- Computer data and software (debatable, but increasingly recognized)

**Not Movable Property**:
- Land, buildings, standing trees, fixtures permanently attached to buildings

#### 3. **Property of Another Person**
- The property must belong to someone other than the accused
- A person cannot commit theft of his own property
- Exception: A person can be guilty of theft of his own property if it is in lawful possession of another (e.g., pledged property)

**Example**: A pawns his watch to B. Later A takes the watch back without B's consent and without repaying the loan. A commits theft of his own watch because it was in lawful possession of B.

#### 4. **Without Consent**
- The taking must be without the consent of the person in possession
- **Consent Obtained by Fraud**: If consent is obtained by putting a person in fear of death or hurt, it is not valid consent
- **Consent of Authorized Person**: If property is in possession of a servant/agent, the consent of the master/principal is required

**Example**: A tells B (a shopkeeper) that he wants to examine a ring. B hands over the ring. A runs away with it. This is theft because B's consent was only for examination, not for taking possession.

#### 5. **Out of Possession**
- The property must be taken "out of the possession" of another
- **Possession** includes actual physical possession or constructive possession (legal control)
- The person from whom property is taken need not be the owner; possession is sufficient

**Example**: A borrows B's car for one day but keeps it for a week without permission. This is not theft at the time of borrowing (consent existed), but may constitute criminal breach of trust for wrongful retention.

#### 6. **Moving the Property**
- There must be some actual movement or severance of the property
- Even slight movement is sufficient if it is in the direction of taking
- The taking need not be completed; attempt to move is enough if intention exists

**Example**: A puts his hand in B's pocket and touches B's wallet but is caught before removing it. This may not be theft (no movement), but preparation for theft. However, if A lifts the wallet even slightly, it is theft.

#### 7. **Intention to Take Permanently or for Unlawful Gain**
- The accused must intend to:
  - Take the property permanently (deprive owner forever), OR
  - Take it temporarily for unlawful gain or wrongful loss to another

**Example 1 (Permanent Taking)**: A takes B's bicycle and sells it. Theft is complete.

**Example 2 (Temporary Taking for Gain)**: A takes B's car for a joyride without permission and returns it after 2 hours. This is NOT theft under IPC (no intention to keep permanently or for unlawful gain), but may be other offences.

**Exception**: If temporary taking causes wrongful loss to owner or wrongful gain to taker, it may constitute theft.

## Explanations to Section 378 (Important Clarifications)

### Explanation 1: Severance and Theft
A thing so long as it is attached to the earth, not being movable property, is not the subject of theft; but it becomes capable of being the subject of theft as soon as it is severed from the earth.

**Example**: A cuts down a tree in B's land with intent to steal the timber. The moment A severs the tree from the soil, theft is committed.

### Explanation 2: Causing a Thing to Move
A person who causes a thing to move is said to move it within the meaning of theft, even if the thing is made to move by the agency of another person or animal.

**Example**: A induces B's bullocks to move, thereby causing the cart attached to them to move out of B's possession. A commits theft.

### Explanation 3: Dishonest Removal of Obstacle
The consent mentioned in the definition may be express or implied, and may be given either by the person in possession, or by any person having for that purpose authority either express or implied.

**Example**: A finds a ring on the highway, not knowing to whom it belongs. If A keeps it without making reasonable efforts to find the owner, this may be theft (dishonest retention). But if A picks it up intending to find the owner, no theft.

### Explanation 4: Lawful Possession and Theft
A person cannot commit theft of property in his own possession unless the property is in the lawful possession of another person, and the taking is dishonest.

**Example**: A deposits goods with B as a pledge. If A takes them back dishonestly without B's consent and without redeeming the pledge, A commits theft, even though the goods belong to A.

### Explanation 5: Wild Animals
When wild animals are tamed or domesticated, they can be the subject of theft while they remain in a domesticated state or within the owner's control.

**Example**: A takes B's trained parrot from B's cage. Theft is committed. But if A catches a wild parrot in the forest, no theft (no owner).

## Punishment for Theft

### Section 379 IPC / Section 304 BNS: Simple Theft
**Punishment**:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to 3 years, OR
- **Fine**, OR
- **Both**

**Nature**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Any Magistrate

### Section 380 IPC / Section 305 BNS: Theft in Dwelling House, Vessel, or Vehicle
Whoever commits theft in any building, tent, or vessel used as a human dwelling, or used for the custody of property, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to 7 years, AND
- **Fine**

**Aggravating Circumstances**:
- Theft committed in a dwelling house (greater violation of trust and safety)
- Theft from a vessel (ship, boat)
- Theft from a vehicle used for custody of property

**Example**: A enters B's house through an open window at night and steals a laptop. This is theft in a dwelling house (Section 380), punishable with up to 7 years.

### Section 381 IPC / Section 306 BNS: Theft by Clerk or Servant
Whoever, being a clerk or servant, or employed in the capacity of a clerk or servant, commits theft in respect of any property in the possession of his master or employer, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to 7 years, AND
- **Fine**

**Rationale**: Breach of trust and confidence reposed in the employee.

**Example**: A, a cashier in a bank, takes Rs. 10,000 from the cash counter for his personal use. This is theft by a servant (Section 381), even if A later returns the money.

**Distinction from Criminal Breach of Trust (Section 405)**:
- **Theft by Servant**: Taking property without being entrusted with dominion over it (mere possession for employer's benefit)
- **Criminal Breach of Trust**: Dishonestly misappropriating or converting property already entrusted with dominion over it

### Section 382 IPC / Section 307 BNS: Theft After Preparation for Causing Death, Hurt, or Restraint
Whoever commits theft, having made preparation for causing death, or hurt, or restraint, or fear of death, or hurt, or restraint, to any person, shall be punished with:
- **Rigorous Imprisonment**: Up to 10 years, AND
- **Fine**

**Aggravating Circumstances**:
- Preparation for causing death, hurt, or wrongful restraint in order to commit theft or to facilitate escape or to retain stolen property

**Example**: A and his associates, armed with weapons, enter B's house with the intention to commit theft and to cause hurt to anyone who resists. Even if no actual violence occurs, the preparation with weapons makes it theft under Section 382.

**Distinction from Robbery**:
- **Section 382 Theft**: Preparation for violence (may not actually use violence)
- **Robbery**: Actual use of violence or putting in fear of instant death/hurt during theft

## Distinction Between Theft and Related Offences

### 1. Theft vs. Robbery (IPC 390)

| **Element** | **Theft (Section 378)** | **Robbery (Section 390)** |
|-------------|-------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Violence** | No violence or putting in fear | Theft is or attempted to be committed with violence or fear of instant death/hurt |
| **Timing of Force** | No force used | Force used during commission of theft or to retain stolen property |
| **Punishment** | Up to 3 years (simple theft) | Minimum 1 year, up to 10 years (rigorous imprisonment) |
| **Nature** | Non-bailable (simple theft) | Non-bailable, more serious |

**Example**: A snatches B's chain from her neck while walking on the street. If A uses force or causes fear of instant harm, it is robbery. If A picks the chain from B's bag without B noticing, it is theft.

### 2. Theft vs. Extortion (IPC 383)

| **Element** | **Theft (Section 378)** | **Extortion (Section 383)** |
|-------------|-------------------------|------------------------------|
| **Mode of Taking** | Taking property dishonestly by moving it | Intentionally putting person in fear to deliver property |
| **Consent** | Without consent | With consent, but consent obtained by fear |
| **Fear Element** | No fear induced | Fear of injury to person or property, or fear of accusation |
| **Delivery** | Accused takes property | Victim delivers property |

**Example**: A threatens to publish defamatory content about B unless B pays Rs. 1 lakh. B pays. This is extortion (consent obtained by fear), not theft.

### 3. Theft vs. Criminal Breach of Trust (IPC 405)

| **Element** | **Theft (Section 378)** | **Criminal Breach of Trust (Section 405)** |
|-------------|-------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| **Entrustment** | No entrustment; property taken without consent | Property already entrusted to accused in fiduciary capacity |
| **Possession** | Accused takes possession dishonestly | Accused already has lawful possession/dominion |
| **Act** | Taking movable property | Misappropriation or conversion or disposal in violation of trust |
| **Intention** | Dishonest intention at time of taking | Dishonest intention at time of misappropriation (may arise later) |

**Example**: A borrows B's book and later sells it. This is criminal breach of trust (book was entrusted to A), not theft (A had lawful possession).

### 4. Theft vs. Cheating (IPC 415)

| **Element** | **Theft (Section 378)** | **Cheating (Section 415)** |
|-------------|-------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Deception** | No deception; property taken | Deception induces victim to deliver property or consent |
| **Consent** | Without consent | With consent, but consent obtained by deception |
| **Delivery** | Accused takes property | Victim delivers property voluntarily (though deceived) |

**Example**: A impersonates a bank official and convinces B to transfer money to A's account. This is cheating (consent obtained by deception), not theft.

## Landmark Cases on Theft

### 1. **Pyare Lal Bhargava v. State of Rajasthan** (1963) - Dishonest Intention
**AIR 1963 SC 1094**

**Facts**: The accused took grain from a shop intending to pay for it later but did not have permission from the shopkeeper.

**Held**: The Supreme Court held that **dishonest intention is the essence of theft**. If there is no dishonest intention at the time of taking, there is no theft. If the accused genuinely believed he had the right to take the property or intended to pay, dishonest intention may be absent.

**Principle**: Dishonest intention must exist at the time of taking the property. Subsequent dishonest act (e.g., not paying later) does not convert innocent taking into theft.

### 2. **State of Maharashtra v. Mohammed Yakub** (1980) - Electricity as Movable Property
**AIR 1980 SC 1111**

**Facts**: The accused was charged with theft of electricity by installing unauthorized connections and bypassing the meter.

**Held**: The Supreme Court held that **electricity is "movable property"** within the meaning of Section 378 IPC, and unauthorized abstraction of electricity constitutes theft.

**Principle**: The definition of "movable property" in Section 22 IPC includes electricity. Dishonestly using or abstracting electricity without payment constitutes theft.

**Legislative Development**: The Electricity Act, 2003 now provides specific provisions for theft of electricity (Section 135), but the IPC provision remains applicable.

### 3. **K.N. Mehra v. State of Rajasthan** (1957) - Temporary Taking
**AIR 1957 SC 369**

**Facts**: The accused took a vehicle for temporary use without the owner's consent and returned it after a short period.

**Held**: For theft, there must be an intention to take the property **permanently** or to cause **wrongful loss** to the owner. Mere temporary deprivation without any intention to cause wrongful loss or wrongful gain does not constitute theft.

**Principle**: Temporary taking without dishonest intention or wrongful gain does not amount to theft. However, if temporary taking causes wrongful loss or gain, it may constitute theft.

### 4. **Ramjas Foundation v. Union of India** (1993) - Removal from Possession
**AIR 1993 SC 852**

**Facts**: The case involved the interpretation of "taking out of possession" in the context of trust property.

**Held**: The Supreme Court clarified that **possession** in Section 378 includes not only physical possession but also **constructive possession** (legal control over property). Taking property out of legal control without consent constitutes theft.

**Principle**: The concept of "possession" for theft is broader than physical custody. It includes legal possession, custody, and control.

### 5. **Shri Narayan v. State** (1962) - Consent Obtained by Deception
**AIR 1962 All 88**

**Facts**: The accused borrowed money from the complainant with no intention of returning it, having given false information about his financial status.

**Held**: If property is obtained with consent but the consent is induced by deception, the offence is **cheating** (Section 415), not theft. Theft requires taking **without consent**, whereas cheating involves obtaining property **with consent procured by deception**.

**Principle**: Distinction between theft (no consent) and cheating (consent obtained by deception) is critical for proper charge framing.

### 6. **State of Haryana v. Rajinder Singh** (2004) - Dishonest Retention
**2004 Cri LJ 4265**

**Facts**: The accused found a lost article and kept it without making efforts to find the owner.

**Held**: Finding lost property and keeping it with dishonest intention (no effort to find owner) can constitute theft if the finder has reasonable means of discovering the owner and intentionally omits to do so.

**Principle**: Dishonest retention of found property with knowledge or means of discovering the owner can be theft. Finder has a duty to make reasonable efforts to return lost property.

### 7. **Shiv Kumar v. State** (1999) - Servant's Temporary Taking
**1999 Cri LJ 3890**

**Facts**: A servant took his employer's money temporarily, intending to return it, but used it for gambling.

**Held**: Even if a servant takes property temporarily, if there is **dishonest intention** at the time of taking (intention to use for personal benefit causing wrongful loss to employer), it constitutes theft by servant under Section 381.

**Principle**: Temporary taking by a servant with dishonest intention is theft if it causes wrongful loss to employer or wrongful gain to servant.

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Simple Theft from Public Place
**Scenario**: Ravi is sitting in a restaurant. He notices that the person sitting next to him, Amit, has left his mobile phone on the table and gone to the washroom. Ravi picks up the phone and puts it in his pocket, intending to sell it later.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Dishonest intention: Ravi intends to wrongfully gain by selling Amit's phone
2. ✅ Movable property: Mobile phone is movable property
3. ✅ Property of another: Phone belongs to Amit
4. ✅ Without consent: Amit did not consent to Ravi taking the phone
5. ✅ Out of possession: Ravi moved the phone from Amit's possession (constructive) to his own
6. ✅ Moving the property: Ravi picked up and pocketed the phone
7. ✅ Intention to take permanently: Ravi intends to sell the phone (permanent deprivation)

**Offence**: Theft under Section 379 IPC / Section 304 BNS (simple theft)
**Punishment**: Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine or both

### Example 2: Theft in Dwelling House
**Scenario**: Priya works as a maid in Mr. Sharma's house. One day, while Mr. Sharma is at work, Priya enters his bedroom and takes a gold necklace from the cupboard, intending to keep it.

**Analysis**:
- All seven ingredients of theft are satisfied (dishonest intention, movable property, without consent, etc.)
- **Aggravating factor**: Theft committed in a dwelling house
- **Additional consideration**: Priya is a servant, but if she was not entrusted with access to the bedroom/cupboard (she took it by entering without permission), it may be theft in dwelling house rather than theft by servant

**Offence**: Theft in Dwelling House under Section 380 IPC / Section 305 BNS
**Punishment**: Up to 7 years imprisonment AND fine

**Alternative**: If Priya had lawful access to the bedroom for cleaning purposes (entrusted access), it could also be theft by servant (Section 381).

### Example 3: Theft by Servant
**Scenario**: Rajesh is a cashier at a retail store. One evening, he takes Rs. 5,000 from the cash register and puts it in his pocket, intending to return it the next day after using it for an urgent personal expense. However, he does not inform his employer or take permission.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ All ingredients of theft are present
2. ✅ Rajesh is a servant (employee) of the store
3. ✅ Property (money) is in the possession of his employer
4. ✅ Even if he intends to return it, the taking is dishonest (wrongful temporary gain, wrongful loss to employer who loses use of money)

**Offence**: Theft by Clerk or Servant under Section 381 IPC / Section 306 BNS
**Punishment**: Up to 7 years imprisonment AND fine

**Note**: If Rajesh had been entrusted with dominion over the money (e.g., authorized to use it for business purposes), his dishonest use would be criminal breach of trust (Section 405) rather than theft.

### Example 4: No Theft (Lack of Dishonest Intention)
**Scenario**: Sanjay sees an umbrella at a restaurant which looks identical to his own. He picks it up thinking it is his and takes it home. Later he realizes it belongs to someone else and returns it immediately.

**Analysis**:
1. ❌ No dishonest intention: Sanjay genuinely believed the umbrella was his own at the time of taking
2. ✅ All other ingredients may be satisfied, but without dishonest intention, no theft

**Offence**: No offence committed (honest mistake)

**Principle**: Dishonest intention at the time of taking is essential for theft. Bona fide mistake negates dishonesty.

### Example 5: Theft After Preparation for Causing Death/Hurt
**Scenario**: A gang of four persons, armed with knives and rods, breaks into a jewelry shop at night. They tie up the security guard and threaten him with death if he raises an alarm. They then take jewelry worth Rs. 10 lakh and flee.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ All ingredients of theft are present
2. ✅ **Aggravating factor**: The gang made preparation for causing death or hurt (armed with weapons, threatened security guard)
3. ✅ The preparation and threat were to facilitate the commission of theft and escape

**Offence**: Theft After Preparation for Causing Death, Hurt, or Restraint under Section 382 IPC / Section 307 BNS

**Punishment**: Up to 10 years rigorous imprisonment AND fine

**Note**: If actual violence was used or the guard was put in fear of **instant** death/hurt during the taking, it would be **robbery** (Section 390), which has higher punishment.

### Example 6: Electricity Theft
**Scenario**: Mohan operates a small factory. To reduce his electricity bills, he installs a device to bypass the electric meter so that his actual consumption is not recorded. He dishonestly abstracts electricity worth Rs. 50,000 over six months.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Dishonest intention: Mohan intends to wrongfully gain by not paying for electricity
2. ✅ Movable property: Electricity is treated as movable property (Supreme Court in Mohammed Yakub case)
3. ✅ Property of another: Electricity belongs to the electricity supply company
4. ✅ Without consent: The company did not consent to unmetered supply
5. ✅ Taking out of possession: Mohan abstracted/diverted electricity from the company's supply

**Offence**: Theft of Electricity under Section 379 IPC / Section 304 BNS
**Alternative Charge**: Theft of electricity under Section 135 of the Electricity Act, 2003 (more specific)

**Punishment**:
- Under IPC: Up to 3 years imprisonment or fine or both
- Under Electricity Act, 2003: Up to 3 years imprisonment AND fine (minimum Rs. 50,000, may extend to twice the value of electricity stolen)

## Defenses to Theft

### 1. **Absence of Dishonest Intention**
- If the accused can show that there was no dishonest intention at the time of taking, theft cannot be proved
- **Example**: Bona fide belief that the property belonged to the accused, or that the accused had a right to take it

**Case Law**: *Pyare Lal Bhargava v. State of Rajasthan* (1963) - Genuine belief of right to property is a defense

### 2. **Consent of the Owner**
- If the owner or person in possession consented to the taking, there is no theft
- The consent must be free, not obtained by fraud or fear (otherwise it may be cheating or extortion)

**Example**: If B lends his car to A, A's taking of the car is not theft (consent exists)

### 3. **Claim of Right (Bona Fide Claim)**
- If the accused had a bona fide belief that he had a legal right to the property, even if the belief is mistaken, it negates dishonest intention
- The claim must be honest, not a mere pretext

**Example**: A takes property from B believing in good faith that it was pledged to him, though the pledge was invalid. If the belief is honest, no theft.

### 4. **Property Belonged to Accused**
- A person cannot commit theft of his own property unless it is in the lawful possession of another
- If accused is the owner and the property is not in lawful possession of another, taking it is not theft

**Example**: A removes his own goods from his own warehouse. No theft, even if creditors have a claim on the goods (unless goods were under legal custody/attachment).

### 5. **No Movement of Property**
- If the property was not actually moved, theft is not complete
- However, even slight movement with dishonest intention may constitute theft

**Example**: A puts his hand in B's pocket but is caught before touching the wallet. No theft (no movement yet).

### 6. **Mistake of Fact**
- If the accused took the property under a genuine mistake of fact (e.g., believed it was his own property), dishonest intention is absent

**Example**: A takes an umbrella from a stand, genuinely believing it to be his own (identical appearance). Later realizes mistake and returns it. No theft.

## Procedural Aspects

### Cognizance and Complaint
- **Simple Theft (Section 379)**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Any Magistrate
- **Aggravated Theft (Sections 380-382)**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Magistrate of First Class

### Investigation
- Police can arrest without warrant (cognizable offence)
- FIR can be registered immediately upon complaint
- Investigation includes: recording statements, recovering stolen property, identifying accused

### Trial
- Charge sheet to be filed after investigation
- Trial before Magistrate (not requiring jury)
- Burden of proof on prosecution to prove all seven ingredients of theft beyond reasonable doubt

### Compounding
- **Simple Theft (Section 379)**: Compoundable with permission of court (Section 320 CrPC / Section 360 BNS)
- **Aggravated Theft (Sections 380-382)**: Non-compoundable

**Compounding**: The complainant and accused can reach a compromise and end the criminal case with court's permission (for simple theft only).

## Compliance Checklist

### For Potential Victims (Preventive Measures)
- [ ] Secure movable property in dwelling houses (locks, safes, alarms)
- [ ] Maintain inventory and records of valuable movable property
- [ ] Install CCTV cameras in vulnerable areas (shops, warehouses, parking)
- [ ] Conduct background verification of domestic servants and employees with access to property
- [ ] Implement access control systems for employees (limit access to cash, valuables)
- [ ] Report lost or stolen property to police immediately (timely FIR)
- [ ] Maintain insurance for valuable property against theft

### For Accused/Suspect (Legal Safeguards)
- [ ] Cooperate with investigation if innocent; provide evidence of lawful possession or consent
- [ ] Claim defenses: absence of dishonest intention, claim of right, mistake of fact, consent
- [ ] Seek bail (theft is bailable only for simple theft under Section 379; aggravated forms are non-bailable)
- [ ] Engage competent defense counsel
- [ ] Return stolen property if possible (may mitigate sentence, though not a complete defense)
- [ ] Explore compounding (compromise with victim) if offence is simple theft under Section 379

### For Investigating Officer
- [ ] Register FIR promptly upon complaint
- [ ] Identify and interview witnesses; record statements under Section 161 CrPC
- [ ] Recover stolen property and prepare recovery memo (seizure list)
- [ ] Establish chain of custody for recovered property
- [ ] Prove all seven ingredients of theft: dishonest intention, movable property, without consent, out of possession, movement, intention to take
- [ ] Collect evidence: CCTV footage, eyewitness testimony, confession (if voluntary), recovery of stolen goods
- [ ] Differentiate theft from related offences (robbery, extortion, breach of trust, cheating)
- [ ] File charge sheet within statutory time limits

### For Prosecutors
- [ ] Frame appropriate charge: Section 379, 380, 381, or 382 based on facts
- [ ] Prove dishonest intention (most critical element)
- [ ] Prove ownership or possession of property by complainant
- [ ] Prove lack of consent
- [ ] Prove movement of property by accused
- [ ] Anticipate and counter defenses (claim of right, consent, mistake)
- [ ] Examine witnesses thoroughly; corroborate evidence

### For Defense Counsel
- [ ] Challenge dishonest intention: establish bona fide claim of right or honest belief
- [ ] Challenge identification of accused (if based on circumstantial evidence)
- [ ] Argue consent of owner (if applicable)
- [ ] Argue lack of movement or possession (if applicable)
- [ ] Challenge ownership/possession of complainant
- [ ] Argue mistake of fact or innocent taking
- [ ] Seek acquittal if any of the seven ingredients is not proved beyond reasonable doubt
- [ ] If conviction likely, argue for leniency: first-time offender, return of property, family circumstances

  - Section 436: Bail (simple theft is bailable; aggravated theft is non-bailable)

- **Indian Evidence Act, 1872 / Bharatiya Sakshya Adhiniyam (BSA), 2023**
  - Section 27: Admissibility of information leading to discovery of stolen property
  - Section 114: Presumption of knowledge (if stolen property found in possession soon after theft)
  - Illustration (a) to Section 114: If a person is found in possession of stolen property soon after the theft, the court may presume he is the thief or received it knowing it to be stolen

### Special Legislations
- **Electricity Act, 2003**: Section 135 (specific provision for theft of electricity)
- **Information Technology Act, 2000**: Cyber theft, unauthorized access to computer systems

## Key Takeaways

1. **Seven Essential Ingredients**: Dishonest intention, movable property, property of another, without consent, out of possession, moving, intention to take permanently or for gain

2. **Dishonest Intention is Paramount**: Without dishonest intention at the time of taking, there is no theft, even if other elements are present

3. **Movable Property Only**: Land and immovable property cannot be subject of theft; only movable property (including electricity by legal fiction)

4. **Consent Negates Theft**: If property is taken with consent, it is not theft (but may be cheating if consent is obtained by deception, or extortion if obtained by fear)

5. **Distinction from Other Offences**:
   - **Theft**: Without consent, no violence, no deception
   - **Robbery**: Theft + violence or fear of instant harm
   - **Extortion**: Consent obtained by fear
   - **Cheating**: Consent obtained by deception
   - **Criminal Breach of Trust**: Property already entrusted, dishonest misappropriation

6. **Aggravated Forms Attract Higher Punishment**:
   - Simple theft: up to 3 years
   - Theft in dwelling house: up to 7 years
   - Theft by servant: up to 7 years
   - Theft after preparation for death/hurt: up to 10 years (rigorous imprisonment)

7. **Defenses**: Claim of right, absence of dishonest intention, consent, mistake of fact, ownership

8. **Compounding**: Simple theft (Section 379) is compoundable with court permission, allowing compromise between victim and accused

---

## Conclusion

Theft is the foundational property offence in Indian criminal law, distinguished by the element of **dishonest taking** of **movable property** without consent. Understanding the seven essential ingredients and the fine distinctions between theft and related offences (robbery, extortion, breach of trust, cheating) is critical for proper charge framing, investigation, and adjudication.

The law balances the need to protect property rights with the requirement of proving dishonest intention beyond reasonable doubt, ensuring that innocent mistakes or bona fide claims of right do not result in wrongful conviction.

With the transition to BNS 2023, the substantive law on theft remains largely unchanged, ensuring continuity and predictability in legal practice.
