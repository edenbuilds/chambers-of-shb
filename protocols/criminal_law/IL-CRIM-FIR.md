## Overview

**First Information Report (FIR)** is the written document prepared by police when they receive information about the commission of a **cognizable offence** (serious crime for which police can arrest without warrant).

The FIR is the **foundation of criminal justice system** - it sets in motion the machinery of investigation, arrest, and prosecution. It is the first step taken by law enforcement upon receiving information about a crime.

### Key Characteristics
1. **"First" Information**: Must be the first information received by police about the offence
2. **Cognizable Offences Only**: FIR is registered only for cognizable offences (non-cognizable offences have different procedure)
3. **Mandatory Registration**: Police must register FIR if information relates to cognizable offence (no discretion to refuse)
4. **Written Document**: Reduced to writing and signed by informant
5. **Copy to Informant**: Free copy given to informant immediately
6. **Sets Investigation in Motion**: Once FIR registered, investigation begins

## Definition and Legal Basis (Section 154 CrPC / Section 173 BNSS)

### Section 154 CrPC: Information in Cognizable Cases

**Every information relating to the commission of a cognizable offence, if given orally to an officer in charge of a police station, shall be reduced to writing by him or under his direction, and be read over to the informant; and every such information, whether given in writing or reduced to writing as aforesaid, shall be signed by the person giving it, and the substance thereof shall be entered in a book to be kept by such officer in such form as the State Government may prescribe in this behalf.**

### Essential Elements of FIR

#### 1. **Information**
- Any knowledge or intelligence about commission of a cognizable offence
- Can be given by: victim, witness, or any person with knowledge
- Can be oral or written
- Can be given in person, by phone, email, or through online portals

**Who Can Give Information?**
- **Victim** (most common)
- **Witness** (person who saw the crime)
- **Informant** (any person with knowledge, even if not victim/witness)
- **Anonymous** (though police may verify before registering)

**Example**: A is assaulted by B. A goes to police station and narrates the incident. This is "information."

#### 2. **Relating to Commission of Cognizable Offence**

**Cognizable Offence** (Section 2(c) CrPC / BNSS): Offence for which police can arrest **without warrant** and start investigation **without magistrate's permission**.

**Examples of Cognizable Offences**:
- Murder (IPC 302)
- Rape (IPC 376)
- Kidnapping (IPC 363)
- Robbery (IPC 392)
- Dacoity (IPC 395)
- Cheating (IPC 420)
- Forgery (IPC 467-469 for certain types)
- Mischief causing damage Rs. 50+ (IPC 427)

**Non-Cognizable Offences** (require different procedure under Section 155):
- Simple hurt (IPC 323)
- Defamation (IPC 500)
- Simple assault (IPC 352)
- Simple mischief (IPC 426)

**How to Check?**: First Schedule to CrPC/BNSS lists all offences and specifies whether cognizable or non-cognizable.

#### 3. **Officer in Charge of Police Station**

FIR must be given to:
- **Station House Officer (SHO)** / Officer In-Charge (OIC) of police station, OR
- Any officer **authorized by State Government** to receive FIR

**Territorial Jurisdiction**: FIR should be lodged at police station having jurisdiction over the place where the crime occurred.

**Exception**: If crime occurred in multiple jurisdictions, FIR can be lodged at any such police station. Also, in cases of urgency or if victim is unable to reach the jurisdictional police station, FIR can be lodged elsewhere (police will transfer to appropriate jurisdiction).

#### 4. **Reduced to Writing**

- If information is given **orally**, police officer must **reduce it to writing**
- If given in **writing**, accepted as is
- Information must be **read over to informant** to ensure accuracy
- Informant must **sign** the FIR

**Language**: FIR can be given in any language. If informant doesn't know English, FIR can be in vernacular language. Translation may be provided if needed.

#### 5. **Entry in Daily Diary / Station Diary**

- **Substance (gist) of information** must be entered in **Station Diary / General Diary (GD Entry)**
- Diary maintained as per prescribed format

#### 6. **Copy to Informant**

**Section 154(2)**: A **copy of the information as recorded** shall be given **forthwith, free of cost**, to the informant.

**Importance**: Copy serves as proof that FIR was registered. Informant can use it to ensure police action is taken.

## Mandatory Registration of FIR (Supreme Court Directions)

### Lalita Kumari v. Govt. of U.P. (2014) - Landmark Judgment
**AIR 2014 SC 187**

**Held**: **Registration of FIR is mandatory under Section 154 CrPC if the information discloses commission of a cognizable offence.** No preliminary inquiry is permissible in such cases.

**Key Principles**:

1. **Mandatory Registration**: If information relates to cognizable offence, police **must** register FIR immediately. No discretion.

2. **No Preliminary Inquiry**: Police cannot refuse to register FIR on grounds that they want to conduct "preliminary inquiry" or "verification."

3. **Exceptions** (where preliminary inquiry allowed before FIR):
   - **Matrimonial disputes / family disputes**: To verify if offence actually occurred
   - **Commercial / civil disputes**: Where criminal complaint is merely to pressurize other party
   - **Medical negligence**: Requires expert opinion
   - **Corruption by public servants**: Requires verification
   - **Cases under Section 498A (cruelty by husband/in-laws)**: Verification may be done to prevent misuse

4. **Preliminary Inquiry Time-Bound**: If preliminary inquiry is conducted (in exceptions), must be completed within **7 days**. If cognizable offence is made out, FIR must be registered.

5. **No Endorsement Technique**: Police cannot merely record information and "endorse" to another station or department without registering FIR.

6. **Accountability**: If FIR is not registered without lawful reason, the police officer can be prosecuted under Section 166A IPC (Public Servant disobeying law to cause injury - up to 1 year imprisonment).

**Impact**: Landmark judgment curbing police tendency to refuse FIR registration. Informants can cite *Lalita Kumari* if police refuse to register FIR.

## Procedure for Registering FIR (Step-by-Step)

### Step 1: Informant Approaches Police Station

- Victim / witness / informant goes to police station (or calls, emails, uses online portal)
- Narrates incident to officer in charge

### Step 2: Information Recorded in Writing

**If Oral**:
- Officer writes down information (in own words or informant's words)
- Reads it back to informant to verify accuracy

**If Written**:
- Informant provides written complaint
- Officer accepts and attaches to FIR

### Step 3: Informant Signs FIR

- Informant signs the FIR after confirming contents are accurate

### Step 4: FIR Number Assigned

- Each FIR is assigned a **unique FIR number** (consists of: number/year/police station code)

**Example**: FIR No. 234/2025 at Kotwali Police Station

### Step 5: Entry in Station Diary / GD

- Substance of FIR entered in General Diary (maintained in chronological order)

### Step 6: Copy Given to Informant

- **Free copy** of FIR given to informant immediately (mandatory)

### Step 7: Information Sent to Magistrate

- Copy of FIR sent to **Magistrate** having jurisdiction (Section 157 CrPC)

### Step 8: Investigation Begins

- Police officer proceeds to investigate under Section 156 CrPC (for cognizable offences)

## Contents of FIR

An FIR typically contains:

1. **Date and Time** of registration
2. **Place of Occurrence** (where crime occurred)
3. **Name and Address of Informant**
4. **Name and Address of Accused** (if known; otherwise "unknown persons")
5. **Description of Offence**: Narrative of what happened
6. **Details of Stolen/Damaged Property** (if applicable)
7. **Names of Witnesses** (if any)
8. **Sections of Law** under which offence falls (IPC sections, other laws)
9. **Signature of Informant**
10. **Signature/Seal of Police Officer** who recorded FIR

**FIR Number and Date** are crucial for tracking the case.

## Zero FIR (Anywhere in India)

### Concept

**Zero FIR**: FIR that can be registered at **any police station**, regardless of jurisdiction. It is later transferred to the police station having territorial jurisdiction.

**Why "Zero"?**: Initially, it is given a temporary number "0" (zero). After transfer, the receiving police station assigns a regular FIR number.

**When Used?**:
- **Emergency situations**: Victim is unable to reach the jurisdictional police station (injured, urgent help needed)
- **Crime occurred while traveling**: Victim doesn't know exact jurisdiction
- **Convenience**: Allows victim to lodge complaint at nearest police station

**Example**: A woman is raped in Delhi while traveling from Mumbai. She lodges Zero FIR at Mumbai police station. Mumbai police register it as Zero FIR and transfer to Delhi police (where crime occurred). Delhi police assign regular FIR number and investigate.

**Legal Basis**: Not explicitly mentioned in CrPC, but recognized by Supreme Court and police practice.

## Online FIR / e-FIR

Many states have introduced **online FIR** filing for certain categories of offences:

**Offences Covered** (varies by state):
- Theft of mobile phone, vehicle, documents
- Loss of property
- Motor vehicle accidents (non-injury cases)

**Procedure**:
1. Victim visits state police website or mobile app
2. Fills online form with details of offence
3. Submits online
4. FIR number generated
5. Victim may be called to police station later for verification / investigation

**Benefits**: Convenience, 24/7 availability, reduces rush at police stations

**Limitations**: Not available for all offences (serious crimes still require physical presence)

## Refusal to Register FIR: Remedies

### What if Police Refuse to Register FIR?

Despite *Lalita Kumari* judgment, police may still refuse to register FIR. **Remedies**:

#### 1. **Request in Writing**

- Give written complaint to police
- Take acknowledgment (diary entry number/receipt)
- If FIR not registered, written complaint serves as evidence of police refusal

#### 2. **Approach Senior Officer**

- Complaint to **Superintendent of Police (SP)** / **Deputy Commissioner of Police (DCP)**
- SP/DCP can direct subordinate police station to register FIR

#### 3. **File Application under Section 156(3) CrPC / Section 175(3) BNSS**

**Section 156(3)**: **Magistrate** can order police to register FIR and investigate.

**Procedure**:
- File application before Magistrate (Chief Judicial Magistrate or any Magistrate)
- Magistrate examines application
- If satisfied that information discloses cognizable offence, magistrate directs police to register FIR and investigate

**Advantage**: Magistrate's order is binding on police. Failure to comply can lead to contempt.

**Timeline**: Magistrate should dispose application expeditiously (typically within a few weeks).

#### 4. **File Writ Petition in High Court (Article 226)**

- Approach High Court with writ petition
- High Court can direct police to register FIR
- Used when lower remedies fail or in urgent/sensitive cases

**Example**: *Arnab Goswami v. State of Maharashtra* (2020) - Supreme Court intervened when Maharashtra police allegedly refused to transfer FIR to different jurisdiction (jurisdiction dispute).

#### 5. **Complaint to State Human Rights Commission / National Human Rights Commission**

- File complaint alleging human rights violation (right to justice)
- Commission can recommend action against erring officers

#### 6. **Private Complaint before Magistrate (Section 190 CrPC / Section 210 BNSS)**

- If all else fails, victim can directly approach Magistrate with **private complaint** (bypassing police)
- Magistrate can take cognizance and issue process (summons/warrant) to accused
- Investigation may be ordered, or trial may proceed directly

**Difference**:
- **FIR route**: Police investigate first, then file charge sheet
- **Private complaint route**: Magistrate takes cognizance; accused summoned directly; no police investigation initially (though magistrate can order investigation)

## FIR vs. Complaint

| **Aspect** | **FIR** | **Complaint** |
|------------|---------|---------------|
| **Legal Basis** | Section 154 CrPC (cognizable offences) | Section 190 CrPC (any offence, filed before Magistrate) |
| **Filed With** | Police Station | Magistrate |
| **Offences** | Cognizable offences only | Any offence (cognizable or non-cognizable) |
| **Investigation** | Police investigate automatically | Magistrate may order investigation or proceed directly |
| **Copy to Informant** | Mandatory free copy | No such provision |
| **Who Files** | Victim / witness / any informant | Victim / complainant |

**Both can lead to prosecution**, but FIR is more common for serious crimes as police investigation happens automatically.

## Importance of FIR

### 1. **Starting Point of Criminal Justice**
- FIR triggers investigation, arrest, and prosecution machinery

### 2. **Evidence**
- FIR can be used as **evidence** in court (though not substantive evidence of facts stated, but relevant for:
  - **Corroboration**: If informant's testimony in court matches FIR, it strengthens prosecution
  - **Contradiction**: If informant's court testimony differs from FIR, defense can use FIR to impeach credibility

### 3. **Protection from False Implication**

- If accused is named in FIR from the beginning, it is **stronger evidence** against him
- If accused's name is added later (during investigation), it raises suspicion of afterthought / false implication

**Example**: FIR names A, B, C as accused. Later, during investigation, police add D's name. D can argue he was falsely implicated later (FIR didn't name him initially).

### 4. **Basis for Bail**

- Bail applications often refer to FIR (nature of accusation, sections charged)

### 5. **Time Stamp**

- FIR establishes when information was first received by police
- Delay in lodging FIR can weaken prosecution case (defense may argue: if crime was serious, why delay in reporting?)

**Example**: A alleges assault by B on January 1, but FIR is lodged on January 15. Defense can question 15-day delay.

**However**: Delay alone doesn't discredit case if satisfactorily explained (victim was hospitalized, threatened, etc.)

## Quashing of FIR (Section 482 CrPC / Section 528 BNSS)

### When Can FIR Be Quashed?

**High Court** has inherent power under **Section 482 CrPC** to quash FIR if:

1. **No cognizable offence is made out**: Even if allegations in FIR are taken as true, no offence is committed

**Example**: FIR alleges civil dispute (property disagreement) without any criminal element. High Court can quash.

2. **Abuse of process of court**: FIR filed with malicious intent to harass accused

**Example**: False FIR filed by business rival to harass competitor.

3. **Settlement between parties** (in compoundable offences)

**Example**: Parties to a cheating case settle dispute amicably. High Court may quash FIR if offence is compoundable.

4. **Continuation would be abuse of justice**

**Example**: FIR based on civil contract dispute; no criminal intent; allowing it to continue would abuse justice.

**Limitations**:
- High Court exercises this power **sparingly**
- If there are disputed questions of fact, High Court won't quash (trial should decide)
- In serious offences (murder, rape, dacoity), quashing is rare (even if parties settle)

## Landmark Cases on FIR

### 1. **Lalita Kumari v. Govt. of U.P. (2014)** - Mandatory FIR Registration
**AIR 2014 SC 187**

**Held**: FIR registration is **mandatory** if information discloses cognizable offence. Police have no discretion. Preliminary inquiry allowed only in exceptions (matrimonial disputes, commercial disputes, medical negligence, corruption).

**Impact**: Strengthened citizens' rights; curbed police tendency to refuse FIR.

### 2. **State of Haryana v. Bhajan Lal (1992)** - Quashing of FIR
**AIR 1992 SC 604**

**Held**: High Court can quash FIR under Section 482 in following situations:
- FIR doesn't disclose cognizable offence
- Allegations are absurd/inherently improbable
- Criminal proceeding is abuse of process
- FIR is to satisfy personal vendetta

**Guidelines**: Court laid down guidelines for exercise of inherent powers to quash FIR/criminal proceedings.

### 3. **Arnesh Kumar v. State of Bihar (2014)** - Arrest Guidelines
**AIR 2014 SC 2756**

**Held**: Police should not arrest mechanically in all cognizable offences. Arrest only if:
- Necessary to prevent further offence
- Necessary for proper investigation
- Accused may flee
- Accused may tamper with evidence/witnesses

**Relation to FIR**: Even if FIR is registered for cognizable offence, arrest is not automatic. Police must justify need for arrest.

**Impact**: Curbed arbitrary arrests based on FIR.

### 4. **T.T. Antony v. State of Kerala (2001)** - Delayed FIR
**AIR 2001 SC 2637**

**Held**: Delay in lodging FIR can affect credibility of prosecution case, but delay alone doesn't discredit case if **satisfactorily explained** (victim was injured, threatened, etc.).

**Principle**: Courts examine reasons for delay; each case decided on facts.

## Practical Guidance

### For Victims / Informants

**Before Going to Police Station**:
- [ ] Gather facts: Date, time, place, what happened, who was involved
- [ ] Identify witnesses (if any)
- [ ] Collect evidence: Photos, videos, documents, medical reports (if injured)
- [ ] Note down names/badge numbers of accused (if known)

**At Police Station**:
- [ ] Narrate incident clearly and chronologically
- [ ] Ensure all important facts are recorded in FIR
- [ ] Read FIR carefully before signing (verify accuracy)
- [ ] Collect **free copy** of FIR (mandatory - Section 154(2))
- [ ] Note down **FIR number** (crucial for tracking)

**If Police Refuse to Register FIR**:
- [ ] Give written complaint; take acknowledgment
- [ ] Approach SP/DCP
- [ ] File application under Section 156(3) before Magistrate
- [ ] If urgent, approach High Court (writ petition)

### For Accused

**If Named in FIR**:
- [ ] Obtain copy of FIR (through advocate or from police)
- [ ] Analyze sections charged and potential punishment
- [ ] Engage criminal defense lawyer immediately
- [ ] Assess bail prospects (bailable vs. non-bailable offence)
- [ ] Prepare defense strategy
- [ ] If FIR is false/mala fide, consider quashing petition in High Court (Section 482)

**Do Not**:
- ❌ Approach complainant or witnesses to settle/threaten (can lead to additional charges)
- ❌ Destroy evidence (Section 201 IPC - destruction of evidence)
- ❌ Abscond (arrest warrant will be issued; bail becomes difficult)

## Key Takeaways

1. **FIR is Mandatory**: If information discloses cognizable offence, police must register FIR (*Lalita Kumari*). No discretion.

2. **Free Copy to Informant**: Informant entitled to free copy of FIR immediately (Section 154(2)).

3. **Zero FIR**: Can be lodged at any police station in emergency; later transferred to jurisdictional police.

4. **Remedies for Refusal**: Section 156(3) application to Magistrate, complaint to SP, writ petition in High Court.

5. **FIR is Evidence**: Can be used for corroboration or contradiction of informant's testimony; establishes timeline.

6. **Delayed FIR**: Delay weakens prosecution if not explained, but not fatal if satisfactory reason exists.

7. **Quashing**: High Court can quash FIR if no cognizable offence, abuse of process, or settlement (Section 482).

8. **No Automatic Arrest**: Even if FIR registered, arrest not automatic (*Arnesh Kumar*); police must justify need.

## Conclusion

The First Information Report (FIR) is the cornerstone of criminal justice in India. It is the citizen's first interface with law enforcement and the official trigger for investigation and prosecution machinery.

The landmark *Lalita Kumari* judgment (2014) strengthened citizens' rights by making FIR registration mandatory, curbing decades of police discretion and refusals. However, challenges persist: reluctance to register FIRs (especially against influential persons), delayed registration, improper recording of facts.

For victims, understanding the FIR process—knowing it's their legal right, obtaining a free copy, and using remedies if refused—is crucial. For the accused, the FIR is often the first indication of criminal proceedings and requires immediate legal response.

With the transition to BNSS 2023, the substantive law on FIR remains unchanged, but technological advancements (e-FIR, online complaints) are making the system more accessible and accountable.
