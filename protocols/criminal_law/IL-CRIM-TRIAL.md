## Overview and Purpose

### What is a Criminal Trial?

A **criminal trial** is the **formal judicial proceeding** where the prosecution presents evidence to prove an accused person guilty beyond reasonable doubt, and the accused has the opportunity to defend against the charges.

### Purpose of Criminal Trial

1. **Determine Guilt or Innocence**: Establish whether accused committed the offence
2. **Ensure Justice**: Fair opportunity to both prosecution and defense
3. **Protect Rights**: Safeguard constitutional and legal rights of accused
4. **Public Confidence**: Transparent process builds trust in justice system
5. **Sentence**: If convicted, determine appropriate punishment
6. **Acquit Innocent**: Protect innocent from wrongful conviction

### Fundamental Principles

1. **Presumption of Innocence**: Accused innocent until proven guilty (Article 21 + Section 101 Evidence Act)
2. **Burden of Proof on Prosecution**: Prosecution must prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt
3. **Right to Fair Trial**: Article 21 - procedure must be fair, just, reasonable
4. **Right to Defense**: Article 22(1) - right to be defended by lawyer
5. **Public Trial**: Open court proceedings (except specific cases)
6. **Speedy Trial**: Article 21 - undue delay violates fundamental rights

## Stages of Criminal Trial

### Pre-Trial Stage

#### 1. Taking Cognizance (Section 190 CrPC / 210 BNSS)

**What is Cognizance?**
Application of judicial mind to the facts of a case to determine if judicial proceedings should be initiated

**Who Takes Cognizance?**
**Magistrate** only (not police, not Sessions Judge initially)

**How Cognizance Taken?** (Section 190)

Three modes:
1. **Upon police report** (charge sheet under Section 173)
2. **Upon complaint** (private complaint by complainant)
3. **Upon information** from any person (other than police report) OR upon own knowledge

**Landmark Case**: **R.R. Chari v. State of U.P. (1951)**
- Cognizance is application of judicial mind
- Magistrate must be satisfied that prima facie case exists
- Mechanical taking of cognizance not permissible

#### 2. Inquiry/Summoning of Accused (Sections 200-204 CrPC / 223-229 BNSS)

**If Cognizance on Complaint**:

**Section 200**: Magistrate to examine complainant on oath

**Section 202**: Magistrate may:
- Postpone issue of process
- Direct investigation by police (if cognizable)
- Inquire himself or order inquiry

**Section 204**: If satisfied offence has been committed:
- **Issue summons** (for appearance of accused)
- OR **Issue warrant** (if case requires)

**If Cognizance on Police Report**:
- Directly proceed to frame charges (if sessions triable)
- OR summon accused (if Magistrate triable)

#### 3. Discharge (Section 239, 245 CrPC / 262, 268 BNSS)

**What is Discharge?**
Release of accused from case at pre-trial stage if no sufficient grounds to proceed

**When Granted?**
- No prima facie case
- Evidence insufficient
- Case based on conjecture

**Difference from Acquittal**:
- **Discharge**: Before trial begins (at framing of charges stage)
- **Acquittal**: After trial, on merits

**Landmark Case**: **State of Bihar v. Ramesh Singh (1977)**
- At discharge stage, court does not require proof beyond reasonable doubt
- Only **prima facie case** to be seen
- If two views possible, one leading to conviction and other to discharge, benefit to accused

#### 4. Framing of Charges (Sections 228, 240, 246 CrPC / 251, 263, 271 BNSS)

**Purpose**: Inform accused of precise accusation

**Procedure**:
1. Prosecution evidence heard (in Sessions cases - Section 228)
2. Accused heard on proposed charges (Section 240)
3. Charges framed in writing (Section 211)
4. **Charges read and explained** to accused (Section 228)
5. Accused asked to plead guilty or claim trial

**Contents of Charge** (Section 211 / 233 BNSS):
- Offence with which accused is charged
- Law and section under which offence punishable
- Particulars of offence (time, place, manner)

**Landmark Case**: **State of Maharashtra v. Som Nath Thapa (1996)**
- Charge is foundation of trial
- Defect in charge may vitiate trial
- But if accused not prejudiced, conviction can be upheld

**Effect of Plea**:
- **Pleads Guilty**: Court may convict on plea (Section 229)
- **Claims Trial**: Trial proceeds

## Types of Trials

### Based on Court

| Trial Type | Court | Offences | Procedure |
|-----------|-------|----------|-----------|
| **Warrant Cases by Magistrate** | Judicial Magistrate | Punishable with >2 years | Sections 238-250 CrPC / 261-274 BNSS |
| **Summons Cases by Magistrate** | Judicial Magistrate | Punishable with ≤2 years | Sections 251-259 CrPC / 275-285 BNSS |
| **Summary Trial** | Magistrate | Petty offences | Sections 260-265 CrPC / 321-326 BNSS |
| **Sessions Trial** | Sessions Court | Serious offences (death, life, >7 years) | Sections 225-237 CrPC / 248-260 BNSS |

### 1. Trial Before Court of Session (Sections 225-237 CrPC / 248-260 BNSS)

**Jurisdiction**: Offences **exclusively triable by Sessions Court**:
- Punishable with death
- Punishable with life imprisonment
- Punishable with imprisonment for term exceeding 7 years (if so specified in law)

**Procedure**:

#### Step 1: Committal (Section 209 CrPC / 230 BNSS)
- **Magistrate commits** case to Sessions Court (after taking cognizance)
- Committal is transferring case from Magistrate to Sessions Court

#### Step 2: Sessions Judge Takes Cognizance
- Charge sheet and case diary forwarded
- Sessions Judge examines record

#### Step 3: Framing of Charge (Section 228 CrPC / 251 BNSS)
- Prosecution evidence examined
- Accused heard
- If prima facie case exists: Charges framed
- If not: Accused discharged (Section 227 / 250 BNSS)

#### Step 4: Plea (Section 229 CrPC / 252 BNSS)
- Charge read and explained to accused
- Accused asked to plead

**If Pleads Guilty**:
- Judge may convict (after satisfying himself plea is voluntary and accused understands consequences)

**If Claims Trial**: Trial proceeds

#### Step 5: Prosecution Evidence (Section 231 CrPC / 254 BNSS)
- **Prosecutor's opening address** (optional)
- **Examination of prosecution witnesses**:
  - **Examination-in-Chief** (by prosecution)
  - **Cross-Examination** (by defense)
  - **Re-Examination** (by prosecution, if needed)
- **Documentary evidence** produced

#### Step 6: Statement of Accused (Section 313 CrPC / 367 BNSS)

**When**: After prosecution evidence concludes

**Purpose**:
- Give accused opportunity to **explain incriminating circumstances**
- Accused confronted with evidence against them
- Accused can offer explanations

**Procedure**:
- Court questions accused on incriminating circumstances
- Answers recorded
- **Not on oath**
- Can be used to corroborate defense but generally not incriminating (if accused gives new version, can be used against)

**Landmark Case**: **Rahim Beg v. State of U.P. (1973)**
- Section 313 examination is **mandatory**
- Failure to examine under Section 313 is **fatal irregularity**
- Trial may be vitiated

#### Step 7: Defense Evidence (Section 233 CrPC / 257 BNSS)

**After Section 313**:
- **Accused may produce defense evidence**:
  - **Defense witnesses** (examined, cross-examined, re-examined)
  - **Documentary evidence**
- Accused may also testify (optional)

**Right to Silence**: Accused cannot be compelled to testify (Article 20(3))

#### Step 8: Arguments (Section 234 CrPC / 258 BNSS)

**Prosecution's Final Arguments**: Summarize evidence, argue guilt

**Defense's Final Arguments**: Counter prosecution, argue innocence, highlight reasonable doubt

#### Step 9: Judgment (Section 235, 354 CrPC / 259, 408 BNSS)

**Court considers**:
- All evidence
- Prosecution arguments
- Defense arguments
- Applies legal principles

**Verdict**:
- **Conviction**: If guilt proved beyond reasonable doubt
- **Acquittal**: If reasonable doubt exists OR prosecution fails to prove guilt

**Judgment Requirements** (Section 354 / 408 BNSS):
- **In writing**
- **Points for determination**
- **Decision on each point** with reasons
- **Language understood** by accused

**Signature**: Signed and dated by Judge

#### Step 10: Sentencing (Section 235(2) CrPC / 259(2) BNSS)

**If Convicted**:
- **Separate hearing on sentence**
- Accused given opportunity to be heard on sentence
- Mitigating and aggravating circumstances considered
- Sentence pronounced

### 2. Warrant Cases by Magistrate (Sections 238-250 CrPC / 261-274 BNSS)

**Jurisdiction**: Offences punishable with **imprisonment >2 years** (but not exclusively triable by Sessions Court)

**Two Procedures**:

#### A. Where Police Report Filed (Section 238 CrPC / 261 BNSS)

**Procedure**:
1. Magistrate takes cognizance on police report
2. If triable by Magistrate, proceeds to hear case
3. **Prosecution evidence** presented
4. **Section 313 statement** of accused
5. **Defense evidence**
6. **Arguments**
7. **Judgment**

#### B. Where Complaint Filed (Section 244 CrPC / 267 BNSS)

**Procedure**:
1. Magistrate takes cognizance on complaint
2. Process issued to accused (summons/warrant)
3. **Accused appears**
4. **Particulars of offence** stated to accused
5. **Accused may be discharged** if no sufficient ground (Section 245 / 268 BNSS)
6. If not discharged, **prosecution evidence** heard
7. **Section 313 statement**
8. **Defense evidence**
9. **Arguments**
10. **Judgment**

### 3. Summons Cases by Magistrate (Sections 251-259 CrPC / 275-285 BNSS)

**Jurisdiction**: Offences punishable with **imprisonment ≤2 years OR fine only OR both**

**Simplified Procedure**:
1. **Substance of accusation** stated to accused (not formal charge)
2. Accused asked to plead
3. **If Pleads Guilty**: Convicted and sentenced
4. **If Claims Trial**:
   - Prosecution evidence
   - Section 313 statement
   - Defense evidence
   - Arguments
   - Judgment

**Key Difference**: No formal charge framing; simplified procedure

### 4. Summary Trial (Sections 260-265 CrPC / 321-326 BNSS)

**Jurisdiction**: **Petty offences** specified in Section 260

**Examples**:
- Theft where value <Rs. 2,000
- Receiving stolen property where value <Rs. 2,000
- Assisting in concealment of stolen property
- Offences under Cattle Trespass Act, etc.

**Procedure**: **Highly Simplified**
- Brief facts recorded
- Plea taken
- Evidence (brief)
- Judgment (reasons may be brief)

**Limitation on Sentence**:
- Imprisonment: **Maximum 3 months**
- Fine: No limit

**Appeal**: To Sessions Court (within 30 days)

## Trial Procedure: Step-by-Step

### 1. Recording of Evidence (Sections 272-283 CrPC / 338-352 BNSS)

**All Evidence Recorded**:
- Witness testimony
- Documentary evidence
- Physical evidence (exhibits)

**Recording Method**:
- **In writing** (or typed/recorded electronically - BNSS enhancement)
- In **open court**
- In **language of court** (or translated)

#### Examination of Witnesses

**Three Stages**:

##### A. Examination-in-Chief
- **By party calling witness** (prosecution calls prosecution witnesses; defense calls defense witnesses)
- **Questions**: Open-ended, non-leading
- **Purpose**: Elicit favorable evidence

**Leading Questions Prohibited** (Section 141 Evidence Act / BSA): Questions suggesting answer

##### B. Cross-Examination
- **By opposite party**
- **Questions**: Can be leading
- **Purpose**: Challenge credibility, elicit favorable facts, contradict

**Right to Cross-Examine**: Fundamental right (Article 21)

**Landmark Case**: **State of U.P. v. Ram Babu Misra (1980)**
- Right to cross-examination is essential component of fair trial
- Denial of cross-examination may vitiate trial

##### C. Re-Examination
- **By party who called witness**
- **Purpose**: Clarify matters arising in cross-examination
- **Cannot introduce new facts** not in examination-in-chief

### 2. Statement Under Section 313 CrPC / 367 BNSS

(Already covered above in Sessions Trial procedure)

**Key Points**:
- **Mandatory** - failure is fatal irregularity
- **After prosecution evidence** concludes
- **Not on oath**
- **Opportunity to explain** incriminating circumstances
- Answers can be used in evidence (but limited use)

### 3. Defense Evidence

**Accused's Rights**:
1. **Remain silent**: No compulsion to testify (Article 20(3))
2. **Testify**: Can give evidence (but then subject to cross-examination)
3. **Call defense witnesses**: Can examine witnesses to support defense
4. **Produce documents**: Can produce documentary evidence

**Burden**: No burden on accused to prove innocence (burden on prosecution to prove guilt)

### 4. Arguments

**Prosecution's Final Arguments**:
- Summarize evidence
- Connect evidence to charges
- Argue guilt beyond reasonable doubt
- Cite law and precedents
- Request conviction and sentence

**Defense's Final Arguments**:
- Challenge prosecution evidence
- Highlight contradictions, lacunae
- Argue reasonable doubt
- Argue innocence or lesser offence
- Request acquittal or lenient sentence

### 5. Judgment

**Requirements** (Section 354 CrPC / 408 BNSS):
1. **Points for determination** clearly stated
2. **Decision on each point** with reasons
3. **Findings** on facts and law
4. **Conviction or acquittal** clearly pronounced
5. **Language** understood by accused

**Standard of Proof**: **Beyond Reasonable Doubt**

**Landmark Case**: **Woolmington v. DPP (1935)** (English case adopted in India)
- **Golden thread of criminal jurisprudence**: Burden on prosecution to prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt
- Duty never shifts to accused to prove innocence

**Reasonable Doubt**: Doubt for which reason can be assigned; not fanciful or imaginary

**If Reasonable Doubt Exists**: Acquittal (benefit of doubt to accused)

### 6. Sentencing (Section 235(2) CrPC / 259(2) BNSS)

**If Conviction**:
- **Separate hearing** on sentence (bifurcated trial)
- **Accused heard on sentence**: Mitigating factors
  - First-time offender
  - Young age
  - Family circumstances
  - Socio-economic background
  - Provocation
  - No premeditation
  - Remorse
- **Prosecution heard**: Aggravating factors
  - Gravity of offence
  - Manner of commission (brutal, heinous)
  - Impact on victim/society
  - No remorse
  - Danger to society
- **Victim Impact Statement**: Can be considered (BNSS enhancement)

**Sentencing Principles**:
- **Proportionality**: Punishment should fit crime
- **Reformative**: Focus on rehabilitation (especially for young offenders)
- **Deterrence**: Deter accused and others from committing crimes
- **Retributive**: Society's retribution for crime

**Death Penalty Cases**: **"Rarest of Rare"** doctrine

**Landmark Case**: **Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980)**
- Death penalty only in **rarest of rare cases**
- When alternative sentence is foreclosed
- Must consider mitigating and aggravating circumstances

## Rights of Accused During Trial

### Constitutional Rights

#### 1. Presumption of Innocence (Article 21 + Section 101 Evidence Act)
- Accused presumed innocent until proven guilty
- Burden on prosecution to prove guilt

#### 2. Right to Fair Trial (Article 21)
- Procedure must be fair, just, reasonable
- No bias, prejudice
- Adequate opportunity to defend

#### 3. Right to Speedy Trial (Article 21)
- Undue delay violates fundamental rights
- Remedy: Quashing of proceedings OR compensation

**Landmark Case**: **Hussainara Khatoon v. State of Bihar (1979)**
- Right to speedy trial is fundamental right
- Prolonged pre-trial detention violates Article 21

#### 4. Right Against Self-Incrimination (Article 20(3))
- Cannot be compelled to be witness against self
- Right to remain silent

#### 5. Right to Legal Representation (Article 22(1))
- Right to be defended by lawyer of choice
- Right to legal aid if indigent (Article 39A + Section 304 CrPC / 358 BNSS)

**Landmark Case**: **Hussainara Khatoon (1979)** + **Khatri v. State of Bihar (1981)**
- Free legal aid is fundamental right of poor, indigent accused
- Denial of legal aid may vitiate trial

#### 6. Right Against Double Jeopardy (Article 20(2))
- Person cannot be prosecuted and punished **twice for same offence**
- After acquittal or conviction, same person cannot be tried again for same offence

**Exceptions**:
- If first trial was invalid (lack of jurisdiction, etc.)
- If error apparent on face of record

**Section 300 CrPC / 354 BNSS**: Codifies double jeopardy protection

### Statutory Rights

#### 1. Right to Be Present During Trial (Section 273 CrPC / 339 BNSS)
- Accused has right to be present during trial (except in certain circumstances)
- Evidence must be taken in presence of accused

**Exceptions**:
- Accused absconding: Trial can proceed ex parte (Section 299 CrPC / 353 BNSS)
- Accused misbehaving: Can be removed temporarily

#### 2. Right to Cross-Examine Prosecution Witnesses
- Fundamental right
- Essential for fair trial

#### 3. Right to Call Defense Witnesses (Section 233 CrPC / 257 BNSS)
- Accused can summon witnesses
- Court to issue summons/warrants

#### 4. Right to Free Copy of Judgment (Section 363 CrPC / 417 BNSS)
- Convicted accused entitled to free copy of judgment (for appeal)

#### 5. Right to Interpreter (Section 281 CrPC / 350 BNSS)
- If accused doesn't understand language of court
- Evidence to be interpreted

#### 6. Right to Medical Examination
- If accused alleges torture or ill-treatment

## Victim's Rights in Trial

### Statutory Rights

#### 1. Right to Compensation (Section 357 CrPC / 411 BNSS)
- Court can order compensation to victim from fine imposed on accused
- State can also provide compensation (Section 357A)

#### 2. Right to Be Heard (Section 301 CrPC / 355 BNSS)
- Victim can oppose acquittal/discharge
- Can file appeal against acquittal (with leave)

#### 3. Right to Victim Impact Statement
- **BNSS Enhancement**: Victim can give statement on impact of crime
- Considered during sentencing

#### 4. Right to Free Legal Aid (Section 304 CrPC / 358 BNSS)
- Victim can get free legal aid
- Especially in sexual offence cases

#### 5. Special Protection in Sexual Offence Cases

**Section 327 CrPC / 391 BNSS**: **In-camera trial**
- Trial of rape and certain sexual offences held **in camera** (closed to public)
- Protect victim's privacy and dignity

**Section 53A CrPC / 52A BNSS**: Medical examination of rape victim
- Within 24 hours of incident
- By female doctor or in presence of female

**Section 164A CrPC / 183A BNSS**: Recording of statement of victim
- By Magistrate
- Under Section 164 (as soon as possible)

**Landmark Case**: **State of Punjab v. Gurmit Singh (1996)**
- Rape victim's testimony must be given utmost consideration
- Conviction can be based on sole testimony of victim if found credible

## Special Categories of Trials

### 1. Juvenile Trials

**Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act 2015**:
- **Child in Conflict with Law** (below 18 years): Not tried in regular criminal court
- **Juvenile Justice Board** (JJB) conducts inquiry
- **Not "trial"** - termed "inquiry"
- **Observation Home**, not jail
- **Rehabilitation focus**, not punishment

**Exception**:
- **Heinous offences** by juveniles 16-18 years: JJB can transfer to Children's Court (regular trial)

### 2. POCSO Trials

**Protection of Children from Sexual Offences (POCSO) Act 2012**:
- Special Courts designated
- **In-camera trial** mandatory (Section 327 CrPC)
- **Speedy trial**: Within **1 year** (as far as possible)
- **Child-friendly** procedures:
  - Support person for child
  - No direct confrontation with accused
  - Frequent breaks
  - Screen/one-way mirror
  - Video recording of testimony

### 3. Rape and Sexual Offence Trials

**In-Camera Trial** (Section 327 CrPC / 391 BNSS):
- Closed to public
- Only parties and lawyers present

**Victim's Identity Protection**:
- Name not disclosed in judgment
- Media cannot publish victim's identity

**Cross-Examination**: Defense can cross-examine, but court ensures no harassment

### 4. Terrorism Trials (NIA Act)

**National Investigation Agency (NIA) Act 2008**:
- Special NIA Courts
- Strict timelines
- Enhanced security
- Witness protection

## Evidence and Proof

### Burden of Proof

**General Rule** (Section 101 Evidence Act / BSA):
- **Burden on prosecution** to prove guilt
- **Never shifts to accused** (except specific statutory exceptions)

**Standard**: **Beyond Reasonable Doubt**

**Exceptions** (Burden shifts to accused):
- **Section 304B IPC / BNS** (Dowry death): Presumption of guilt if soon after marriage and cruelty proved
- **Section 113A Evidence Act / BSA** (Abetment to suicide): Presumption if soon before suicide and cruelty
- **Section 79 IT Act 2000**: Digital signature presumption
- **Section 4 PMLA 2002**: Burden to prove property not proceeds of crime (recently held unconstitutional in part)

### Types of Evidence

**See Protocol**: IL-CRIM-EVIDENCE.md

**Key Types**:
1. **Direct Evidence**: Eyewitness testimony
2. **Circumstantial Evidence**: Indirect evidence (circumstances forming chain)
3. **Documentary Evidence**: Documents, records
4. **Expert Evidence**: Forensic reports, medical opinions
5. **Dying Declaration**: Statement of person about cause of death (highly reliable)

**Circumstantial Evidence**: Conviction possible on circumstantial evidence alone if **chain is complete** and points **only to guilt** (no other hypothesis)

**Landmark Case**: **Sharad Birdhichand Sarda v. State of Maharashtra (1984)** (Panchsheel Principles):
1. Circumstances must be **fully established**
2. Circumstances must be **consistent only with guilt**
3. Circumstances must be **conclusive**
4. Must **exclude every hypothesis except guilt**
5. Chain of circumstances must be **complete** (no missing links)

## Trial Defects and Irregularities

### Curable Irregularities (Section 465 CrPC / 517 BNSS)

**General Rule**: Finding, sentence, or order not reversed **merely** because of error/irregularity in:
- Complaint
- Summons/warrant
- Charge
- Proclamation
- Order
- Proceedings

**Condition**: If **no failure of justice** resulted

**Principle**: **Substantial justice** over **technical niceties**

**Landmark Case**: **State of West Bengal v. Laisal Haque (1989)**
- Minor procedural irregularities do not vitiate trial
- Court looks at whether accused was prejudiced
- If substantial justice done, conviction upheld

### Fatal Defects (Not Curable)

**Examples**:
1. **Lack of jurisdiction**: Court had no authority to try case
2. **Violation of Section 313**: Failure to examine accused (Rahim Beg case)
3. **Violation of right to legal aid**: Indigent accused denied lawyer
4. **Violation of in-camera trial**: Rape trial held in open court
5. **Denial of cross-examination**: Prosecution witnesses not cross-examined (if defense wanted)

**Effect**: Trial vitiated; conviction set aside

## Judgment and Sentencing

### Judgment (Section 354 CrPC / 408 BNSS)

**Contents**:
1. **Serial number** of case
2. **Name of parties**
3. **Offence** charged
4. **Points for determination**
5. **Decision** on each point with reasons
6. **Findings** on facts and law
7. **Conviction or acquittal**
8. **If conviction**: Offence and section
9. **Sentence** (if convicted)
10. **Language** understood by accused

**Signature**: Judge signs and dates

**Pronouncement**: In open court

**Delivery**: Copy to accused (free if convicted)

### Sentencing Principles

**Competing Theories**:
1. **Deterrence**: Prevent crime by fear of punishment
2. **Retribution**: Society's vengeance/punishment for wrong
3. **Reformation**: Rehabilitate offender
4. **Incapacitation**: Remove dangerous offenders from society

**Indian Approach**: **Balancing** all theories

**Landmark Cases**:

#### Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980)
- Death penalty only in **rarest of rare cases**
- Mitigating and aggravating circumstances to be balanced
- Life imprisonment is rule, death is exception

#### Machhi Singh v. State of Punjab (1983)
- Rarest of rare: Manner of commission, motive, anti-social character, magnitude
- When crime shocks collective conscience

#### Mithu v. State of Punjab (1983)
- Mandatory death sentence unconstitutional (Section 303 IPC struck down)
- Judicial discretion necessary

#### Dhananjoy Chatterjee v. State of West Bengal (1994)
- Rape and murder of minor girl
- Held: Rarest of rare case; death penalty confirmed
- Actually executed (2004) - rare instance

## Speedy Trial

### Constitutional Right (Article 21)

**Hussainara Khatoon v. State of Bihar (1979)**:
- Speedy trial is fundamental right
- Prolonged detention without trial violates Article 21
- Especially if accused already served period exceeding possible sentence

### Statutory Provisions

**Section 309 CrPC / 363 BNSS**: Trial to be held day-to-day
- Court to hear case **day-to-day** until witnesses examined
- Adjournments only for **special reasons** (to be recorded)
- **Maximum adjournment**: Not exceeding 15 days

### Remedies for Delay

1. **Writ Petition**: For quashing proceedings due to inordinate delay
2. **Compensation**: Article 32/226 - compensation for violation of speedy trial right
3. **Discharge/Acquittal**: If delay prejudices accused

**Landmark Case**: **Abdul Rehman Antulay v. R.S. Nayak (1992)**
- Right to speedy trial is fundamental
- Inordinate delay can be ground for quashing
- Balancing test: Prejudice to accused vs societal interest

- Video conferencing for witnesses

## Practical Compliance Checklist

### For Prosecution

#### Pre-Trial
- [ ] File charge sheet within **60/90 days** (if accused in custody)
- [ ] Ensure evidence properly collected and preserved
- [ ] List all witnesses (material and formal)
- [ ] Prepare case diary and investigation papers

#### At Cognizance/Framing of Charges
- [ ] Assist court in taking cognizance
- [ ] Oppose discharge application with evidence
- [ ] Ensure charges properly framed (complete, specific)

#### During Trial
- [ ] Present prosecution witnesses systematically
- [ ] Examine witnesses **in chief** effectively
- [ ] **Re-examine** to clarify matters from cross-examination
- [ ] Produce all **documentary evidence** with proper proof
- [ ] Object to inadmissible defense evidence
- [ ] Present **final arguments** comprehensively:
  - Summarize evidence
  - Connect evidence to charges
  - Establish guilt beyond reasonable doubt
- [ ] If conviction, argue for appropriate sentence (aggravating factors)

#### Post-Trial
- [ ] If acquittal, evaluate grounds for appeal
- [ ] If conviction, file appeal if sentence inadequate

### For Defense

#### Pre-Trial
- [ ] Apply for **bail** (anticipatory or regular)
- [ ] Collect **exculpatory evidence**
- [ ] Identify defense witnesses
- [ ] File for **discharge** if no prima facie case

#### At Framing of Charges
- [ ] Challenge **defective charges**
- [ ] Apply for discharge (if no sufficient grounds)
- [ ] Ensure charges specific and not vague

#### During Trial
- [ ] **Cross-examine** prosecution witnesses thoroughly:
  - Challenge credibility
  - Highlight contradictions
  - Elicit favorable facts
- [ ] Ensure accused's **Section 313 statement** properly recorded
  - Explain all incriminating circumstances
- [ ] Present **defense evidence**:
  - Defense witnesses
  - Alibi evidence
  - Documentary evidence
- [ ] Decide whether accused should testify (pros and cons)
- [ ] Present **final arguments** effectively:
  - Challenge prosecution evidence
  - Highlight reasonable doubt
  - Argue innocence or lesser offence

#### Sentencing (If Convicted)
- [ ] Present **mitigating circumstances**:
  - First-time offender
  - Young age
  - Provocation
  - Socio-economic background
  - Family responsibilities
  - Remorse
- [ ] Request lenient sentence

#### Post-Trial
- [ ] File **appeal** if convicted (within limitation period)
- [ ] Apply for **suspension of sentence** pending appeal

### For Courts

#### Cognizance and Framing of Charges
- [ ] Apply judicial mind to material (R.R. Chari principle)
- [ ] Issue process only if **prima facie case** exists
- [ ] Frame charges **precisely** (Section 211 / 233 BNSS)
- [ ] Discharge if no sufficient grounds (State of Bihar v. Ramesh Singh test)

#### During Trial
- [ ] Ensure **fair trial**:
  - Unbiased, impartial
  - Equal opportunity to both sides
- [ ] Record evidence **in open court** (except in-camera cases)
- [ ] Ensure **day-to-day trial** (Section 309 / 363 BNSS)
- [ ] Avoid unnecessary adjournments
- [ ] Conduct **Section 313 examination** properly (Rahim Beg mandate)
- [ ] Give accused opportunity to present defense
- [ ] If indigent accused, provide **free legal aid** (Hussainara Khatoon)

#### Judgment
- [ ] Apply **beyond reasonable doubt** standard (Woolmington principle)
- [ ] Write **reasoned judgment** (Section 354 / 408 BNSS):
  - Points for determination
  - Decision on each point with reasons
  - Findings on facts and law
- [ ] If **reasonable doubt**, acquit (benefit of doubt to accused)
- [ ] If **conviction**, conduct separate **sentencing hearing** (Section 235(2) / 259(2))
- [ ] Consider **mitigating and aggravating** circumstances
- [ ] Impose **proportionate sentence**
- [ ] Provide **free copy of judgment** to convicted accused

## Conclusion

Criminal trial is the **crucible of justice** where:
- **Prosecution must prove guilt** beyond reasonable doubt
- **Accused has right to fair trial** under Article 21
- **Presumption of innocence** is sacrosanct
- **Speedy trial** is fundamental right
- **Burden never shifts** to accused (except statutory exceptions)

**Key Principles**:
1. **Fair trial is fundamental right** (Article 21)
2. **Presumption of innocence** (Section 101 Evidence Act)
3. **Burden on prosecution** (Woolmington principle)
4. **Standard: Beyond reasonable doubt**
5. **Benefit of doubt to accused**
6. **No double jeopardy** (Article 20(2))
7. **Right to legal aid** (Hussainara Khatoon)
8. **Speedy trial** (Article 21)
9. **Section 313 examination mandatory** (Rahim Beg)
10. **Victim's rights** enhanced (BNSS)

**Sentencing**:
- **Death penalty**: Rarest of rare (Bachan Singh)
- **Balance**: Deterrence, retribution, reformation
- **Victim impact statement**: Now considered (BNSS)

The transition from CrPC to BNSS **retains trial procedures** with **enhancements**:
- Electronic evidence recording
- Victim impact statements
- Enhanced witness protection
- Use of technology (video conferencing, digital case management)

**Ultimate Goal**: **Justice** - conviction of guilty, acquittal of innocent, fair procedure for all.
