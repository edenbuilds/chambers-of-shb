## Overview

**Corruption** is the abuse of entrusted power for private gain. In the criminal law context, it primarily refers to **bribery** (giving or taking illegal gratification) and **criminal misconduct** by public servants.

Corruption undermines:
- **Good governance** and rule of law
- **Public trust** in institutions
- **Economic development** (discourages investment, distorts markets)
- **Social justice** (favors corrupt over deserving)

### Key Characteristics
1. **Public Servant Focused**: Primary offences target public servants (government officials)
2. **Dual Liability**: Both giver (bribe giver) and taker (public servant) are liable
3. **Strict Punishment**: Imprisonment from 6 months to life, with forfeiture of assets
4. **Preventive Measures**: Disproportionate assets provision (Section 13(1)(e)) allows prosecution without proving specific bribe
5. **Special Courts**: Designated Special Courts for trial of corruption cases
6. **Sanction Required**: Prior government sanction required for prosecution (Section 19)

## Who is a "Public Servant" (Section 2(c) PCA)

### Comprehensive Definition

A **"public servant"** includes:

1. **Government Employees**: Central, State, Local Government officials (IAS, IPS, clerks, etc.)
2. **Judges**: All judicial officers (Supreme Court, High Courts, subordinate courts)
3. **Ministers and Legislators**: MPs, MLAs, MLCs
4. **Police Officers**: All ranks
5. **Military Personnel**: Army, Navy, Air Force officers and personnel
6. **Public Corporation Employees**: Employees of government companies, public sector undertakings
7. **University/Educational Institution Employees**: Teachers, administrative staff in government-funded institutions
8. **Local Authority Members**: Municipal corporators, panchayat members, employees
9. **Court Receivers, Commissioners**: Appointed by courts
10. **Arbitrators**: Appointed to decide disputes
11. **Persons Performing Public Duty**: Any person authorized by government to perform public functions

**Key Principle**: The definition is **very broad** to capture all persons exercising public functions or entrusted with public duties.

**Example 1 (Public Servant)**: A clerk in a government office processing license applications.

**Example 2 (Public Servant)**: A teacher in a government school.

**Example 3 (Not Public Servant)**: An employee of a private company (unless company is performing public functions under government contract).

## Bribery Offences (Sections 7-11)

### Section 7 PCA: Public Servant Taking Gratification Other Than Legal Remuneration

#### Definition

Whoever, being a **public servant**, accepts or obtains or agrees to accept or attempts to obtain **any gratification** (other than legal remuneration) as a **motive or reward** for doing or forbearing to do any official act, commits the offence of **taking undue advantage**.

#### Essential Ingredients

1. **Accused is a Public Servant**
   - Must fall within the definition of public servant

2. **Accepts or Obtains Gratification**
   - **Gratification**: Money, valuable thing, or any other benefit (including sexual favors, property, job for relative, etc.)
   - **Accepts**: Actually receives
   - **Obtains**: Successfully procures
   - **Agrees to accept**: Enters into agreement to receive (offence complete even if not yet received)
   - **Attempts to obtain**: Tries to get (offence complete even if attempt fails)

3. **Other Than Legal Remuneration**
   - Not salary, allowances, or lawful fees
   - Any extra-legal payment or benefit

4. **As Motive or Reward**
   - **Motive** (bribe before act): To induce public servant to do or forbear from doing act
   - **Reward** (bribe after act): As reward for having done or forborne from doing act

5. **For Doing or Forbearing Official Act**
   - Official act: Act within public servant's duties
   - Forbearing: Refraining from doing something he is duty-bound to do

**Examples**:

**Example 1 (Bribe Before Act - Motive)**: A applicant gives Rs. 10,000 to B (a licensing officer) to approve A's license application (which may or may not be eligible). B accepts. Offence complete under Section 7.

**Example 2 (Bribe After Act - Reward)**: B (a police officer) releases A from custody without proper procedure. A gives Rs. 5,000 to B as reward. Offence complete.

**Example 3 (Agreement to Accept)**: B (a public servant) agrees to accept Rs. 50,000 from A to give contract to A's company. Even before money is handed over, offence is complete (agreement itself is punishable).

**Important Principle**: The official act need **not be illegal**. Even if the public servant was duty-bound to do the act anyway, accepting gratification for doing it is corruption.

**Example**: A is entitled to a ration card. B (ration office clerk) is duty-bound to issue it. A gives Rs. 500 to B to "speed up" the process. B accepts. Even though issuing ration card is B's duty and A is entitled to it, B commits offence by accepting gratification.

#### Punishment (Section 17)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment from **6 months to 5 years** AND fine

**Nature**: Cognizable (police can arrest without warrant), Non-bailable (bail requires court approval)

### Section 8 PCA: Taking Gratification to Influence Public Servant

#### Definition

Whoever accepts or obtains or agrees to accept or attempts to obtain any gratification as a **motive or reward** for **inducing any public servant** (by corrupt or illegal means or by exercise of personal influence) to do or forbear from doing any official act, commits an offence.

#### Difference from Section 7

- **Section 7**: Public servant himself takes bribe for doing/forbearing official act (direct bribery)
- **Section 8**: Person (not necessarily public servant) takes gratification to **influence another public servant** (intermediary/tout)

**Essential Ingredients**:
1. Accepts/obtains/agrees to accept gratification
2. To induce another public servant (by corrupt means or personal influence)
3. To do or forbear official act

**Example**: A (a tout/middleman, not a public servant) takes Rs. 50,000 from C promising to use his influence to get C's contract approved by D (a public servant). A commits offence under Section 8, even if A has no real influence and even if D never receives bribe.

#### Punishment (Section 17)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment from **6 months to 5 years** AND fine

### Section 9 PCA: Taking Gratification for Exercise of Personal Influence

Similar to Section 8, but focuses on taking gratification to exercise **personal influence** (not corrupt means) with public servant.

**Example**: A claims to be a friend of Minister M and takes Rs. 1 lakh from B promising to use personal influence to get B's project approved. Offence under Section 9.

#### Punishment (Section 17)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment from **6 months to 5 years** AND fine

### Section 10 PCA: Abetment by Public Servant

If a **public servant** abets (assists, encourages, or conspires in) an offence under Section 8 or 9 (taking gratification to influence public servant), the public servant is also liable.

**Example**: Public servant P suggests to A (a tout) that A can collect money from people promising P will help. P and A share the proceeds. P is liable under Section 10.

#### Punishment (Section 17)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment from **6 months to 5 years** AND fine

### Section 11 PCA: Public Servant Obtaining Undue Advantage Without Consideration

#### Definition

If a public servant obtains for himself or any other person any **valuable thing or pecuniary advantage** without any **public interest** or **without consideration** (payment) or for **inadequate consideration**, knowing that he is not entitled to it, he commits offence.

**Essential Ingredients**:
1. Public servant obtains valuable thing or benefit
2. Without public interest OR without consideration OR inadequate consideration
3. Knowing he is not entitled

**Example**: A (a government officer) awards a contract to B's company (owned by A's relative) at inflated rates without competitive bidding. A receives kickbacks. Offence under Section 11.

#### Punishment (Section 17)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment from **6 months to 5 years** AND fine

## Criminal Misconduct (Section 13)

### Section 13(1) PCA: Criminal Misconduct by Public Servant

A public servant is said to commit **criminal misconduct** if he:

#### (a) Dishonest or Fraudulent Misappropriation of Property

Habitually accepts or obtains or agrees to accept or attempts to obtain **any valuable thing** without consideration or for inadequate consideration from any person with whom he has official dealings, or who has business with government.

**Example**: A customs officer routinely accepts "gifts" from importers whose consignments he clears.

#### (b) Dishonest or Fraudulent Misappropriation of Government Property

Dishonestly or fraudulently **misappropriates** or **converts to own use** any property entrusted to him or under his control as public servant.

**Example**: A government clerk in charge of relief funds diverts funds to his personal account.

#### (c) Accumulation of Resources Disproportionate to Known Sources

By **corrupt or illegal means** or by abusing his position as public servant, obtains for himself or any other person any **valuable thing or pecuniary advantage**.

**Example**: A police officer demands and receives protection money from illegal businesses using his official position.

#### (d) Possessing Assets Disproportionate to Known Sources of Income

While holding office as public servant, possesses **pecuniary resources or property disproportionate** to his **known sources of income**, which he **cannot satisfactorily account for**.

**This is the "Disproportionate Assets" provision** - most powerful anti-corruption tool.

**Essential Ingredients**:
1. Public servant holds office (during or after service)
2. Possesses assets (movable/immovable property, cash, investments, etc.)
3. Assets are disproportionate to known sources of income (salary, ancestral property, lawful business, etc.)
4. Public servant cannot satisfactorily account for excess

**Example**: A government clerk with monthly salary of Rs. 30,000 owns 5 houses worth Rs. 2 crore, luxury cars, and Rs. 50 lakh in bank deposits. He has no known source of income other than salary. He commits criminal misconduct under Section 13(1)(d) (previously Section 13(1)(e) before 2018 amendment).

**Burden of Proof**: Once prosecution proves disproportion (usually threshold: 10-20% excess), **burden shifts to accused** to satisfactorily explain source of assets. If accused cannot explain, conviction follows.

**How Disproportion is Calculated**:
1. **Check Period**: Usually the period the accused held office (from appointment to retirement or investigation date)
2. **Assets at Beginning**: Value of assets at start of check period
3. **Assets at End**: Value of assets at end of check period
4. **Income**: Total known income during check period (salary, allowances, lawful business, inheritance, gifts received lawfully, etc.)
5. **Expenditure**: Estimated living expenses during check period
6. **Disproportion Formula**:
   - (Assets at End - Assets at Beginning) - (Income - Expenditure) = Excess
   - If Excess > certain percentage of income (often 10-20%), disproportion established

**Example Calculation**:
- Check Period: 2010-2020 (10 years)
- Assets at Start (2010): Rs. 10 lakh
- Assets at End (2020): Rs. 5 crore
- Total Income (salary + lawful sources): Rs. 50 lakh
- Estimated Expenditure: Rs. 30 lakh
- Expected Assets at End: Rs. 10 lakh + (Rs. 50 lakh - Rs. 30 lakh) = Rs. 30 lakh
- Actual Assets: Rs. 5 crore
- Excess: Rs. 5 crore - Rs. 30 lakh = Rs. 4.7 crore (disproportionate)
- Disproportion %: (Rs. 4.7 crore / Rs. 50 lakh) × 100 = 940% (far exceeds threshold)

**Burden**: Accused must explain source of Rs. 4.7 crore. If he cannot, conviction under Section 13.

### Punishment for Criminal Misconduct (Section 13)

**Previous Law (before 2018 amendment)**:
- Minimum: 1 year
- Maximum: 7 years OR fine OR both

**Current Law (after Prevention of Corruption (Amendment) Act, 2018)**:
- Minimum: **3 years**
- Maximum: **7 years** AND fine

**Additional Consequences**:
- **Forfeiture of Property**: Court may order forfeiture of disproportionate assets to government
- **Dismissal from Service**: Administrative penalty
- **Pension Forfeiture**: Loss of pension benefits
- **Disqualification**: Disqualification from holding public office

## Liability of Bribe Giver

### Section 7 PCA (Giver's Liability)

While Section 7 primarily targets public servants **taking** bribe, the person **offering or giving** the bribe is also liable:
- **Under Section 12 IPC**: Abetment of offence under Section 7 PCA
- **As principal offender** if he actively offers or gives gratification

**Exception**: If bribe was given under **coercion or threat** by the public servant, the giver may have a defense.

**Example**: Public servant P demands Rs. 10,000 from A for issuing A's passport (to which A is legally entitled). A reluctantly pays under duress. A may have defense of coercion, though courts assess this case-by-case.

**Practical Reality**: Historically, bribe givers were rarely prosecuted. The 2018 Amendment introduced Section 17A requiring prior sanction for prosecution of public servants, making enforcement more complex.

## Protection for Complainants and Witnesses

### Whistleblower Protection

**Whistleblowers Protection Act, 2014** (though not yet fully implemented):
- Protects persons making complaints of corruption against victimization
- Conceals identity of complainant
- Punishes disclosure of complainant's identity

**Section 21 PCA**: Complainant's identity protection in certain cases.

### Section 24 PCA: Statement Made by Bribe Giver

Notwithstanding anything in IEA, a **statement made by a person** in any proceedings against a public servant for offence under PCA, or for conspiracy or abetment, that he offered or agreed to offer **any gratification**, shall **not subject him to prosecution**.

**Rationale**: Encourages bribe givers to testify truthfully without fear of self-incrimination.

**Practical Application**: In sting operations or voluntary disclosures, bribe giver's testimony is crucial. This provision protects them from prosecution if they cooperate.

## Investigation and Prosecution

### Sanction for Prosecution (Section 19)

**No court can take cognizance** of offence under PCA alleged to have been committed by a **public servant** except with **previous sanction**:
- **Central Government employees**: Sanction from Central Government or authorized officer
- **State Government employees**: Sanction from State Government or authorized officer

**Rationale**: Protects public servants from frivolous or politically motivated prosecutions.

**Criticism**: Often used to shield corrupt officials; sanction may be denied on flimsy grounds.

**2018 Amendment (Section 17A)**: Requires prior sanction even for **investigation** of certain offences (not just prosecution), further strengthening protection for public servants (controversial provision).

**Exceptions**:
- Sanction not required if public servant is **caught red-handed** accepting bribe
- Sanction not required for offences committed after retirement (in some interpretations)

### Special Courts (Section 3 PCA)

State governments shall establish **Special Courts** for trial of offences under PCA.

**Benefits**:
- Faster trials (dedicated judges)
- Expertise in anti-corruption cases
- Priority over other cases

### Time Limit for Completion of Trial

**Section 4A PCA (added by 2018 Amendment)**:
- Trial should be completed within **2 years** from date of filing charge sheet
- If not completed, court must record reasons
- May be extended for further periods not exceeding 6 months each with reasons

**Rationale**: Prevent indefinite delays which favor the accused and cause evidence deterioration.

## Landmark Cases on Corruption

### 1. **N. Sivakumar v. State of Tamil Nadu** (2004) - Disproportionate Assets Calculation
**AIR 2004 SC 2951**

**Held**: For proving disproportionate assets, the prosecution must:
1. Identify **check period** (period of public service under investigation)
2. Calculate **assets at beginning and end** of check period
3. Calculate **known sources of income** during check period
4. Calculate **estimated expenditure** (living expenses)
5. Show **disproportion** between assets acquired and income

**Burden shifts to accused** to explain source of disproportionate assets.

**Principle**: Systematic method for calculating disproportion; burden of proof shifts once prosecution establishes prima facie case.

### 2. **C.M. Sharma v. State of A.P.** (2005) - Sanction for Prosecution
**AIR 2005 SC 3578**

**Held**: **Sanction for prosecution** under Section 19 PCA is mandatory. Without valid sanction, trial is void.

**Sanction must be valid**:
- Granted by competent authority
- After application of mind to facts
- Specifying the offence for which sanction is granted

**Invalid sanction**: Mechanical sanction (rubber-stamp approval) without examining evidence is invalid.

**Principle**: Sanction is a safeguard against frivolous prosecution, but must be genuine, not perfunctory.

### 3. **P.V. Narasimha Rao v. State** (1998) - Trap and Bribery
**AIR 1998 SC 2120**

**Facts**: Former Prime Minister P.V. Narasimha Rao was alleged to have bribed MPs to vote in favor of government in no-confidence motion.

**Held**: **Trap evidence** (where anti-corruption agency sets up a sting to catch public servant red-handed) is admissible and carries high evidentiary value.

**Chemical analysis** of currency notes (detecting chemical powder used in traps) and **witness testimony** (trap witnesses) are crucial evidence.

**Principle**: Trap operations (stings) are lawful investigative tools in corruption cases.

### 4. **Subramanian Swamy v. CBI** (2014) - 2G Spectrum Case
**Writ Petition (Criminal) 100/2011**

**Context**: Corruption in allocation of 2G spectrum licenses causing loss of thousands of crores to exchequer.

**Held**: Courts can intervene to **direct CBI investigation** in cases of gross corruption involving massive public loss, even if sanction for prosecution is initially denied.

**Principle**: Public interest in prosecuting large-scale corruption may override bureaucratic reluctance to grant sanction.

**Impact**: Led to prosecution of ministers, bureaucrats, and corporate executives in one of India's largest corruption scandals.

### 5. **State of Madhya Pradesh v. Ram Singh** (2000) - Presumption of Corruption
**AIR 2000 SC 2613**

**Held**: Under Section 20 PCA, when a public servant is proved to have **accepted gratification**, the court shall **presume** that he accepted it as a **motive or reward** for doing or forbearing from doing official act, **unless the contrary is proved**.

**Burden shifts to accused** to prove the gratification was not a bribe (e.g., gift from relative, lawful payment).

**Principle**: Presumption under Section 20 eases prosecution's burden; accused must rebut presumption.

### 6. **Vineet Narain v. Union of India** (1998) - CBI Independence (Hawala Case)
**AIR 1998 SC 889**

**Context**: Hawala scandal involving politicians receiving illegal payments.

**Held**: CBI must have **operational autonomy** in investigating corruption. Government cannot interfere with ongoing investigations for political reasons.

**Directions**: CBI Director's tenure protected; certain offences require CBI investigation without government interference.

**Principle**: Independence of anti-corruption agencies is essential for effective prosecution.

## Preventive Measures Against Corruption

### Statutory Declarations

**Income Tax Returns**: Public servants must file annual IT returns (mandatory).

**Property Returns**: Many categories of public servants must file annual property returns with department.

**Gifts**: Public servants must declare gifts received; gifts above certain value must be deposited with government.

### Whistleblower Protections

**Whistleblowers Protection Act, 2014**: Protects complainants reporting corruption (though full implementation pending).

### Right to Information (RTI) Act, 2005

Citizens can seek information about government functioning, exposing corruption and delays.

### Lokpal and Lokayuktas Act, 2013

**Lokpal** (at Central level) and **Lokayuktas** (at State level): Ombudsmen to investigate corruption allegations against public servants, including Prime Minister and Ministers.

**Lokpal Act**: Provides for a high-level anti-corruption authority with power to investigate and prosecute.

### Declaration of Assets

**Section 44 of Lokpal Act**: Public servants must declare assets publicly (online publication).

## IPC vs. PCA Provisions

| **Aspect** | **IPC Sections 161-165** | **PCA 1988** |
|------------|---------------------------|---------------|
| **Applicability** | General law | Special law (overrides IPC by virtue of Section 26 PCA) |
| **Punishment** | Lower (up to 3 years) | Higher (6 months to 7 years) |
| **Presumption** | No statutory presumption | Section 20: Presumption that gratification was bribe |
| **Disproportionate Assets** | Covered under IPC 165A (now repealed) | Section 13(1)(d) PCA (effective provision) |
| **Trial** | Regular courts | Special Courts (Section 3 PCA) |
| **Sanction** | Not mandatory for all cases | Mandatory (Section 19 PCA) |

**Practical Application**: PCA is the primary law for prosecuting corruption; IPC provisions are rarely used (PCA prevails as special law).

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Bribery in License Approval (Section 7)
**Scenario**: Ajay applies for a trade license. The licensing officer, Ramesh (a public servant), demands Rs. 5,000 to "process the application quickly." Ajay pays the amount. Ramesh approves the license (Ajay was entitled to it anyway).

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Ramesh is a public servant
2. ✅ Accepted gratification (Rs. 5,000) other than legal remuneration
3. ✅ As motive for doing official act (approving license)
4. ✅ Even though Ramesh was duty-bound to approve (Ajay was eligible), accepting gratification is offence

**Offence**: Section 7 PCA (public servant taking gratification)
**Punishment**: 6 months to 5 years imprisonment AND fine

**Ajay's Liability**: Ajay (bribe giver) may be liable for abetment, but if he can prove coercion (Ramesh demanded), he may have defense.

### Example 2: Disproportionate Assets (Section 13(1)(d))
**Scenario**: Suresh is a government clerk with monthly salary of Rs. 40,000 (Rs. 4.8 lakh per year). He served from 2010 to 2023 (13 years). In 2010, he had assets worth Rs. 5 lakh. In 2023, investigation reveals he owns:
- 3 houses worth Rs. 3 crore
- 2 luxury cars worth Rs. 50 lakh
- Bank deposits of Rs. 1 crore
- Gold jewelry worth Rs. 30 lakh
- Total: Rs. 4.8 crore

His total legitimate income over 13 years: Rs. 4.8 lakh × 13 = Rs. 62.4 lakh. Estimated expenditure: Rs. 40 lakh. Expected assets: Rs. 5 lakh + (Rs. 62.4 lakh - Rs. 40 lakh) = Rs. 27.4 lakh.

**Disproportion**: Rs. 4.8 crore actual vs. Rs. 27.4 lakh expected = Rs. 4.52 crore excess (1650% disproportion).

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Suresh is public servant
2. ✅ Possesses assets disproportionate to known income
3. ✅ Cannot satisfactorily account for source

**Burden**: Shifts to Suresh to explain source of Rs. 4.52 crore. If he cannot provide satisfactory explanation (lawful gifts, inheritance, lottery, etc.), conviction.

**Offence**: Criminal Misconduct (Section 13(1)(d) PCA)
**Punishment**: 3-7 years imprisonment AND fine + forfeiture of disproportionate assets

### Example 3: Trap Case (Sting Operation)
**Scenario**: Anti-Corruption Bureau (ACB) receives complaint that traffic police officer Mohan demands Rs. 2,000 from motorists to avoid challans (traffic tickets). ACB sets up trap:
1. Complainant meets Mohan
2. ACB provides marked currency notes (Rs. 2,000) treated with chemical powder
3. Complainant hands notes to Mohan; Mohan accepts
4. ACB team apprehends Mohan immediately
5. Chemical test on Mohan's hands shows presence of powder (proves he handled marked notes)

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Mohan is public servant (police officer)
2. ✅ Caught red-handed accepting gratification (Rs. 2,000)
3. ✅ As reward for not issuing challan (forbearing official act)
4. ✅ Chemical and witness evidence corroborate

**Offence**: Section 7 PCA
**Punishment**: 6 months to 5 years imprisonment AND fine

**Evidentiary Advantages in Trap Cases**:
- Direct evidence (witnesses saw acceptance)
- Chemical evidence (scientific proof)
- Marked currency recovery
- High conviction rate

### Example 4: Middleman Taking Bribe (Section 8)
**Scenario**: Vikram (a private individual, not a public servant) claims to have influence with Government Officer P. Vikram takes Rs. 50,000 from A, promising to get A's contract approved by P. Vikram has no real influence; he just pockets the money. P never receives any bribe.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Vikram accepts gratification (Rs. 50,000)
2. ✅ To influence public servant P (claims to use personal influence)
3. ✅ Offence complete even if Vikram has no real influence and even if P never receives bribe

**Offence**: Section 8 or 9 PCA (taking gratification to influence public servant)
**Punishment**: 6 months to 5 years imprisonment AND fine

**Note**: A (the giver) may also be liable for abetment.

### Example 5: Contract Kickback (Section 11)
**Scenario**: Public servant Ravi awards government construction contract to Company X (owned by Ravi's brother-in-law) at inflated rates without competitive bidding. Contract value: Rs. 10 crore (fair value: Rs. 6 crore). Ravi receives Rs. 50 lakh kickback.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Ravi is public servant
2. ✅ Obtains pecuniary advantage (Rs. 50 lakh) and benefits relative (brother-in-law's company)
3. ✅ Without public interest (no competitive bidding, inflated rates)
4. ✅ Knowing he is not entitled

**Offence**:
- Section 11 PCA (obtaining undue advantage)
- Section 13 PCA (criminal misconduct)

**Punishment**: 6 months to 7 years imprisonment AND fine

**Additional Consequences**: Criminal conspiracy charges, forfeiture of kickback amount, loss of job

## Compliance Checklist

### For Public Servants (Preventive Measures)
- [ ] Refuse all gratifications other than legal remuneration
- [ ] Declare gifts received; deposit gifts above prescribed value with government
- [ ] File annual property returns (if required for your category)
- [ ] File income tax returns regularly
- [ ] Avoid conflicts of interest (awarding contracts to relatives, etc.)
- [ ] Maintain transparency in decision-making
- [ ] Document reasons for official decisions
- [ ] Report corruption by colleagues to authorities

### For Citizens (Dealing with Public Servants)
- [ ] Do not offer bribes; report demands for bribes to anti-corruption authorities
- [ ] Use RTI Act to seek information about processes and timelines
- [ ] File complaints through official channels (departmental complaints, Lokpal, anti-corruption bureau)
- [ ] Preserve evidence of corruption (recordings, receipts, witnesses) where lawful
- [ ] Support whistleblowers and anti-corruption initiatives

### For Organizations (Corporate Compliance)
- [ ] Implement anti-bribery policies (prohibit employees from bribing public officials)
- [ ] Train employees on anti-corruption laws
- [ ] Maintain records of all interactions with government officials
- [ ] Report attempted solicitation of bribes
- [ ] Comply with transparency norms (especially for government contractors)

### For Accused Public Servant
- [ ] Seek immediate legal counsel
- [ ] Cooperate with investigation (within legal rights; avoid self-incrimination)
- [ ] Prepare explanation for assets (if disproportionate assets charge)
- [ ] Gather evidence of lawful sources of income (gifts, inheritance, business)
- [ ] **Bail is difficult** (non-bailable offence; apply to Special Court or High Court)
- [ ] Challenge sanction for prosecution if defective
- [ ] Argue for minimal punishment if conviction likely (first-time offender, family circumstances, return of bribe amount)

## Key Takeaways

1. **Dual Liability**: Both bribe giver and taker are liable (though givers rarely prosecuted in practice).

2. **Broad Definition of Public Servant**: Includes all government employees, judges, legislators, PSU employees, and anyone performing public functions.

3. **Disproportionate Assets Provision**: Powerful tool - public servant must explain source of assets; failure to explain leads to conviction even without proving specific bribe.

4. **Burden of Proof Shifts**: Once prosecution establishes prima facie case (disproportion, acceptance of gratification), burden shifts to accused.

5. **Sanction Required**: Prosecution requires prior government sanction (protection for public servants, but often shields corrupt officials).

6. **Special Courts and Presumptions**: Dedicated courts and statutory presumptions (Section 20) aid prosecution.

7. **Strict Punishment**: Minimum 3 years (after 2018 amendment) for criminal misconduct; forfeiture of assets.

8. **Trap Evidence**: Sting operations lawful and carry high evidentiary value; chemical tests on marked currency notes are conclusive.

---

## Conclusion

Corruption is a cancer that undermines governance, economic development, and social justice. India's anti-corruption framework, centered on the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988, provides stringent provisions targeting public servants who abuse their positions for private gain.

The disproportionate assets provision (Section 13(1)(d)) is particularly powerful, allowing prosecution without proving specific bribery transactions - mere possession of unexplained wealth is sufficient.

However, enforcement challenges persist: sanction requirements, slow trials, lack of convictions, and inadequate protection for whistleblowers. The 2018 amendments introduced reforms (faster trials, higher minimum punishment) but also controversial provisions (sanction for investigation) that may dilute effectiveness.

Ultimately, combating corruption requires not just strong laws but also political will, independent investigation agencies, an empowered judiciary, and a vigilant civil society.
