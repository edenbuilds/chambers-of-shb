## Overview and Purpose

### What is Criminal Investigation?

Criminal investigation is the **systematic process of gathering, preserving, and analyzing evidence** to determine whether a crime has been committed, identify the perpetrator(s), and build a case for prosecution. Under Indian criminal law, investigation is a **police function** governed primarily by the Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC) 1973 and the new Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS) 2023.

### Definition Under Law

**CrPC Section 2(h) / BNSS Section 2(h)**:

"Investigation" includes all the proceedings under this Code for the collection of evidence conducted by a police officer or by any person (other than a Magistrate) who is authorized by a Magistrate in this behalf.

### Purpose of Investigation

1. **Discover Truth**: Ascertain facts and circumstances of alleged crime
2. **Collect Evidence**: Gather admissible evidence for prosecution
3. **Identify Perpetrators**: Determine who committed the offence
4. **Preserve Evidence**: Prevent tampering, destruction, or loss
5. **Prepare Case**: Build strong foundation for trial
6. **Justice**: Enable fair determination of guilt/innocence

## Stages and Process of Investigation

### Seven Key Stages of Criminal Investigation

#### Stage 1: Registration of FIR (Section 154 CrPC / 173 BNSS)

**When Information Received**:
- Officer in charge of police station receives information of cognizable offence
- **Mandatory registration** if information discloses cognizable offence (Lalita Kumari principle)
- FIR is the foundation document

**See Protocol**: IL-CRIM-FIR.md

#### Stage 2: Preliminary Investigation (Section 157 CrPC / 176 BNSS)

**Purpose**: Determine if there are grounds to proceed

**Officer's Options**:
1. **Investigate personally** (if cognizable offence clearly made out)
2. **Direct subordinate to investigate** (depute investigation)
3. **Refuse to investigate** (if non-cognizable or no substance) - must record reasons
4. **Report to Magistrate** (if offence outside local jurisdiction)

**Timeline**: Should be done expeditiously; no fixed timeline in statute

#### Stage 3: Collection of Evidence (Sections 160-165 CrPC / 179-184 BNSS)

**Powers of Investigating Officer**:

##### A. Examination of Witnesses (Section 161 CrPC / 180 BNSS)
- **Power**: Examine orally any person who appears acquainted with facts
- **Bound to Answer**: Person legally bound to answer all questions (except self-incriminating)
- **Not on Oath**: Statements under Section 161 are NOT on oath
- **Recording**: Officer must reduce statement to writing
- **Not Admissible**: Section 161 statements NOT admissible as evidence (but can be used for contradiction under Section 145 Evidence Act)
- **Exception**: Statement to police officer below rank of Sub-Inspector cannot be used for any purpose

##### B. Summoning Attendance (Section 160 CrPC / 179 BNSS)
- **Power**: Require attendance of any person (except women before sunrise or after sunset)
- **Women**: Cannot be required to attend at any place other than where she resides
- **Exemption**: Women, children below 15, infirm persons have protection

##### C. Inspection and Examination (Section 165A CrPC / 184A BNSS)
- **Medical examination** of accused with consent
- **Forensic examination** of scene of crime
- **Expert opinion** requisition

#### Stage 4: Search and Seizure (Sections 100-105, 165 CrPC / 99-104, 184 BNSS)

**Section 165 CrPC / 184 BNSS**: Police officer making investigation may:
1. **Search any place** (with or without warrant depending on circumstances)
2. **Seize any property** suspected to be stolen or relevant to investigation

**Procedural Safeguards**:
- **List of seized articles** must be prepared (Section 100 CrPC / 99 BNSS)
- **Witnesses**: Two independent witnesses (preferably of locality)
- **Female search**: By female officer with strict decency (Section 51 CrPC / 50 BNSS)
- **Inventory**: Complete list to be given to person from whom seized
- **Magistrate's supervision**: Regular reporting required

**Landmark Case**: **State of Punjab v. Baldev Singh (1999)**
- Supreme Court held that police officer can conduct search without warrant if circumstances require immediate action
- But procedural safeguards must be strictly followed

#### Stage 5: Arrest (If Necessary)

**See Protocol**: IL-CRIM-ARREST-CUSTODY.md

**Investigating officer may arrest**:
- If person has committed cognizable offence
- Subject to restrictions under Section 41 CrPC / 41A BNSS
- Must follow D.K. Basu guidelines (handcuffing, arrest memo, medical examination)

#### Stage 6: Forensic Analysis and Expert Opinion

**Types of Forensic Evidence**:
1. **Fingerprints** (Section 53A CrPC / 53A BNSS)
2. **DNA samples** (with consent or Magistrate's order)
3. **Ballistics** (firearms, bullets)
4. **Handwriting analysis**
5. **Digital forensics** (computers, mobiles, servers)
6. **Chemical analysis** (drugs, poisons, blood alcohol)
7. **Post-mortem examination** (Section 174 CrPC / 193 BNSS)

**Expert Opinion Admissible**: Section 45 Evidence Act / BSA 2023

**Landmark Case**: **State of Maharashtra v. Damu (2000)**
- Supreme Court emphasized importance of proper collection and chain of custody
- Forensic evidence must be handled scientifically to be admissible

#### Stage 7: Completion of Investigation and Report

**Section 173 CrPC / 192 BNSS**: Investigation must conclude with report to Magistrate

**Two Types of Final Reports**:

##### A. Charge Sheet (FIR with Evidence)
Filed when sufficient evidence exists to prosecute:
- **Contents**: Details of offence, accused, evidence collected, witnesses
- **Timeline**: Within 60/90 days if accused in custody (Section 167)
- **Witnesses**: List of prosecution witnesses
- **Documents**: All investigation papers

##### B. Final Report (Closure Report)
Filed when no offence made out or insufficient evidence:
- **Grounds**: False complaint, no evidence, civil dispute, accused unknown
- **Magistrate's powers**: Can accept or reject and order further investigation
- **Victim's rights**: Can file protest petition

## Powers of Investigating Officer

### Statutory Powers (Sections 154-176 CrPC / 173-195 BNSS)

#### 1. Power to Record Information (Section 154)
- Duty-bound to record FIR for cognizable offences
- Cannot refuse if cognizable offence disclosed

#### 2. Power to Investigate Without Magistrate's Order (Section 156)
- **Cognizable cases**: Can investigate suo motu
- **Non-cognizable cases**: Need Magistrate's order

#### 3. Power to Examine Witnesses (Section 161)
- Oral examination of any person
- Legal obligation on person to answer
- Statements in writing

#### 4. Power to Summon Persons (Section 160)
- Male witnesses can be summoned to police station
- Women cannot be summoned (must go to their residence)

#### 5. Power to Search (Section 165)
- Search of place where property suspected
- Emergency search without warrant

#### 6. Power to Seize Property (Section 102)
- Anything necessary for investigation
- Stolen property, offensive weapons, forged documents

#### 7. Power to Arrest (Sections 41, 41A)
- Subject to restrictions and conditions
- Procedure must be followed strictly

#### 8. Power to Conduct Inquest (Section 174)
- In case of unnatural death or death in custody
- Panchnama, post-mortem examination

#### 9. Power to Requisition Experts (Section 165A)
- Medical experts, forensic experts
- Expert opinion admissible under Section 45 Evidence Act

### Limitations on Investigating Officer Powers

#### Cannot:
1. **Compel self-incrimination** (Article 20(3))
2. **Torture or use third degree** (D.K. Basu guidelines)
3. **Search without legal authority**
4. **Fabricate evidence** (punishable under IPC 193, 211)
5. **Delay investigation unreasonably** (violation of fair trial)
6. **Investigate judicial acts** (outside scope)
7. **Override constitutional rights**

#### Must:
1. **Act impartially and fairly**
2. **Follow procedural safeguards**
3. **Maintain chain of custody of evidence**
4. **Complete investigation expeditiously**
5. **File report to Magistrate**
6. **Respect human rights**

## Special Types of Investigation

### 1. Inquest Investigation (Section 174 CrPC / 193 BNSS)

**When Required**:
- Unnatural death (suicide, accident, homicide)
- Death in police custody or within 24 hours of police custody
- Sudden death under suspicious circumstances

**Procedure**:
1. **Police officer proceeds to spot**
2. **Prepares inquest report (Panchnama)**
3. **Arranges post-mortem examination**
4. **Section 174A**: Mandatory videography in certain cases (BNSS enhancement)
5. **Sends report to Magistrate**

**Important**: Not the same as formal investigation; preliminary inquiry

### 2. Investigation by Magistrate (Section 156(3) CrPC / 175(3) BNSS)

**When**: Magistrate can order investigation
- If police refuses to investigate
- If complainant approaches Magistrate directly
- Magistrate satisfied cognizable offence disclosed

**Effect**: Directions binding on police to investigate

**Landmark Case**: **Lalita Kumari v. Govt. of U.P. (2014)**
- Magistrate's power under Section 156(3) is wide
- Can be exercised at pre-cognizance stage

### 3. Further Investigation (Section 173(8) CrPC / 192(8) BNSS)

**When**: After filing final report/charge sheet
- New evidence discovered
- Fresh witnesses come forward
- Lacunae in investigation identified

**Procedure**:
- Police can conduct further investigation
- Must file supplementary report
- Can be done even after cognizance taken

**Landmark Case**: **Vinubhai Haribhai Malaviya v. State of Gujarat (2019)**
- Supreme Court held police can conduct further investigation at any stage
- Even after trial commenced (with Court's permission)
- But cannot be used to fill lacunae to help prosecution

### 4. Re-investigation

**Difference from Further Investigation**:
- **Re-investigation**: Fresh investigation from scratch
- **Further investigation**: Continuation of original investigation

**Power**: Generally, no statutory power for re-investigation
- But Courts can order in exceptional circumstances
- CBI/Special Investigating Teams can be appointed

### 5. Parallel Investigation

**Generally Prohibited**: Two agencies cannot investigate same offence simultaneously

**Exception**:
- Different aspects of same crime (e.g., CBI for corruption + ED for money laundering)
- Special statutes allow (PMLA, NDPS, etc.)

## Investigation Timeline and Deadlines

### Constitutional Mandate: "Speedy Investigation"

**Article 21**: Right to speedy trial includes right to speedy investigation

### Statutory Timelines

#### 1. Remand Period (Section 167 CrPC / 187 BNSS)

**If Accused Arrested and Detained**:

| Type of Offence | Investigation Must Complete Within |
|----------------|-----------------------------------|
| Punishable with death, life imprisonment, or ≥10 years | **90 days** |
| Other offences | **60 days** |

**Consequence of Delay**: Accused entitled to **default bail** (indefeasible right)

**Landmark Case**: **Sanjay Dutt v. State (1994)**
- Held that default bail is an indefeasible right
- Once period expires, accused must be released (unless charge sheet filed)

#### 2. General Investigation (No Arrest)

**No Statutory Deadline**: If accused not arrested, no fixed timeline
- But must be completed within reasonable time
- Unreasonable delay can be challenged

#### 3. Supreme Court Guidelines

**In Re: Expeditious Trial of Cases**: Supreme Court recommended:
- Investigation should complete within **6 months**
- Charge sheet should be filed within **60 days** of arrest

## Duties and Obligations of Investigating Officer

### Primary Duties (Section 157 CrPC / 176 BNSS)

1. **Proceed to Spot**: Visit scene of crime without delay
2. **Ascertain Facts**: Determine what offence committed, by whom
3. **Collect Evidence**: Gather all relevant evidence
4. **Arrest Accused**: If necessary and lawful
5. **Report to Magistrate**: File charge sheet or final report
6. **Maintain Diary**: Daily diary of investigation (Section 172)

### Case Diary (Section 172 CrPC / 191 BNSS)

**What is Case Diary?**
- **Daily record** of investigation proceedings
- **Contents**: Day-to-day progress, statements, evidence collected, observations
- **Not Evidence**: Case diary NOT admissible as evidence
- **Use**: Court can use to refresh memory of witness or to contradict investigating officer

**Landmark Case**: **State of Punjab v. Sucha Singh (2001)**
- Case diary is confidential document
- Cannot be supplied to accused or complainant
- Court can use for limited purposes only

### Duty to Act Fairly and Impartially

**Supreme Court Guidelines**:
1. **No bias**: Must investigate with open mind
2. **Exculpatory evidence**: Must collect evidence favorable to accused also
3. **No fabrication**: Planting evidence is serious misconduct
4. **No torture**: Third degree methods prohibited
5. **Follow procedure**: Strict adherence to procedural law

**Landmark Case**: **Kartar Singh v. State of Punjab (1994)**
- Investigating officer is a judicial officer during investigation
- Must act fairly, impartially, and in accordance with law
- Any violation vitiates investigation

## Rights of Accused During Investigation

### Constitutional Rights

#### 1. Right Against Self-Incrimination (Article 20(3))
- Cannot be compelled to be witness against himself
- Protection against testimonial compulsion
- Does not cover physical evidence (fingerprints, blood samples with consent)

**Landmark Case**: **Nandini Satpathy v. P.L. Dani (1978)**
- Right begins when accused is interrogated
- Extends to police interrogation
- Silence cannot be used as evidence of guilt

#### 2. Right to Consult Lawyer (Article 22(1))
- Right to consult legal practitioner of choice
- Right to be defended by lawyer

**During Investigation**: Some debate, but generally:
- No absolute right to have lawyer present during interrogation
- But can consult lawyer for legal advice

#### 3. Right to Fair Investigation (Article 21)
- Investigation must be fair, impartial
- No malafides, no mala fide delay
- Evidence must be collected scientifically

### Statutory Rights

#### 1. Right to Copy of FIR (Section 154 CrPC / 173 BNSS)
- Free copy of FIR to informant
- Accused can also obtain copy

#### 2. Right to Bail
- Anticipatory bail (Section 438 CrPC / 482 BNSS)
- Regular bail (Section 437 CrPC / 483 BNSS)
- Default bail (Section 167 CrPC / 187 BNSS)

**See Protocol**: IL-CRIM-BAIL.md

#### 3. Right to Medical Examination (Section 54 CrPC / 53 BNSS)
- Arrested person can request medical examination
- To record injuries, if any

#### 4. Right to Be Informed of Grounds of Arrest (Section 50 CrPC / 49 BNSS)
- Police must inform grounds of arrest
- Right to be produced before Magistrate within 24 hours

#### 5. Right Against Illegal Detention
- Cannot be detained beyond 24 hours without Magistrate's remand
- Habeas corpus petition if illegally detained

## Judicial Oversight and Safeguards

### Magistrate's Role in Investigation

#### 1. Pre-Investigation Stage
- **Section 156(3)**: Can order police to investigate
- **Lalita Kumari principle**: Monitoring mandatory FIR registration

#### 2. During Investigation
- **Remand**: Police custody remand (max 15 days in stages)
- **Bail applications**: Hear and decide
- **Anticipatory bail**: Pre-arrest bail orders

#### 3. Post-Investigation
- **Cognizance**: Take cognizance of offence based on charge sheet
- **Discharge/frame charges**: If insufficient evidence, discharge accused
- **Protest petitions**: Hear victim's objections to closure report

### Remedies Against Faulty Investigation

#### 1. Writ Petition (Article 226 / 32)
- **High Court / Supreme Court**
- Grounds: Violation of constitutional rights, mala fides, illegal detention

#### 2. Complaint to Senior Officers
- SP/DIG/Commissioner
- Internal departmental action

#### 3. Application to Magistrate
- **Section 156(3)**: For fresh investigation
- **Section 173(8)**: For further investigation

#### 4. Private Complaint (Section 200 CrPC / 223 BNSS)
- Bypass police investigation
- Directly file complaint before Magistrate
- Magistrate can take cognizance

#### 5. Transfer of Investigation
- Court can transfer to different agency (CBI, State CID)
- **Landmark Case**: **Rubabbuddin Sheikh v. State of Gujarat (2010)**
  - Supreme Court can transfer investigation if state police lacks credibility

## Special Investigation Agencies

### 1. Central Bureau of Investigation (CBI)

**Establishment**: DSPE Act 1946 (statutory basis)

**Jurisdiction**:
- **Exclusive**: Central government employees, Union Territories
- **Concurrent**: State consent required (except UTs)
- **Special cases**: Court-ordered investigations

**Powers**: Same as State Police under CrPC

### 2. National Investigation Agency (NIA)

**Statute**: NIA Act 2008

**Jurisdiction**:
- Terrorism, bomb blasts, hijacking
- Counterfeit currency, narco-terrorism
- Cyber-terrorism

**Special Powers**: Pan-India jurisdiction without state consent

### 3. Enforcement Directorate (ED)

**Statute**: PMLA 2002, FEMA 1999

**Jurisdiction**:
- Money laundering
- Foreign exchange violations

**Powers**: Search, seizure, arrest, attachment of property

### 4. Narcotics Control Bureau (NCB)

**Statute**: NDPS Act 1985

**Jurisdiction**: Drug trafficking, narcotics offences

**Powers**: Same as police officer under NDPS Act

### 5. State CID / Special Investigation Teams

**State Level**: Serious crimes, sensitive cases
**Court Appointment**: SIT for impartial investigation

## Investigation of Specific Offences

### 1. Murder and Serious Crimes

**Procedure**:
- Inquest under Section 174 CrPC
- Post-mortem mandatory
- Scene of crime preservation
- Forensic evidence critical
- Swift arrest often necessary

### 2. Sexual Offences

**Special Provisions**:
- **Section 164A CrPC / 183A BNSS**: Statement of rape victim before Magistrate
- **Medical examination**: Within 24 hours (Section 53A, 164A)
- **Privacy**: In-camera proceedings
- **Timeline**: Investigation must complete within 2 months (POCSO Act)

### 3. Economic Offences

**Characteristics**:
- Complex, document-heavy
- Forensic audit required
- CA/expert assistance
- Multi-jurisdictional

**Agencies**: CBI, ED, SFIO (corporate frauds)

### 4. Cyber Crimes

**Statute**: IT Act 2000

**Special Investigation**:
- **Digital forensics**: Preservation of electronic evidence
- **Section 65B Evidence Act**: Certificate for electronic records
- **Cyber forensic labs**: Specialized expertise
- **International cooperation**: Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties (MLAT)

**Landmark Case**: **Anvar P.V. v. P.K. Basheer (2014)**
- Electronic evidence must comply with Section 65B
- Certificate from person in control of device mandatory

### 5. Corruption Cases

**Statute**: Prevention of Corruption Act 1988

**Special Requirements**:
- **Trap**: Catching in act (phenolphthalein test)
- **Sanction**: Prior sanction for prosecution (Section 19 PCA)
- **Assets verification**: Disproportionate assets inquiry

## Evidence Collection and Preservation

### Chain of Custody

**Definition**: Documented process showing seizure, custody, control, transfer, analysis, and disposition of evidence

**Importance**: Ensures evidence is not tampered with

**Steps**:
1. **Seizure**: Proper panchnama, seizure memo
2. **Sealing**: Tamper-proof sealing
3. **Storage**: Secure malkhana (police storeroom)
4. **Transfer**: Documented handover (to FSL, court)
5. **Analysis**: Forensic examination
6. **Court production**: With seal intact

**Landmark Case**: **State of H.P. v. Jai Lal (1999)**
- Supreme Court held that break in chain of custody can vitiate evidence
- Prosecution must prove unbroken chain

### Types of Evidence

#### 1. Physical Evidence
- Weapons, clothes, blood-stained articles
- Proper sealing and labeling mandatory

#### 2. Documentary Evidence
- Original documents preferred
- Certified copies if originals not available

#### 3. Electronic Evidence
- **Section 65B Evidence Act**: Certificate required
- **Hash value verification**: To prove integrity
- **Clone imaging**: Forensic copies

#### 4. Expert Evidence
- Forensic reports, medical reports
- Expert opinion admissible under Section 45 Evidence Act

#### 5. Testimonial Evidence
- Witness statements (Section 161 not admissible, but can contradict)
- Dying declaration (highly reliable)

### Scientific Evidence

**DNA Profiling**:
- Highly reliable (99.9% accuracy)
- Proper collection and analysis critical
- **Landmark Case**: **Santosh Kumar Singh v. State (2010)** - DNA evidence held admissible and reliable

**Fingerprints**:
- NCRB database (NAFIS - National Automated Fingerprint Identification System)
- Expert comparison required

**Ballistics**:
- Firearms, bullets, cartridges
- Comparison with test fires

## Common Investigation Defects and Irregularities

### Fatal Defects (Can Vitiate Prosecution)

1. **Non-registration of FIR**: Mandatory registration violated
2. **Violation of arrest procedure**: D.K. Basu guidelines not followed
3. **Illegal custody**: Beyond 24 hours without remand
4. **Fabricated evidence**: Planting of evidence
5. **Torture/coercion**: Third degree methods
6. **Breach of chain of custody**: Evidence integrity compromised
7. **Violation of Section 164A**: Rape victim's statement not recorded before Magistrate

### Curable Defects (Can Be Overlooked if Not Prejudicial)

1. **Minor procedural lapses**: If substantial compliance exists
2. **Delay in sending evidence**: If explained and no tampering
3. **Absence of independent witnesses**: If not fatal to prosecution
4. **Non-examination of all witnesses**: If material witnesses examined

**Principle**: **Substantial compliance** vs **technical violation**

**Landmark Case**: **State of U.P. v. Krishna Gopal (1988)**
- Minor irregularities do not vitiate investigation if substantial justice done
- Court looks at overall fairness

## IPC to BNS and CrPC to BNSS Transition

### Investigation Procedure: CrPC 1973 vs BNSS 2023

| Aspect | CrPC 1973 | BNSS 2023 | Change |
|--------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Definition of Investigation** | Section 2(h) | Section 2(h) | Same definition |
| **FIR Registration** | Section 154 | Section 173 | Section number changed; Lalita Kumari principle continues |
| **Investigation powers** | Sections 154-176 | Sections 173-195 | Section numbers changed; powers remain similar |
| **Videography of search** | Not mandatory | Section 176(3) - mandatory in certain cases | **Enhancement** |
| **Digital evidence** | Limited provisions | Enhanced provisions for electronic records | **Enhancement** |
| **Forensic investigation** | Section 165A (added 2013) | Section 184A | Continues with emphasis |
| **Case diary** | Section 172 | Section 191 | Same principle |
| **Investigation completion** | Section 173 | Section 192 | Same timeline (60/90 days) |

### Key BNSS Enhancements

1. **Mandatory Videography** (Section 176(3)):
   - Search and seizure in certain cases must be videographed
   - Ensures transparency

2. **Electronic Communication**:
   - Summons can be served electronically
   - Statements can be recorded electronically (with safeguards)

3. **Forensic Examination**:
   - Mandatory forensic examination for offences punishable with 7+ years (Section 184A)

4. **Timeline**: Same 60/90 day rule continues

## Landmark Supreme Court Cases

### 1. **Lalita Kumari v. Government of U.P. (2014)**

**Issue**: Is FIR registration mandatory?

**Held**:
- **FIR registration is MANDATORY** if information discloses cognizable offence
- No preliminary inquiry permitted (except in matrimonial, medical negligence, corruption cases)
- Police cannot refuse to register FIR

**Impact**: Transformed investigation landscape; ended police discretion to refuse FIR

### 2. **D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal (1997)**

**Issue**: Custodial torture and deaths

**Guidelines**:
1. Arrest memo with names of witnesses
2. Information to family/friend
3. Right to inform family
4. Medical examination at time of arrest and release
5. Inspection of lock-ups
6. Magistrate to record statement if requested
7. Legal aid
8. Police diary to show arrests

**Impact**: Fundamental safeguards against custodial abuse

### 3. **Vinubhai Haribhai Malaviya v. State of Gujarat (2019)**

**Issue**: Can police conduct further investigation after filing charge sheet?

**Held**:
- **Yes**, police can conduct further investigation at any stage (Section 173(8))
- Even after cognizance taken and trial commenced (with Court's permission)
- But cannot be used to fill lacunae to help prosecution

**Impact**: Clarified scope of further investigation

### 4. **State of Maharashtra v. Damu (2000)**

**Issue**: Scientific investigation and expert evidence

**Held**:
- Courts should insist on scientific evidence
- Forensic analysis should be conducted properly
- Chain of custody must be maintained

**Impact**: Emphasized scientific temper in investigation

### 5. **Kartar Singh v. State of Punjab (1994)**

**Issue**: Role of investigating officer

**Held**:
- Investigating officer is a judicial officer during investigation
- Must act fairly, impartially, without bias
- Must collect exculpatory evidence also (favorable to accused)

**Impact**: Established duty of fairness in investigation

### 6. **Zahira Habibulla Sheikh v. State of Gujarat (2006)** (Best Bakery Case)

**Issue**: Fair investigation and trial

**Held**:
- Investigation must be fair, thorough, and impartial
- Witnesses must be protected
- Court can order re-investigation if investigation is tainted

**Impact**: Witness protection, fair investigation imperative

### 7. **Anvar P.V. v. P.K. Basheer (2014)**

**Issue**: Electronic evidence admissibility

**Held**:
- Electronic evidence must comply with Section 65B Evidence Act
- Certificate from person in control of device mandatory
- Without certificate, not admissible

**Impact**: Stringent requirements for digital evidence

## Practical Compliance Checklist

### For Investigating Officers

#### At FIR Stage
- [ ] Register FIR immediately if cognizable offence disclosed
- [ ] Provide free copy to informant
- [ ] Enter in daily diary
- [ ] Send report to superior officer

#### During Investigation
- [ ] Visit scene of crime without delay
- [ ] Preserve scene, collect physical evidence
- [ ] Prepare detailed panchnama
- [ ] Take photographs/videos
- [ ] Collect forensic evidence (fingerprints, DNA, etc.)
- [ ] Examine witnesses under Section 161
- [ ] Record statements accurately
- [ ] Maintain case diary daily
- [ ] If arrest required, follow D.K. Basu guidelines
- [ ] Medical examination of arrested person
- [ ] Produce before Magistrate within 24 hours
- [ ] If search required, follow Section 100 procedure (independent witnesses)
- [ ] Seize evidence with proper sealing and labeling
- [ ] Maintain chain of custody
- [ ] Send evidence to FSL for analysis
- [ ] Complete investigation within 60/90 days if accused in custody

#### At Completion Stage
- [ ] File charge sheet if sufficient evidence
- [ ] File final report if no offence made out
- [ ] List all witnesses
- [ ] Submit all case property
- [ ] Forward case diary to Magistrate

### For Accused/Suspects

#### Know Your Rights
- [ ] Right against self-incrimination - can remain silent
- [ ] Right to consult lawyer
- [ ] Right to medical examination
- [ ] Right to be informed of grounds of arrest
- [ ] Right to be produced before Magistrate within 24 hours
- [ ] Right to bail (anticipatory/regular/default)
- [ ] Right to fair investigation

#### During Investigation
- [ ] Cooperate but don't incriminate yourself
- [ ] Exercise right to silence if needed
- [ ] Request medical examination if arrested
- [ ] Inform family/lawyer immediately
- [ ] Apply for anticipatory bail if apprehension of arrest
- [ ] Challenge illegal detention through habeas corpus
- [ ] Obtain copy of FIR

### For Victims/Complainants

#### During Investigation
- [ ] Ensure FIR registered (approach Magistrate under 156(3) if refused)
- [ ] Provide all evidence to investigating officer
- [ ] Cooperate in investigation
- [ ] Identify accused if known
- [ ] Preserve any evidence in your possession
- [ ] Follow up regularly on investigation progress

#### If Closure Report Filed
- [ ] File protest petition before Magistrate
- [ ] Provide additional evidence if available
- [ ] Request further investigation
- [ ] Consider private complaint under Section 200 CrPC

   - DNA profiling
   - Fingerprint analysis (NAFIS)
   - Ballistics
   - Chemical analysis
   - Autopsy/post-mortem

5. **Witness Protection**:
   - Protection of identity
   - Security arrangements
   - Relocation if necessary

### Documentation

1. **Mahazar/Panchnama**: Detailed, clear, signed by independent witnesses
2. **Case Diary**: Daily entries, comprehensive
3. **Photographs**: Scene of crime, evidence, injuries
4. **Sketch**: Crime scene layout
5. **Statements**: Accurate recording under Section 161

### Quality Control

1. **Supervision**: Senior officers to monitor
2. **Peer review**: Investigation scrutiny by colleagues
3. **Magistrate oversight**: Regular reporting
4. **Timeline adherence**: Complete within statutory period
5. **Legal advice**: Consult legal cell/public prosecutor

## Conclusion

Criminal investigation is the **backbone of the criminal justice system**. A fair, thorough, and scientific investigation is essential for:
- **Conviction of guilty**: Strong evidence ensures successful prosecution
- **Acquittal of innocent**: Exculpatory evidence protects the innocent
- **Public confidence**: Transparency and fairness build trust
- **Deterrence**: Swift and effective investigation deters crime

**Key Principles**:
1. **Fairness**: Impartial, unbiased investigation
2. **Legality**: Strict adherence to procedure
3. **Scientific temper**: Use of forensic science
4. **Timeliness**: Expeditious completion
5. **Human rights**: Respect for constitutional rights
6. **Quality**: Thorough, meticulous evidence collection

The transition from CrPC to BNSS brings enhanced focus on **digital evidence, mandatory forensics, and videography** - making investigations more scientific and transparent.

**Investigating officers must remember**: They are **ministers of justice**, not merely prosecution agents. Their duty is to uncover the truth - whether it favors prosecution or defense.
