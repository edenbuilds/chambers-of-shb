## Overview

**Culpable Homicide** is the genus offense for unlawful killing of a human being under Indian criminal law. **Murder** (IPC 300) is a species within this genus, representing the most aggravated form. This protocol covers:

- Definition of culpable homicide (IPC 299)
- Distinction between culpable homicide and murder (degree of intention/knowledge)
- Punishment under IPC Section 304 (two parts: with/without knowledge)
- Causing death by negligence (IPC 304A - non-culpable homicide)
- Judicial principles distinguishing murder from culpable homicide
- IPC to BNS transition

---

## Statutory Framework

### IPC (Pre-July 1, 2024)

**Section 299**: Definition of Culpable Homicide
**Section 304**: Punishment for culpable homicide not amounting to murder (Part I and Part II)
**Section 304A**: Causing death by negligence (rash or negligent act)

### BNS (Post-July 1, 2024)

**Section 100**: Definition of Culpable Homicide (corresponds to IPC 299)
**Section 105**: Punishment for culpable homicide not amounting to murder (corresponds to IPC 304)
**Section 106**: Causing death by negligence (corresponds to IPC 304A)

## Definition of Culpable Homicide (IPC Section 299 / BNS Section 100)

### Statutory Text

**IPC Section 299**: "Whoever causes death by doing an act with the intention of causing death, or with the intention of causing such bodily injury as is likely to cause death, or with the knowledge that he is likely by such act to cause death, commits the offence of culpable homicide."

### Three Clauses (Mens Rea)

Culpable homicide occurs when death is caused by an act done with:

#### **Clause 1**: Intention of Causing Death
- **Mens Rea**: Direct intention to kill
- **Example**: A shoots B intending to kill B, but B survives for 10 days before dying → Culpable homicide (if exception reduces from murder)

#### **Clause 2**: Intention of Causing Such Bodily Injury as is Likely to Cause Death
- **Mens Rea**: Intention to cause specific injury + that injury is **likely** (not certain) to cause death
- **Likelihood**: Probability exists, but not high probability (contrast with murder Section 300 "Secondly" - **knows** to be likely)
- **Example**: A stabs B in thigh (femoral artery area). A intended stab + knew thigh stab **likely** causes death (blood loss) → Clause 2

#### **Clause 3**: Knowledge that Act is Likely to Cause Death
- **Mens Rea**: Knowledge that the act (not specific injury) is **likely** to cause death
- **Likelihood**: Less than "in all probability" (contrast with murder Section 300 "Fourthly" - so imminently dangerous **in all probability**)
- **Example**: A pushes B off 2nd floor balcony. A knows 2nd floor fall **likely** causes death (not certain) → Clause 3

## Culpable Homicide vs. Murder: The Critical Distinction

### Foundational Principle

> **"All murder is culpable homicide, but not all culpable homicide is murder."**
> — Reg v. Govinda (1876 Bombay High Court)

**Relationship**:
- **Genus**: Culpable homicide (IPC 299)
- **Species**: Murder (IPC 300 - specific types of culpable homicide with higher mens rea)

---

### Comparative Analysis Table

| Element | **Culpable Homicide (IPC 299)** | **Murder (IPC 300)** |
|---------|----------------------------------|----------------------|
| **Intention Threshold** | Intention to cause death OR such injury as is **likely** to cause death | Intention to cause death OR such injury as accused **knows to be likely** to cause death |
| **Knowledge Threshold** | Knowledge act **likely** to cause death | Knowledge act **so imminently dangerous** that **in all probability** will cause death |
| **Degree of Probability** | Likelihood (probability may be < 50%) | High probability (> 50%) or virtual certainty |
| **Mental State** | Recklessness suffices (knew of risk, took it anyway) | Deliberation OR extreme recklessness required |
| **Exceptions Apply?** | N/A (culpable homicide is baseline after exceptions reduce murder) | Yes, 5 exceptions reduce murder → culpable homicide |
| **Punishment** | Part I: Life OR 10 years + fine<br>Part II: 10 years OR fine OR both | **Death penalty** OR **Life imprisonment** (mandatory minimum life) |

---

### Degree of Intention: The Pokhar Singh Test

**Pokhar Singh v. State of Rajasthan (1973)**

Supreme Court articulated the distinction:

**IPC 299 (Culpable Homicide)**:
- "**Likely**" = Probable, but not inevitable or highly probable
- Accused knew of **risk**, but not certainty or high probability

**IPC 300 (Murder)**:
- "**Knows to be likely**" (Secondly) = High degree of knowledge, approaching certainty
- "**In all probability**" (Fourthly) = Virtual certainty, extreme danger

**Illustration**:
- A strikes B's leg with iron rod → Fracture likely, death possible (low probability) → **IPC 299** (culpable homicide)
- A strikes B's head with iron rod → Skull fracture + death highly probable → **IPC 300** (murder under "Thirdly" - objectively sufficient injury)

---

### Judicial Tests for Distinguishing

#### Test 1: Objective Sufficiency (Virsa Singh Test)

From **Virsa Singh v. State of Punjab (1958)**:

**Questions**:
1. Is the injury **sufficient in ordinary course of nature** to cause death? (Medical evidence)
2. Did accused **intend** to inflict that specific injury?

**If YES to both** → **Murder** (IPC 300 "Thirdly")
**If NO** → **Culpable Homicide** (IPC 299)

**Example**:
- 20 stab wounds to chest/abdomen → Objectively sufficient → Murder
- Single punch to jaw (skull fracture due to victim's thin skull) → Not objectively sufficient → Culpable homicide

---

#### Test 2: Knowledge vs. Likelihood (Reg v. Govinda Test)

**Reg v. Govinda (1876)**:

**Culpable Homicide**: Accused knew act **likely** to cause death (probability exists, but not high)

**Murder**: Accused knew act **so imminently dangerous** that death **in all probability** will result (high probability or certainty)

**Illustration from Govinda Case**:
- Wife placed in dark room with cobra to frighten her → Death resulted
- Court: Did husband know cobra bite **in all probability** causes death? YES → Murder
- Had he used non-venomous snake (death unlikely) → Culpable homicide

## Punishment for Culpable Homicide Not Amounting to Murder (IPC Section 304)

### Two Parts: Distinct Mens Rea

**Section 304 Part I** (More Serious):

> "If the act by which death is caused is done with the **intention of causing death**, or of causing such bodily injury as is **likely to cause death** → Shall be punished with **imprisonment for life** OR imprisonment up to **10 years** + fine."

**Mens Rea**: Intention (direct purpose) to cause death or likely fatal injury

**When Applied**: Murder reduced by Exception 1-5 (provocation, sudden fight, etc.) where accused **intended** death or likely fatal injury

---

**Section 304 Part II** (Less Serious):

> "If the act is done with the **knowledge** that it is likely to cause death, but **without any intention** to cause death or likely fatal injury → Shall be punished with imprisonment up to **10 years** OR **fine** OR **both**."

**Mens Rea**: Knowledge (awareness of risk) without intention

**When Applied**: Accused knew act risky (might cause death) but did not intend death

---

### Distinction: Part I vs. Part II

| Aspect | **Part I** | **Part II** |
|--------|-----------|-------------|
| **Mens Rea** | **Intention** to cause death or likely fatal injury | **Knowledge** that act likely causes death (no intention) |
| **Mental State** | Purposeful (wanted result) | Reckless (knew risk, took it anyway) |
| **Punishment** | **Life imprisonment** OR 10 years + fine (more severe) | 10 years OR fine OR both (less severe) |
| **Example** | A stabs B in chest intending to kill (Exception 1 provocation applies) → Part I | A drives recklessly, knows accident likely, B dies → Part II |

---

### Landmark Case: Part I vs. Part II

**State of Andhra Pradesh v. Rayavarapu Punnayya (1976)**

**Facts**: Accused administered abortifacient drug to pregnant woman intending to cause abortion (not death). Woman died from drug's toxic effect.

**Issue**: Part I (intention) or Part II (knowledge)?

**Analysis**:
- **Intention**: Accused intended abortion, NOT death → No intention to cause death
- **Knowledge**: Accused knew abortifacient drug **likely** to cause death (toxic drug) → Had knowledge

**Holding**: Convicted under **Section 304 Part II** (knowledge, not intention).

**Principle**: Intention to cause **different injury** (abortion) ≠ Intention to cause death. But if accused knew different injury likely causes death → Part II applies.

## Causing Death by Negligence (IPC Section 304A / BNS Section 106)

### Statutory Text

**IPC Section 304A**: "Whoever causes death of any person by doing any rash or negligent act not amounting to culpable homicide, shall be punished with imprisonment up to **2 years** OR **fine** OR **both**."

### Key Elements

1. **Death caused** by accused's act
2. **Act was rash OR negligent** (no mens rea - no intention or knowledge)
3. **Not amounting to culpable homicide** (excludes IPC 299 - if knowledge/intention present, then 299/300/304)

---

### Rash vs. Negligent Acts

**Rashness** (Active):
- Doing something with knowledge of risk but without excuse
- Conscious risk-taking (accused aware of danger, proceeds anyway)
- **Example**: Speeding at 120 km/h in crowded market knowing risk of accident

**Negligence** (Passive):
- Omission or failure to exercise reasonable care
- Inadvertence, lack of foresight
- **Example**: Doctor forgot to check patient's allergy before administering drug → Patient dies

---

### Test for Negligence: Bolam Standard (Medical Cases)

**Dr. Suresh Gupta v. Govt. of NCT Delhi (2004)**

**Medical Negligence Standard**:
- Must be **gross negligence** (not mere lack of care)
- Assessed by peer medical opinion: Would competent doctor of ordinary skill have acted similarly?
- Standard of care expected from average competent professional (not best expert)

**Criminal Negligence**: More than civil negligence; must be gross, reckless disregard of life safety.

**Example**:
- Surgeon leaves forceps inside patient → Patient dies from infection → **Gross negligence** → Section 304A
- Surgeon makes error in judgment (2 valid treatment options, chooses one, patient dies) → **Not gross negligence** → No criminal liability

---

### Punishment

**Section 304A**: Imprisonment up to **2 years** OR fine OR both

**Contrast with Culpable Homicide**:
- IPC 304 Part II: Up to **10 years** (knowledge of likelihood)
- IPC 304A: Up to **2 years** (no knowledge, mere negligence)

---

## Exceptions Reducing Murder to Culpable Homicide (IPC 300 Exceptions)

### Summary of 5 Exceptions

| Exception | Scenario | Effect | Cross-Reference |
|-----------|----------|--------|-----------------|
| **Exception 1** | Grave and sudden provocation | Murder → Culpable Homicide (Section 304) | IL-CRIM-MURDER.md |
| **Exception 2** | Private defense (exceeded right in good faith) | Murder → Culpable Homicide | IL-CRIM-GENERAL-EXCEPTIONS.md |
| **Exception 3** | Public servant exceeding lawful powers in good faith | Murder → Culpable Homicide | IL-CRIM-GENERAL-EXCEPTIONS.md |
| **Exception 4** | Sudden fight (heat of passion, no undue advantage) | Murder → Culpable Homicide | IL-CRIM-MURDER.md |
| **Exception 5** | Consent of victim (18+ years) to death/risk | Murder → Culpable Homicide | IL-CRIM-MURDER.md |

**Effect**: If murder (IPC 300) proven BUT exception applies → Reduced to **IPC 304** (culpable homicide not amounting to murder).

**Burden of Proof**: Prosecution must prove murder (beyond reasonable doubt). If accused raises exception, burden shifts to accused to prove exception on **balance of probabilities** (Section 105 Evidence Act).

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Culpable Homicide (IPC 299) - Likelihood, Not High Probability

**Scenario**: A and B are brothers disputing property inheritance. During heated argument, A shoves B. B falls, hits head on marble floor, dies from skull fracture. Medical evidence: shove itself not dangerous; marble floor + thin skull caused death.

**Analysis**:
- **Mens Rea**: A intended to shove (minor injury), knew shove **likely** causes fall (not death)
- **Likelihood**: Fall on floor **likely** to cause injury, but death unlikely (unless thin skull - unpredictable)
- **Murder Test**: Shove NOT objectively sufficient in ordinary course to cause death → NOT Murder Section 300 "Thirdly"
- **Culpable Homicide**: A knew shove **likely** causes injury (fall), injury caused death → Section 299 Clause 2

**Charge**: **IPC Section 304 Part II** (knowledge that shove likely causes injury, injury caused death)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to 10 years OR fine OR both.

---

### Example 2: Culpable Homicide (IPC 304 Part I) - Exception 1 (Provocation)

**Scenario**: A discovers spouse in bed with B. A immediately picks up vase and smashes B's head, causing death. Time gap: < 30 seconds.

**Analysis**:
- **Prima Facie Murder**: A intended to cause injury (head smash) knowing likely to cause death → Section 300 "Secondly"
- **Exception 1** (Provocation):
  - Grave: Adultery discovered in flagrante delicto
  - Sudden: < 30 seconds (immediate)
  - Self-control lost: Reasonable person would lose control
  - Not self-induced: B's act provoked A
- **Applies**: YES → Murder reduced to culpable homicide
- **Intention**: A **intended** to cause injury (head smash) likely to cause death → **Part I** (not Part II - knowledge only)

**Charge**: **IPC Section 304 Part I** (culpable homicide not amounting to murder via Exception 1)

**Punishment**: Life imprisonment OR imprisonment up to 10 years + fine.

---

### Example 3: Culpable Homicide (IPC 304 Part II) - Knowledge, No Intention

**Scenario**: A drives car at 100 km/h in residential area (speed limit 30 km/h) at night. A knows speeding dangerous, likely to cause accident. Pedestrian B crosses road. A hits B, B dies.

**Analysis**:
- **Intention**: A did NOT intend to hit pedestrian (no direct purpose to cause death)
- **Knowledge**: A knew speeding in residential area **likely** to cause accident/death
- **Mens Rea**: Knowledge (awareness of risk), not intention (purpose)
- **Section 299**: Knowledge act likely to cause death → Clause 3 culpable homicide

**Charge**: **IPC Section 304 Part II** (knowledge, not intention)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to 10 years OR fine OR both.

**Alternative Charge**: If prosecution cannot prove **knowledge** (e.g., A argues "did not know speeding causes death") → **IPC Section 304A** (causing death by rash act - max 2 years).

---

### Example 4: Section 304A (Negligence) - No Mens Rea

**Scenario**: Doctor administers penicillin injection to patient without checking medical history. Patient allergic to penicillin, dies from anaphylactic shock. Standard practice: Always check allergy history before injecting.

**Analysis**:
- **Intention/Knowledge**: Doctor did NOT intend death, did NOT know patient allergic → No mens rea for Section 299/304
- **Negligence**: Failure to check allergy history = **gross negligence** (deviation from standard medical practice)
- **Causation**: Negligent act (not checking allergy) caused death

**Charge**: **IPC Section 304A** (causing death by negligent act)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to 2 years OR fine OR both.

**Medical Negligence Standard** (Dr. Suresh Gupta): Assessed by whether competent doctor would check allergy history → YES (standard practice) → Gross negligence proven.

---

### Example 5: Thin Skull Rule (Eggshell Skull Doctrine)

**Scenario**: A punches B once in jaw (minor assault). B has pre-existing thin skull (unknown to A). B falls, skull fractures, dies.

**Analysis**:
- **Objective Test**: Single punch to jaw NOT sufficient in ordinary course to cause death → NOT Murder Section 300 "Thirdly"
- **Thin Skull Rule**: Accused takes victim as found. A liable even if victim's thin skull made injury fatal.
- **Mens Rea**: A intended punch (bodily injury), knew punch **likely** causes injury (not death)
- **Causation**: Punch caused fall → Skull fracture (thin skull) → Death

**Charge**: **IPC Section 304 Part II** (knowledge punch likely causes injury, injury caused death due to thin skull)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to 10 years.

**Principle**: Accused liable for death even if victim's pre-existing condition made injury fatal. "Take victim as you find them."

## Landmark Cases: Culpable Homicide Principles

### 1. **Reg v. Govinda (1876)**

**Facts**: Husband locked wife in dark room with cobra to frighten her (not intending death). Cobra bit wife, wife died.

**Issue**: Culpable homicide or murder?

**Analysis**:
- **Intention**: Husband intended to **frighten**, not kill
- **Knowledge**: Husband knew cobra bite **in all probability** causes death (venomous snake, confined space)

**Holding**: **Murder** (IPC 300 "Fourthly" - knowledge act so imminently dangerous in all probability causes death)

**Principle**: Knowledge that act **in all probability** causes death = Murder (even without intention to kill). High degree of knowledge elevates culpable homicide to murder.

---

### 2. **Pokhar Singh v. State of Rajasthan (1973)**

**Facts**: Accused hit victim with farming tool (kulhadi) on head once during sudden quarrel. Victim died.

**Issue**: Murder or culpable homicide?

**Analysis**:
- **Injury**: Single blow to head with blunt tool (not sharp weapon)
- **Objective Test**: Single blow with farming tool (not designed to kill) NOT objectively sufficient in ordinary course → NOT Section 300 "Thirdly"
- **Knowledge**: Accused knew blow to head **likely** causes injury (not certainty of death)

**Holding**: **Culpable Homicide** (IPC 304 Part II - knowledge, not intention to kill)

**Principle**: Distinction between murder and culpable homicide lies in **degree of likelihood**. "Likely" (IPC 299) vs. "In all probability" (IPC 300).

---

### 3. **State of Andhra Pradesh v. Rayavarapu Punnayya (1976)**

**Facts**: Accused administered abortifacient drug to pregnant woman to cause abortion. Woman died from drug's toxic effect (not abortion itself).

**Issue**: Section 304 Part I (intention) or Part II (knowledge)?

**Holding**: **Section 304 Part II** (knowledge, not intention)

**Reasoning**:
- **Intention**: Accused intended **abortion** (not death of woman)
- **Knowledge**: Accused knew abortifacient drug **likely** to cause death (toxic drug, high-risk procedure)
- **Transfer of Mens Rea**: Intention to cause X (abortion) does NOT automatically become intention to cause Y (death)

**Principle**: Intention must be specific to the result (death). If intention is for different result (abortion), but knowledge of death-causing risk exists → Part II (knowledge), not Part I (intention).

---

### 4. **Dr. Suresh Gupta v. Govt. of NCT Delhi (2004)**

**Facts**: Doctor administered spinal anesthesia to patient for surgery. Patient died from anesthesia overdose. Prosecution alleged gross negligence (doctor failed to check dosage).

**Issue**: Criminal negligence (IPC 304A) or civil negligence?

**Holding**: Supreme Court quashed criminal proceedings. Medical error was civil negligence, NOT gross criminal negligence.

**Standard**:
- **Criminal Negligence** (IPC 304A): Gross, reckless disregard of life safety (e.g., drunk doctor, unqualified person)
- **Civil Negligence**: Mere error in judgment, lack of reasonable care (civil compensation, not criminal)

**Test (Bolam Standard)**: Would competent doctor of ordinary skill have made same error? If medical opinion divided (2 valid approaches) → NOT gross negligence.

**Principle**: Not every medical error is criminal. Criminal liability only for **gross negligence** shocking to conscience.

## Compliance Checklist: Investigation & Prosecution

### For Investigating Officer

**Mens Rea Determination**:
- [ ] Interview accused: Did you **intend** to cause death? (Part I)
- [ ] Interview witnesses: Did accused make statements showing intention? ("I'll kill you," premeditation)
- [ ] Examine circumstances: Weapon used (lethal vs. non-lethal), number of blows, vital areas targeted
- [ ] Medical evidence: Nature of injury (objectively sufficient to cause death?)

**Exception Investigation** (if murder prima facie):
- [ ] Exception 1 (Provocation): Time gap between provocation and act? < 1 minute (sudden) or hours (premeditated)?
- [ ] Exception 4 (Sudden fight): Mutual combat? Heat of passion? Cruel manner?
- [ ] Interview witnesses: What triggered fight? Who initiated?

**Negligence Cases** (IPC 304A):
- [ ] Expert opinion: Was act negligent per professional standards? (medical, engineering, driving)
- [ ] Standard of care: Did accused deviate from ordinary prudent person standard?
- [ ] Causation: Did negligent act directly cause death?

---

### For Public Prosecutor

**Charge Framing**:
- [ ] Primary charge: IPC 302 (murder) if objective sufficiency OR high knowledge
- [ ] Alternative charge: IPC 304 Part I (if exception may apply, intention present)
- [ ] Alternative charge: IPC 304 Part II (if knowledge but no intention)
- [ ] Alternative charge: IPC 304A (if mens rea doubtful, negligence clear)

**Trial Strategy**:
- [ ] Prove mens rea beyond reasonable doubt (intention OR knowledge)
- [ ] Anticipate exception defense (provocation, sudden fight) → Negate exception
- [ ] Medical expert: Establish objective sufficiency of injury (Virsa Singh test)
- [ ] Witness testimony: Establish timeline (sudden vs. premeditated)

---

### For Defense Counsel

**Reduction Strategy**:
- [ ] Plead exception (provocation, sudden fight, self-defense) → Reduce murder to 304
- [ ] Challenge mens rea: No intention to kill, only to injure → Reduce 302 to 304 Part II
- [ ] Challenge causation: Intervening cause (medical negligence after injury) broke chain
- [ ] Argue thin skull (unpredictable pre-existing condition) → Reduce 302 to 304

**Section 304A Defense** (Negligence):
- [ ] Challenge gross negligence standard: Mere error in judgment, not recklessness
- [ ] Expert opinion: Peer medical/professional opinion supports accused's conduct
- [ ] Standard of care: Accused met ordinary prudent person standard

## IPC to BNS Transition

### Section Mapping

| IPC Section | BNS Section | Change |
|-------------|-------------|--------|
| IPC 299 | BNS 100 | Definition unchanged |
| IPC 304 Part I | BNS 105 Part I | Punishment unchanged (life OR 10 years + fine) |
| IPC 304 Part II | BNS 105 Part II | Punishment unchanged (10 years OR fine OR both) |
| IPC 304A | BNS 106 | Punishment unchanged (2 years OR fine OR both) |

### Effective Date: July 1, 2024

**Offenses Before July 1, 2024**: Prosecuted under **IPC 299/304/304A**
**Offenses On/After July 1, 2024**: Prosecuted under **BNS 100/105/106**

**Pending Trials**: Trials pending as of July 1, 2024 continue under IPC (no retrospective BNS application).
