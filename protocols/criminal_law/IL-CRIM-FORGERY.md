## Overview

**Forgery** is the making of a false document or electronic record with the intent to cause damage or injury to the public or any person, or to support any claim or title, or to cause any person to part with property.

Forgery strikes at the reliability and authenticity of documents, which are essential for commerce, administration of justice, property rights, and trust in written records. It is both a property offence and an offence against public justice.

### Key Characteristics
1. **False Document**: Making a document or electronic record that purports to be genuine but is not
2. **Intent to Defraud or Injure**: Specific intention to cause damage, support false claim, or induce delivery of property
3. **Document-Centric**: Offence relates to documents (paper or electronic records)
4. **Preparatory Offence**: Even mere making of false document (without using it) is punishable
5. **Wide Application**: Applies to all types of documents (wills, contracts, court records, securities, accounts, etc.)

## Definition of Forgery (IPC Section 463 / BNS Section 336)

### Full Legal Definition

**IPC Section 463**: Whoever makes any **false document or false electronic record** or **part of a document or electronic record**, with **intent to cause damage or injury** to the public or to any person, or to support any claim or title, or to cause any person to part with property, or to enter into any express or implied contract, or with intent to commit fraud or that fraud may be committed, commits **forgery**.

### Essential Ingredients of Forgery

#### 1. **Making a False Document or Electronic Record**

**False Document** is defined in **Section 464**. A person makes a false document if he:

**A. Dishonestly or Fraudulently Makes, Signs, Seals, or Executes a Document**
- Creates a document that appears to be genuine but is not
- Makes it with dishonest or fraudulent intention

**B. Makes Any Material Alteration in a Document**
- Changes a genuine document in a material (significant) way
- The alteration must affect the legal effect or meaning of the document

**Examples of Making False Document**:

1. **Creating Entirely Fake Document**
   - A creates a fake land sale deed claiming B sold property to A

2. **Forging Signature**
   - A signs B's name on a cheque without B's authorization

3. **Altering Existing Document**
   - A changes the amount on a genuine cheque from Rs. 5,000 to Rs. 50,000

4. **Backdating or Misdating Document**
   - A creates a contract dated one year ago to give false impression of priority

5. **Forging Seal or Stamp**
   - A creates a fake court seal and affixes it to a document

6. **Electronic Record Forgery**
   - A creates a fake digital signature or alters an electronic document

**Material Alteration**: The alteration must be significant enough to affect the document's legal meaning or effect. Mere corrections or immaterial changes are not forgery.

**Example (Material)**: Changing beneficiary name in a will is material alteration (affects inheritance).

**Example (Immaterial)**: Correcting a typo in a non-essential clause is not material forgery.

#### 2. **Intent to Cause Damage or Injury**

The accused must have one of the following intentions:

**A. Intent to Cause Damage or Injury to Public or Any Person**
- Intention to harm public interest or injure any individual

**Example**: A forges a court order to illegally confine B, causing injury to B's liberty.

**B. Intent to Support Any Claim or Title**
- Intention to use the forged document to assert a false claim or right

**Example**: A forges a will to claim he is the rightful heir to property.

**C. Intent to Cause Any Person to Part with Property**
- Intention to induce someone to transfer property based on the forged document

**Example**: A forges B's signature on a sale deed to fraudulently acquire B's land.

**D. Intent to Cause Any Person to Enter into Contract**
- Intention to induce someone to enter into a contract (express or implied) based on the forged document

**Example**: A forges a reference letter from a reputable company to get a job contract.

**E. Intent to Commit Fraud or That Fraud May Be Committed**
- General fraudulent intention

**Example**: A forges a university degree certificate to commit fraud in job applications.

**Important Principle**: The **intent** at the time of making the false document is crucial. If the document is made with innocent intention but later used fraudulently, the forgery occurs at the time of making (if fraudulent intent existed then) or using (if intent arose later).

#### 3. **Document or Electronic Record**

**Document** (Section 29 IEA): Any matter expressed or described on any substance by means of letters, figures, marks, or symbols, or by more than one of those means, intended to be used for recording that matter.

**Electronic Record** (IT Act, 2000): Data generated, image or sound stored, received or sent in an electronic form, or micro film or computer-generated micro fiche.

**Types of Documents Subject to Forgery**:
- **Wills and testaments**
- **Deeds and conveyances** (sale deeds, lease agreements)
- **Contracts and agreements**
- **Valuable securities** (promissory notes, cheques, share certificates)
- **Court records and judgments**
- **Government records** (birth certificates, passports, licenses)
- **Educational certificates**
- **Invoices, bills, and accounts**
- **Electronic records** (emails, digital signatures, electronic contracts)

## What Constitutes a "False Document" (Section 464 / BNS Section 337)

### Section 464: Making a False Document

A person is said to make a **false document** who:

#### 1. **Dishonestly or Fraudulently Makes, Signs, Seals, or Executes a Document or Electronic Record**

**A. Makes or Executes a Document Purporting to Be Made by Another**
- Creating a document and falsely claiming someone else made it

**Example**: A creates a power of attorney claiming B authorized it, but B never did.

**B. Makes or Executes a Document Purporting to Be Made by Authority of Person Who Did Not Give Authority**
- Creating a document claiming authorization that does not exist

**Example**: A creates a letter of recommendation claiming it's from Professor X, who never authorized it.

**C. Makes or Executes a Document Purporting to Be Made at a Time/Place When/Where It Was Not Made**
- Misdating or misrepresenting the time/place of execution

**Example**: A creates a contract backdated to avoid a legal deadline or tax liability.

**D. Makes or Executes a Document Purporting to Be a Copy of Another Document When No Such Document Exists**
- Creating a fake copy of a non-existent original

**Example**: A creates a "copy" of a will claiming the original was destroyed, but no such will ever existed.

#### 2. **Makes Any Material Alteration in a Document or Electronic Record**

**A. Dishonestly or Fraudulently Alters a Genuine Document**
- Changing any material part of a genuine document

**Example**: A changes the amount on a genuine cheque from Rs. 1,000 to Rs. 10,000.

**B. Adds or Removes Content from a Document**
- Inserting false content or deleting genuine content

**Example**: A adds an extra clause to a signed contract, creating a false term.

**Material vs. Immaterial Alteration**:
- **Material**: Affects legal meaning, rights, or obligations (forgery)
- **Immaterial**: Minor corrections or non-essential changes (not forgery)

**Explanation 1 to Section 464**: A person commits forgery even if the document purports to be made by a **fictitious person** (non-existent person).

**Example**: A creates a recommendation letter from "Dr. John Smith, Harvard University" when no such person exists. This is forgery.

**Explanation 2**: A person commits forgery even if the false document is **not made in the name of any person**, if it is made with fraudulent intent.

**Example**: A creates a fake invoice without any issuer's name but uses it to defraud. This is forgery.

**Explanation 3**: A person commits forgery even if the false document is **not complete** or does not purport to be on oath.

**Example**: A creates a half-completed fake contract. Even if incomplete, it is forgery if made with fraudulent intent.

## Forged Document (Section 470 / BNS Section 343)

### Definition

**IPC Section 470**: A **false document made wholly or in part by forgery** is called a **forged document**.

**Principle**: Once a document is created through forgery, it becomes a "forged document" which can then be the subject of other offences (e.g., using forged document under Section 471, possessing forged document under Section 474).

## Punishment for Forgery

### Section 465 IPC / Section 338 BNS: Simple Forgery
**Punishment**:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **2 years**, OR
- **Fine**, OR
- **Both**

**Nature**: Non-cognizable, Bailable, Triable by Any Magistrate

**Applies to**: Forgery not covered by aggravated forms (Sections 466-469).

**Example**: A forges a letter of recommendation to apply for a job. Simple forgery under Section 465.

### Section 466 IPC / Section 339 BNS: Forgery of Record of Court or Public Register
Whoever forges a document purporting to be a **record or proceeding of a court of justice**, or a **register of birth, baptism, marriage, or burial**, or a register kept by a public servant, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **7 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Non-cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Magistrate of First Class

**Rationale**: Court records and public registers are relied upon by the public and judicial system. Forging such documents undermines trust in the administration of justice and public records.

**Examples**:
- Forging a court judgment or order
- Creating a fake birth certificate
- Altering marriage register entries

### Section 467 IPC / Section 340 BNS: Forgery of Valuable Security, Will, or Authority to Make/Transfer Property
Whoever forges a document which purports to be a **valuable security** or a **will**, or an **authority to adopt a son**, or which purports to give authority to any person to make or transfer any valuable security, or to receive any property, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment for life**, OR
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **10 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Non-cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Court of Session

**Rationale**: Valuable securities (shares, bonds, promissory notes, wills, property transfer authorities) involve high-value property. Forging them can cause massive financial loss and disrupt property rights.

**Examples**:
- Forging a will to claim false inheritance
- Creating a fake share certificate
- Forging a power of attorney to transfer property
- Forging a cheque or promissory note

**Most Serious Form**: Section 467 carries the highest punishment (life imprisonment or up to 10 years) for forgery offences.

### Section 468 IPC / Section 341 BNS: Forgery for Purpose of Cheating
Whoever commits forgery with the **intent that the forged document shall be used for the purpose of cheating**, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **7 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Non-cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Magistrate of First Class

**Rationale**: Forgery combined with intent to cheat is aggravated.

**Example**: A forges a fake invoice to overcharge a client (cheating). Forgery for cheating under Section 468.

**Distinction from Section 467**: Section 468 applies when the forged document is intended for cheating but is not a valuable security or will. If it is a valuable security, Section 467 applies (higher punishment).

### Section 469 IPC / Section 342 BNS: Forgery for Purpose of Harming Reputation
Whoever commits forgery with the intent to cause **damage to the reputation** of any party, or knowing that it is likely to be used for that purpose, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **3 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Non-cognizable, Bailable, Triable by Any Magistrate

**Example**: A forges a compromising letter purporting to be written by B, intending to defame B's reputation. Forgery for harming reputation under Section 469.

## Using Forged Document (Section 471 / BNS Section 344)

### Definition and Punishment

**IPC Section 471**: Whoever **fraudulently or dishonestly uses as genuine any document or electronic record which he knows or has reason to believe to be a forged document or record**, shall be punished in the **same manner as if he had forged such document or record**.

**Punishment**: Same as the underlying forgery offence (Section 465, 466, 467, 468, or 469 depending on type of document).

**Essential Ingredients**:
1. **Document is forged** (made by someone through forgery)
2. **Accused knows or has reason to believe** it is forged
3. **Accused uses it as genuine** (presents it as if it were authentic)
4. **Use is fraudulent or dishonest**

**Principle**: Even if the accused did not forge the document himself, **using a forged document knowing it is forged** is equivalent to forgery.

**Example**: A obtains a forged degree certificate from B. A submits it in a job application knowing it is forged. A commits offence under Section 471 (using forged document), punishable same as Section 465 or 467 (depending on document type).

**Distinction**:
- **Section 463-469**: Making a false document (forgery itself)
- **Section 471**: Using a forged document made by another (or by oneself), knowing it is forged

## Possession of Forged Documents (Sections 472-474)

### Section 472 IPC / Section 345 BNS: Making or Possessing Counterfeit Seal with Intent to Commit Forgery
Whoever **makes or counterfeits any seal, plate, or other instrument** for making an impression, **intending** that the same shall be used for the purpose of committing any forgery, or who **possesses any such counterfeit seal or instrument** with such intent, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **7 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Magistrate of First Class

**Rationale**: Possession of tools for forgery (fake seals, stamps, plates) is preparatory to forgery and is criminalized to prevent future forgeries.

**Example**: A manufactures a fake government seal intending to use it to forge documents. A is liable under Section 472 even before using the seal.

### Section 473 IPC / Section 346 BNS: Making or Possessing Counterfeit Seal for Valuable Security
Whoever makes or counterfeits any seal or instrument for making an impression, intending that the same shall be used for forging **valuable securities**, or who possesses such counterfeit seal or instrument knowing the same to be counterfeit and intending to use it, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment for life**, OR
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **7 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Court of Session

**Rationale**: Possession of tools to forge valuable securities (shares, bonds, wills) is the most serious preparatory offence.

### Section 474 IPC / Section 347 BNS: Having Possession of Forged Document (Court Record or Valuable Security)
Whoever has in his possession any document or electronic record, knowing the same to be forged, and intending that it shall be fraudulently or dishonestly used as genuine, shall, if the document is one of the description mentioned in **Section 466** (court record) or **Section 467** (valuable security), be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **7 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Non-cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Magistrate of First Class

**Example**: A has a forged will in his possession, knowing it is forged, and intends to use it to claim inheritance. A is liable under Section 474.

## Counterfeiting Devices for Authentication (Sections 475-476)

### Section 475 IPC / Section 348 BNS: Counterfeiting Device or Mark Used for Authenticating Documents
Whoever counterfeits upon or in the substance of any material any **device or mark** used for the purpose of authenticating any document (other than a stamp issued by government), intending that such device or mark shall be used for forging documents, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **7 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Examples**: Creating fake company seals, university stamps, notary seals.

### Section 476 IPC / Section 349 BNS: Counterfeiting Device for Authenticating Valuable Securities
Whoever counterfeits any device or mark used for authenticating **documents prescribed by law to be stamped** (e.g., stamp paper, court fees), or valuable securities (other than currency notes or bank notes), with intent to forge, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment for life**, OR
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **7 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Example**: Creating fake stamp paper watermarks or security features.

## Fraudulent Cancellation or Destruction (Section 477 / BNS Section 350)

### Definition and Punishment

**IPC Section 477**: Whoever **fraudulently or dishonestly**, or with intent to cause damage or injury to the public or any person, **cancels, destroys, or defaces**, or attempts to cancel, destroy, or deface, or **secretes** (hides):
- Any **will**
- Any **authority to adopt a son**
- Any **valuable security**
- Or causes any of the above to be cancelled, destroyed, defaced, or secreted

With intent to cause damage or injury, shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment for life**, OR
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **7 years**, AND
- **Fine**

**Nature**: Non-cognizable, Non-bailable, Triable by Magistrate of First Class

**Rationale**: Destroying or hiding wills, adoption authorities, or valuable securities can cause severe property loss and legal consequences.

**Example**: A destroys B's will (in which A is not a beneficiary) so that B's property will devolve according to intestate succession (benefiting A). A is liable under Section 477.

## Falsification of Accounts (Section 477A / BNS Section 351)

### Definition and Punishment

**IPC Section 477A**: Whoever, being a **clerk, officer, or servant**, or employed in that capacity, **willfully and with intent to defraud**:
- Destroys, alters, mutilates, or falsifies any **book, paper, writing, valuable security, or account** belonging to his employer
- Makes or abets making of any false entry in any such book, account, etc.
- Omits or abets omission of any material particular from any such book, account, etc.

Shall be punished with:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **7 years**, OR
- **Fine**, OR
- **Both**

**Nature**: Non-cognizable, Bailable, Triable by Magistrate of First Class

**Rationale**: Employees who falsify accounts betray their employer's trust and facilitate fraud.

**Examples**:
- An accountant alters company books to hide embezzlement
- A clerk makes false entries in ledgers to show fake expenses
- An employee destroys invoices to conceal theft

**Distinction from Forgery**:
- **Forgery (Section 463)**: Making a false document with intent to defraud
- **Falsification of Accounts (Section 477A)**: Specifically applies to employees who falsify their employer's accounts

## Landmark Cases on Forgery

### 1. **State of Maharashtra v. Dr. Praful B. Desai** (2003) - Intent Essential
**AIR 2003 SC 2053**

**Facts**: The accused, a doctor, was alleged to have forged medical certificates. The prosecution could not prove fraudulent intent.

**Held**: For forgery under Section 463, **fraudulent or dishonest intent** at the time of making the false document is essential. If the document was made with innocent intention (e.g., genuine mistake, no intent to defraud), forgery is not established.

**Principle**: **Mens rea** (guilty mind) is the essence of forgery. Mere making of a false document without fraudulent intent is not forgery.

**Test**: Was the document made with intent to cause damage, support false claim, or commit fraud? If no such intent, no forgery.

### 2. **Gopal Lal v. State of Rajasthan** (1979) - Material Alteration
**AIR 1979 SC 824**

**Facts**: The accused made minor corrections in a document. The prosecution alleged forgery.

**Held**: For forgery, the alteration must be **material** (affecting the legal meaning or effect of the document). **Immaterial or minor alterations** (e.g., correcting typos, non-essential changes) do not constitute forgery.

**Principle**: Not every alteration is forgery. Only material alterations that change the legal character or effect of the document constitute forgery.

**Example (Material)**: Changing the beneficiary name in a will is material forgery.

**Example (Immaterial)**: Correcting a spelling mistake in a witness's name (if it does not affect identity) is not material forgery.

### 3. **K. Chinnaswamy Reddy v. State of A.P.** (1962) - Using Forged Document
**AIR 1962 SC 1788**

**Facts**: The accused used a forged document (made by another person) in a legal proceeding, knowing it was forged.

**Held**: Under **Section 471**, **using a forged document as genuine**, knowing it to be forged, is punishable in the same manner as forgery itself. The accused need not have forged the document himself.

**Principle**: Liability extends to users of forged documents, not just makers. Knowingly using a forged document is equivalent to forgery.

**Application**: If A forges a document and gives it to B, and B uses it knowing it is forged, both A (forger) and B (user) are liable.

### 4. **Bishwanath Prasad v. State of Bihar** (1974) - Signature Forgery
**AIR 1974 SC 1717**

**Facts**: The accused forged another person's signature on a deed to fraudulently transfer property.

**Held**: **Forging a signature** is one of the most common forms of forgery. If a person signs another's name without authorization, with intent to defraud or cause damage, it is forgery under Section 463.

**Principle**: Signature forgery is classic forgery if done with fraudulent intent.

**Evidence**: Forensic handwriting analysis is critical in proving signature forgery (expert witnesses compare disputed signature with genuine samples).

### 5. **State of Rajasthan v. Nand Lal** (2003) - Backdating of Documents
**2003 Cri LJ 2945**

**Facts**: The accused created a document backdated to give false impression of priority over competing claims.

**Held**: **Backdating a document** with fraudulent intent (to create false priority, avoid legal deadlines, or defraud) constitutes forgery under Section 464 (making document purporting to be made at a time when it was not made).

**Principle**: Misdating (backdating or post-dating) with intent to defraud is forgery.

**Application**: Backdating contracts to avoid stamp duty, tax liability, or legal deadlines is forgery.

### 6. **Dalmia Cement Ltd. v. Galaxy Traders** (2001) - Corporate Forgery
**AIR 2001 SC 1325**

**Facts**: Corporate documents (invoices, bills) were forged to facilitate tax evasion and financial fraud.

**Held**: Forgery in corporate context (fake invoices, false accounts, forged resolutions) is punishable under Section 463 (forgery) and Section 477A (falsification of accounts).

**Principle**: Corporate entities and their officers can be prosecuted for forgery of business documents.

**Additional Liability**: Companies Act, 2013 Section 447 (fraud) provides additional punishment for corporate fraud involving forgery.

### 7. **Vijay Kumar Ghai v. State of Punjab** (2007) - Electronic Forgery
**AIR 2007 SC 2817**

**Facts**: The accused altered electronic records (email contents) to defraud.

**Held**: **Electronic records** are subject to forgery provisions under IPC (as amended by IT Act, 2000). Altering, creating fake, or manipulating electronic records with fraudulent intent is forgery.

**Principle**: Forgery laws apply to electronic documents, digital signatures, emails, and computer-generated records.

**Application**: Email forgery, fake digital signatures, manipulated PDFs, altered databases are all punishable as forgery.

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Forging Signature on Cheque
**Scenario**: Ramesh steals a blank cheque from Priya's cheque book. He forges Priya's signature and writes a cheque for Rs. 50,000 payable to himself. He attempts to cash it at the bank.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Making false document: Ramesh forged Priya's signature on the cheque
2. ✅ Fraudulent intent: Ramesh intended to cause Priya to part with Rs. 50,000 (fraudulently)
3. ✅ Valuable security: Cheque is a valuable security (negotiable instrument)

**Offence**: Forgery of Valuable Security under Section 467 IPC / Section 340 BNS
**Punishment**: Imprisonment for life OR up to 10 years AND fine

**Additional Charges**: Theft (stealing cheque book), cheating (Section 420), offences under Negotiable Instruments Act

### Example 2: Forging Educational Certificate
**Scenario**: Sunil creates a fake MBA degree certificate from a prestigious university to apply for a job. The certificate claims Sunil graduated in 2020, but he never attended the university. He submits it with his job application.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Making false document: Sunil created a fake degree certificate
2. ✅ Fraudulent intent: Sunil intended to commit fraud (obtain job by false credentials)
3. ✅ Not valuable security or will: Degree certificate is a regular document (not valuable security)

**Offence**:
- Forgery under Section 465 IPC / Section 338 BNS (for making the certificate)
- Using Forged Document under Section 471 (for submitting it)
**Punishment**: Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine or both

**Alternative Charge**: Forgery for Purpose of Cheating (Section 468) if prosecution emphasizes intent to cheat employer - up to 7 years + fine

### Example 3: Forging Will
**Scenario**: After his uncle's death, Vikram forges a will claiming the uncle left all property to Vikram. In reality, the uncle died intestate (without a will). Vikram presents the forged will in court to claim inheritance.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Making false document: Vikram created a fake will
2. ✅ Fraudulent intent: Vikram intended to support false claim (inheritance claim)
3. ✅ Will: The document is a will (specifically mentioned in Section 467)
4. ✅ Using forged document: Vikram presented it in court

**Offence**:
- Forgery of Will under Section 467 IPC / Section 340 BNS
- Using Forged Document under Section 471
**Punishment**: Imprisonment for life OR up to 10 years AND fine

**Additional Charges**: Cheating (if property is obtained), fraud

### Example 4: Forging Court Order
**Scenario**: A creates a fake court order directing the release of B (who is in police custody). A presents the forged order to the police, and B is released.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Making false document: A created a fake court order
2. ✅ Fraudulent intent: A intended to cause injury to public (obstruct justice) and injure administration of law
3. ✅ Court record: The document purports to be a court order (record of court)

**Offence**: Forgery of Record of Court under Section 466 IPC / Section 339 BNS
**Punishment**: Up to 7 years imprisonment AND fine

**Additional Charges**: Obstruction of justice, aiding escape of prisoner, forgery for purpose of cheating (Section 468)

### Example 5: Falsification of Company Accounts
**Scenario**: Mohan, an accountant at XYZ Ltd, makes false entries in the company's books to hide embezzlement of Rs. 10 lakh. He creates fake invoices and alters ledger entries.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Clerk/officer/servant: Mohan is an employee (accountant)
2. ✅ Willfully and with intent to defraud: Mohan intentionally falsified accounts to hide embezzlement
3. ✅ Belonging to employer: The books belong to XYZ Ltd (employer)
4. ✅ False entries and alterations: Created fake invoices, altered ledgers

**Offence**: Falsification of Accounts under Section 477A IPC / Section 351 BNS
**Punishment**: Up to 7 years imprisonment OR fine OR both

**Additional Charges**: Criminal breach of trust (Section 408 - by servant), cheating, forgery (Section 463)

### Example 6: Using Forged Document (Not the Forger)
**Scenario**: Ravi obtains a forged property deed from his friend Amit (who forged it). Ravi knows the deed is forged but uses it to claim ownership of land in a legal dispute.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Document is forged: The deed was forged by Amit
2. ✅ Ravi knows it is forged: Ravi is aware the deed is fake
3. ✅ Uses as genuine: Ravi presents it in legal proceedings as if genuine
4. ✅ Fraudulent/dishonest use: Ravi intends to fraudulently claim land

**Offence**: Using Forged Document as Genuine under Section 471 IPC / Section 344 BNS
**Punishment**: Same as if Ravi had forged the document himself (Section 467 applies since it's a property transfer document - life or up to 10 years + fine)

**Note**: Amit (the forger) is liable under Section 467. Ravi (the user) is liable under Section 471.

### Example 7: Backdating Contract for Tax Evasion
**Scenario**: A company enters into a contract with a vendor on April 15, 2024. To claim tax benefit available only for contracts signed before March 31, 2024, the company backdates the contract to March 30, 2024.

**Analysis**:
1. ✅ Making false document: Backdating creates a false impression of timing
2. ✅ Fraudulent intent: Intent to defraud government (claim false tax benefit)
3. ✅ Purporting to be made at different time: Contract falsely dated March 30

**Offence**: Forgery under Section 464/465 IPC (making false document purporting to be made at different time)
**Punishment**: Up to 2 years imprisonment or fine or both

**Additional Charges**: Tax evasion under Income Tax Act / GST Act, criminal breach of trust (if public funds involved)

## Defenses to Forgery

### 1. **No Fraudulent Intent**
- If the document was made without intent to defraud, cause damage, or support false claim, no forgery
- Genuine mistake or innocent creation of document is not forgery

**Example**: A signs B's name on a document with B's verbal authorization (but not written authorization). If A genuinely believed authorization existed, intent to forge may be absent.

### 2. **Immaterial Alteration**
- If the alteration to a document is immaterial (does not affect legal meaning or effect), not forgery

**Example**: Correcting a typo in a non-essential clause of a contract is not forgery.

### 3. **Authorization or Consent**
- If the person whose signature was used or document was altered gave authorization, no forgery

**Example**: B authorizes A to sign B's name on documents. A's signature is not forgery (authorized).

### 4. **Lack of Knowledge (For Section 471 - Using Forged Document)**
- If the accused did not know and had no reason to believe the document was forged, Section 471 does not apply

**Example**: A receives a certificate from a university. A submits it in a job application. Later it is discovered to be forged by the university (not by A). If A had no knowledge it was forged, A is not liable under Section 471.

### 5. **No Use as Genuine (For Section 471)**
- If the forged document was not used or presented as genuine, Section 471 does not apply

**Example**: A possesses a forged document but never uses it. Possession alone may not be Section 471 (but could be Section 474 if intent to use exists).

## Procedural Aspects

### Cognizance and Complaint
- **Simple Forgery (465, 469)**: Non-cognizable, Bailable
- **Aggravated Forgery (466, 467, 468)**: Non-cognizable, Non-bailable
- **Possession of Counterfeit Seal (472, 473)**: Cognizable, Non-bailable
- **Falsification of Accounts (477A)**: Non-cognizable, Bailable

### Investigation
- For non-cognizable offences: Complaint to magistrate; investigation with magistrate's order
- Evidence: Forensic document examination (handwriting analysis, ink analysis, paper analysis), expert testimony, comparison with genuine documents

### Trial
- Burden of proof on prosecution to establish: making of false document, fraudulent intent, materiality of alteration
- Expert evidence critical for proving forgery (handwriting experts, forensic document examiners)

### Compounding
- **Forgery offences are generally non-compoundable** (except Section 465 - simple forgery, which is compoundable with court permission)

## Compliance Checklist

### For Individuals and Organizations (Preventive Measures)
- [ ] Verify authenticity of documents (check seals, signatures, watermarks, security features)
- [ ] Use secure storage for important documents (wills, deeds, securities)
- [ ] Implement digital security for electronic records (encryption, digital signatures, access controls)
- [ ] Conduct background verification before accepting documents (especially employment certificates, degrees)
- [ ] Report suspected forgery immediately to police or authorities
- [ ] Preserve original documents and avoid giving originals to untrusted parties

### For Businesses (Compliance)
- [ ] Implement internal controls for document authentication (approval processes, dual signatures)
- [ ] Conduct audits to detect account falsification (Section 477A)
- [ ] Use secure systems for financial records (tamper-proof accounting software)
- [ ] Train employees on forgery prevention and detection
- [ ] Report forgery incidents to law enforcement and regulatory authorities

### For Accused/Suspect (Legal Safeguards)
- [ ] Claim defenses: no fraudulent intent, authorization, immaterial alteration, lack of knowledge
- [ ] Demonstrate genuine mistake or innocent creation of document
- [ ] Return or surrender forged document if applicable
- [ ] Seek compounding (for simple forgery only) with court permission
- [ ] Engage forensic experts to challenge prosecution's handwriting/document analysis
- [ ] Seek bail (bailable for simple forgery; apply to court for aggravated forms)

### For Investigating Officer
- [ ] Secure original documents as evidence
- [ ] Engage forensic document examiner (handwriting analysis, ink dating, paper analysis)
- [ ] Obtain exemplar (sample) signatures/handwriting of suspected forger for comparison
- [ ] Establish fraudulent intent (motive, statements, circumstances)
- [ ] Frame appropriate charge based on type of document (Section 465, 466, 467, or 468)

### For Prosecutors
- [ ] Prove making of false document (forensic evidence, expert testimony)
- [ ] Prove fraudulent intent (critical element)
- [ ] Prove materiality of alteration (if applicable)
- [ ] Present expert witnesses (handwriting experts, forensic document examiners)
- [ ] Anticipate defenses (authorization, immaterial alteration, lack of intent)

### For Defense Counsel
- [ ] Challenge authenticity of prosecution's expert analysis (cross-examine forensic experts)
- [ ] Argue lack of fraudulent intent or genuine mistake
- [ ] Challenge materiality of alteration
- [ ] Seek acquittal if prosecution fails to prove intent or making of false document beyond reasonable doubt
- [ ] If conviction likely, argue for minimum sentence: first-time offender, return of documents, no actual harm caused

## Key Takeaways

1. **Making False Document is Forgery**: Creating, altering, backdating, or signing another's name on a document with fraudulent intent is forgery.

2. **Intent is Critical**: Fraudulent or dishonest intent at the time of making the document is essential. Innocent mistake or immaterial alteration is not forgery.

3. **Material Alteration**: Only material alterations (affecting legal meaning or effect) constitute forgery. Minor corrections are not forgery.

4. **Using Forged Document = Forgery**: Under Section 471, using a forged document knowing it is forged carries the same punishment as forging it.

5. **Graduated Punishment Based on Document Type**:
   - Simple forgery: up to 2 years
   - Court records: up to 7 years
   - Valuable securities/wills: life or up to 10 years
   - For cheating: up to 7 years

6. **Electronic Records Covered**: Forgery laws apply to electronic documents, emails, digital signatures, and computer-generated records.

7. **Preparatory Offences Criminalized**: Possessing counterfeit seals, tools for forgery, or forged documents with intent to use is punishable even before actual use.

8. **Forensic Evidence Critical**: Handwriting analysis, ink dating, and document examination by experts are crucial for proving forgery.

## Conclusion

Forgery strikes at the foundation of trust in written and electronic records, which are essential for commerce, property rights, and administration of justice. The law provides stringent punishment for forgery, with the most serious forms (forgery of wills, valuable securities) punishable with life imprisonment.

Understanding the distinction between making a false document (forgery) and using a forged document (Section 471), as well as the importance of fraudulent intent and material alteration, is critical for proper charge framing and defense.

With the transition to BNS 2023, the substantive law on forgery remains unchanged, ensuring continuity in legal practice while explicitly recognizing electronic records in the digital age.
