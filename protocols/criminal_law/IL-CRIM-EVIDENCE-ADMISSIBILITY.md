## Overview and Purpose

### What is Evidence?

**Evidence** is any **material** presented before a court to **prove or disprove facts** in issue in a legal proceeding.

**Section 3 Evidence Act / BSA**: "Evidence means and includes:
1. All statements which court permits or requires to be made before it by witnesses, in relation to matters of fact under inquiry (**oral evidence**)
2. All documents including electronic records produced for inspection of court (**documentary evidence**)"

### Purpose of Evidence Law

1. **Determine Truth**: Establish facts accurately
2. **Ensure Fairness**: Both parties present their case
3. **Exclude Unreliable Evidence**: Only credible evidence admitted
4. **Protect Rights**: Prevent prejudice, ensure fair trial
5. **Facilitate Justice**: Enable court to reach correct decision

### Types of Evidence

| Type | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Oral Evidence** | Testimony of witnesses | Eyewitness account, expert opinion |
| **Documentary Evidence** | Written/recorded documents | Contracts, letters, photographs, CCTV footage |
| **Physical/Real Evidence** | Tangible objects | Weapons, stolen goods, drugs, bloodstained clothes |
| **Electronic Evidence** | Digital/electronic records | Emails, WhatsApp messages, computer files, GPS data |
| **Direct Evidence** | Directly proves fact | Eyewitness seeing accused commit crime |
| **Circumstantial Evidence** | Indirect proof | Accused found with stolen goods, motive, opportunity |
| **Primary Evidence** | Original document | Original contract |
| **Secondary Evidence** | Copy/substitute | Certified copy, oral account of contents |

### Primary Legislation

#### Indian Evidence Act 1872
- Comprehensive law on evidence

- **Article 20(3)**: No self-incrimination
- **Article 21**: Fair trial includes right to produce evidence

## Fundamental Concepts

### Fact (Section 3 Evidence Act / BSA)

**Fact** means:
1. Any thing, state of things, or relation of things capable of being perceived by senses
2. Any mental condition of which any person is conscious

**Examples**:
- Physical fact: "Gun was fired"
- Psychological fact: "Accused intended to kill"

### Fact in Issue (Section 3)

**Facts in issue** are facts which **prosecution must prove** OR **accused must prove** (if claiming exception)

**Example in Murder Trial**:
- Facts in issue: (1) Death occurred, (2) Accused caused death, (3) Accused had intention/knowledge, (4) No exception applies

### Relevant Fact (Sections 5-55)

**Relevant facts** are facts **connected to facts in issue** that help prove or disprove them

**Example**: Motive is relevant fact (helps prove intention - fact in issue)

**Admissibility**: Only **relevant facts** are admissible (Section 5)

## Admissibility of Evidence: Core Principles

### Section 5 Evidence Act / BSA: Evidence May Be Given of Facts in Issue and Relevant Facts

**"Evidence may be given in any suit or proceeding of the existence or non-existence of every fact in issue and of such other facts as are hereinafter declared to be relevant, and of no others."**

**Two-Part Test for Admissibility**:
1. **Is it fact in issue?** (directly proves/disproves charge), OR
2. **Is it relevant fact?** (per Sections 6-55)

**If neither**: **Inadmissible**

### Relevancy (Sections 6-55 Evidence Act / BSA)

#### Section 6: Res Gestae (Facts Forming Part of Same Transaction)

**"Facts which, though not in issue, are so connected with a fact in issue as to form part of the same transaction, are relevant."**

**Res Gestae**: "Things done" - spontaneous statements/acts contemporaneous with incident

**Example**: Woman screaming "You're killing me!" while being strangled
- Statement is part of same transaction (res gestae)
- Admissible even though hearsay

**Landmark Case**: **Ratten v. The Queen (1972)** (Privy Council, adopted in India)
- Wife called police, said "Get me the police please" in distressed voice
- Call cut off; wife found murdered
- Statement admissible as res gestae (spontaneous, no time to fabricate)

**Indian Case**: **State of Punjab v. Sanjeev (2013)**
- Dying person's cry "Sanjeev has killed me" while neighbors rushed to scene
- Admissible as res gestae

#### Section 7-8: Conduct, Motive, Preparation

**Facts showing**:
- **Motive** (Section 8)
- **Preparation** (Section 8)
- **Conduct** before/during/after (Section 8)
- **Opportunity** (Section 7)

**Relevant to prove fact in issue**

**Example**: Accused purchased poison day before victim poisoned
- Preparation; relevant

#### Section 9: Facts Necessary to Explain or Introduce Relevant Facts

Background facts necessary to understand relevant facts are admissible

#### Section 10: Things Said or Done by Conspirator

In conspiracy, anything said/done/written by any conspirator **in reference to common intention** is admissible against all

**Limitation**: Only acts **in furtherance of conspiracy**

#### Section 11: Facts Not Otherwise Relevant Become Relevant if Inconsistent with Other Relevant Facts

If person's conduct inconsistent with their claim, that inconsistency is relevant

**Example**: Accused claims alibi (was elsewhere) but his phone GPS shows presence at crime scene
- Inconsistency relevant

#### Section 14: Facts Showing System or Similar Facts

Evidence of **similar fact evidence** or **system/course of conduct** admissible

**Example**: Accused charged with murder by poisoning; evidence that accused poisoned previous spouse also
- Similar fact evidence; admissible to show system/intent

**Landmark Case**: **Makin v. Attorney General for New South Wales (1894)** (Privy Council)
- Couple charged with murdering baby
- Bodies of other babies found buried in their previous residences
- Held: Similar fact evidence admissible to rebut accident defense

**Indian Case**: **Om Prakash v. State of U.P. (2006)**
- Accused charged with wife's murder
- Evidence of accused's habit of beating wife admissible
- Shows course of conduct

#### Section 24-30: Admissions and Confessions

**See separate subsection below**

#### Section 32: Statements by Person Who Cannot Be Called as Witness (Dying Declaration)

**See separate subsection below**

#### Section 45-51: Expert Opinion

**See separate subsection below**

#### Section 60: Oral Evidence Must Be Direct

**Best Evidence Rule**: Oral evidence must be **direct** (firsthand)

**Hearsay inadmissible** (with exceptions)

## Oral Evidence (Sections 59-60 Evidence Act / BSA)

### Section 59: Proof of Facts by Oral Evidence

**All facts except documents** may be proved by oral evidence

**Oral Evidence**: Statements made by witnesses in court

### Section 60: Oral Evidence Must Be Direct

**"Oral evidence must in all cases whatever be direct."**

**Meaning**: Witness must have **personally perceived** the fact (seen, heard, touched, smelled, tasted)

**Hearsay Inadmissible**: "I heard X say that Y did it" - inadmissible (unless falls under exception)

**Exceptions to Hearsay Rule**:
1. **Dying declaration** (Section 32(1))
2. **Res gestae** (Section 6)
3. **Admissions** (Sections 17-23)
4. **Statements in furtherance of conspiracy** (Section 10)
5. **Expert opinion** (Section 45)

**Landmark Case**: **Subramaniam v. Public Prosecutor (1956)** (Privy Council)
- Accused claimed acted under duress (terrorists threatened him)
- Statement "Terrorists told me to do X or they'd kill me" NOT hearsay
- Not adduced to prove terrorists actually said it, but to prove accused's state of mind (duress)
- Admissible

## Documentary Evidence (Sections 61-90 Evidence Act / BSA)

### Section 61: Proof of Contents of Documents

Contents of documents proved by:
1. **Primary evidence** (Section 62): Original document
2. **Secondary evidence** (Section 63): Copies, oral accounts

### Section 62: Primary Evidence

**"Primary evidence means the document itself produced for inspection of the court."**

**Preference**: Primary evidence preferred over secondary

**Electronic Records**: Section 65A/65B (see below)

### Section 63: Secondary Evidence

**Secondary evidence includes**:
1. **Certified copies** (from record-keeping authority)
2. **Copies made from original** by mechanical process (photocopies, scanned copies)
3. **Copies compared with original**
4. **Counterparts** of documents
5. **Oral account** of contents by person who has seen it

### Section 65: When Secondary Evidence Can Be Given

Secondary evidence admissible **only if**:
1. **Original lost or destroyed** (not by party seeking to use it)
2. **Original cannot be moved** (e.g., public records)
3. **Original in possession of opponent** who refuses to produce after notice
4. **Original in possession of person beyond jurisdiction** or cannot be compelled
5. **Document is public document** (certified copy admissible)

**Landmark Case**: **Best Bakery Case - Zahira Habibulla Sheikh v. State of Gujarat (2006)**
- Original witness statements lost/destroyed
- Copies from Magistrate's record admissible as certified copies

### Section 65A-65B: Electronic Records (Added by IT Act 2000)

**Section 65A**: Contents of electronic records may be proved with **Section 65B certificate**

**Section 65B**: Electronic evidence admissible if:
1. Produced by computer during regular use
2. Information regularly fed into computer
3. Computer functioning properly (or if not, doesn't affect output)
4. **Certificate** from person in charge of computer operation

**Certificate Must Contain** (Section 65B(4)):
- Identify electronic record
- Describe manner of production
- Provide particulars of device
- State conditions during period record was produced
- Signed by responsible person

**Landmark Case**: **Anvar P.V. v. P.K. Basheer (2014)**
- Supreme Court held: Electronic evidence **must comply with Section 65B**
- Certificate mandatory
- Without certificate, **inadmissible**

**Effect**: Emails, WhatsApp messages, CCTV footage, computer files require Section 65B certificate

**Practical Challenge**: Many cases electronic evidence rejected due to lack of certificate

## Confessions and Admissions (Sections 17-30 Evidence Act / BSA)

### Admission (Section 17)

**"Admission is a statement, oral or documentary, which suggests any inference as to any fact in issue or relevant fact."**

**Characteristics**:
- Can be **oral** or **written**
- **Against interest** of maker (or neutral)
- **Not conclusive** proof (can be explained away)

**Admissibility**: Admissions are **relevant** (Section 21)

### Confession (Section 17 Explanation 2)

**"Confession is an admission stating or suggesting the inference that accused committed the offence."**

**Characteristics**:
- Specific type of admission
- **Acceptance of guilt**
- Self-incriminatory

### Admissibility of Confessions

**General Rule**: Confessions **admissible** if voluntary

**Exceptions** (Confessions **Inadmissible**):

#### Section 24: Confession Caused by Inducement, Threat, Promise

**"Confession made by accused is irrelevant if it appears to court to have been caused by inducement, threat or promise having reference to charge, proceeding from person in authority, and sufficient in opinion of court to give accused grounds to suppose that by making it he would gain any advantage or avoid any evil of a temporal nature."**

**Four Conditions for Inadmissibility**:
1. **Inducement, threat, or promise**
2. **Having reference to charge**
3. **From person in authority** (police, magistrate, employer, etc.)
4. **Sufficient** to make accused believe he'd gain advantage/avoid evil

**Landmark Case**: **State of Rajasthan v. Talevar (2011)**
- Confession obtained after promising leniency
- Held: Inadmissible under Section 24

#### Section 25: Confession to Police Officer Not Admissible

**"No confession made to a police officer shall be proved as against a person accused of any offence."**

**Absolute Bar**: Confession to police **never admissible** (even if voluntary)

**Rationale**: Prevent torture, third-degree methods by police

**Landmark Case**: **Dagdu v. State of Maharashtra (1977)**
- Confession to police inspector
- Held: Inadmissible under Section 25 (even if voluntary)

**Who is Police Officer**: Includes officers of all ranks (Constable to DGP)

**Exception**: If confession leads to **discovery of fact** (Section 27)

#### Section 26: Confession in Police Custody Not Admissible

**"No confession made by any person whilst he is in custody of a police officer shall be proved as against such person."**

**Even if not to police officer**: If person in police custody, confession to anyone inadmissible

**Example**: Accused in police custody confesses to friend who visits
- Inadmissible (person was in police custody)

**Exception**: Confession to **Magistrate** under Section 164 admissible (see Section 28)

#### Section 27: Information Leading to Discovery

**"When any fact is deposed to as discovered in consequence of information received from a person accused of any offence, in the custody of a police officer, so much of such information, whether it amounts to a confession or not, as relates distinctly to the fact thereby discovered, may be proved."**

**Exception to Section 25-26**: Part of confession that **leads to discovery of fact** is admissible

**Conditions**:
1. Person in **custody**
2. Information given to **police**
3. Discovery of **fact** as a result
4. **Only portion** relating distinctly to fact discovered is admissible

**Example**: Accused tells police "I hid the knife under the bridge"
- Police find knife under bridge
- **Admissible**: "I hid the knife under the bridge"
- **Inadmissible**: Any other confessional portion not relating to discovery

**Landmark Case**: **State of Punjab v. Baldev Singh (1999)**
- Information leading to recovery of weapon
- Portion of statement leading to discovery admissible
- Rest inadmissible

**Practical Importance**: Major exception to Section 25-26; widely used in criminal trials

#### Section 28: Confession to Magistrate Admissible (With Conditions)

**Exception to Section 26**: Confession made to **Magistrate under Section 164 CrPC** is admissible

**Conditions** (Section 164 CrPC / 183 BNSS):
1. Magistrate must be **Judicial Magistrate or Metropolitan Magistrate**
2. Magistrate must **not be police officer**
3. Magistrate must **not be Magistrate who will try the case**
4. Accused must be **warned** that not bound to confess
5. Warned confession may be used as evidence
6. Confession must be **voluntary**
7. Magistrate records confession **in presence of accused**

**Landmark Case**: **Shankaria v. State of Rajasthan (1978)**
- Proper procedure under Section 164 not followed
- Magistrate did not warn accused
- Confession inadmissible

**Practical Importance**: Section 164 confessions are strong evidence (if voluntary and procedurally correct)

#### Section 29: Proof Against Co-Accused

Confession of one accused **admissible against co-accused**

**But**: Court treats with caution; requires **corroboration**

**Landmark Case**: **Kashmira Singh v. State of M.P. (1952)**
- Confession of co-accused not substantive evidence
- Can be used to **lend assurance** to other evidence
- Cannot be sole basis of conviction

#### Section 30: Confession of Co-Accused in Joint Trial

When multiple persons tried jointly, confession of one is **relevant** against all

**Limitation**: Same as Section 29 - requires corroboration

### Retracted Confessions

**Retraction**: Accused withdraws confession, claims it was false/coerced

**Effect**: Confession can still be used **if court satisfied it was voluntary**

**Burden**: On prosecution to prove confession was voluntary

**Landmark Case**: **State of Rajasthan v. Raja Ram (2003)**
- Retracted confession can form basis of conviction
- If court satisfied confession was true and voluntary
- But should be corroborated

## Dying Declaration (Section 32(1) Evidence Act / BSA)

### What is Dying Declaration?

**Statement made by person** about **cause of their death** or **circumstances of transaction** resulting in death, when person is **in expectation of death**

**Section 32(1)**: Statements by person who cannot be called as witness (because dead or unfit) are relevant if:
- Statement made by person **as to cause of death**
- Or as to **circumstances** of transaction resulting in death
- In cases where person's death comes into question

### Why Admissible (Exception to Hearsay)?

**Rationale**: **"Nemo moriturus praesumitur mentire"** - "A person about to die is not presumed to lie"

**Reliability**: Person facing death unlikely to lie

### Conditions for Admissibility

**Not Rigid Requirements**:
1. **Expectation of death**: Person must believe they are dying
2. **Cause of death**: Statement relates to cause of death/transaction
3. **Death occurs**: Person dies (otherwise, can call as witness)

**No Requirement**:
- **NO** requirement of oath
- **NO** requirement of question-answer format
- **NO** requirement to be written
- Can be **oral**, **written**, or **by gestures**

**Landmark Case**: **Laxman v. State of Maharashtra (2002)** (Leading case on dying declaration)

**Supreme Court held**:
1. Dying declaration can be **sole basis** of conviction if court satisfied it is **true and voluntary**
2. **No corroboration** required (if court convinced of truthfulness)
3. **Magistrate need not record** (can be to anyone - doctor, police, relative)
4. **Question-answer form not necessary**
5. Person must be in **fit state of mind** (capable of making statement)
6. **Multiple dying declarations**: If multiple, court prefers latest (closer to death)

### When Court Accepts Dying Declaration

**Factors**:
1. **Fit state of mind**: Person conscious and capable
2. **Voluntary**: Not result of tutoring or prompting
3. **Consistent**: Statement is coherent and consistent
4. **Certified by doctor**: Medical certificate showing person fit to make statement (helpful but not mandatory)

**Landmark Case**: **Khushal Rao v. State of Bombay (1958)**
- Dying declaration must be **truthful** and made in **fit state of mind**
- If satisfied, can be sole basis of conviction

**Rejection Grounds**:
- Person **not in fit state** (unconscious, delirious)
- Statement **tutored** or prompted
- Statement **inconsistent** or incoherent
- **Suspicious circumstances**

## Expert Opinion Evidence (Sections 45-51 Evidence Act / BSA)

### Section 45: Opinion of Experts

**"When court has to form opinion upon a point of foreign law, or of science, or art, or as to identity of handwriting or finger impressions, the opinions upon that point of persons specially skilled in such foreign law, science, or art, or in questions as to identity of handwriting or finger impressions are relevant facts."**

**Expert Opinion Relevant** for:
1. **Foreign law**
2. **Science** (forensic science, medicine, ballistics, etc.)
3. **Art**
4. **Handwriting** identification
5. **Fingerprint** identification

**Examples**:
- **Medical expert**: Cause of death, injuries
- **Forensic expert**: DNA analysis, fingerprints
- **Ballistics expert**: Whether bullet fired from particular gun
- **Handwriting expert**: Whether signature forged
- **Chemical analyst**: Presence of poison, drugs

### Section 45A: Opinion of Examiner of Electronic Evidence

**Expert opinion** on electronic evidence (added by IT Act 2000)

**Example**: Computer forensic expert opining on authenticity of email, tampering of digital records

### Section 46: Facts Bearing Upon Opinion of Expert

**Facts on which expert bases opinion** must be proved

**Example**: Ballistics expert gives opinion bullet fired from particular gun
- Must prove: (1) Bullet recovered from body, (2) Gun seized from accused, (3) Test firing conducted

### Section 47: Opinion as to Handwriting

Person familiar with handwriting can give opinion on its genuineness

**Not limited to experts**: Anyone acquainted with handwriting

### Section 48-49: Opinion on Existence of Custom, Right

Opinion of persons knowledgeable about custom relevant

### Section 51: Grounds of Opinion

Expert must state **grounds** of opinion

**Court Not Bound**: Expert opinion is relevant but **not conclusive**

**Court's Duty**: Evaluate expert opinion like any other evidence

**Landmark Case**: **State of Maharashtra v. Damu (2000)**
- Expert opinion helpful but not binding
- Court must independently evaluate
- Quality of expert's reasoning matters

## Character Evidence (Sections 52-55 Evidence Act / BSA)

### Section 52: Good Character Relevant in Criminal Cases

**Accused's good character** is **relevant** in criminal cases

**But**: Prosecution **cannot lead evidence** of accused's bad character (unless accused puts character in issue)

**Rationale**: Presumption of innocence; accused's past should not prejudice

### Section 53: Previous Convictions Relevant

**Previous convictions** relevant to:
- Enhance credibility of witness
- Show **system/habit** (if relevant under Section 14)

**In sentencing**: Previous convictions very relevant (aggravating factor)

### Section 54: Previous Good Character Relevant in Civil Cases

Good character relevant in civil cases too

### Section 55: Character as Affecting Damages

Character may affect quantum of damages in civil cases

## Statements Under CrPC/BNSS (Admissibility)

### Section 161 CrPC / 180 BNSS: Statement to Police During Investigation

**Not Admissible**: Statement made to police during investigation **cannot be used as evidence**

**But**: Can be used to **contradict witness** under Section 145 Evidence Act

**Limitation**: Statement to officer **below rank of Sub-Inspector** cannot be used for any purpose

**Landmark Case**: **Tahsildar Singh v. State of U.P. (1959)**
- Section 161 statements not substantive evidence
- Can only contradict/corroborate

### Section 164 CrPC / 183 BNSS: Statement Before Magistrate

**Admissible**: Statement recorded by Magistrate under Section 164 is admissible

**Conditions**:
1. **Magistrate** (Judicial or Metropolitan, not Executive)
2. **Not magistrate who will try** the case
3. Accused **warned** not bound to make statement
4. Warned statement may be used against him
5. Statement recorded **in writing**
6. Read over to person, signed

**Two Types of Section 164 Statements**:
1. **Confession** of accused (see Section 28 above)
2. **Statement of witness** (especially in sensitive cases like rape)

**Landmark Case**: **Shankaria v. State of Rajasthan (1978)**
- Section 164 procedure must be followed strictly
- Non-compliance may render statement inadmissible

### Section 313 CrPC / 367 BNSS: Statement of Accused

**Not Evidence**: Accused's statement under Section 313 **not evidence**

**Purpose**: Opportunity to explain incriminating circumstances

**Use**: Can be used to corroborate defense, but generally not incriminating

**See Protocol**: IL-CRIM-TRIAL.md

## Circumstantial Evidence

### What is Circumstantial Evidence?

**Indirect evidence**: Proves facts from which **inference** can be drawn about facts in issue

**Example**: Accused had **motive** + **opportunity** + found with **stolen goods** + gave **false alibi**
- Each circumstance is piece of puzzle
- Together point to guilt

### Admissibility

Circumstantial evidence **admissible** if relevant

### Conviction on Circumstantial Evidence

**Conviction possible** on circumstantial evidence alone **if**:
1. **Chain is complete** (no missing links)
2. Circumstances **consistent only with guilt** (not with innocence)
3. Circumstances **exclude every reasonable hypothesis except guilt**

**Landmark Case**: **Sharad Birdhichand Sarda v. State of Maharashtra (1984)** (Panchsheel Principles)

**Five Principles** for conviction on circumstantial evidence:
1. Circumstances from which guilt inferred must be **fully established**
2. Facts must be **consistent only with guilt**
3. Circumstances must be of **conclusive nature and tendency**
4. Must **exclude every hypothesis except guilt**
5. **Chain must be complete** - no missing link

**Application**: Widely followed in cases relying on circumstantial evidence

**Example Cases**:

**Hanumant v. State of M.P. (1952)**:
- Last seen together + motive + false alibi + concealment
- Conviction upheld on circumstantial evidence

**Bodh Raj v. State of J&K (2002)**:
- Missing links in circumstantial chain
- Conviction set aside

## Presumptions (Sections 86-114 Evidence Act / BSA)

### Types of Presumptions

**See Protocol**: IL-CRIM-BURDEN-PROOF.md for detailed analysis

**Three Types**:
1. **May presume** (Section 86-88, 114): Discretionary
2. **Shall presume** (Section 79-85): Mandatory but rebuttable
3. **Conclusive proof** (Section 41, 112): Irrebuttable

### Key Presumptions in Criminal Cases

**Section 79**: Judicial acts presumed regular

**Section 81**: Telegraphic messages presumed genuine

**Section 113A**: Abetment of suicide (dowry cases) - if cruelty proved + suicide within 7 years, presumption accused abetted

**Section 113B**: Dowry death - if death within 7 years + cruelty/harassment for dowry proved, presumption accused caused death

**Section 114**: Court may presume possession of recently stolen property = guilt (Illustration (a))

## Evidence Act 1872 vs BSA 2023: Admissibility Provisions

### Comparison

| Aspect | Evidence Act 1872 | BSA 2023 | Change |
|--------|-------------------|----------|---------|
| **Total sections** | 167 sections | 170 sections | **3 more sections** |
| **Relevancy** | Sections 5-55 | Sections 5-55 | **No change** |
| **Oral evidence** | Sections 59-60 | Sections 59-60 | **No change** |
| **Documentary evidence** | Sections 61-90 | Sections 61-90 | **Enhanced electronic provisions** |
| **Electronic records** | Sections 65A-65B (added 2000) | Sections 65A-65B | **Strengthened** |
| **Confessions** | Sections 24-30 | Sections 24-30 | **No change** |
| **Dying declaration** | Section 32 | Section 32 | **No change** |
| **Expert opinion** | Sections 45-51 | Sections 45-51 | **No change** |
| **Character** | Sections 52-55 | Sections 52-55 | **No change** |
| **Presumptions** | Sections 79-114 | Sections 79-114 | **No change** |

### Key BSA 2023 Enhancements

**Electronic Evidence**:
- Strengthened Section 65B provisions
- Recognition of digital signatures (Section 79A)
- Video recordings, electronic messages explicitly recognized

**Modern Communication**:
- Email, social media, messaging apps explicitly covered
- Hash values, metadata considered

**Otherwise**: **No material change** in admissibility principles
- All judicial precedents continue to apply
- Section numbers mostly unchanged

## Practical Compliance Checklist

### For Prosecution (Ensuring Evidence Admissible)

#### Investigation Stage
- [ ] **Oral evidence**: Identify and protect **eyewitnesses**
- [ ] **Documentary evidence**: Collect original documents (primary evidence)
- [ ] **Physical evidence**: Preserve with **chain of custody**
  - Seize properly (Section 100 CrPC / 99 BNSS)
  - Seal with witnesses
  - Store securely
  - Send to FSL with tamper-proof seal
- [ ] **Electronic evidence**: Obtain **Section 65B certificate**
  - From person in charge of computer
  - As soon as possible after seizure
- [ ] **Dying declaration**: If victim dying, record statement
  - Get medical certificate (fit state of mind)
  - Preferably before Magistrate
  - Otherwise to doctor, police (admissible but less weight)

#### Trial Stage
- [ ] **Examine witnesses**: Direct examination eliciting relevant facts
- [ ] **Produce documents**: Primary evidence (originals)
  - If original unavailable, explain (Section 65)
  - Produce certified copy or secondary evidence
- [ ] **Electronic evidence**: Ensure Section 65B certificate produced
- [ ] **Expert evidence**: Call forensic experts
  - Doctor for post-mortem
  - FSL expert for DNA, fingerprints, etc.
  - Ensure expert states grounds of opinion
- [ ] **Confessions** (if any): Ensure voluntary
  - Section 164 confession procedurally correct
  - Section 27 recovery proper (only portion leading to discovery used)
- [ ] **Chain of custody**: Prove unbroken
  - Seizure witness
  - IO who sent to FSL
  - FSL receipt
  - FSL expert

### For Defense (Challenging Admissibility)

#### Pre-Trial
- [ ] **Review evidence**: Identify inadmissible evidence
  - Confessions to police (Section 25)
  - Section 161 statements (not substantive)
  - Electronic evidence without Section 65B
  - Secondary evidence without explaining original's absence

#### Trial Stage
- [ ] **Object to inadmissible evidence**:
  - Confessions obtained improperly
  - Hearsay (without exception)
  - Electronic evidence lacking certificate
  - Secondary evidence without foundation
  - Irrelevant evidence
- [ ] **Cross-examine**:
  - Witnesses on inconsistencies, contradictions
  - Experts on qualifications, methodology, grounds
  - Police on chain of custody breaks
- [ ] **Present defense evidence**:
  - Alibi witnesses
  - Character witnesses (good character)
  - Expert rebuttal (if prosecution expert flawed)

### For Courts

#### Admissibility Determination
- [ ] **Two-part test**:
  - Is it fact in issue OR relevant fact? (Section 5)
  - Is it proved properly? (Best evidence, not hearsay, etc.)
- [ ] **Confessions**: Check voluntariness
  - If to police, inadmissible (Section 25)
  - If Section 164, check procedure
  - If coerced, inadmissible (Section 24)
- [ ] **Electronic evidence**: Ensure Section 65B certificate
  - Without certificate, inadmissible (Anvar P.V. case)
- [ ] **Expert opinion**: Evaluate critically
  - Qualifications of expert
  - Methodology used
  - Grounds stated
  - Not binding on court
- [ ] **Dying declaration**: Assess trustworthiness
  - Fit state of mind?
  - Voluntary?
  - Consistent?
- [ ] **Circumstantial evidence**: Apply Panchsheel Principles (Sharad Sarda)
  - Chain complete?
  - Circumstances consistent only with guilt?
  - Every hypothesis except guilt excluded?

## Common Errors and Misconceptions

### Errors

1. **Using Section 161 statements as evidence**: Inadmissible; only for contradiction

2. **Admitting confession to police**: Absolutely inadmissible (Section 25)

3. **Electronic evidence without Section 65B certificate**: Inadmissible (Anvar P.V.)

4. **Hearsay admitted without exception**: Violates Section 60

5. **Breaking chain of custody**: Evidence integrity compromised

### Misconceptions

**Misconception 1**: "Confession is king of evidence"
**Reality**: Only if **voluntary** and **properly recorded** (Section 164); confession to police inadmissible (Section 25)

**Misconception 2**: "Circumstantial evidence is weak"
**Reality**: Conviction possible on circumstantial evidence alone if **chain complete** and **consistent only with guilt** (Sharad Sarda)

**Misconception 3**: "Dying declaration requires corroboration"
**Reality**: NO - can be **sole basis** of conviction if **truthful and voluntary** (Laxman case)

**Misconception 4**: "Expert opinion is conclusive"
**Reality**: NO - relevant but **not binding**; court evaluates independently (Damu case)

**Misconception 5**: "Section 161 statements are evidence"
**Reality**: NO - not substantive evidence; only for **contradiction** under Section 145

## Conclusion

Evidence law is the **foundation of fact-finding** in criminal justice, ensuring:
- **Only reliable evidence** considered
- **Fair opportunity** to both sides
- **Truth** discovered through proper procedure
- **Rights protected** (Article 20(3), 21)

**Core Principles**:
1. **Only relevant evidence admissible** (Section 5)
2. **Oral evidence must be direct** (Section 60) - hearsay inadmissible (with exceptions)
3. **Primary evidence preferred** (Section 62)
4. **Electronic evidence needs Section 65B certificate** (Anvar P.V.)
5. **Confessions to police inadmissible** (Section 25)
6. **Section 164 confessions admissible** if voluntary and procedurally correct
7. **Section 27 discovery exception** to Section 25-26
8. **Dying declaration** can be sole basis (Laxman) if truthful
9. **Expert opinion relevant** but not conclusive (Damu)
10. **Circumstantial evidence** can sustain conviction if **Panchsheel Principles** met (Sharad Sarda)

**Evidence Act 1872 vs BSA 2023**:
- **Minimal change** in admissibility principles
- **Enhanced electronic evidence** provisions
- All precedents continue to apply

**Practical Importance**:
- Prosecution must ensure evidence **properly collected and presented**
- Defense must **challenge inadmissible evidence**
- Courts must act as **gatekeepers** - admit only reliable, relevant evidence

**Ultimate Goal**: **Determine truth** while ensuring **fair trial** and protecting constitutional rights.
