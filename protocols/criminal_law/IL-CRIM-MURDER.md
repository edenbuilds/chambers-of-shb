# IL-CRIM-MURDER

**Protocol ID**: IL-CRIM-MURDER
**Domain**: Criminal Law > Crimes Against Person
**Version**: 1.0.0

**Statutory Basis**: Indian Penal Code (IPC) Sections 300-302 | Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023 Sections 101-103

## Overview

**Murder** is the most serious offense against the person under Indian criminal law, distinguished from culpable homicide by the **degree of intention** and specific circumstances. This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on:

- Definition and essential ingredients of murder
- Distinction between murder and culpable homicide not amounting to murder
- Exceptions that reduce murder to culpable homicide
- Punishment provisions (death penalty vs life imprisonment)
- IPC to BNS transition (effective July 1, 2024)
- Landmark judicial precedents establishing key principles

---

## Statutory Framework

### IPC (Pre-July 1, 2024)

**Section 300**: Definition of Murder
**Section 301**: Culpable homicide by causing death of person other than person whose death was intended
**Section 302**: Punishment for murder

### BNS (Post-July 1, 2024)

**Section 101**: Definition of Murder (corresponds to IPC 300)
**Section 102**: Death caused by act done with intent to cause death of another (corresponds to IPC 301)
**Section 103**: Punishment for murder (corresponds to IPC 302)

## Definition of Murder (IPC Section 300 / BNS Section 101)

### Essential Ingredients

For an act to constitute murder, **ALL four ingredients** must be present:

1. **Culpable Homicide**: Death of a human being caused by the accused
2. **Mens Rea (Mental Element)**: Specific intention or knowledge as enumerated below
3. **No Exception Applies**: None of the 5 exceptions in Section 300 apply
4. **No General Exception**: No general exception (IPC Sections 76-106) applies

### Four Clauses of Murder (Section 300)

Culpable homicide is **murder** if the act by which death is caused is done with the intention of causing death in the following circumstances:

#### **Firstly**: Intention to Cause Death
- **Test**: Accused's subjective intention was to cause death
- **Mens Rea**: Direct intention (purposeful killing)
- **Example**: A shoots B in the head at point-blank range → Clear intention to cause death

#### **Secondly**: Intention to Cause Such Bodily Injury as Offender Knows to Be Likely to Cause Death
- **Test**: Accused intended to cause specific injury + knew that injury was likely to cause death
- **Mens Rea**: Knowledge that the specific injury would likely cause death
- **Example**: A stabs B in the chest (vital organ) knowing such injury causes death

#### **Thirdly**: Intention to Cause Bodily Injury Sufficient in Ordinary Course of Nature to Cause Death
- **Test**: Injury intended is objectively sufficient to cause death in the ordinary course
- **Mens Rea**: Intention to cause that specific injury (knowledge of likelihood not required)
- **Objective Standard**: Court determines whether injury is ordinarily fatal
- **Example**: A inflicts 10 deep stab wounds in vital organs → Objectively sufficient to cause death

#### **Fourthly**: Knowledge that Act is So Imminently Dangerous that in All Probability It Will Cause Death
- **Test**: Accused knew the act (not specific injury) would probably cause death
- **Mens Rea**: Knowledge of extreme danger without excuse for incurring risk
- **Example**: A fires into a crowd knowing that bullets will probably kill someone

## Distinction: Murder vs. Culpable Homicide Not Amounting to Murder

### Foundational Principle (Pokhar Singh v. State of Rajasthan, 1973)

> **"All murder is culpable homicide, but not all culpable homicide is murder."**

The distinction lies in the **degree of intention** and **knowledge**:

| Aspect | **Culpable Homicide (IPC 299)** | **Murder (IPC 300)** |
|--------|----------------------------------|----------------------|
| **Intention** | Intention to cause death OR such bodily injury as is **likely** to cause death | Intention to cause death OR such bodily injury as accused **knows** to be likely to cause death |
| **Knowledge** | Knows act is **likely** to cause death | Knows act is **so imminently dangerous** that it will **in all probability** cause death |
| **Degree** | Likelihood (probability < 50%) | High probability (> 50%) or certainty |
| **Punishment** | Life imprisonment OR imprisonment up to 10 years + fine | Death penalty OR life imprisonment (mandatory minimum life) |

### Judicial Test (Virsa Singh v. State of Punjab, 1958)

**Three-Step Analysis**:

**Step 1**: Establish that bodily injury is present
**Step 2**: Determine nature of injury (IPC Section 300 Secondly/Thirdly?)
**Step 3**: Apply exception (if any) to reduce to culpable homicide

## Five Exceptions to Murder (Section 300 Exceptions)

These exceptions **reduce murder** to **culpable homicide not amounting to murder** (punishable under IPC Section 304):

### Exception 1: Grave and Sudden Provocation

**Requirements**:
1. **Provocation** caused accused to lose self-control
2. **Grave and sudden** (not trivial, not premeditated revenge)
3. **Deprived of self-control** at time of act
4. **Not sought or voluntarily provoked** by accused

**Test**: Would a **reasonable person** of the same class/circumstances have been provoked?

**Limitations** (Provocation does NOT apply if):
- Provocation given by lawful authority (police arrest)
- Provocation given in obedience to law
- Self-induced by accused to create defense

**Landmark Case**: **K.M. Nanavati v. State of Maharashtra (1962)**

**Facts**: Naval officer Nanavati discovered wife's adultery with Prem Ahuja. After 2-hour gap, Nanavati shot Ahuja with service revolver (3 bullets).

**Issue**: Did 2-hour gap negate "sudden" provocation?

**Holding**: Supreme Court held provocation NOT sudden (2-hour gap allowed "cooling time"). Conviction for murder upheld (later pardoned by Governor).

**Principle**: "Sudden" means immediacy; time gap suggests premeditation, not loss of control.

---

### Exception 2: Private Defence (Exceeding Right)

**Scenario**: Accused exercised private defence BUT:
- Used more force than necessary (excessive force)
- Acted in good faith without intending more harm than necessary

**Reduction**: Murder → Culpable homicide not amounting to murder

**Example**: A attacked by B with stick. A shoots B (killing him) when knocking B down with stick would suffice. A used excessive force but in good faith → Section 304, not 302.

**Cross-Reference**: IL-CRIM-GENERAL-EXCEPTIONS.md (Private Defence Sections 96-106)

---

### Exception 3: Public Servant Exceeding Lawful Powers

**Scenario**: Public servant acting for advancement of justice but:
- Exceeded lawful powers
- Acted in good faith without malice

**Reduction**: Murder → Culpable homicide not amounting to murder

**Example**: Police officer shoots fleeing suspect (non-violent offender) believing suspect armed. Officer acted in good faith but exceeded lawful power → Section 304.

---

### Exception 4: Sudden Fight (Heat of Passion)

**Requirements**:
1. **Sudden fight** (unpremeditated quarrel)
2. **Heat of passion** (not cold-blooded)
3. **No undue advantage taken**
4. **Not acted in cruel/unusual manner**

**Key Difference from Exception 1**: No need to prove provocation from victim; mutual combat suffices.

**Example**: A and B quarrel in bar. Both pick up bottles. A smashes bottle into B's head causing death. Sudden fight + heat of passion + no cruel manner → Section 304.

**Landmark Case**: **Jai Prakash v. State of Uttar Pradesh (1983)**

**Principle**: Sudden fight means both parties involved; no premeditation; fight escalated from trivial dispute.

---

### Exception 5: Consent (Person Above 18 Years)

**Scenario**: Victim (18+ years) consented to death OR risk of death

**Requirements**:
1. Victim 18+ years of age
2. Consent freely given (no coercion, undue influence, intoxication)
3. Death caused as consented

**Reduction**: Murder → Culpable homicide not amounting to murder

**Example**: Euthanasia case (mercy killing). Terminally ill patient (18+ years) begs doctor to end suffering. Doctor administers lethal dose with consent → Section 304 (NOT legal in India, but if charged, Exception 5 applies).

**Note**: **Euthanasia is NOT legal** in India (passive withdrawal of treatment allowed per Aruna Shanbaug case 2011, not active euthanasia).

## Punishment for Murder (IPC Section 302 / BNS Section 103)

### Sentencing Options

**Section 302**: Punishment for murder

> "Whoever commits murder shall be punished with **death** OR **imprisonment for life**, and shall also be liable to **fine**."

### Death Penalty vs. Life Imprisonment

**Constitutional Validity**: Death penalty upheld as constitutional (Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab, 1980)

**"Rarest of Rare" Doctrine** (Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab, 1980):

Death penalty should be imposed only in **"rarest of rare cases"** where:
1. **Crime Method**: Extremely brutal, shocking to collective conscience
2. **Victim**: Helpless, innocent (child, elderly)
3. **Motive**: Heinous (e.g., monetary greed, sexual lust)
4. **Magnitude**: Multiple murders, planned execution
5. **Societal Impact**: Creates insecurity, collective condemnation

**Aggravating vs. Mitigating Circumstances**:

**Aggravating**:
- Extreme brutality
- Multiple victims
- Victim helpless (child, elderly, disabled)
- Pre-planned execution
- Killed witness to cover crime

**Mitigating**:
- Young age of accused
- No prior criminal record
- Acted under provocation (partial)
- Mental illness/diminished capacity
- Possibility of reformation

**Judicial Trend**: Post-2014, Supreme Court has **reduced death sentences** to life imprisonment in several cases, emphasizing reformation over retribution (Shankar Kisanrao Khade v. State of Maharashtra, 2013).

## IPC to BNS Transition (Effective July 1, 2024)

### Section Mapping

| IPC Section | BNS Section | Change |
|-------------|-------------|--------|
| IPC 300 | BNS 101 | Definition unchanged |
| IPC 301 | BNS 102 | Transfer of malice unchanged |
| IPC 302 | BNS 103 | Punishment unchanged (death OR life + fine) |

### Transitional Provisions

**Rule**: Offenses committed **before July 1, 2024** continue to be prosecuted under **IPC** (old law applies).

**Example**:
- Murder committed: June 15, 2024 → Tried under **IPC Section 302**
- Murder committed: July 5, 2024 → Tried under **BNS Section 103**

**Pending Cases**: Trials pending as of July 1, 2024 continue under IPC (no retrospective BNS application).

## Landmark Cases: Principles Established

### 1. **Virsa Singh v. State of Punjab (1958)**

**Facts**: Virsa Singh stabbed Kirpa Singh twice (one blow penetrated left kidney, causing death). Dispute over land.

**Issue**: IPC Section 300 "Thirdly" (bodily injury sufficient in ordinary course of nature to cause death)?

**Holding**: Supreme Court convicted under Section 302. Single blow to vital organ (kidney) is sufficient in ordinary course to cause death.

**Principle**:
- **Objective Test**: Court determines whether injury is ordinarily fatal (doctor testimony, medical evidence critical).
- **No Need to Prove Knowledge**: Under "Thirdly," prosecution need NOT prove accused knew injury would cause death; objective sufficiency of injury suffices.

**Application**: Stab to vital organ (heart, liver, kidney, lungs) → Section 300 "Thirdly" murder.

---

### 2. **K.M. Nanavati v. State of Maharashtra (1962)**

**Facts**: Commander Nanavati (Indian Navy) learned of wife Sylvia's affair with Prem Ahuja. After 2-hour gap (during which Nanavati drove home, took service revolver, went to Ahuja's flat), he shot Ahuja 3 times.

**Defense**: Grave and sudden provocation (Exception 1).

**Prosecution**: 2-hour gap = premeditation, not sudden provocation.

**Holding**: Supreme Court upheld murder conviction. "Sudden" means immediate; 2-hour gap allowed cooling time, negating loss of self-control.

**Principle**:
- **Temporal Element**: Time gap between provocation and act is critical. Even minutes can negate "suddenness" if accused had time to reflect.
- **Subjective + Objective Test**: Court examines (i) accused's actual mental state + (ii) whether reasonable person would have cooled down.

**Aftermath**: Nanavati pardoned by Governor of Maharashtra (political controversy). Last jury trial in India; jury system abolished 1962.

---

### 3. **Bachan Singh v. State of Punjab (1980)**

**Facts**: Bachan Singh murdered 3 persons (2 adults, 1 child) of Desa Singh's family over land dispute. Planned execution.

**Issue**: Constitutional validity of death penalty under Article 21 (Right to Life).

**Holding**: Supreme Court upheld death penalty as constitutional but restricted to **"rarest of rare cases."**

**Principle**:
- **Sentencing Guidelines**: Death penalty only when:
  1. Alternative option (life imprisonment) unquestionably foreclosed
  2. Crime shocks collective conscience of society
  3. Aggravating circumstances outweigh mitigating circumstances
- **Balancing Test**: Judge must balance aggravating (brutality, helpless victim) vs. mitigating (age, no prior record) factors.

**Impact**: Post-Bachan Singh, death sentences **reduced significantly** (80%+ commuted to life imprisonment).

---

### 4. **State of Andhra Pradesh v. Rayavarapu Punnayya (1976)**

**Facts**: Accused administered abortifacient to pregnant woman causing her death (not intending woman's death, only abortion).

**Issue**: Intention to cause abortion (not death) → Murder or culpable homicide?

**Holding**: Conviction under IPC Section 304 (culpable homicide not amounting to murder). Accused did not intend woman's death; intended only abortion.

**Principle**:
- **Specific Intention**: Section 300 requires intention to cause **death** or specific injury known to cause death. Intention to cause different injury (abortion) NOT murder.
- **Transfer of Mens Rea**: Intention to cause X (abortion) does NOT automatically transfer to Y (death), UNLESS X is known to likely cause Y.

---

### 5. **Jai Prakash v. State of Uttar Pradesh (1983)**

**Facts**: Sudden quarrel over trivial matter. Both parties picked up bricks. Jai Prakash hit victim's head with brick, causing death.

**Defense**: Exception 4 (sudden fight).

**Holding**: Supreme Court reduced murder to culpable homicide under Exception 4. Sudden fight, heat of passion, no undue advantage, no cruelty.

**Principle**:
- **Sudden Fight Elements**:
  1. No premeditation (escalated from trivial dispute)
  2. Both parties involved (mutual combat)
  3. Heat of passion (not calculated revenge)
  4. No cruel/unusual manner (single blow)
- **Time Element**: "Sudden" in Exception 4 is more liberal than Exception 1 (provocation). Brief quarrel → escalation → act can qualify.

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Direct Intention (Section 300 "Firstly")

**Scenario**: A and B are business rivals. A hires contract killer to murder B. Killer shoots B 5 times in head and chest. B dies.

**Analysis**:
- **Intention**: A's subjective intention = cause B's death (hired killer, shot 5 times in vital areas)
- **Knowledge**: A knew shooting in head/chest would cause death
- **Exception**: None apply (no provocation, no fight, no consent)

**Charge**: **IPC Section 302** (murder under "Firstly") + **IPC Section 120B** (criminal conspiracy)

**Punishment**: **Death penalty OR life imprisonment** (likely life, as not "rarest of rare" - single victim, monetary rivalry).

---

### Example 2: Knowledge of Likelihood (Section 300 "Secondly")

**Scenario**: A stabs B in abdomen (not vital organ) with kitchen knife. A knows abdominal stab can cause death (infection, blood loss). B dies 3 days later from sepsis.

**Analysis**:
- **Intention**: A intended to stab abdomen (specific injury)
- **Knowledge**: A knew abdominal stab **likely** to cause death (not certain, but likely due to infection/blood loss)
- **Exception**: None apply

**Charge**: **IPC Section 302** (murder under "Secondly")

**Punishment**: Life imprisonment (not death penalty - single blow, not extreme brutality).

---

### Example 3: Objective Sufficiency (Section 300 "Thirdly")

**Scenario**: A inflicts 15 deep wounds on B's chest, abdomen, and neck with machete. Medical evidence: individually, each wound not fatal, but cumulatively caused exsanguination (blood loss). A claims did not intend to kill, only to "teach lesson."

**Analysis**:
- **Intention**: A need not intend death; need only intend the injuries inflicted
- **Objective Test**: Are 15 deep machete wounds to vital areas sufficient in ordinary course of nature to cause death? **YES** (medical evidence: exsanguination certain from such injuries)
- **Exception**: None apply

**Charge**: **IPC Section 302** (murder under "Thirdly")

**Principle** (Virsa Singh): Accused's claim "did not intend to kill" **irrelevant** under "Thirdly." Objective sufficiency of injury determines guilt.

---

### Example 4: Imminently Dangerous Act (Section 300 "Fourthly")

**Scenario**: A sets fire to crowded cinema hall during movie. 50 persons die in stampede and flames. A's motive: insurance fraud (cinema owner). A did not intend specific persons' deaths but knew act would probably cause multiple deaths.

**Analysis**:
- **Act**: Setting fire to crowded hall = imminently dangerous
- **Knowledge**: A knew fire in crowded hall would **in all probability** cause deaths (not specific to 50 victims, but deaths generally)
- **No Excuse**: Insurance fraud is NOT excuse for incurring risk of mass death

**Charge**: **IPC Section 302** (murder under "Fourthly") x 50 counts

**Punishment**: **Death penalty** (rarest of rare - multiple innocent victims, premeditated for financial gain, shocking to conscience).

---

### Example 5: Exception 1 (Provocation) - Successful Defense

**Scenario**: A returns home unexpectedly and finds B (spouse's lover) in bed with A's spouse. A immediately picks up bedside lamp and strikes B's head, causing death. Time gap: < 1 minute.

**Analysis**:
- **Prima Facie**: Murder (intention to cause injury known to cause death)
- **Exception 1 Test**:
  - Provocation: Adultery discovered in flagrante delicto (grave)
  - Sudden: < 1 minute gap (immediate)
  - Loss of self-control: Reasonable person would lose control
  - Not self-induced: B's act (adultery) was provocation
- **Applies**: YES

**Charge**: **IPC Section 304 Part I** (culpable homicide not amounting to murder)

**Punishment**: Life imprisonment OR imprisonment up to 10 years + fine (NOT Section 302).

**Contrast**: If A discovered adultery, left, returned 2 hours later and killed B → Section 302 (murder) per Nanavati case.

## Compliance Checklist: Murder Investigation & Prosecution

### For Investigating Officer (Police)

**Scene of Crime**:
- [ ] Secure crime scene immediately (prevent contamination)
- [ ] Photograph/video from multiple angles (body position, injuries, weapon)
- [ ] Collect physical evidence (weapon, blood samples, fingerprints)
- [ ] Measure distances (body to weapon, entry/exit points)
- [ ] Note weather, lighting conditions (affects witness visibility)

**Post-Mortem Examination**:
- [ ] Ensure autopsy conducted by experienced forensic pathologist
- [ ] Preserve viscera for toxicology (poison, alcohol, drugs)
- [ ] Document all injuries (entry/exit wounds, defense injuries, ligature marks)
- [ ] Obtain opinion on: (i) Cause of death, (ii) Manner of death, (iii) Time of death

**Witness Statements**:
- [ ] Record FIR under Section 154 CrPC (dying declaration if victim conscious before death)
- [ ] Examine eye-witnesses under Section 161 CrPC
- [ ] Record statements of medical experts (doctors, forensic analysts)
- [ ] Identify and examine hostile witnesses (relatives, business associates)

**Accused's Statement**:
- [ ] Arrest under Section 41 CrPC (warrant or without warrant if cognizable offense)
- [ ] Inform accused of grounds of arrest + right to legal counsel
- [ ] Medical examination of accused (injuries suggesting fight/defense)
- [ ] Record accused's statement under Section 313 CrPC (after investigation complete)

**Charge Sheet**:
- [ ] File charge sheet under Section 173 CrPC within 60/90 days (accused in custody/bail)
- [ ] Charge under Section 302 (murder) + relevant sections (conspiracy 120B, destruction of evidence 201)
- [ ] Submit evidence list (physical evidence, witness statements, post-mortem report)

---

### For Public Prosecutor

**Framing of Charges**:
- [ ] Draft charge under Section 302 IPC (specify which clause: Firstly/Secondly/Thirdly/Fourthly)
- [ ] Include alternative charge under Section 304 (if exceptions may apply)
- [ ] Include related offenses (conspiracy, attempt, abetment)

**Trial Strategy**:
- [ ] Establish corpus delicti (body of crime: death occurred + caused by accused)
- [ ] Prove mens rea (intention/knowledge per Section 300)
- [ ] Anticipate defense (alibi, provocation, self-defense, insanity)
- [ ] Negate exceptions (prove provocation not sudden, defense not excessive, etc.)

**Evidence Presentation**:
- [ ] Call eye-witnesses (establish presence, weapon used, manner of attack)
- [ ] Call medical experts (cause of death, nature of injuries, sufficiency to cause death)
- [ ] Present physical evidence (weapon, blood-stained clothes, forensic reports)
- [ ] Dying declaration (if available, admissible under Section 32 Evidence Act)

**Sentencing Arguments** (Post-Conviction):
- [ ] Prepare aggravating circumstances (brutality, helpless victim, planned execution)
- [ ] Counter mitigating circumstances (age, prior record, reformation possibility)
- [ ] Argue for death penalty (only if rarest of rare) OR life imprisonment

---

### For Defense Counsel

**Pre-Trial**:
- [ ] Verify legality of arrest (Section 41 CrPC complied?)
- [ ] Apply for bail (Section 437/439 CrPC - unlikely in murder, but if weak evidence)
- [ ] Inspect charge sheet + evidence (Section 207 CrPC - supply of documents)
- [ ] Identify weaknesses (missing witnesses, contradictory statements, procedural lapses)

**Trial Defense**:
- [ ] Challenge identification (if eye-witness identification weak)
- [ ] Challenge forensic evidence (chain of custody broken, contamination)
- [ ] Plead general exception (private defense, accident, insanity Section 84)
- [ ] Plead special exception (provocation Exception 1, sudden fight Exception 4)

**Alternative Strategy**:
- [ ] Seek reduction to Section 304 (culpable homicide not amounting to murder)
- [ ] Plea bargaining (NOT available for offenses punishable with death/life per Section 265D CrPC)

**Sentencing Arguments** (Post-Conviction):
- [ ] Present mitigating circumstances (age, first offender, family circumstances)
- [ ] Argue against death penalty (reformative potential, not rarest of rare)
- [ ] Request psychiatric evaluation (diminished capacity, mental illness)
