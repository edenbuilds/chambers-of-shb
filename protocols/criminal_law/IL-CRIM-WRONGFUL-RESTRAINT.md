# IL-CRIM-WRONGFUL-RESTRAINT

**Protocol ID**: IL-CRIM-WRONGFUL-RESTRAINT
**Domain**: Criminal Law > Crimes Against Person
**Version**: 1.0.0
**Last Updated**: 2025-12-06
**Statutory Basis**: Indian Penal Code (IPC) Sections 339-348 | Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (BNS) 2023 Sections 126-135

---

## Overview

**Wrongful Restraint and Wrongful Confinement** are offenses against personal liberty under Indian criminal law. They protect an individual's freedom of movement, ranging from temporary obstruction (wrongful restraint) to complete deprivation of liberty (wrongful confinement). This protocol covers:

- Definitions and distinction between restraint vs. confinement
- Punishment provisions (escalating with duration and circumstances)
- Aggravated forms (for extortion, slavery, secret confinement)
- Landmark cases and constitutional implications (Article 21 - Right to Liberty)

---

## Statutory Framework

### IPC (Pre-July 1, 2024)

**Section 339**: Wrongful restraint
**Section 340**: Wrongful confinement
**Sections 341-348**: Punishment provisions (simple and aggravated forms)

### BNS (Post-July 1, 2024)

**Sections 126-135**: Corresponds to IPC wrongful restraint/confinement provisions

## Wrongful Restraint (IPC Section 339 / BNS Section 126)

### Statutory Text

**Section 339**: "Whoever voluntarily obstructs any person so as to prevent that person from proceeding in any direction in which that person has a right to proceed, is said to wrongfully restrain that person."

### Essential Elements

1. **Voluntary Obstruction**: Accused intentionally obstructs victim
2. **Prevents Proceeding**: Victim unable to proceed in desired direction
3. **Right to Proceed**: Victim had lawful right to proceed in that direction
4. **No Total Deprivation**: Movement restricted, not completely prevented (if total deprivation → wrongful confinement)

---

### Element Analysis

#### 1. Voluntary Obstruction

**Voluntary**: With knowledge and intention to obstruct

**Obstruction** (Physical or Constructive):
- **Physical**: Standing in doorway, blocking path, holding victim's arm
- **Constructive**: Threat of force ("If you leave, I'll beat you"), psychological intimidation

**Not Obstruction**: Mere request or persuasion without force/threat.

---

#### 2. Prevents Proceeding

**Prevents**: Makes it impossible or substantially difficult to proceed

**Direction**: Any direction victim wishes to go (not just specific path)

**Temporary Obstruction Suffices**: Even momentary obstruction = wrongful restraint (duration not critical element)

**Example**: A stands in office doorway preventing B from leaving for 2 minutes → Wrongful restraint (even though temporary).

---

#### 3. Right to Proceed

**Lawful Right**: Victim must have legal right to proceed in that direction

**No Right to Proceed** (No Wrongful Restraint):
- Trespasser prevented from entering private property
- Criminal prevented from fleeing arrest by police
- Student prevented from leaving classroom during exam (lawful authority)

**Example**: Security guard prevents trespasser from entering office building → NOT wrongful restraint (trespasser has no right to proceed).

---

#### 4. Restraint vs. Confinement

**Wrongful Restraint**: Partial obstruction (victim can still move in other directions)

**Wrongful Confinement**: Total deprivation of liberty (victim cannot move in any direction)

**Illustration from Section 339**:
- A obstructs path on public road, B can walk around → **Restraint**
- A locks B in room, B cannot exit → **Confinement**

---

### Punishment (IPC Section 341 / BNS Section 127)

> "Whoever wrongfully restrains any person shall be punished with simple imprisonment up to **1 month** OR fine up to **₹500** OR both."

**Nature**: Non-cognizable (police cannot arrest without warrant), bailable, triable by any Magistrate.

---

## Wrongful Confinement (IPC Section 340 / BNS Section 128)

### Statutory Text

**Section 340**: "Whoever wrongfully restrains any person in such a manner as to prevent that person from proceeding **beyond certain circumscribing limits**, is said to **wrongfully confine** that person."

### Essential Elements

1. **Wrongful Restraint**: All elements of Section 339 satisfied
2. **Circumscribing Limits**: Victim prevented from proceeding beyond defined boundary
3. **Total Deprivation**: Victim cannot move in **any** direction beyond limits

---

### Circumscribing Limits

**Boundary Definition**:
- **Physical**: Locked room, fenced yard, guarded compound
- **Constructive**: Threats preventing victim from crossing boundary ("If you cross this line, I'll shoot you")

**Size Irrelevant**: Limits can be large area (entire building) or small (single room)

**Duration**: Even temporary confinement = wrongful confinement (1 hour confinement suffices)

---

### Illustrations from Section 340

1. **A locks Z in room** → Wrongful confinement (Z cannot exit room)
2. **A causes Z to go within walled space, prevents Z from climbing wall** → Wrongful confinement (walled space = circumscribing limits)
3. **A prevents Z from leaving town by posting men at all exits** → Wrongful confinement (town = circumscribing limits, though large area)

---

### Punishment (IPC Section 342 / BNS Section 129)

> "Whoever wrongfully confines any person shall be punished with imprisonment up to **1 year** OR fine up to **₹1,000** OR both."

**Nature**: Cognizable (police can arrest without warrant), bailable, triable by any Magistrate.

---

## Distinction: Wrongful Restraint vs. Wrongful Confinement

| Aspect | **Wrongful Restraint (IPC 339)** | **Wrongful Confinement (IPC 340)** |
|--------|----------------------------------|-----------------------------------|
| **Degree of Obstruction** | Partial obstruction (victim can move in some directions) | Total deprivation of liberty (victim cannot move beyond limits) |
| **Circumscribing Limits** | No defined boundary (just obstruction of specific path) | Defined boundary (room, building, area) |
| **Example** | A blocks doorway, B can go to window → Restraint | A locks room, B cannot exit anywhere → Confinement |
| **Punishment** | Simple imprisonment 1 month OR ₹500 fine | Imprisonment 1 year OR ₹1,000 fine |
| **Cognizance** | **Non-cognizable** (warrant required for arrest) | **Cognizable** (arrest without warrant allowed) |
| **Seriousness** | Less serious (minor obstruction) | More serious (complete deprivation of liberty) |

## Aggravated Forms of Wrongful Confinement

### Confinement for 3+ Days (IPC Section 343 / BNS Section 130)

**Duration**: Wrongful confinement for **3 or more days**

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **2 years** OR fine OR both

**Rationale**: Extended confinement increases harm to victim (psychological trauma, loss of livelihood).

---

### Confinement for 10+ Days (IPC Section 344 / BNS Section 131)

**Duration**: Wrongful confinement for **10 or more days**

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **3 years** + fine

**Aggravation**: Prolonged confinement treated as serious offense.

---

### Secret Confinement (IPC Section 346 / BNS Section 133)

**Elements**:
1. Wrongful confinement
2. **In secret** manner (victim hidden, location concealed from authorities/family)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **2 years** + fine

**Rationale**: Secret confinement prevents rescue, increases danger to victim (potential murder/trafficking).

**Example**: A kidnaps B, confines B in underground cellar unknown to police/family → Section 346.

---

### Confinement to Extort (IPC Section 347 / BNS Section 134)

**Elements**:
1. Wrongful confinement
2. **Intent**: To extort property, valuable security, OR to compel victim to do illegal act

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **3 years** + fine

**Example**: A confines B in room, demands ₹10 lakh ransom for release → Section 347.

---

### Confinement to Extort Confession (IPC Section 348 / BNS Section 135)

**Elements**:
1. Wrongful confinement
2. **Intent**: To extort confession OR compel victim to restore property

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **3 years** + fine

**Example**: Police illegally confine suspect in lock-up for 24 hours to extract confession → Section 348 (illegal police custody).

## Constitutional Dimensions: Article 21 (Right to Liberty)

### Article 21 Protection

**Constitution of India, Article 21**: "No person shall be deprived of his **life or personal liberty** except according to procedure established by law."

**Personal Liberty** includes:
- Freedom of movement (right to go anywhere within India)
- Freedom from arbitrary arrest/detention
- Right against wrongful confinement

---

### Wrongful Confinement as Violation of Article 21

**Landmark Case**: **Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)**

**Principle**: Article 21 "personal liberty" is not confined to freedom from arrest/detention; includes all facets of personal freedom.

**Application to Wrongful Confinement**:
- Wrongful confinement violates **constitutional right** to personal liberty (Article 21)
- Victim can file **writ petition** (Habeas Corpus) under Article 226 for immediate release
- Criminal prosecution under IPC Sections 342-348 runs parallel to constitutional remedy

---

### Habeas Corpus Writ (Article 226)

**Purpose**: Immediate release of person wrongfully confined

**Procedure**:
1. Victim or relative files writ petition in High Court
2. Court issues notice to detainer (person confining victim)
3. Detainer must produce victim before court + justify confinement
4. If confinement unlawful → Court orders immediate release

**Timeframe**: Expedited hearing (24-48 hours for hearing, immediate release if no justification)

**Example**: A confines B in basement. B's wife files Habeas Corpus writ → High Court orders A to produce B + release immediately.

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Wrongful Restraint (IPC Section 341)

**Scenario**: A (security guard) blocks B from entering office building because B is not wearing formal attire (office has dress code). A stands at entrance preventing B from entering. B has valid office ID and right to enter.

**Analysis**:
- **Obstruction**: A physically blocks B's entry
- **Prevents Proceeding**: B cannot proceed into office
- **Right to Proceed**: B has employment contract → Right to enter office (dress code is internal policy, not legal bar to entry)
- **Restraint, Not Confinement**: B can leave premises, just cannot enter → Restraint only

**Charge**: **IPC Section 341** (wrongful restraint)

**Punishment**: Simple imprisonment 1 month OR ₹500 fine.

---

### Example 2: Wrongful Confinement (IPC Section 342)

**Scenario**: A (employer) locks B (employee) in office cabin during work hours because B is trying to resign and leave company. A wants B to complete pending work before leaving. A locks door, takes key. B confined for 3 hours.

**Analysis**:
- **Wrongful Restraint**: A obstructs B from leaving
- **Circumscribing Limits**: Cabin is defined boundary (room)
- **Total Deprivation**: B cannot exit cabin in any direction
- **Duration**: 3 hours (exceeds momentary confinement)

**Charge**: **IPC Section 342** (wrongful confinement)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to 1 year OR ₹1,000 fine.

**Note**: Employer has NO legal right to confine employee, even to complete work. Resignation is lawful act.

---

### Example 3: Confinement for 10+ Days (IPC Section 344)

**Scenario**: A confines B in farmhouse basement for 15 days. A provides food/water but does not allow B to leave. No ransom demand, no extortion (personal dispute).

**Analysis**:
- **Wrongful Confinement**: B confined in basement (circumscribing limits)
- **Duration**: 15 days (exceeds 10 days)
- **Aggravated Form**: Section 344 applies (10+ days confinement)

**Charge**: **IPC Section 344** (wrongful confinement for 10+ days)

**Punishment**: Imprisonment up to **3 years** + fine.

---

### Example 4: Secret Confinement (IPC Section 346)

**Scenario**: A kidnaps B (wealthy businessman) and confines B in secret underground bunker. A does not inform police or B's family of B's location. B held for 5 days before police rescue.

**Analysis**:
- **Wrongful Confinement**: B confined in bunker
- **Secret**: Location hidden from authorities and family
- **Duration**: 5 days (exceeds 3 days, but less than 10 days)

**Charge**: **IPC Section 346** (wrongful confinement in secret) + **IPC Section 363** (kidnapping, if applicable)

**Punishment**: Section 346: Imprisonment up to 2 years + fine; Section 363: Up to 7 years + fine.

---

### Example 5: Confinement to Extort Confession (IPC Section 348)

**Scenario**: Police arrest suspect without warrant, confine suspect in police station for 48 hours without magisterial remand. Police beat suspect to extract confession to theft. Suspect confesses under torture.

**Analysis**:
- **Wrongful Confinement**: Illegal custody without magisterial remand (CrPC Section 57 - 24-hour limit)
- **Intent**: To extort confession (torture for confession)
- **Illegal Police Action**: Custodial torture violates Article 21 + IPC 348

**Charge**: **IPC Section 348** (wrongful confinement to extort confession) + **IPC Section 330** (voluntarily causing hurt to extort confession)

**Punishment**: Section 348: Up to 3 years + fine; Section 330: Up to 7 years + fine.

**Constitutional Remedy**: Victim can file writ petition for compensation + prosecution of police officers (DK Basu Guidelines on custodial torture).

## Landmark Cases

### 1. **Maneka Gandhi v. Union of India (1978)**

**Facts**: Maneka Gandhi's passport impounded by Government of India under Passports Act. She challenged passport seizure as violation of Article 21 (right to travel abroad = personal liberty).

**Issue**: Does "personal liberty" (Article 21) include freedom of movement, including international travel?

**Holding**: Supreme Court held "personal liberty" in Article 21 is not restricted to freedom from arrest/detention; includes **all facets of personal freedom** (movement, livelihood, dignity).

**Principle**:
- **Broad Interpretation**: Article 21 personal liberty = comprehensive protection of individual freedom
- **Wrongful Confinement Implication**: Any deprivation of personal liberty (including wrongful confinement) violates Article 21
- **Dual Remedy**: Victim has (i) Constitutional remedy (Habeas Corpus writ), (ii) Criminal remedy (IPC prosecution)

---

### 2. **Ramlal Puri v. State of Madhya Pradesh (1974)**

**Facts**: Accused confined victim in room for 10 hours, preventing victim from attending to business. No physical force, but accused threatened to call police if victim attempted to leave (false accusation threat).

**Issue**: Does psychological threat (without physical force) constitute wrongful confinement?

**Holding**: Madhya Pradesh High Court held **constructive confinement** (threat of force) = wrongful confinement. Physical force NOT required if victim reasonably believes force will be used.

**Principle**:
- **Constructive Obstruction**: Threat of force (credible threat) suffices for wrongful confinement
- **Victim's Reasonable Belief**: If victim reasonably believes force will be used upon leaving → Confinement proven
- **Psychological Coercion**: Fear-induced restraint = wrongful confinement

---

### 3. **DK Basu v. State of West Bengal (1997)**

**Facts**: Petitioners challenged custodial deaths and torture by police. Police routinely confined suspects beyond 24 hours without magisterial remand, extracted confessions through torture.

**Issue**: What safeguards required to prevent illegal police custody and custodial torture?

**Holding**: Supreme Court issued **DK Basu Guidelines** (11 commandments for police custody):

**Key Guidelines**:
1. Arrest memo with time/date, countersigned by witness
2. Person arrested has right to inform relative/friend of arrest
3. Medical examination within 24 hours of arrest (document injuries)
4. Magisterial remand required beyond 24 hours (CrPC Section 57)
5. Interrogation in presence of lawyer (if requested)
6. Accused produced before magistrate within 24 hours

**Principle**:
- **Illegal Custody = Wrongful Confinement**: Police custody beyond 24 hours without magisterial remand violates Article 21 + IPC Section 348
- **Custodial Torture**: Extracting confession through confinement/torture = IPC Section 348 + IPC Section 330
- **Compensation**: State liable to pay compensation for custodial violations

**Application**: Police officer confining suspect beyond 24 hours without remand → IPC Section 348 + departmental action + victim compensation.

## Compliance Checklist: Investigation & Prosecution

### For Investigating Officer

**Immediate Actions**:
- [ ] Record FIR (Section 341 - non-cognizable, Section 342 - cognizable)
- [ ] If wrongful confinement (cognizable): Arrest accused without warrant (if necessary)
- [ ] Rescue victim from confinement (immediate priority)
- [ ] Medical examination of victim (document injuries, psychological trauma)

**Evidence Collection**:
- [ ] Interview victim: Duration of confinement, location, manner of confinement (locked room, guarded, threatened)
- [ ] Inspect confinement location: Photograph/video (locked doors, windows, escape routes blocked)
- [ ] Interview witnesses: Who saw victim being confined? Who saw accused preventing victim's exit?
- [ ] Seize evidence: Keys, locks, messages/emails showing intent to confine

**Charge Determination**:
- [ ] Section 341 (restraint) OR Section 342 (confinement)? → Test: Could victim move in any direction beyond limits? (No → Confinement)
- [ ] Duration-based aggravation: ≥3 days (Section 343), ≥10 days (Section 344)
- [ ] Secret confinement (Section 346)? → Location hidden from authorities/family?
- [ ] Intent-based aggravation: Extortion (Section 347), confession extraction (Section 348)?

---

### For Public Prosecutor

**Proving Elements**:
- [ ] **Voluntary Obstruction**: Prove accused intentionally confined victim (witness testimony, accused's statements)
- [ ] **Circumscribing Limits**: Prove victim prevented from proceeding beyond defined boundary (floor plan, photographs, victim testimony)
- [ ] **No Right to Confine**: Prove accused had no lawful authority to confine victim (employment contract does not authorize confinement)
- [ ] **Duration**: Prove exact duration (victim testimony, CCTV timestamps, witness observations)

**Anticipate Defenses**:
- [ ] **Lawful Authority**: Accused claims right to confine (e.g., parent confining minor child for discipline) → Prove confinement exceeded lawful parental authority
- [ ] **Consent**: Accused claims victim consented to stay → Prove no consent OR consent vitiated by fear/coercion
- [ ] **No Confinement**: Accused claims victim free to leave → Prove doors locked, guards posted, threats made

---

### For Defense Counsel

**Challenge Confinement Element**:
- [ ] Argue victim free to leave (doors unlocked, no guards, no threats) → Prove no circumscribing limits
- [ ] Argue mere request to stay (persuasion, not obstruction) → No wrongful restraint/confinement

**Lawful Authority Defense**:
- [ ] Prove accused had lawful authority to restrain/confine (e.g., security guard preventing trespasser, parent disciplining minor child)
- [ ] Prove confinement was reasonable exercise of lawful authority (not excessive force/duration)

**Consent Defense**:
- [ ] Prove victim consented to stay (e.g., guest voluntarily remaining in host's home)
- [ ] Produce evidence of consent (messages, witness testimony showing victim's willingness)

**Duration Dispute**:
- [ ] Challenge duration calculation (dispute whether confinement lasted 3/10 days) → Cross-examine victim/witnesses on timeline

## IPC to BNS Transition

### Section Mapping

| IPC Section | BNS Section | Change |
|-------------|-------------|--------|
| IPC 339 (Wrongful Restraint) | BNS 126 | Definition unchanged |
| IPC 340 (Wrongful Confinement) | BNS 128 | Definition unchanged |
| IPC 341 (Punishment - Restraint) | BNS 127 | Punishment unchanged (1 month OR ₹500 fine) |
| IPC 342 (Punishment - Confinement) | BNS 129 | Punishment unchanged (1 year OR ₹1,000 fine) |
| IPC 343 (Confinement 3+ days) | BNS 130 | Punishment unchanged (2 years OR fine) |
| IPC 344 (Confinement 10+ days) | BNS 131 | Punishment unchanged (3 years + fine) |
| IPC 346 (Secret Confinement) | BNS 133 | Punishment unchanged (2 years + fine) |
| IPC 347 (Confinement to Extort) | BNS 134 | Punishment unchanged (3 years + fine) |
| IPC 348 (Confinement for Confession) | BNS 135 | Punishment unchanged (3 years + fine) |

### Effective Date: July 1, 2024

**Offenses Before July 1, 2024**: Prosecuted under **IPC 339-348**
**Offenses On/After July 1, 2024**: Prosecuted under **BNS 126-135**
