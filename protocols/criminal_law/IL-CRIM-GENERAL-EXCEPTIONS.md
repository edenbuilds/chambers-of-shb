## Overview and Purpose

### What are General Exceptions?

**General Exceptions** are **circumstances** or **conditions** that **exempt a person from criminal liability** even though they committed an act that would otherwise constitute an offence.

### Purpose

1. **Recognize Absence of Mens Rea**: No guilty mind due to mistake, coercion, insanity
2. **Justify Conduct**: Act done in lawful exercise of right (e.g., self-defense, official duty)
3. **Balance Rights**: Individual's right to defend vs state's power to punish
4. **Fairness**: Don't punish those who lack culpability

### Burden of Proof (Section 105 Evidence Act / BSA)

**Burden on Accused**: If accused claims benefit of general exception, burden on **accused** to prove exception exists

**Standard**: **Preponderance of probabilities** (not beyond reasonable doubt)

**See Protocol**: IL-CRIM-BURDEN-PROOF.md

## Classification of General Exceptions

### Category 1: Absence of Criminal Intent (Mens Rea)

**Principle**: No guilty mind, no crime

**Exceptions**:
1. Act of judge (Section 77-78 / BNS 7)
2. Mistake of fact (Section 76, 79 / BNS 6, 9)
3. Accident (Section 80 / BNS 10)
4. Infancy (Sections 82-83 / BNS 11-12)
5. Insanity (Section 84 / BNS 13)
6. Intoxication (Sections 85-86 / BNS 18-19)

### Category 2: Acts Done Under Compulsion

**Principle**: Person coerced, no free will

**Exceptions**:
7. Necessity (Section 81 / BNS 8)
8. Duress (Section 94 / BNS 20)

### Category 3: Acts Justified by Law

**Principle**: Act done in lawful exercise of authority/right

**Exceptions**:
9. Judicial acts (Section 77 / BNS 7)
10. Acts under authority of law (Section 76 / BNS 6)
11. Acts done by consent (Sections 87-93 / BNS 21-22)
12. Communication in good faith (Section 79 / BNS 9)

### Category 4: Right of Private Defense

**Principle**: Inherent right to protect self and property

**Exceptions**:
13. Private defense of body (Sections 96-106 / BNS 14-24)
14. Private defense of property (Sections 96-106 / BNS 14-24)

## Section-by-Section Analysis

### Category 1: Absence of Criminal Intent

#### Section 76 IPC / BNS Section 6: Act Done by Person Bound by Law

**Provision**: Nothing is an offence which is done by a person who is **bound by law** to do it.

**Rationale**: Person obeying law cannot be penalized

**Example**: Police officer arresting person on warrant from court
- Officer bound by law to execute warrant
- Protected by Section 76

**Limitation**: Protection only if act truly mandated by law

**Landmark Case**: **Emperor v. Khwaja Nazir Ahmad (1945)**
- Official acting in good faith belief of legal duty protected
- But if patently illegal, no protection

#### Section 77 IPC / BNS Section 7: Act of Judge When Acting Judicially

**Provision**: Nothing is an offence which is done by a Judge when acting **judicially** in exercise of powers believed in good faith to be within his authority.

**Rationale**: Judicial immunity - judges must decide without fear of prosecution

**Conditions**:
1. Act done by **Judge**
2. When acting **judicially**
3. In exercise of powers **believed in good faith** to be within authority

**Limitation**: Does not protect **malicious** or **corrupt** acts

**Example**: Judge sentences accused to imprisonment; later sentence found to be beyond jurisdiction
- If done in good faith, judge protected by Section 77

**Landmark Case**: **Tukaram Ganpat v. State of Maharashtra (1979)**
- Judicial immunity extends to orders passed in good faith
- Does not cover acts done maliciously or in bad faith

#### Section 78 IPC (Merged into Section 77 BNS 7): Act Done Pursuant to Judgment of Court

**Provision**: Nothing is an offence done in pursuance of judgment or order of court, even if court lacked jurisdiction, if person believed in good faith court had jurisdiction.

**Rationale**: Person executing court order should not be penalized for court's jurisdictional error

**Example**: Bailiff executes attachment order; later court found to lack jurisdiction
- Bailiff protected if acted in good faith

#### Section 79 IPC / BNS Section 9: Act Done in Good Faith Belief of Justification by Law

**Provision**: Nothing is an offence done by person who is **justified by law** or who **believes in good faith** he is justified by law.

**Conditions**:
1. Act done in **good faith**
2. **Belief** that law justifies act
3. Belief is **reasonable**

**Example**: Police officer arrests person mistakenly believing arrest warrant valid
- If belief in good faith and reasonable, officer protected

**Landmark Case**: **State of Orissa v. Ram Bahadur Thapa (1960)**
- Good faith means honesty of intention
- Mistake must be reasonable

#### Section 80 IPC / BNS Section 10: Accident in Doing Lawful Act by Lawful Means

**Provision**: Nothing is an offence which is done by **accident** or **misfortune**, without criminal intention or knowledge, in doing **lawful act** by **lawful means** with proper care and caution.

**Five Essential Ingredients**:
1. Act done by **accident or misfortune**
2. Without **criminal intention** or **knowledge**
3. Act was **lawful**
4. Means were **lawful**
5. Proper **care and caution** exercised

**Example**: Person driving carefully; suddenly dog runs on road; person swerves but hits pedestrian
- Lawful act (driving), lawful means (following traffic rules), proper care
- Accident protected by Section 80

**Limitation**: **No protection if negligence**

**Landmark Case**: **Tulsidas Kanolkar v. State of Goa (2003)**
- Accused driving rashly; hit pedestrian
- NOT protected by Section 80 (rash and negligent driving not "proper care")

**Difference from Section 79**:
- **Section 79**: Mistake of law/fact
- **Section 80**: Pure accident (no fault at all)

#### Sections 82-83 IPC / BNS Sections 11-12: Act of Child

##### Section 82 IPC / BNS Section 11: Act of Child Under Seven Years
**"Nothing is an offence which is done by a child **under seven years** of age."**

**Absolute Immunity**: Child below 7 years **incapable** of committing crime (doli incapax)

**Rationale**: Child lacks capacity to understand nature and consequences of act

**No Exceptions**: Applies to **all offences** (including murder)

**Example**: 6-year-old child fires gun, killing person
- Child protected by Section 82; cannot be prosecuted

##### Section 83 IPC / BNS Section 12: Act of Child Above Seven and Under Twelve Years
**"Nothing is an offence which is done by a child **above seven and under twelve years** who has not attained **sufficient maturity** of understanding to judge nature and consequences of conduct."**

**Conditional Immunity**: Child 7-12 years protected **unless** has maturity to understand

**Burden**: Prosecution must prove child had **sufficient maturity** to understand

**Factors**: Intelligence, education, environment, nature of act

**Landmark Case**: **Ulla Mahapatra v. King (1950)**
- 11-year-old boy pushed another child off moving train (causing death)
- Held: Boy had sufficient maturity to understand consequences
- Section 83 did not protect

**Juvenile Justice Act 2015**:
- Now special provisions for children below 18 years
- JJ Act overrides IPC/BNS for juveniles
- Section 82-83 still relevant for children 7-12 if JJ Act not applicable

#### Section 84 IPC / BNS Section 13: Act of Person of Unsound Mind

**"Nothing is an offence which is done by a person who, at the time of doing it, by reason of **unsoundness of mind**, is **incapable** of knowing nature of act OR that what he is doing is **wrong or contrary to law**."**

**Three Essential Elements**:
1. **Unsoundness of mind** at time of act
2. **Incapable** of knowing:
   - **Nature of act**, OR
   - That act is **wrong or contrary to law**

**Based on M'Naghten Rules (English law)**: "Disease of mind" preventing knowledge of nature/wrongfulness

**Burden**: On **accused** to prove insanity (Section 105 Evidence Act)

**Standard**: **Preponderance of probabilities**

**Presumption**: Every person is presumed **sane** until contrary proved

**Medical vs Legal Insanity**:
- **Medical insanity**: Broad (any mental disorder)
- **Legal insanity** (Section 84): Narrow (must be incapable of knowing nature/wrongfulness at time of act)

**Landmark Cases**:

##### Dahyabhai Chhaganbhai Thakker v. State of Gujarat (1964)
- Burden on accused to prove unsoundness of mind
- Must show unsoundness existed at time of offence
- Medical evidence helpful but not conclusive

##### Ratan Lal v. State of M.P. (1971)
- Insanity must be proved at **time of commission** of offence
- Post-offence insanity irrelevant
- Onus on accused; standard is preponderance

##### Surendra Mishra v. State of Jharkhand (2011)
- Conduct before and after offence relevant to prove insanity
- Sudden impulse without motive may indicate insanity
- But burden remains on accused

**Difference from Diminished Responsibility**:
- **Section 84**: Complete exemption (acquittal)
- **Diminished Responsibility**: Not recognized in India (unlike UK)

#### Sections 85-86 IPC / BNS Sections 18-19: Intoxication

##### Section 85 IPC / BNS Section 18: Act of Person Incapable of Judgment by Intoxication

**"Nothing is an offence done by person who, at time of doing it, is **incapable of knowing nature** of act OR that what doing is wrong or contrary to law, by reason of **intoxication** caused by **involuntary** consumption of intoxicating substance."**

**Conditions for Protection**:
1. **Intoxication** at time of act
2. **Incapable** of knowing nature or wrongfulness
3. Intoxication was **involuntary** (without knowledge or against will)

**Voluntary Intoxication**: **NO protection** under Section 85

**Rationale**: Person who voluntarily intoxicates cannot claim exemption

**Landmark Case**: **Basdev v. State of Pepsu (1956)**
- Accused murdered wife while drunk
- Voluntary intoxication; Section 85 not applicable
- Convicted

##### Section 86 IPC / BNS Section 19: Intoxication When Defense and When Not

**Provides nuance to Section 85**:

**Rule 1**: Voluntary intoxication is **defense** ONLY if offence requires **specific intent** (mens rea) AND intoxication prevented formation of that intent

**Example**: Murder requires **intention** to kill
- If voluntary intoxication prevented formation of intention
- May be convicted of **lesser offence** (e.g., culpable homicide not amounting to murder)

**Rule 2**: Voluntary intoxication is **NOT defense** if offence requires only **general intent** OR **knowledge** that act is dangerous

**Landmark Case**: **Toofan Singh v. State of Punjab (2008)**
- Accused voluntarily drunk; committed murder
- Held: Voluntary intoxication not defense for murder
- Intention inferred from act

**Involuntary Intoxication**:
- If truly involuntary (drugged without knowledge)
- Complete defense under Section 85

### Category 2: Acts Done Under Compulsion

#### Section 81 IPC / BNS Section 8: Act Likely to Cause Harm Done Without Criminal Intent for Lawful Object

**"Nothing is an offence by reason of any harm which it may cause to a person for whose benefit it is done in **good faith**, even without that person's consent, if circumstances are such that it is **impossible to obtain consent** OR person is **incapable of giving consent**, and person doing act **does not intend** to cause harm and **does not know** act is likely to cause harm other than harm which person intended to **prevent or avoid**."**

**Principle**: **Necessity** - lesser harm to prevent greater harm

**Example**: Surgeon amputates gangrenous leg to save patient's life (patient unconscious, cannot consent)
- Harm (amputation) done to prevent greater harm (death)
- Protected by Section 81

**Conditions**:
1. Act done in **good faith**
2. For **benefit** of person harmed
3. **Impossible to obtain consent** OR person **incapable** of consenting
4. No **intent** to cause harm beyond what necessary
5. **Lawful object** (preventing/avoiding harm)

**Landmark Case**: **Dr. Jacob Mathew v. State of Punjab (2005)**
- Medical negligence case
- Section 81 protects doctors acting in good faith, without gross negligence
- Simple lack of skill not criminal if good faith

#### Section 94 IPC / BNS Section 20: Act Compelled by Threats

**"Nothing is an offence if person coerced to do it by **threats** which reasonably cause **apprehension** of **instant death**."**

**Three Essential Elements**:
1. **Threat** of instant death
2. Threat creates **reasonable apprehension**
3. Person **compelled** to do act

**Exceptions** (Section 94 Explanation):
Does **NOT apply** to:
- Murder
- Offences against state (treason, waging war)

**Rationale**: Person acting under mortal fear lacks free will

**Landmark Case**: **Nathi Lal v. State of H.P. (1999)**
- Accused claimed acted under duress
- Court held threat must be **immediate** and **instant death**
- Remote or future threat not sufficient

**Limitation**: Very narrow scope
- Threat must be of **instant death** (not mere injury or future death)
- Not applicable to murder or state offences

### Category 3: Acts Justified by Law

#### Sections 87-93 IPC / BNS Sections 21-22: Consent

**General Principle**: Act not an offence if done with **valid consent**

##### Section 87 IPC / BNS Section 21: Act Not Intended to Cause Death, Done with Consent

**"Nothing which is not intended to cause death or grievous hurt, and which is not known to be likely to cause death or grievous hurt, is an offence **by reason of any harm** which it may cause with **consent** of person suffering harm."**

**Application**: Sports, medical procedures, tattooing

**Example**: Boxing match - punches cause hurt
- Both boxers consent
- No intent to cause death/grievous hurt
- Protected by Section 87

**Limitation**:
- **Death**: If death or grievous hurt intended/likely, consent irrelevant
- Cannot consent to being killed (euthanasia not legal in India)

##### Section 88 IPC / BNS Section 22: Act Not Intended to Cause Death, Done for Benefit with Consent

**Medical Procedures**: Act done in good faith for person's benefit with consent (even if causes harm)

**Example**: Surgery causing pain and scars
- Done for benefit (treatment)
- With consent
- Protected

**Landmark Case**: **Samira Kohli v. Dr. Prabha Manchanda (2008)**
- "Informed consent" necessary for medical procedures
- Patient must be informed of risks, alternatives
- Consent must be voluntary

##### Section 89 IPC: Act Done for Benefit of Child/Insane Person Without Consent

**Guardian's Consent**: Act done in good faith for benefit of child (<12) or insane person, without consent, by guardian

**Example**: Parent allowing surgery on child unable to consent

##### Section 90 IPC / BNS Section 23: Consent Not Given Freely

**Consent is **NOT valid** if**:
1. Given under **fear of injury** or **misconception of fact**
2. Person giving consent is **incapable** (child, insane, intoxicated)

**Example**: Sexual intercourse with woman by impersonating her husband
- Consent obtained by misconception of fact (she thought partner was husband)
- NOT valid consent; rape (IPC 375 / BNS 63)

**Landmark Case**: **State of Karnataka v. Manjanna (2000)**
- Sexual intercourse on pretext of marriage
- Consent obtained by misconception
- Rape

##### Sections 91-93 IPC: Exclusions from Consent Protection

Acts where **consent does NOT protect**:
- **Section 91**: Consent to suffer harm from act not known/believed to cause death/grievous hurt (but does cause it) - **NO protection**
- **Section 92**: Act done in good faith for benefit WITHOUT consent (if consent could have been obtained) - **NO protection**
- **Section 93**: Communication made in good faith - **protected**

### Category 4: Right of Private Defense

**Sections 96-106 IPC / BNS Sections 14-24**

**Fundamental Right**: Every person has **inherent right** to defend:
1. **Self** (private defense of body)
2. **Property** (private defense of property)

#### Section 96 IPC / BNS Section 14: Right of Private Defense

**"Nothing is an offence which is done in exercise of **right of private defense**."**

**Two Types**:
1. Private defense of **body** (Sections 97-106)
2. Private defense of **property** (Sections 97, 99-106)

**Rationale**: State cannot be everywhere; individuals must protect themselves from immediate threats

**Constitutional Right**: Included in **right to life** (Article 21)

#### Section 97 IPC / BNS Section 15: When Right Extends

**Right of private defense extends** against acts which are **offences** affecting:
- Human body (assault, wrongful restraint, etc.)
- Property (theft, robbery, mischief, trespass)

**Limitations**:
- Does not extend against **public servants** acting in good faith (even if technically beyond powers)
- Does not extend if reasonable opportunity to seek protection from public authorities

**Example**: Cannot use private defense against police officer arresting (even if arrest unlawful) - must challenge in court

#### Section 99 IPC / BNS Section 16: Restrictions on Right of Private Defense

**Six Restrictions** (Right does **NOT extend** to):

##### 1. Acts Not Reasonably Apprehended to Cause Harm
- If no reasonable apprehension of danger, no right to defend
- Example: Person jokingly approaches; no actual threat - no right to use force

##### 2. Time for Seeking Public Protection Available
- If time to seek police/public authorities, must do so instead of using force
- Example: Person threatens to attack tomorrow - seek police protection, don't attack preemptively

##### 3. Risk of Harm to Innocent Person
- If defending likely to harm innocent bystander, better to refrain
- Balance harm to self vs harm to innocent

##### 4. More Harm Than Necessary
- Can use **only force necessary** to repel attack
- **Proportionality** principle
- Cannot use excessive force

**Landmark Case**: **Darshan Singh v. State of Punjab (2010)**
- Accused shot attacker with gun when pushed
- Excessive force; not proportionate to attack
- Right of private defense not available

##### 5. Against Public Servant Acting in Good Faith
- Cannot defend against public servant (police, etc.) acting within authority, even if minor excess
- Exception: If public servant acts in **bad faith** or **grossly exceeds** authority

##### 6. Not Commenced or Continued After Reasonable Apprehension Ceases
- Right exists **only while danger exists**
- Once danger passed, cannot continue attacking
- Example: Attacker knocked unconscious; cannot continue beating - danger ceased

#### Section 100 IPC / BNS Section 17: When Right of Private Defense of Body Extends to Causing Death

**Right to cause death** in private defense of body extends if offence:
1. Reasonably causes **apprehension of death**
2. Reasonably causes **apprehension of grievous hurt**
3. **Assault with intent** to commit rape
4. **Assault with intent** to gratify unnatural lust
5. **Assault with intent** to kidnap or abduct
6. **Acid attack** or attempt
7. **Wrongful confinement** if apprehension cannot otherwise be removed

**Principle**: Life-threatening attack justifies lethal force in defense

**Landmark Case**: **Vidhya Singh v. State of M.P. (1971)**
- Accused shot deceased who attacked with lathi (stick)
- Held: Grievous hurt apprehended; right to cause death applied
- Acquitted

#### Section 101 IPC / BNS Section 18: When Right Begins and Continues

**Right begins**: As soon as **reasonable apprehension** of danger arises

**Right continues**: Until danger ceases

**Important**: Need not wait for actual attack; reasonable apprehension sufficient

**Example**: Person advancing with weapon, threatening to kill
- Right begins when reasonable apprehension arises (when person starts advancing)
- Need not wait for first blow

#### Section 102 IPC / BNS Section 19: Right of Private Defense Against Death

**Right extends to causing death** if offense reasonably causes apprehension of death or grievous hurt

**Applies to**: Assault, kidnapping, rape, acid attack, wrongful confinement (as per Section 100)

#### Section 103 IPC / BNS Section 20: Right of Private Defense of Body Against Acts Not Covered by Section 100

**For offences NOT covered by Section 100**:
- Right to private defense exists
- But **NOT to cause death**
- Can cause **any harm other than death**

**Example**: Person pushes you (simple assault, no grievous hurt apprehended)
- Right to push back, restrain
- But NOT to kill

#### Sections 97-106 IPC / BNS Sections 15-24: Private Defense of Property

**Similar Principles** apply to property defense:

##### Section 105 IPC / BNS Section 23: Right to Cause Death in Defense of Property

**When extends to causing death**:
1. **Robbery** (property taken with violence)
2. **House-breaking at night**
3. **Mischief by fire** (arson) to dwelling/place of worship
4. **Theft, mischief, trespass** if offender causes death/grievous hurt/wrongful restraint to prevent defense

**Rationale**: Serious attacks on property (especially home at night) justify lethal defense

##### Section 106 IPC / BNS Section 24: When Right Begins and Continues (Property)

**Right begins**: When reasonable apprehension of danger to property arises

**Right continues**: Until danger ceases

**Example**: Person breaking into house at night
- Right to defend begins when breaking in starts
- Continues until person leaves or subdued

## Burden of Proof for General Exceptions

**Section 105 Evidence Act / BSA**:
"Burden of proving the existence of circumstances bringing the case within any of the General Exceptions... is upon [the accused]."

**Standard**: **Preponderance of probabilities** (not beyond reasonable doubt)

**Procedure**:
1. **Prosecution proves** commission of offence beyond reasonable doubt
2. **Accused claims** general exception applies (e.g., self-defense, insanity)
3. **Accused must prove** exception on preponderance

**If Accused Proves Exception**: Acquittal

**If Accused Fails to Prove**: Conviction (if prosecution proved guilt)

**Landmark Case**: **Dahyabhai Chhaganbhai Thakker v. State of Gujarat (1964)**
- Burden on accused to prove exception
- But standard is preponderance, not beyond reasonable doubt

**See Protocol**: IL-CRIM-BURDEN-PROOF.md

### Key Observations

**Substantive Law**: **No material change** in general exceptions from IPC to BNS
- Principles remain same
- Conditions for exemption unchanged
- Judicial precedents continue to apply

**Structural Changes**:
- **Section numbers changed**
- **Some consolidation** (e.g., Sections 77-78 IPC merged into Section 7 BNS)
- **Language modernized**

**Practical Impact**: **Minimal**
- Lawyers/courts can rely on IPC-era precedents
- Same defenses available
- Same burden of proof

## Practical Compliance Checklist

### For Accused/Defense

#### Identifying Applicable Exception
- [ ] Analyze facts to determine if any exception applies:
  - [ ] Lack of capacity (child, insane, intoxicated)
  - [ ] Mistake of fact
  - [ ] Accident
  - [ ] Necessity
  - [ ] Duress
  - [ ] Consent
  - [ ] Self-defense

#### Building Exception Defense
- [ ] **Gather evidence** proving exception:
  - [ ] Witness testimony (eyewitnesses to self-defense, etc.)
  - [ ] Medical evidence (insanity, injury from attack)
  - [ ] Documentary evidence (age proof for child)
  - [ ] Expert evidence (psychiatrist for insanity, toxicologist for intoxication)

#### At Trial
- [ ] **Plead exception** clearly in statement/pleading
- [ ] **Produce evidence** during defense stage:
  - Defense witnesses
  - Medical/expert reports
  - Accused's testimony (if advisable)
- [ ] **Cross-examine** prosecution witnesses to elicit facts supporting exception
- [ ] In final arguments, show exception proved on **preponderance of probabilities**

#### Common Exceptions - Specific Preparation

**Self-Defense (Sections 96-106 / 14-24 BNS)**:
- [ ] Prove **imminent threat** (danger was immediate)
- [ ] Prove **no safe retreat** (couldn't escape or seek police)
- [ ] Prove **proportionate force** (used only necessary force)
- [ ] Show danger **ceased** (didn't continue attacking after threat ended)

**Insanity (Section 84 / 13 BNS)**:
- [ ] Medical evidence of **mental disorder**
- [ ] Expert testimony (psychiatrist)
- [ ] Evidence of **conduct before/after** offence (irrational behavior)
- [ ] Show **incapacity to know nature** of act OR that it was wrong

**Accident (Section 80 / 10 BNS)**:
- [ ] Show act was **lawful** (e.g., lawful driving)
- [ ] Show means were **lawful** (no rash/negligent driving)
- [ ] Show **proper care** exercised
- [ ] Show harm was **unforeseeable accident**

### For Prosecution

#### Countering Exception Defense
- [ ] **Challenge evidence** of exception:
  - Cross-examine defense witnesses
  - Present contradictory evidence
  - Show accused's conduct inconsistent with claimed exception

#### Specific Counters

**Self-Defense**:
- [ ] Show **no imminent threat** (accused was aggressor OR threat not immediate)
- [ ] Show **excessive force** used (disproportionate to attack)
- [ ] Show **continued after danger ceased** (retaliation, not defense)

**Insanity**:
- [ ] Challenge medical evidence (expert rebuttal)
- [ ] Show **conduct before/after** was rational (planned offence, concealed evidence)
- [ ] Show accused **knew nature** of act and that it was wrong

**Accident**:
- [ ] Show **rash or negligent** conduct (not "proper care")
- [ ] Show act or means were **unlawful**

#### In Final Arguments
- [ ] Show accused **failed to prove** exception on preponderance
- [ ] Highlight **inconsistencies** in exception defense
- [ ] Request conviction as prosecution proved guilt beyond reasonable doubt

### For Courts

#### Evaluating Exception Defense
- [ ] **Burden on accused** to prove exception (Section 105)
- [ ] **Standard**: Preponderance of probabilities (not beyond reasonable doubt)
- [ ] Consider **all evidence** (prosecution and defense)

#### If Exception Proved
- [ ] **Acquit** accused (if exception fully established)
- [ ] OR **Convict of lesser offence** (if exception partially applies)

**Example**: Murder charge; voluntary intoxication proved
- Section 86: May reduce murder to culpable homicide not amounting to murder
- If intoxication prevented formation of specific intent

#### If Exception Not Proved
- [ ] **Convict** if prosecution proved guilt beyond reasonable doubt
- [ ] Write **reasoned judgment** explaining why exception not accepted

## Common Errors and Misconceptions

### Errors

1. **Confusing burden**: Thinking prosecution must disprove exception (NO - burden on accused per Section 105)

2. **Confusing standard**: Thinking accused must prove exception beyond reasonable doubt (NO - only preponderance)

3. **Claiming multiple exceptions**: Claiming accident AND self-defense simultaneously (usually mutually exclusive)

4. **Excessive force in self-defense**: Using gun to defend against fist fight (disproportionate; Section 99)

5. **Continuing after danger ceased**: Continuing to beat attacker after they're unconscious (danger ceased; Section 99)

### Misconceptions

**Misconception 1**: "Voluntary intoxication is complete defense"
**Reality**: NO - Section 85 protects only **involuntary** intoxication; Section 86 allows voluntary intoxication as defense ONLY if offence requires specific intent AND intoxication prevented its formation

**Misconception 2**: "Can kill anyone in self-defense"
**Reality**: NO - Can cause death ONLY if offence falls under Section 100 / 17 BNS (apprehension of death, grievous hurt, rape, kidnapping, acid attack)

**Misconception 3**: "Child can never be prosecuted"
**Reality**: NO - Child <7 years has absolute immunity (Section 82); child 7-12 years protected ONLY if lacks maturity (Section 83); child >12 years can be prosecuted (but JJ Act applies if <18)

**Misconception 4**: "Consent always protects"
**Reality**: NO - Cannot consent to death or grievous hurt (Section 87 limitation); consent invalid if obtained by fear/misconception/from incapable person (Section 90)

**Misconception 5**: "Insanity = any mental illness"
**Reality**: NO - Legal insanity (Section 84) requires **incapacity to know nature of act OR that it was wrong**; mere mental illness insufficient

## Conclusion

**General Exceptions** are **crucial safeguards** ensuring:
- **No punishment without culpability**: Recognizing absence of mens rea
- **Protection of lawful conduct**: Judicial acts, official duty, consent
- **Right to defend**: Inherent right to protect self and property
- **Fairness**: Balancing state power with individual rights

**Key Principles**:
1. **Burden on accused** to prove exception (Section 105)
2. **Standard**: Preponderance of probabilities (not beyond reasonable doubt)
3. **Insanity** (Section 84/13 BNS): Incapacity to know nature or wrongfulness
4. **Self-Defense** (Sections 96-106 / 14-24 BNS): Imminent threat, proportionate force, danger must cease
5. **Consent** (Sections 87-93 / 21-23 BNS): Valid only if free, informed; cannot consent to death/grievous hurt
6. **Accident** (Section 80/10 BNS): Lawful act, lawful means, proper care
7. **Duress** (Section 94/20 BNS): Threat of instant death (NOT applicable to murder)
8. **Intoxication** (Sections 85-86 / 18-19 BNS): Only involuntary intoxication is complete defense

**IPC to BNS Transition**:
- **No material change** in general exceptions
- Section numbers changed (76-106 IPC → 6-26 BNS)
- Principles, conditions, burden - all same
- All IPC-era precedents continue to apply

**Practical Importance**:
- Defense lawyers must identify applicable exceptions early
- Gather evidence to prove exception on preponderance
- Courts must apply Section 105 correctly (burden on accused, preponderance standard)
- Prosecution must challenge exception evidence effectively

**Ultimate Safeguard**: General exceptions ensure criminal law punishes only those with **mens rea** (guilty mind) and protects those acting in **lawful exercise of rights** or under **disabling circumstances**.
