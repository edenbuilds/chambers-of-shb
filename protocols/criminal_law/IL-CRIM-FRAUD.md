## Overview

**Fraud** in Indian criminal law is the intentional deception or dishonest act to secure unfair or unlawful gain, or to deprive another of a legal right. While the IPC covers general fraud through cheating provisions (Section 420), specialized statutes address corporate fraud, securities fraud, insurance fraud, banking fraud, and cyber fraud.

Fraud is distinguished from mere breach of contract by the element of **criminal intent** (mens rea) - the deliberate intention to deceive from the inception.

### Key Characteristics
1. **Deception**: Fraudulent misrepresentation or concealment of material facts
2. **Criminal Intent**: Dishonest intention from the beginning (not mere failure to perform)
3. **Economic Loss**: Usually involves financial loss to victim or wrongful gain to perpetrator
4. **Multiple Statutes**: Fraud is covered by IPC (general), Companies Act (corporate), SEBI Act (securities), IT Act (cyber fraud)
5. **Severe Punishment**: Up to 10 years imprisonment for corporate fraud; varying penalties for other forms

## Types of Fraud

### 1. Corporate Fraud (Companies Act, 2013 - Section 447)

#### Definition

**Section 447**: Any person found to be **guilty of fraud** involving an amount of at least **Rs. 10 lakh** or **1% of the turnover** of the company (whichever is lower) shall be punishable with imprisonment from **6 months to 10 years** AND fine (at least the amount involved, may extend to 3 times the amount).

If fraud involves amount less than Rs. 10 lakh or 1% turnover, imprisonment up to **5 years** OR fine up to Rs. 50 lakh OR both.

#### What Constitutes Fraud (Explanation to Section 447)

**"Fraud"** includes any act, omission, concealment, or abuse of position committed by any person with intent to **deceive**, to gain **undue advantage**, or to **injure the interests** of:
- The company
- Shareholders
- Creditors
- Any other person

Whether or not there is any wrongful gain or wrongful loss.

#### Common Forms of Corporate Fraud

**A. Financial Statement Fraud**
- Manipulating financial statements to show false profits/losses
- Overstating assets or understating liabilities
- Creating fictitious revenues or hiding expenses

**Example**: Satyam Computer Services scandal (2009) - Chairman Ramalinga Raju admitted to inflating cash and bank balances by Rs. 5,040 crore, overstating revenues, and creating fictitious assets.

**B. Asset Misappropriation**
- Embezzlement of company funds
- Theft of company assets (inventory, equipment, intellectual property)
- Fraudulent expense claims

**Example**: Director of a company diverts Rs. 50 crore from company accounts to personal offshore accounts.

**C. Corruption and Bribery**
- Kickbacks in procurement contracts
- Bribes to obtain government contracts or licenses
- Conflict of interest transactions

**Example**: Company pays bribes to government officials to secure telecom licenses (2G spectrum scam).

**D. Securities Fraud (Insider Trading, Market Manipulation)**
- Covered primarily by SEBI Act (discussed below)
- But may also constitute corporate fraud under Companies Act

**E. Fraudulent Related-Party Transactions**
- Directors awarding contracts to related entities at inflated prices
- Transferring company assets to promoter-owned firms at undervalued prices

**Example**: Company sells land worth Rs. 100 crore to a promoter's relative for Rs. 10 crore (fraudulent undervaluation).

#### Punishment for Corporate Fraud

**If amount involved ≥ Rs. 10 lakh or 1% turnover**:
- **Imprisonment**: 6 months to **10 years**, AND
- **Fine**: At least the amount involved in fraud, may extend to **3 times** the amount

**If amount < Rs. 10 lakh or 1% turnover**:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **5 years**, OR
- **Fine**: Up to **Rs. 50 lakh**, OR
- **Both**

**Nature**: Cognizable (if amount ≥ Rs. 10 lakh), Non-bailable

**Additional Consequences**:
- **Disqualification from being director** (Section 164(2)) - 5 years
- **Debarment from managing companies**
- **Criminal liability for directors, auditors, executives**
- **Civil liability**: Shareholders/creditors can sue for damages
- **Disgorgement of profits**: Court may order return of fraudulently obtained gains

#### Who Can Be Prosecuted?

**Section 447 applies to**:
- **Directors** (executive and non-executive)
- **Key Managerial Personnel** (CEO, CFO, Company Secretary)
- **Officers in default** (those responsible for company compliance)
- **Auditors** (if connived in fraud)
- **Promoters and shareholders** (if they perpetrated fraud)
- **Company itself** (through directors/officers)

**Vicarious Liability**: If company commits fraud, every person who was responsible and consented/connived is deemed guilty.

### 2. Securities Fraud (SEBI Act, 1992)

#### Section 12A SEBI Act: Fraudulent and Unfair Trade Practices

SEBI has power to investigate and prosecute fraudulent and unfair trade practices in securities markets, including:

**A. Insider Trading**
- Trading in securities while in possession of **unpublished price-sensitive information** (UPSI)

**Definition (SEBI Insider Trading Regulations, 2015)**:
- **Insider**: Person with access to UPSI (directors, employees, connected persons)
- **UPSI**: Information not available to public, which if published would likely affect price of securities

**Example**: CFO of Company X knows (before public announcement) that company will report huge quarterly losses. CFO sells his shares at current high price before announcement. After announcement, share price crashes. CFO committed insider trading.

**Punishment**:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **10 years** (under SEBI Act and Companies Act)
- **Fine**: Up to **Rs. 25 crore** or **3 times the profit** made (whichever is higher)
- **Disgorgement**: Return of illegal profits

**Famous Cases**:
- Rajat Gupta case (2012): Former McKinsey head and Goldman Sachs director convicted in US for insider trading; also prosecuted in India by SEBI
- Rajesh Sharma case (2014): SEBI imposed Rs. 15 crore penalty for insider trading

**B. Market Manipulation**
- **Price Rigging**: Artificially inflating or deflating stock prices
- **Front Running**: Broker trading for own account before executing large client orders
- **Pump and Dump**: Artificially inflating stock price through false recommendations, then selling holdings at inflated price
- **Circular Trading**: Creating artificial volumes through synchronized buy-sell orders among connected parties

**Example (Pump and Dump)**: A stock operator spreads false rumors that Company Y will be acquired by a multinational, causing share price to jump from Rs. 50 to Rs. 200. Operator sells his holdings at Rs. 200 and exits. When truth emerges, price crashes to Rs. 30. Innocent investors lose money.

**Punishment**:
- **Imprisonment**: Up to **10 years**
- **Fine**: Up to **Rs. 25 crore** or **3 times the profit** made
- **Market ban**: SEBI can debar from trading for specified period

**Famous Case**: Harshad Mehta Scam (1992) - Stock market manipulation involving fake bank receipts and circular trading; Mehta artificially inflated stock prices causing market crash.

**C. Ponzi Schemes and Fraudulent Collective Investment Schemes**
- Promising high returns but using new investors' money to pay old investors (unsustainable)
- Not genuine investment schemes

**Example**: Saradha chit fund scam (2013) - Rs. 2,500 crore Ponzi scheme in West Bengal; promised high returns, collapsed leaving lakhs of investors defrauded.

**Punishment**: Companies Act fraud provisions + SEBI penalties + IPC cheating (Section 420)

### 3. Banking Fraud

#### Definition (RBI Guidelines & Banking Regulation Act)

**Bank fraud** includes:
- **Loan fraud**: Obtaining loans by submitting false documents, inflating assets, or diverting loan funds
- **Credit card fraud**: Unauthorized use of credit cards, card cloning
- **Account fraud**: Forged cheques, unauthorized withdrawals
- **Letter of credit fraud**: Presenting forged documents to claim payment

**Section 135 of Banking Regulation Act**: Punishment for bank fraud up to **3 years** imprisonment OR fine OR both.

**IPC Provisions**: Cheating (Section 420), forgery (Section 463-477), criminal breach of trust (Section 405-409)

#### Common Forms of Banking Fraud

**A. Non-Performing Asset (NPA) Fraud / Wilful Default**
- **Wilful Default**: Borrower has capacity to repay but deliberately defaults
- Diversion of loan funds to unauthorized purposes
- Siphoning off funds through related entities

**Example**: Vijay Mallya case - Kingfisher Airlines defaulted on Rs. 9,000 crore loans. Alleged wilful default, diversion of funds, fraudulent documentation. Mallya fled India; declared fugitive economic offender.

**Consequences**:
- **Criminal prosecution**: Cheating, fraud, criminal breach of trust
- **Civil recovery**: SARFAESI Act (bank can auction collateral without court)
- **Fugitive Economic Offenders Act, 2018**: Confiscation of all assets if declared fugitive

**B. Letter of Credit (LC) Fraud**
- Presenting forged/fake documents to claim LC payment
- Inflating invoice values

**Example**: Nirav Modi-PNB Fraud (2018) - Rs. 14,000 crore fraud involving fraudulent Letters of Undertaking (LoUs) issued by PNB officials to facilitate import payments. Nirav Modi and associates siphoned funds through shell companies.

**Charges**: Companies Act fraud, cheating, criminal conspiracy, money laundering (PMLA)

**C. Credit Card Fraud**
- Card cloning (skimming devices at ATMs/POS)
- Phishing (stealing card details through fake websites/emails)
- Unauthorized transactions

**Punishment**: IT Act provisions (Section 66C - identity theft, Section 66D - cheating by personation) + IPC cheating/theft

### 4. Insurance Fraud

#### Definition

**Insurance fraud** includes:
- **False claims**: Claiming insurance for non-existent loss, accident, or death
- **Staged accidents**: Creating fake accidents to claim insurance
- **Inflated claims**: Exaggerating value of loss
- **Fraudulent policies**: Taking policies on fictitious persons or with false information

**Example**: A sets fire to his own property and claims fire insurance of Rs. 50 lakh (arson + insurance fraud).

#### Punishment

- **IPC Section 420**: Cheating (up to 7 years + fine)
- **Insurance Act provisions**: Cancellation of policy, denial of claim
- **IRDA regulations**: Penalties by regulator

**Famous Case**: Motor insurance fraud rackets - Staged road accidents with inflated medical bills to claim insurance; several gangs busted in major cities.

### 5. Cyber Fraud

#### IT Act, 2000 Provisions

**Section 66C**: **Identity Theft**
- Fraudulently using another person's electronic signature, password, or unique identification
- **Punishment**: Up to **3 years** imprisonment AND fine up to **Rs. 1 lakh**

**Section 66D**: **Cheating by Personation Using Computer Resource**
- Impersonating another person online to cheat or defraud
- **Punishment**: Up to **3 years** imprisonment AND fine up to **Rs. 1 lakh**

**Section 43**: **Penalty for Damage to Computer Systems, Data Theft**
- Unauthorized access, data theft, virus introduction
- **Compensation**: Up to **Rs. 1 crore**

#### Common Forms of Cyber Fraud

**A. Phishing**
- Fake emails/websites mimicking banks, e-commerce sites to steal login credentials, card details

**Example**: Email claiming to be from State Bank asks user to "verify account" by clicking link and entering credentials. Link leads to fake website; credentials stolen.

**B. Online Shopping Fraud**
- Fake e-commerce sites taking payment but not delivering goods
- Selling counterfeit/substandard products

**C. UPI/Digital Wallet Fraud**
- Tricking victims into sharing OTP or QR code to steal money from digital wallets

**Example**: Scammer calls victim claiming to be from bank, asks for OTP "to reverse wrong transaction." Victim shares OTP; money is debited from account.

**D. Job/Lottery/Prize Scams**
- Fake job offers demanding "registration fee"
- Fake lottery wins asking for "processing fee"

**E. Romance/Social Media Scams**
- Fake profiles on dating apps/social media to gain trust and extract money

#### Reporting and Remedies

**Report to**:
- **Cybercrime Police** (dedicated cyber cells in every state)
- **National Cybercrime Reporting Portal**: cybercrime.gov.in
- **Bank/Payment Gateway**: For transaction reversals

## Distinction Between Fraud and Civil Breach of Contract

| **Aspect** | **Criminal Fraud** | **Civil Breach of Contract** |
|------------|--------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Intent** | **Dishonest intent from beginning** (mens rea) | Genuine contract, later inability or unwillingness to perform |
| **Deception** | Fraudulent misrepresentation or concealment | No deception; honest agreement |
| **Remedy** | **Criminal prosecution** (imprisonment + fine) | **Civil suit** for damages or specific performance |
| **Standard of Proof** | Beyond reasonable doubt (higher standard) | Preponderance of probabilities (lower standard) |
| **Example** | A takes advance for construction with no intention to build | A genuinely enters contract to build but fails due to business loss |

**Supreme Court Principle** (*K. Nagireddy v. State of A.P.*, 1975): Mere breach of contract does not constitute fraud unless there was **criminal intent from inception**.

## Landmark Cases on Fraud

### 1. **Satyam Computer Services Fraud** (2009) - Corporate Fraud
**Case**: Ramalinga Raju (Chairman) v. SEBI & State

**Facts**: Raju admitted to manipulating Satyam's balance sheet:
- Inflated cash and bank balances by Rs. 5,040 crore
- Inflated revenues, understated liabilities
- Fictitious assets and interest income

**Charges**:
- Companies Act: Fraud (Section 447 equivalent)
- IPC: Cheating, forgery, criminal conspiracy
- SEBI: Market manipulation, misleading investors

**Outcome**:
- Raju and associates convicted; sentenced to 7 years imprisonment
- Rs. 1,849 crore fine imposed
- Debarred from holding director position
- Civil suits by shareholders for damages

**Significance**: Landmark case exposing corporate governance failures; led to stricter auditing norms and SEBI regulations.

### 2. **Nirav Modi - Punjab National Bank Fraud** (2018) - Banking Fraud
**Amount**: Rs. 14,000 crore

**Facts**:
- Fraudulent Letters of Undertaking (LoUs) issued by PNB officials to Nirav Modi's companies
- Funds used for import payments, but imports either didn't happen or were overvalued
- Money siphoned through shell companies to offshore accounts
- Nirav Modi fled India

**Charges**:
- Companies Act: Fraud
- IPC: Cheating, criminal conspiracy, criminal breach of trust
- PMLA: Money laundering
- Fugitive Economic Offenders Act: Declared fugitive; assets confiscated

**Status**: Nirav Modi arrested in UK (2019); extradition proceedings ongoing; assets worth thousands of crores attached.

**Significance**: India's largest banking fraud; exposed vulnerabilities in banking systems; led to Fugitive Economic Offenders Act, 2018.

### 3. **Harshad Mehta Scam** (1992) - Securities Fraud
**Amount**: Rs. 5,000 crore (1992 values)

**Facts**:
- Stock broker Harshad Mehta manipulated stock market through:
  - Fake Bank Receipts (BRs) - forged documents to borrow from banks
  - Circular trading - artificially inflating stock prices
  - Pump and dump scheme
- Sensex crashed from 4,500 to 2,500 after fraud exposed

**Charges**: IPC cheating, forgery, criminal breach of trust; SEBI violations

**Outcome**: Mehta arrested; died in custody before trial completion; associates prosecuted.

**Significance**: Led to major reforms in securities regulation, establishment of NSE, strengthening of SEBI powers.

### 4. **Sahara Group Case** (2011-ongoing) - Ponzi Scheme
**Amount**: Rs. 24,000 crore

**Facts**:
- Sahara companies raised Rs. 24,000 crore from 3 crore investors through Optionally Fully Convertible Debentures (OFCDs)
- SEBI found it was a collective investment scheme (Ponzi) operating without regulatory approval
- Sahara failed to refund investors

**Charges**: SEBI violations, Companies Act fraud

**Outcome**:
- Supreme Court ordered Sahara to refund Rs. 24,000 crore + interest to SEBI for investor protection
- Sahara chief Subrata Roy jailed for contempt (released on bail after partial payment)
- Refund process ongoing

**Significance**: Regulatory action against unregulated collective investment schemes.

### 5. **Vijay Mallya - Kingfisher Airlines Default** (2012-ongoing) - Wilful Default
**Amount**: Rs. 9,000 crore

**Facts**:
- Kingfisher Airlines defaulted on bank loans
- Alleged wilful default - Mallya had capacity to repay but diverted funds
- Mallya fled India (2016)

**Charges**:
- IPC: Cheating, criminal conspiracy
- PMLA: Money laundering
- Fugitive Economic Offenders Act: Declared fugitive (2019)

**Outcome**:
- Mallya arrested in UK (2017); extradition proceedings
- Assets worth Rs. 12,900 crore confiscated
- SEBI banned from securities market

**Significance**: Led to Fugitive Economic Offenders Act, 2018 enabling confiscation of assets of economic offenders who flee.

### 6. **Fortis Healthcare Fraud** (2018) - Corporate Fraud by Promoters
**Case**: Malvinder & Shivinder Singh (promoters) v. Shareholders

**Facts**:
- Promoters allegedly siphoned Rs. 500 crore from Fortis Healthcare through related-party transactions
- Fraudulent loans to shell companies controlled by promoters
- Funds diverted to personal investments

**Charges**: Companies Act fraud, criminal breach of trust, cheating

**Outcome**: SEBI investigation; criminal prosecution ongoing; promoters arrested.

**Significance**: Highlighted need for stricter oversight of related-party transactions.

## Preventive Measures and Compliance

### For Companies (Corporate Governance)

**Mandatory Compliance**:
- [ ] Appoint independent directors (at least 1/3 of board)
- [ ] Establish Audit Committee with independent directors
- [ ] Conduct statutory audits by qualified auditors
- [ ] Implement whistle-blower policy for reporting fraud
- [ ] Maintain internal controls and risk management systems
- [ ] Comply with disclosure norms (quarterly results, material events)
- [ ] Ensure related-party transactions are at arm's length and disclosed

**Best Practices**:
- Periodic internal audits and forensic audits
- Fraud risk assessment
- Ethics training for employees
- Separation of duties (prevent single person control over funds)
- Use of technology (data analytics to detect anomalies)

### For Investors (Due Diligence)

- [ ] Verify company credentials (CIN, registration) on MCA portal
- [ ] Check SEBI warnings/alerts about fraudulent schemes
- [ ] Review financial statements and audit reports
- [ ] Beware of unrealistic return promises (Ponzi schemes)
- [ ] Diversify investments (don't put all funds in one scheme)
- [ ] Deal only with SEBI-registered intermediaries (brokers, advisors)

### For Individuals (Cyber Fraud Prevention)

- [ ] Never share OTP, passwords, CVV with anyone (banks never ask)
- [ ] Verify website URLs before entering credentials (check https:// and domain)
- [ ] Use strong, unique passwords; enable two-factor authentication
- [ ] Install antivirus software and keep systems updated
- [ ] Report suspicious emails/messages to bank/authorities
- [ ] Monitor bank statements regularly for unauthorized transactions
- [ ] Use credit cards (better fraud protection than debit cards) for online transactions

## Reporting Fraud

### Corporate Fraud
- **Registrar of Companies (ROC)**: File complaint under Companies Act
- **SEBI**: For listed companies, securities fraud
- **Economic Offences Wing (EOW)**: State police specialized in economic crimes
- **Serious Fraud Investigation Office (SFIO)**: Central agency for complex corporate frauds

### Banking Fraud
- **Bank's fraud monitoring cell**: Report immediately
- **RBI Banking Ombudsman**: For grievance redressal
- **Police/EOW**: File FIR for criminal prosecution

### Cyber Fraud
- **National Cybercrime Reporting Portal**: cybercrime.gov.in
- **Local cybercrime police station**
- **Bank/Payment gateway**: For transaction reversal/freeze

### Securities Fraud
- **SEBI Complaints Redress System (SCORES)**: scores.gov.in
- **Stock exchanges**: NSE, BSE investor grievance cells

## Punishment Summary

| **Type of Fraud** | **Statute** | **Punishment** | **Nature** |
|-------------------|-------------|----------------|------------|
| **Corporate Fraud (≥ Rs. 10L)** | Companies Act 447 | 6 months - 10 years + fine (≥ fraud amount, up to 3x) | Cognizable, Non-bailable |
| **Corporate Fraud (< Rs. 10L)** | Companies Act 447 | Up to 5 years OR fine up to Rs. 50L OR both | Non-cognizable, Bailable |
| **Insider Trading** | SEBI Act + Companies Act | Up to 10 years + fine up to Rs. 25 crore or 3x profit | Cognizable, Non-bailable |
| **Cheating (General)** | IPC 420 | Up to 7 years + fine | Cognizable, Non-bailable |
| **Banking Fraud** | Banking Regulation Act + IPC | Up to 3 years (BRA) + 7 years (IPC 420) | Cognizable, Non-bailable |

| **Cyber Fraud (Cheating by Personation)** | IT Act 66D | Up to 3 years + fine up to Rs. 1 lakh | Cognizable, Bailable |

## Key Takeaways

1. **Fraud Requires Criminal Intent**: Mere breach of contract or business failure is not fraud; there must be dishonest intent from inception.

2. **Multiple Statutes**: Fraud is prosecuted under Companies Act (corporate), SEBI Act (securities), IT Act (cyber), IPC (general), and specialized laws.

3. **Severe Punishment**: Corporate fraud can result in up to 10 years imprisonment + fines up to 3 times fraud amount.

4. **Director Liability**: Directors, KMPs, and auditors can be personally prosecuted for corporate fraud (vicarious liability).

5. **Fugitive Economic Offenders Act**: Economic offenders who flee India can have all assets confiscated without trial.

6. **Investor Protection**: SEBI has strong powers to investigate, prosecute, and penalize securities fraud; disgorgement of profits.

7. **Cyber Fraud Rising**: Online fraud (phishing, identity theft, UPI fraud) is increasing; awareness and reporting critical.

8. **Civil and Criminal Remedies**: Fraud victims can pursue both criminal prosecution (imprisonment) and civil suits (damages).

## Conclusion

Fraud is a pervasive economic crime that undermines trust in financial systems, markets, and commerce. India has developed a comprehensive anti-fraud framework across multiple statutes - Companies Act for corporate fraud, SEBI Act for securities fraud, IT Act for cyber fraud, and IPC for general fraud.

Recent landmark cases (Satyam, Nirav Modi, Vijay Mallya) have exposed systemic vulnerabilities and led to legislative reforms: stricter auditing standards, Fugitive Economic Offenders Act, enhanced SEBI powers, and mandatory corporate governance norms.

However, enforcement challenges persist: complex investigations, lengthy trials, accused fleeing jurisdiction, and proving criminal intent. Effective fraud prevention requires a multi-pronged approach: robust regulatory oversight, corporate governance, investor awareness, technological safeguards, and a swift justice system.

As India's economy grows and digital transactions proliferate, vigilance against fraud - from boardroom conspiracies to WhatsApp scams - is more critical than ever.
