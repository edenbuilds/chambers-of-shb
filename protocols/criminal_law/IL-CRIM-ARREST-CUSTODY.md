## Overview and Purpose

### What is Arrest?

**Arrest** is the **act of depriving a person of liberty by legal authority** for the purpose of holding or detaining them to answer a criminal charge or to prevent commission of a criminal offence.

### Definition Under Law

**Section 41 CrPC / 41 BNSS**: Defines when police may arrest without warrant

**Black's Law Dictionary**: "The apprehending or detaining of the person in order to be forthcoming to answer an alleged or suspected crime"

### Purpose of Arrest

1. **Ensure Appearance**: Secure accused's presence during investigation and trial
2. **Prevent Escape**: Stop accused from fleeing jurisdiction
3. **Prevent Evidence Tampering**: Protect witnesses and evidence
4. **Public Safety**: Protect society from further offences
5. **Investigation**: Facilitate questioning and investigation

### Types of Arrest

| Type | Legal Basis | Warrant Required? |
|------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Arrest with warrant** | Section 70-81 CrPC / 69-80 BNSS | Yes |
| **Arrest without warrant** | Section 41 CrPC / 41 BNSS | No (if conditions met) |
| **Anticipatory bail** | Section 438 CrPC / 482 BNSS | Court protection |
| **Citizen's arrest** | Section 43 CrPC / 43 BNSS | No (limited situations) |

## Legal Framework and Statutory Basis

### Constitutional Foundation

#### Article 21: Right to Life and Personal Liberty
- Liberty is fundamental right
- Arrest must be in accordance with procedure established by law
- Arbitrary arrest prohibited

#### Article 22: Protection Against Arrest and Detention

**Article 22(1)**: Right to be informed of grounds of arrest
**Article 22(2)**: Right to consult and be defended by lawyer of choice
**Article 22(3)**: No detention beyond 24 hours without Magistrate's order

### Primary Legislation

#### Code of Criminal Procedure (CrPC) 1973
- **Chapter V**: Arrest of Persons (Sections 41-60A)
- **Chapter XII**: Remand (Section 167)

#### Bharatiya Nagarik Suraksha Sanhita (BNSS) 2023
- **Chapter V**: Arrest of Persons (Sections 41-60A)
- **Chapter XIII**: Remand (Section 187)
- Effective from **July 1, 2024**
- Enhanced safeguards for arrest

### Judicial Precedents (Constitutional Validity)

**D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal (1997)**: Landmark case establishing 11 commandments for arrest and custody

**Arnesh Kumar v. State of Bihar (2014)**: Mandatory notice of appearance for offences punishable with less than 7 years

**Joginder Kumar v. State of U.P. (1994)**: Arrest is not mandatory even if power exists

## When Can Police Arrest Without Warrant?

### Section 41 CrPC / 41 BNSS: Conditions for Arrest

Police officer may arrest **without warrant** only if:

#### Category A: Cognizable Offences (Section 41(1))

**ANY police officer** may arrest without warrant:

##### (a) Person Who Has Committed Cognizable Offence
- In officer's presence
- Or against whom reasonable complaint/credible information/reasonable suspicion exists

##### (b) Person in Possession of Stolen Property
- Reasonably suspected to be in possession of stolen property

##### (c) Obstruction of Police Officer
- Obstructs police officer in discharge of duty
- Or escapes or attempts to escape from lawful custody

##### (d) Proclaimed Offender
- Against whom proclamation issued under Section 82 CrPC / 81 BNSS

##### (e) In Possession of Implements of Housebreaking
- Without lawful excuse

##### (f) Proclaimed as Deserter
- From Armed Forces

##### (g) Outside India Offences
- Committed act outside India which if committed in India would be cognizable

##### (h) Released Convict Committing Breach
- Of conditions of release

##### (i) Reasonable Suspicion of Being Habitual Offender

#### Category B: Restricted Arrest (Section 41(1) Proviso & Section 41A)

**MAJOR RESTRICTION** (Added by CrPC Amendment 2008, continued in BNSS):

Police **CANNOT arrest** even for cognizable offence if:
1. Offence punishable with **imprisonment less than 7 years** OR **only fine**
2. AND accused is **not a habitual offender**
3. AND officer is **satisfied** about identity and residence

**Instead of arrest**, officer must issue **Notice of Appearance** asking accused to appear before officer/court

**Conditions for Notice** (Section 41A):
- Person must sign undertaking to appear
- If refuses to sign or fails to appear, then arrest permitted

**Landmark Case**: **Arnesh Kumar v. State of Bihar (2014)**
- Supreme Court mandated compliance with Section 41A
- **No automatic arrest** for offences below 7 years
- Arrest is exception, not rule
- Magistrate must scrutinize grounds of arrest in remand applications

### Section 41(2) CrPC / 41 BNSS: Further Restrictions

Even if power to arrest exists, officer may arrest only if:

1. Officer has **reason to believe** arrest is necessary:
   - To prevent accused from committing further offence; OR
   - For proper investigation; OR
   - To prevent tampering with evidence; OR
   - To prevent influencing witnesses; OR
   - To prevent accused from fleeing

2. **Unless arrest is necessary, arrest should not be made**

**Principle**: **Arrest is the exception, not the rule** (Joginder Kumar principle)

## Arrest With Warrant

### When Warrant Required?

**Non-Cognizable Offences**: Arrest only with warrant issued by Magistrate (Section 155)

**Cognizable Offences**: Warrant may be issued if Magistrate considers necessary

### Execution of Warrant (Sections 70-81 CrPC / 69-80 BNSS)

#### How Warrant Executed:
1. **Show warrant** to person to be arrested (Section 75)
2. **Inform** about substance of warrant
3. **Apprehend** the person
4. **Use reasonable force** if person resists (Section 46)

#### Territorial Limits:
- Generally within jurisdiction of issuing court
- Can be endorsed for execution outside jurisdiction (Section 77)

#### Validity: Until executed or cancelled

## Procedure for Arrest

### Mandatory Procedures (Sections 46-50, 50A, 54 CrPC / 45-49, 49A, 53 BNSS)

#### 1. Information of Grounds of Arrest (Section 50 CrPC / 49 BNSS)

**Constitutional Mandate** (Article 22(1)):
- Person arrested must be **informed of grounds of arrest** as soon as possible
- Cannot be denied this right

**Procedure**:
- Inform in language person understands
- In writing if requested
- Communicate full particulars of offence

**Exception**: Need not inform if arrested in another's presence

#### 2. Right to Bail (Section 50(2))

If arrested for **bailable offence**, officer must **inform** person of:
- Right to be released on bail
- Right to arrange sureties

#### 3. Search of Arrested Person (Section 51 CrPC / 50 BNSS)

Officer may **search** person arrested and:
- Seize any articles (weapons, stolen property, evidence)
- Make inventory
- Two witnesses required

**Female Search**:
- By another **female** only
- With **strict regard to decency** (Section 51(2))

#### 4. Medical Examination (Section 53, 53A, 54 CrPC / 52, 52A, 53 BNSS)

##### Section 53: Examination of Accused by Medical Practitioner
- **When**: If arrested person requests OR officer in charge directs
- **Purpose**: Document injuries, if any (protect against torture allegations)
- **Registered Medical Practitioner** to conduct
- **Blood, DNA samples**: Can be taken with consent or Magistrate's order

##### Section 54: Examination of Person Arrested by Police
- Medical examination **at time of arrest**
- Medical examination **after release** (to show condition)
- **D.K. Basu mandate**: Compulsory medical examination

#### 5. Production Before Magistrate (Section 56-57 CrPC / 55-56 BNSS)

**Article 22(3) + Section 57**:
- Person arrested **without warrant** must be produced before **Magistrate within 24 hours**
- 24 hours excludes journey time from place of arrest to Magistrate
- **Cannot be detained beyond 24 hours** without Magistrate's remand order

**Purpose**: Judicial oversight over detention

**Landmark Case**: **Khatri v. State of Bihar (1981)** - Bhagalpur blinding case
- 24-hour rule is fundamental right
- Cannot be violated under any circumstances

## D.K. Basu Guidelines (Arrest Safeguards)

### D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal (1997)

**Issue**: Custodial torture and deaths in police custody

**Supreme Court Directions** (11 Commandments):

### Safeguards at Time of Arrest

#### 1. Arrest Memo
- Prepare **Arrest Memo** at time of arrest
- Countersigned by arrested person
- Attested by one witness (preferably family member)
- Counter-signature need not be insisted if arrested person refuses

#### 2. Inform Family/Friend
- **Inform** a friend/relative/known person of arrest
- Inform that person is being taken to particular police station

#### 3. Right to Inform
- Arrested person has **right to inform** one person (family/friend) about arrest and place of custody

#### 4. Entry in Diary
- **Name and address** of person informed about arrest to be entered in **station diary**

### Safeguards During Custody

#### 5. Medical Examination (Timing)
- **Medical examination** of arrested person by competent doctor:
  - **At time of arrest** (to record pre-existing injuries)
  - **After release/bail** (to show condition)
- Copy of medical report to arrested person/relative

#### 6. Inspection Rights
- **Inspection of lock-up** by officer of rank of Deputy Superintendent or above
- Surprise visits to ensure no torture

#### 7. Interrogation Memo
- Interrogation to take place in presence of lawyer (if requested)
- Memo of interrogation prepared, signed by arrested person

#### 8. Copies of Documents
- Arrested person entitled to **copies** of all documents including:
  - Arrest memo
  - Medical examination report
  - Interrogation memo

### Safeguards for Remand

#### 9. Magistrate's Duty
- Magistrate to **satisfy** that arrest is in accordance with law
- Accused must be **produced in person**
- Magistrate to record satisfaction about no torture

#### 10. Legal Aid
- Inform arrested person of **right to legal aid**
- Provide legal aid if indigent

#### 11. Recording Statement
- If arrested person requests, Magistrate to **record statement** (to check torture)

**Status**: These guidelines have **force of law** until legislated (Prakash Singh case 2006)

**Implementation**: Now incorporated in Section 41B CrPC / 41B BNSS (Rights of Arrested Person)

## Police Custody and Remand

### Types of Custody

| Type | Granted By | Duration | Purpose |
|------|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Police Custody** | Magistrate (Section 167) | Max 15 days (in stages) | Investigation, interrogation |
| **Judicial Custody** | Magistrate (Section 167) | Max 60/90 days total | Awaiting investigation completion |
| **Judicial Custody (post charge sheet)** | Court | Until trial concludes | Awaiting trial |

### Section 167 CrPC / 187 BNSS: Procedure When Investigation Cannot Be Completed

#### Situation: Accused Arrested and Detained

**24-Hour Rule**:
- Accused must be produced before Magistrate within 24 hours
- Magistrate may authorize **detention** (remand)

#### Police Custody Remand

**Section 167(2)**:
- **Maximum**: 15 days **in total** (not continuous)
- Can be given in **stages** (e.g., 3 days, then 5 days, then 7 days)
- **First 15 days** from date of arrest
- Cannot exceed 15 days even if investigation not complete

**Purpose**: For investigation purposes (interrogation, confrontation, recovery)

**Conditions**:
- Magistrate must record **reasons** for police custody
- **"Adequate grounds"** must exist
- Not automatic; prosecution must justify

**Landmark Case**: **Niranjan Singh v. Prabhakar Rajaram (1980)**
- Police custody is exception
- Should be minimum necessary for investigation

#### Judicial Custody

**After police custody period or if police custody not needed**:
- Accused remanded to **judicial custody**
- Kept in **jail**, not police station
- No interrogation by police (without Court permission)

#### Maximum Detention Without Charge Sheet

**Section 167(2) Proviso**:

| Type of Offence | Maximum Detention |
|----------------|-------------------|
| Punishable with **death, life imprisonment, or ≥10 years** | **90 days** |
| **Other offences** | **60 days** |

**Consequence if charge sheet not filed within period**:
- Accused entitled to **DEFAULT BAIL** (indefeasible right)
- Must be released (even if serious offence)

**Landmark Case**: **Sanjay Dutt v. State (1994)**
- Default bail is **indefeasible statutory right**
- Once period expires, accused must be released
- This is mandatory, not discretionary

### Default Bail (Section 167(2) Proviso)

**Conditions**:
1. Accused arrested and detained
2. 60/90 day period expired
3. Charge sheet not filed

**Effect**: Accused **must** be released on bail

**Nature**: Statutory right (not discretionary)

**Important**: This is **in case custody** (person already arrested)
- Different from regular bail under Section 437

## Rights of Arrested Person

### Constitutional Rights

#### 1. Right to Be Informed of Grounds of Arrest (Article 22(1))
- Immediately on arrest
- In language understood
- Full particulars of offence

#### 2. Right to Consult Lawyer (Article 22(1))
- Right to consult legal practitioner of choice
- Right to be defended by lawyer

#### 3. Right Not to Be Detained Beyond 24 Hours (Article 22(3))
- Without Magistrate's order
- Fundamental right, cannot be violated

#### 4. Right Against Self-Incrimination (Article 20(3))
- Cannot be compelled to be witness against self
- Right to remain silent
- Applies during interrogation

#### 5. Right to Life and Liberty (Article 21))
- Arrest must be in accordance with fair procedure
- No torture, no custodial violence
- Humane treatment

### Statutory Rights (Section 41B, 50, 50A, 54, 56 CrPC / BNSS)

#### Section 41B CrPC / 41B BNSS: Rights of Arrested Person

Every arrested person has right to be informed:
1. **Grounds of arrest**
2. **Right to bail** (if bailable offence)
3. **Right to inform** friend/relative/known person about arrest and place of detention
4. **Right to consult lawyer** of choice
5. **Right to medical examination**
6. **Right to be produced before Magistrate** within 24 hours

#### Section 50A CrPC / 49A BNSS: Obligation to Inform About Arrest

**Police must inform** nominated person about arrest:
- **Immediately** after arrest
- By **letter** dispatched within 8-12 hours
- **Free of cost**

**If Nominated Person Outside State**:
- Additional communication to police officer of that area
- That officer to inform nominated person

#### Rights During Custody

1. **Medical examination** (Section 54): At time of arrest and release
2. **No handcuffs** (except in exceptional circumstances - Prem Shankar Shukla case)
3. **Human treatment**: Food, clothing, sanitation
4. **Protection from torture**: D.K. Basu safeguards
5. **Access to lawyer**: For legal advice
6. **Magistrate production**: Regular production if remand extended

## Restrictions on Arrest

### Constitutional and Judicial Restrictions

#### 1. No Arbitrary Arrest (Article 21)

**Joginder Kumar v. State of U.P. (1994)**:
- Arrest **only if necessary**
- Not mandatory even if power exists
- Officer must justify necessity

#### 2. Arnesh Kumar Principle (2014)

For offences **punishable with less than 7 years**:
- **No arrest** unless necessary
- Issue **notice of appearance** (Section 41A)
- Arrest only if person absconds or doesn't comply

#### 3. Anticipatory Bail Protection (Section 438 CrPC / 482 BNSS)

Person **apprehending arrest** can approach court for protection
- **See Protocol**: IL-CRIM-BAIL.md

### Statutory Restrictions

#### 1. Female Arrest (Section 46(4) CrPC / 45(4) BNSS)

**Special Protection**:
- Female cannot be arrested **after sunset and before sunrise**
- **Exception**: With permission of Magistrate in exceptional circumstances
- **Search**: Only by female officer with strict decency

#### 2. Arrest of Children (Juvenile Justice Act 2015)

- **Child in Conflict with Law** (below 18 years): Cannot be arrested like adults
- Special procedure under JJ Act
- Brought before **Juvenile Justice Board**, not Magistrate
- Kept in **observation home**, not jail
- **No handcuffs**

#### 3. Arrest of Mentally Ill (Section 330 CrPC / 395 BNSS)

- Special provisions for custody
- Must be kept in suitable place
- Treatment required

#### 4. Arrest of Members of Parliament/Legislative Assemblies

**Article 22(2)**: No special immunity
**BUT**: Information to Speaker/Chairman required (Rules of House)

#### 5. Arrest of Public Servants (Section 41(1)(a) CrPC)

For offences under Prevention of Corruption Act:
- **Sanction required** for prosecution (Section 19 PCA 1988)
- But **no sanction required for arrest** (CBI v. V.C. Shukla)
- However, practically sanction sought before arrest

## Use of Force in Arrest

### Section 46 CrPC / 45 BNSS: Arrest How Made

#### General Rule
- Police officer may use **all means necessary** to effect arrest
- Arrested person must **submit** to custody

#### Use of Force
**Section 46(2)**: Force permitted if:
1. Person **resists** or attempts to evade arrest
2. **Only reasonable force** can be used
3. No more force than necessary

#### Restriction on Causing Death
**Section 46(3)**:
- **Cannot cause death** of person accused of offence punishable with:
  - **Fine only**, or
  - Imprisonment **not exceeding 7 years**

- For offences punishable with **death or life imprisonment**:
  - Can cause death **if person cannot be otherwise apprehended**
  - **Last resort only**

**Principle**: Proportionality - force must be proportionate to offence and resistance

**Landmark Case**: **Darshan Singh v. State of Punjab (2010)**
- Use of excessive force in arrest amounts to assault
- Can lead to criminal prosecution of police officer
- Only reasonable force permitted

### Handcuffing

**General Rule**: Handcuffing is **violation of human dignity**

**Landmark Case**: **Prem Shankar Shukla v. Delhi Administration (1980)**
- Supreme Court held handcuffing is prima facie **inhuman and unreasonable**
- Violates Article 21 (right to human dignity)

**When Permitted**:
- **Only in exceptional circumstances**:
  1. Accused is extremely dangerous
  2. History of violence or escape
  3. Accused may cause harm to self or others
  4. Specific reasons must be recorded in writing

**Section 49 CrPC / 48 BNSS**: Person arrested not to be subjected to more restraint than necessary

**Guidelines** (Citizens for Democracy v. State of Assam, 1995):
- Handcuffing prohibited as routine
- Only in exceptional cases with justification
- Alternative restraint methods preferred
- If used, reasons to be recorded

## Arrest by Private Person (Citizen's Arrest)

### Section 43 CrPC / 43 BNSS: When Private Person May Arrest

**Any private person** may arrest:

1. Person who commits **non-bailable and cognizable offence** in his presence
2. **Proclaimed offender**

**Condition**: Must **without unnecessary delay** hand over person to police

**Limitation**: Cannot detain; must immediately give to police

**Section 45**: Private person acting under Section 43 is **protected** (same as police officer)

**Practical Examples**:
- Shopkeeper catching shoplifter red-handed
- Citizen apprehending person committing robbery
- Security guard catching trespasser committing theft

**Caution**: Risk of false arrest lawsuit if mistake made

## Arrest in Special Circumstances

### 1. Arrest Outside Jurisdiction

**Section 43 CrPC / 43 BNSS**: Police officer can pursue into other jurisdiction
**Section 48**: Transit warrant for taking accused through other states

### 2. Arrest at Night

**Generally Permitted**: No bar on arrest at night for cognizable offences
**Female**: Cannot arrest after sunset and before sunrise (Section 46(4))

### 3. Arrest Without Uniform

**Section 42A CrPC / 42A BNSS**:
- Officer not in uniform must **show identity card**
- Must carry authority letter from senior officer

### 4. Re-arrest After Release

**Permitted**: Person released on bail can be re-arrested
- If bail cancelled
- If commits fresh offence
- If violates bail conditions

### 5. Arrest of Accused Already on Bail

**Section 437(5) CrPC / 483(5) BNSS**: Can be arrested if:
- Court cancels bail
- Fresh offence committed
- Bail conditions violated

## Remedies Against Illegal/Wrongful Arrest

### 1. Habeas Corpus (Article 226 / 32)

**Most Effective Remedy**:
- Writ petition to **High Court** (Article 226) or **Supreme Court** (Article 32)
- For **immediate release** if detention illegal
- Court issues writ directing production of person
- If detention found illegal, person released immediately

**Grounds**:
- Arrest without authority
- Detention beyond 24 hours without remand
- Violation of arrest procedure
- Mala fide arrest

**Landmark Case**: **Rudul Sah v. State of Bihar (1983)**
- Person detained **14 years after acquittal**
- Supreme Court ordered release and awarded **compensation**

### 2. Bail Application

- **Anticipatory bail** (Section 438 CrPC / 482 BNSS): Before arrest
- **Regular bail** (Section 437 CrPC / 483 BNSS): After arrest
- **Default bail** (Section 167(2)): If charge sheet not filed in 60/90 days

**See Protocol**: IL-CRIM-BAIL.md

### 3. Complaint to Senior Officers

- SP/Commissioner/DIG
- Complaint of illegal arrest, torture
- Departmental inquiry against erring officer

### 4. Complaint to Magistrate (Section 155(2) CrPC / 174(2) BNSS)

- Non-cognizable offence complaint
- Magistrate can order investigation

### 5. Criminal Prosecution of Police Officer

**Offences by Police**:
- **IPC Section 166 / BNS 199**: Public servant disobeying law (illegal arrest)
- **IPC Section 220 / BNS 212**: Illegal detention
- **IPC Section 342 / BNS 127**: Wrongful confinement
- **IPC Section 348 / BNS 133**: Wrongful confinement with threat
- **IPC Section 323-326 / BNS 115-121**: Assault (if force used)
- **IPC Section 330 / BNS 127**: Voluntarily causing hurt to extort confession

**Requirement**: Generally, sanction required for prosecution of public servant (Section 197 CrPC / 218 BNSS)

### 6. Civil Suit for Damages

**Malicious Prosecution**:
- Suit for damages in civil court
- Must prove: (1) prosecution initiated, (2) without reasonable cause, (3) maliciously, (4) prosecution terminated in accused's favor

**False Imprisonment**:
- Tort action for unlawful detention
- Compensation for loss of liberty

**Landmark Case**: **Bhim Singh v. State of J&K (1986)**
- MLA illegally arrested and detained
- Supreme Court awarded **Rs. 50,000 compensation** for violation of fundamental rights

## Custodial Deaths and Torture

### Constitutional and Legal Framework

**Article 21**: Protection of life and personal liberty includes protection from custodial torture

**IPC Sections** (now BNS):
- **Section 330 / BNS 127**: Voluntarily causing hurt to extort confession
- **Section 331 / BNS 128**: Voluntarily causing grievous hurt to extort confession

### D.K. Basu Safeguards

(Already covered above - 11 commandments)

### National Human Rights Commission (NHRC)

**NHRC Act 1993**:
- Mandatory reporting of **custodial deaths** to NHRC within 24 hours
- NHRC inquiry and recommendations
- Compensation to victims

### Landmark Cases

#### 1. **D.K. Basu v. State of West Bengal (1997)**
- Leading case on custodial torture
- 11 safeguards laid down (detailed above)

#### 2. **Nilabati Behera v. State of Orissa (1993)**
- Youth died in police custody
- Supreme Court awarded **Rs. 1.5 lakh compensation** to mother
- Under **public law remedy** (Article 32)

#### 3. **Sheela Barse v. State of Maharashtra (1983)**
- Women in police lock-ups
- Directions for protection of women in custody

#### 4. **Khatri v. State of Bihar (1981)** - Bhagalpur Blinding Case
- Police blinded under-trial prisoners with acid
- Supreme Court ordered compensation and prosecution of officers
- Emphasized 24-hour production rule

## Arrest Under Special Statutes

### Differences from CrPC/BNSS

Many special statutes have **stricter provisions** for arrest and bail:

| Statute | Arrest Provision | Bail Provision |
|---------|------------------|----------------|
| **NDPS Act 1985** (Drugs) | Section 41, 42, 43 | Section 37 - **twin conditions** for bail |
| **PMLA 2002** (Money Laundering) | Section 19 | Section 45 - **very strict** twin conditions |
| **UAPA 1967** (Terrorism) | Section 43 | Section 43D - restrictive bail |
| **PCA 1988** (Corruption) | No special provision | Regular CrPC bail applies |
| **POCSO Act 2012** (Child Sexual Abuse) | CrPC applies | Anticipatory bail **generally not granted** |

**Twin Conditions** (NDPS Section 37, PMLA Section 45):
For bail, court must be satisfied:
1. There are **reasonable grounds** to believe accused **not guilty**, AND
2. Accused **not likely to commit** any offence while on bail

**Effect**: Very difficult to get bail in these cases

**Landmark Case**: **Nikesh Tarachand Shah v. Union of India (2018)**
- Supreme Court upheld constitutional validity of Section 45 PMLA
- Stringent bail conditions justified due to seriousness of money laundering

## CrPC to BNSS Transition (Arrest Provisions)

### Arrest Procedure: CrPC 1973 vs BNSS 2023

| Aspect | CrPC 1973 | BNSS 2023 | Change |
|--------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| **Arrest without warrant** | Section 41 | Section 41 | Same conditions |
| **Notice of Appearance** | Section 41A | Section 41A | Same (Arnesh Kumar principle continues) |
| **Rights of arrested person** | Section 41B | Section 41B | Same |
| **Procedure of arrest** | Sections 46-60 | Sections 45-60 | Largely same |
| **Female arrest** | Section 46(4) - not after sunset | Section 45(4) - same | **No change** |
| **Information to person** | Section 50A | Section 49A | Same (inform nominated person) |
| **Medical examination** | Sections 53, 54 | Sections 52, 53 | Same |
| **24-hour production** | Section 56, 57 | Section 55, 56 | Same constitutional mandate |
| **Remand** | Section 167 | Section 187 | **Section number changed; 60/90 day rule continues** |
| **Handcuffing** | Not specifically addressed | Section 35(3) - **new explicit provision** | **Enhancement** |

### Key BNSS Enhancement: Handcuffing Provision

**Section 35(3) BNSS**:
- Explicit provision on **use of handcuffs**
- Can be used only if:
  1. Person has committed **heinous crime** (punishable with death or life), AND
  2. **Reasons recorded in writing** by police officer
- Codifies Prem Shankar Shukla judgment

**Impact**: Statutory restriction on handcuffing (previously only judicial precedent)

## Practical Compliance Checklist

### For Police Officers (Arrest Procedure)

#### Before Arrest
- [ ] Verify if offence is cognizable
- [ ] Check if offence punishable with <7 years (if yes, issue notice under Section 41A instead of arrest unless exceptional)
- [ ] Assess **necessity** of arrest (Joginder Kumar / Arnesh Kumar principle)
- [ ] Record reasons for arrest in writing (Section 41(2))
- [ ] If female, ensure arrest not after sunset/before sunrise (except with Magistrate permission)
- [ ] Prepare to inform grounds of arrest

#### At Time of Arrest
- [ ] **Inform** person of **grounds of arrest** (Section 50 / Article 22(1))
- [ ] Prepare **arrest memo** with details (D.K. Basu)
- [ ] Get counter-signature of arrested person (or record refusal)
- [ ] Attest by one witness (preferably family member)
- [ ] **Inform** one friend/relative/known person about arrest and place of detention
- [ ] Enter details in **station diary**
- [ ] If bailable offence, inform person of **right to bail**
- [ ] Show **identity card** if not in uniform (Section 42A)

#### Immediately After Arrest
- [ ] Conduct **medical examination** by registered medical practitioner (Section 54)
- [ ] Record all injuries (pre-existing)
- [ ] Give copy of medical report to arrested person or relative
- [ ] Search arrested person (Section 51) with two witnesses; if female, search by female officer
- [ ] Seize articles and prepare inventory
- [ ] Dispatch **letter to nominated person** within 8-12 hours (Section 50A)

#### Within 24 Hours
- [ ] **Produce** arrested person before **Magistrate within 24 hours** (Article 22(3) + Section 57)
- [ ] 24 hours excludes journey time
- [ ] Prepare case diary entry

#### During Custody
- [ ] If remand sought, file application with grounds
- [ ] Magistrate to record satisfaction
- [ ] Police custody maximum 15 days in stages
- [ ] Ensure no torture, no third degree (D.K. Basu safeguards)
- [ ] Provide food, clothing, sanitation
- [ ] Regular medical check-ups if needed
- [ ] No handcuffing unless heinous crime and reasons recorded (Section 35(3) BNSS)

#### Before Release
- [ ] Conduct **medical examination** again (Section 54)
- [ ] Compare with arrest-time medical report
- [ ] Give copy to person released
- [ ] Return personal belongings

### For Arrested Person (Know Your Rights)

#### At Time of Arrest
- [ ] Ask for **grounds of arrest** in writing
- [ ] Request to **inform** one family member/friend
- [ ] Ask for **medical examination** (right under Section 54)
- [ ] Cooperate but do not self-incriminate (Article 20(3))
- [ ] **Sign arrest memo** or refuse (your choice)

#### Immediately After
- [ ] Insist on medical examination to record any injuries
- [ ] Get copy of medical report
- [ ] Contact **lawyer** (right under Article 22(1))
- [ ] If indigent, request legal aid

#### Production Before Magistrate (Within 24 Hours)
- [ ] You **must be produced** within 24 hours
- [ ] Inform Magistrate if tortured or threatened
- [ ] Request Magistrate to record your statement (D.K. Basu right)
- [ ] Apply for bail if applicable
- [ ] If police seeking remand, you can oppose

#### During Custody
- [ ] Insist on human treatment
- [ ] No torture - report immediately if occurs
- [ ] Request lawyer visit
- [ ] Keep track of custody period (60/90 days)
- [ ] Apply for **default bail** if charge sheet not filed within 60/90 days

### For Family/Friends

- [ ] If informed about arrest, note down:
  - Time of arrest
  - Place of custody (which police station)
  - Arresting officer's name
- [ ] Contact lawyer immediately
- [ ] Visit police station to verify arrest
- [ ] Ensure person medically examined
- [ ] File **habeas corpus** if detention illegal or person not produced within 24 hours
- [ ] Apply for anticipatory bail if arrest apprehended
- [ ] Apply for regular bail after arrest

## Conclusion

Arrest and custody are **necessary evils** in the criminal justice system. While necessary to ensure:
- Presence of accused during investigation and trial
- Prevention of further crimes
- Protection of witnesses and evidence

They also represent **severe curtailment of personal liberty**, which is a fundamental right under Article 21.

**Key Principles**:
1. **Arrest is the exception, not the rule** (Joginder Kumar)
2. **No automatic arrest for offences <7 years** (Arnesh Kumar)
3. **Strict procedural safeguards** (D.K. Basu)
4. **Production within 24 hours** (Article 22(3))
5. **Default bail if charge sheet not filed** in 60/90 days (Section 167(2))
6. **No torture, human treatment** (Article 21)
7. **Right to legal aid and fair trial**

The transition from CrPC to BNSS continues these safeguards with **explicit provisions on handcuffing** (Section 35(3)) - codifying Supreme Court judgments.

**Bottom Line**: Liberty is precious. Arrest must be justified, procedurally correct, and subject to strict judicial oversight.
