## Overview and Purpose

### What is Burden of Proof?

**Burden of Proof** is the **obligation to prove or disprove** a disputed fact or issue in a legal proceeding. In criminal law, it determines **who must prove what** to establish guilt or innocence.

### Purpose

1. **Allocate Responsibility**: Clarify which party bears duty to prove facts
2. **Protect Innocence**: Prevent wrongful conviction by requiring prosecution to prove guilt
3. **Ensure Fairness**: Balance between state's resources and accused's rights
4. **Guide Adjudication**: Help court determine when case is proved or not

### Two Aspects of Burden

#### 1. Burden of Proof (Legal Burden)
**Who must prove** the fact to win the case

**In Criminal Law**: Generally on **prosecution** to prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt

#### 2. Burden of Producing Evidence (Evidential Burden)
**Who must produce evidence** on a particular issue

**May Shift**: During trial, burden of producing evidence may shift, but burden of proof generally does not

## Fundamental Principle: Burden on Prosecution

### Section 101 Evidence Act / BSA

**"Whoever desires any Court to give judgment as to any legal right or liability dependent on the existence of facts which he asserts, must prove that those facts exist."**

**In Criminal Cases**:
- **Prosecution asserts** accused committed offence
- **Prosecution must prove** those facts

### Golden Thread of Criminal Jurisprudence

**Woolmington v. DPP (1935)** (English case adopted in India):

> "Throughout the web of the English Criminal Law one golden thread is always to be seen—that it is the duty of the prosecution to prove the prisoner's guilt..."

**Principle**: **Burden on prosecution to prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt; burden NEVER shifts to accused** (except specific statutory exceptions)

**Indian Adoption**: This principle is **foundation of Indian criminal law**

**Landmark Case**: **State of West Bengal v. Mir Mohammad Omar (2000)**
- Supreme Court held: Burden is on prosecution to prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt
- Accused need not prove innocence
- If prosecution fails, accused entitled to acquittal

## Standard of Proof in Criminal Cases

### "Beyond Reasonable Doubt"

**Criminal Standard**: Prosecution must prove guilt **beyond reasonable doubt**

**NOT**:
- Beyond all doubt (impossible standard)
- Balance of probabilities (civil standard)

**Reasonable Doubt**:
- Doubt for which **reason can be assigned**
- Not fanciful, imaginary, or hypothetical doubt
- Based on evidence or lack thereof

**Landmark Case**: **Sharad Birdhichand Sarda v. State of Maharashtra (1984)**
- Proof beyond reasonable doubt means:
  - Moral certainty (not mathematical certainty)
  - Degree of proof which satisfies conscience of court
  - Evidence so cogent that court can safely act upon it

### Benefit of Doubt

**If Reasonable Doubt Exists**: Accused entitled to **benefit of doubt** and must be **acquitted**

**Landmark Case**: **Kali Ram v. State of H.P. (1973)**
- Benefit of doubt is not to be given lightly
- But if prosecution fails to prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt, acquittal mandatory

## Sections 101-114A Evidence Act / BSA: Detailed Analysis

### Section 101: Burden of Proof

**"Whoever desires any Court to give judgment as to any legal right or liability dependent on the existence of facts which he asserts, must prove that those facts exist."**

**Application**:
- **Prosecution asserts** accused guilty
- **Prosecution proves** commission of offence

**Burden Never Shifts**: In criminal cases, burden of proving guilt never shifts to accused (except statutory exceptions)

### Section 102: On Whom Burden of Proof Lies

**"The burden of proof in a suit or proceeding lies on that person who would fail if no evidence at all were given on either side."**

**Application in Criminal Cases**:
- If no evidence presented by either side, prosecution would **fail**
- Therefore, **burden on prosecution**

**Illustration**: If accused charged with theft, and no evidence presented:
- Prosecution fails (cannot prove theft)
- Accused acquitted (presumption of innocence)

### Section 103: Burden of Proof as to Particular Fact

**"The burden of proof as to any particular fact lies on that person who wishes the Court to believe in its existence, unless it is provided by any law that the proof of that fact shall lie on any particular person."**

**Application**:
- **General Rule**: Party asserting fact must prove it
- **Exception**: Unless statute provides otherwise (reverse burden)

**Criminal Law Example**:
- Prosecution asserts accused committed murder (fact)
- Prosecution must prove: (1) death occurred, (2) accused caused it, (3) with intention/knowledge
- If accused claims alibi (fact that he was elsewhere), accused must produce evidence (evidential burden)

### Section 104: Burden of Proving Fact to Be Proved to Make Evidence Admissible

**Preliminary facts** for admissibility must be proved by party relying on evidence

**Example**: If prosecution wants to use confession:
- Must prove confession was voluntary (not coerced)
- Burden on prosecution

### Section 105: Burden of Proving Exception

**"When a person is accused of any offence, the burden of proving the existence of circumstances bringing the case within any of the General Exceptions in the Indian Penal Code, or within any special exception or proviso contained in any other part of the same Code, or in any law defining the offence, is upon him, and the Court shall presume the absence of such circumstances."**

**Key Provision**: **Exception to "burden on prosecution" rule**

**Application**:
- If accused claims benefit of **general exception** (e.g., self-defense, insanity, mistake of fact)
- **Burden on accused** to prove existence of exception

**Standard for Accused**: **Preponderance of probabilities** (not beyond reasonable doubt)
- Accused need not prove beyond reasonable doubt
- Only needs to show more probable than not

**Landmark Case**: **Dahyabhai Chhaganbhai Thakker v. State of Gujarat (1964)**
- Supreme Court held Section 105 is exception to general rule
- Burden on accused to prove general exceptions
- But standard is preponderance of probabilities, not beyond reasonable doubt

**Examples of General Exceptions** (IPC Sections 76-106 / BNS Sections 6-26):
- Section 79 IPC / BNS 6: Act done by person justified by law
- Section 81 IPC / BNS 8: Act likely to cause harm done without criminal intent for lawful object
- Section 82-83 IPC / BNS 10-11: Act of child below 7 years / above 7 below 12 years
- Section 84 IPC / BNS 13: Act of person of unsound mind
- Section 96-106 IPC / BNS 14-24: Private defense of body and property

**See Protocol**: IL-CRIM-GENERAL-EXCEPTIONS.md for detailed analysis

### Section 106: Burden of Proving Fact Especially Within Knowledge

**"When any fact is especially within the knowledge of any person, the burden of proving that fact is upon him."**

**Rationale**: If fact is within accused's exclusive knowledge, accused must explain

**Landmark Case**: **State of West Bengal v. Mir Mohammad Omar (2000)**
- Section 106 does not shift burden of proof
- Only shifts **burden of producing evidence** (evidential burden)
- Prosecution still bears burden of proving guilt beyond reasonable doubt

**Application**:
- Used when accused has exclusive or special knowledge of fact
- Accused must offer explanation

**Example**: **Sharad Birdhichand Sarda v. State of Maharashtra (1984)**
- Wife went missing after being last seen with husband
- Husband did not explain what happened
- Section 106 applied: Husband had special knowledge of wife's whereabouts
- Failure to explain is circumstance against him

**Limitation**: Section 106 is **rebuttable presumption**, not conclusive
- Accused's failure to explain is one circumstance
- Does not automatically prove guilt
- Prosecution must still prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt

### Sections 107-114: Specific Presumptions

#### Section 107: Burden of Proving Death Within 30 Years
- Burden on person asserting death occurred within 30 years
- If neither party proves, presumption that person is **alive**

#### Section 108: Burden of Proving Death of Person Known to Be Alive Within 30 Years
- If person was alive within 30 years and not heard of for 7 years by those who would naturally hear
- **Presumption**: Person is dead

#### Section 109: Burden of Proof as to Relationship
- Burden on person asserting relationship exists

#### Section 110: Burden of Proof as to Ownership
- Burden on person asserting ownership

#### Section 111: Proof of Good Faith in Transactions Where One Party is in Relation of Active Confidence
- If transaction between parties in fiduciary relationship
- Burden on beneficiary to prove good faith

#### Section 111A: Presumption as to Electronic Records
- In any proceedings involving secure electronic record:
  - Presumed that electronic signature is affixed by subscriber with intent
  - Secure electronic record has not been altered
  - **Added by IT Act 2000**

#### Section 112: Birth During Marriage - Legitimacy
- Child born during valid marriage is **presumed legitimate**
- Burden on person denying legitimacy

#### Section 113: Proof of Cession of Territory
- Burden on person asserting cession

#### Section 113A: Presumption as to Abetment of Suicide by Married Woman
**Specific to Dowry Cases**:
- If woman commits suicide within 7 years of marriage
- If husband/relatives subjected her to cruelty
- **Presumption**: Husband/relatives abetted suicide

**Applicability**: IPC Section 306 / BNS Section 104 (Abetment of Suicide)

**Standard**: Rebuttable presumption
- Prosecution must prove: (1) suicide, (2) within 7 years of marriage, (3) cruelty
- If proved, presumption arises
- Accused can rebut

**Landmark Case**: **Ude Singh v. State of Haryana (2019)**
- Section 113A is rebuttable presumption
- Prosecution must first prove ingredients
- Then presumption aids prosecution

#### Section 113B: Presumption as to Dowry Death
**Dowry Death Cases**:
- If woman dies within 7 years of marriage (otherwise than under normal circumstances)
- If shown she was subjected to cruelty or harassment for dowry before death
- **Presumption**: Husband/relatives caused dowry death

**Applicability**: IPC Section 304B / BNS Section 80 (Dowry Death)

**Effect**: **Reverse burden** - accused must prove innocence

**Landmark Case**: **Kaliyaperumal v. State of Tamil Nadu (2004)**
- Section 113B creates strong presumption
- Once prosecution proves: (1) death within 7 years, (2) cruelty/harassment for dowry
- Presumption arises; accused must rebut

**Critical Distinction**:
- **Section 113A** (Abetment of Suicide): Rebuttable presumption (aids prosecution)
- **Section 113B** (Dowry Death): Stronger presumption (near-reverse burden)

#### Section 114: Court May Presume Existence of Certain Facts

**Discretionary Presumptions**: Court **may presume** (not must):

**Illustrations** (from Section 114):

##### (a) Judicial and Official Acts Regular
- Court may presume judicial and official acts have been regularly performed
- Example: Magistrate's order presumed to be within jurisdiction

##### (b) Common Course of Business
- Court may presume common course of business has been followed
- Example: Cheque sent by post presumed to be received

##### (c) Evidence Withheld
- If party could produce evidence and deliberately withholds it:
- **Presumption**: Evidence, if produced, would be unfavorable to that party

**Landmark Case**: **Ramesh Chandra Agarwal v. Regency Hospital Ltd. (2009)**
- Adverse inference can be drawn against party who withholds evidence
- But discretionary; court considers facts

##### (d) Accomplice is Unworthy of Credit
- Evidence of accomplice should be treated with caution
- But can be basis of conviction if corroborated

**Landmark Case**: **State of Punjab v. Balwinder Singh (2012)**
- Accomplice evidence must be corroborated
- Conviction on sole testimony of accomplice risky (but legally permissible)

##### (e) Altered Documents
- If document appears to be altered:
- Presumption: Alteration made after execution

##### (f) Tampering
- If document appears tampered:
- Presumption: Tampering done for improper purpose

##### (g) Property Stolen
- If person in possession of recently stolen property:
- **Presumption**: Person either stole it or received it knowing it was stolen

**Illustration**: Accused found with stolen goods within short time after theft
- Section 114(a) presumption: Accused either thief or receiver

**Landmark Case**: **Rama Shankar v. State of U.P. (2015)**
- Possession of recently stolen property gives rise to presumption under Section 114
- Accused must explain possession
- If fails to explain satisfactorily, can be convicted

## Reverse Burden: Statutory Exceptions

### When Burden Shifts to Accused

**General Rule**: Burden on prosecution

**Exception**: Specific statutes create **reverse burden** (accused must prove innocence or exception)

### Examples of Reverse Burden Provisions

#### 1. Dowry Death (IPC Section 304B / BNS Section 80 + Evidence Act Section 113B)
- Prosecution proves: Death within 7 years of marriage + Cruelty/harassment for dowry
- **Presumption**: Accused caused dowry death
- **Burden shifts**: Accused must prove innocence

#### 2. Abetment of Suicide (IPC Section 306 / BNS Section 104 + Evidence Act Section 113A)
- Prosecution proves: Suicide within 7 years + Cruelty
- **Presumption**: Accused abetted suicide
- Accused must rebut

#### 3. Prevention of Corruption Act 1988
**Section 20 PCA** (now Section 24 after 2018 amendment):
- If accused accepted gratification (bribe) other than legal remuneration:
- **Presumption**: Gratification was for corrupt motive
- **Burden on accused** to prove it was not corrupt

**Landmark Case**: **Neeraj Dutta v. State (NCT of Delhi) (2023)**
- Supreme Court held Section 20 PCA (pre-2018) created mandatory presumption
- Once prosecution proved acceptance of gratification, presumption arose
- Accused must rebut

#### 4. Prevention of Money Laundering Act (PMLA) 2002
**Section 24 PMLA** (Pre-2024 Amendment):
- **Reverse burden**: Accused must prove property not proceeds of crime

**Controversial**: Constitutional validity challenged

**Vijay Madanlal Choudhary v. Union of India (2022)**:
- Supreme Court **upheld** constitutional validity of Section 24 PMLA
- Held reverse burden justified given nature of money laundering

**2024 Amendment**: Section 24 PMLA **struck down** by Supreme Court in subsequent decision
- Reverse burden now removed in PMLA cases
- **Update**: Check latest position post-2024

#### 5. Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances (NDPS) Act 1985
**Section 35 NDPS**:
- Presumption of **culpable mental state** (mens rea)
- If accused found in possession of drugs:
- **Presumption**: Accused possessed knowingly and intentionally
- Burden on accused to prove lack of knowledge/intent

**Section 54 NDPS** (Reverse Burden in Bail):
- While not direct reverse burden of guilt, bail provisions (Section 37) create **twin conditions**
- Accused must show grounds to believe not guilty (quasi-reverse burden)

#### 6. Information Technology Act 2000
**Section 79 IT Act**:
- If electronic signature verified:
- **Presumption**: Document was signed by person whose signature it purports to be

#### 7. Arms Act 1959
**Section 25 Arms Act**:
- If accused found in possession of arms/ammunition:
- **Presumption**: Possession with intent to use for purposes prejudicial to public peace
- Burden on accused to prove lawful purpose

## Presumptions: Rebuttable vs Irrebuttable

### Rebuttable Presumptions (Most Presumptions)

**Can be Rebutted**: Accused can produce evidence to disprove presumption

**Examples**:
- Section 105 (General Exceptions): Accused can prove exception
- Section 106 (Special Knowledge): Accused can explain
- Section 113A (Abetment of Suicide): Accused can rebut
- Section 113B (Dowry Death): Accused can prove innocence
- Section 114 (Discretionary Presumptions): Can be rebutted

**Standard for Rebuttal**: **Preponderance of probabilities** (not beyond reasonable doubt)

### Irrebuttable Presumptions (Conclusive)

**Cannot be Rebutted**: Once proved, conclusive

**Examples** (in Evidence Act):
- **Section 41**: Judgments in rem (conclusive as to status)
- **Section 112**: Legitimacy of child (very difficult to rebut; DNA evidence may rebut)

**Criminal Law**: Very few irrebuttable presumptions (fairness requires rebuttal opportunity)

## Burden of Proving General Exceptions (Section 105)

### General Exceptions in IPC (Sections 76-106) / BNS (Sections 6-26)

**See Protocol**: IL-CRIM-GENERAL-EXCEPTIONS.md for detailed analysis

**Burden**: Section 105 places burden on accused to prove general exception

**Standard**: **Preponderance of probabilities** (not beyond reasonable doubt)

### Examples

#### 1. Self-Defense (IPC Sections 96-106 / BNS Sections 14-24)
- If accused claims killed in self-defense:
- **Burden on accused** to prove: (1) imminent threat, (2) no safe retreat, (3) force proportionate
- Standard: Preponderance of probabilities

**Landmark Case**: **Darshan Singh v. State of Punjab (2010)**
- Accused claiming self-defense must prove it
- But standard is preponderance of probabilities
- If accused shows more likely than not, entitled to acquittal

#### 2. Insanity (IPC Section 84 / BNS Section 13)
- If accused claims was insane at time of offence:
- **Burden on accused** to prove insanity
- Standard: Preponderance of probabilities

**Landmark Case**: **Surendra Mishra v. State of Jharkhand (2011)**
- Burden on accused to prove unsound mind at time of offence
- Medical evidence helpful but not mandatory
- Conduct before/after offence relevant

#### 3. Mistake of Fact (IPC Section 76, 79 / BNS Section 6, 9)
- If accused claims acted under bona fide mistake of fact:
- Burden on accused to prove mistake
- Must show belief was reasonable

#### 4. Intoxication (IPC Section 85-86 / BNS Section 18-19)
- If accused claims was intoxicated (involuntary):
- Burden on accused to prove involuntary intoxication
- Voluntary intoxication generally not defense (except for specific intent crimes)

## Practical Application: Burden in Different Stages of Trial

### 1. Framing of Charges / Discharge

**Standard**: **Prima facie case**

**Burden**: On prosecution to show prima facie case

**Landmark Case**: **State of Bihar v. Ramesh Singh (1977)**
- At discharge stage, court does not require proof beyond reasonable doubt
- Only prima facie case
- If two views possible, one leading to conviction and other to discharge, benefit to accused

### 2. Trial - Prosecution Evidence

**Burden**: On prosecution to prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt

**Procedure**:
- Prosecution examines witnesses
- Prosecution produces documentary/physical evidence
- Establishes corpus delicti (body of crime)
- Connects accused to crime

**If Prosecution Fails**: Acquittal at this stage (no defense needed)

**Landmark Case**: **State of W.B. v. Orilal Jaiswal (1994)**
- If prosecution evidence so weak that no case made out:
- Accused can be acquitted without calling defense
- Section 232 CrPC / 256 BNSS

### 3. Section 313 Statement

**Not Evidence**: Accused's statement under Section 313 not evidence

**Purpose**: Opportunity to explain incriminating circumstances

**Burden**: No burden on accused to explain (right to silence)
- But failure to explain can be circumstance against accused (not determinative)

### 4. Defense Evidence

**No Burden**: Accused has **no burden to prove innocence**

**But**: If accused claims exception (e.g., self-defense), burden on accused to prove exception (Section 105)

**Procedure**:
- Accused may remain silent (Article 20(3))
- OR produce defense evidence:
  - Alibi witnesses
  - Character witnesses
  - Documentary evidence
- OR testify himself (subject to cross-examination)

### 5. Final Arguments

**Prosecution**: Summarizes evidence, argues guilt proved beyond reasonable doubt

**Defense**: Challenges prosecution evidence, highlights reasonable doubt, argues exceptions/defenses

### 6. Judgment

**Court Considers**:
- Has prosecution proved guilt beyond reasonable doubt?
- Has accused proved any exception (if claimed)?

**If Reasonable Doubt**: Acquittal (benefit of doubt to accused)

## Burden vs Standard: Key Distinctions

### Burden of Proof

**Who must prove**: Prosecution (generally)

**Exceptions**: Accused must prove general exceptions (Section 105), rebut presumptions

### Standard of Proof

| Party | Standard | Explanation |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| **Prosecution** (proving guilt) | **Beyond reasonable doubt** | Moral certainty; high standard |
| **Accused** (proving exception/rebuttal) | **Preponderance of probabilities** | More likely than not; lower standard |
| **Civil cases** | **Preponderance of probabilities** | Balance of probabilities |

**Rationale**: Criminal cases involve liberty/life; high standard protects innocent

## Practical Compliance Checklist

### For Prosecution

#### Building Case
- [ ] Identify **all elements** of offence that must be proved
- [ ] Collect **evidence** to prove each element:
  - Witness testimony
  - Documentary evidence
  - Physical evidence
  - Expert evidence
- [ ] Ensure evidence proves guilt **beyond reasonable doubt**
- [ ] Prepare to rebut **any exception** accused may claim

#### At Trial
- [ ] Present evidence **systematically** (proving each element)
- [ ] Examine witnesses **effectively** (examination-in-chief)
- [ ] Produce **all material evidence**
- [ ] **Cross-examine** defense witnesses to challenge credibility
- [ ] In final arguments, show **guilt proved beyond reasonable doubt**:
  - Recap evidence
  - Connect evidence to each element
  - Address any reasonable doubt
  - Argue no exception applies (if claimed)

#### If Accused Claims Exception (e.g., Self-Defense)
- [ ] Prosecution does not bear burden to disprove exception
- [ ] But should present evidence challenging exception if available
- [ ] In arguments, show accused failed to prove exception on preponderance

### For Defense

#### Case Analysis
- [ ] Identify **weaknesses** in prosecution case:
  - Missing evidence
  - Contradictions in testimony
  - Gaps in chain of evidence
  - Lack of motive/opportunity
- [ ] Determine if **exception** applies (self-defense, insanity, etc.)
- [ ] If exception claimed, gather **evidence** to prove exception

#### At Trial
- [ ] **Cross-examine** prosecution witnesses thoroughly:
  - Challenge credibility
  - Highlight contradictions
  - Elicit favorable facts
- [ ] If claiming exception, **produce defense evidence**:
  - Witnesses supporting exception
  - Documentary/expert evidence (e.g., medical evidence for insanity)
  - Accused's testimony (if advisable)
- [ ] In final arguments:
  - Highlight **reasonable doubt** in prosecution case
  - Show **exception proved** on preponderance (if claimed)
  - Request acquittal or conviction of lesser offence

#### Burden Strategy
- [ ] Remember: **No burden to prove innocence**
- [ ] Right to remain silent (Article 20(3))
- [ ] But if claiming exception (Section 105), **must produce evidence**
- [ ] Standard for exception: **Preponderance of probabilities** (not beyond reasonable doubt)

### For Courts

#### Framing of Charges / Discharge
- [ ] Apply **prima facie standard** (State of Bihar v. Ramesh Singh)
- [ ] If no prima facie case, **discharge** accused
- [ ] If two views possible (one conviction, one discharge), prefer discharge

#### During Trial
- [ ] Ensure **fair trial**: Equal opportunity to both sides
- [ ] Remind self: **Burden on prosecution** to prove guilt
- [ ] **No burden on accused** to prove innocence

#### Judgment
- [ ] Apply **beyond reasonable doubt** standard for guilt
- [ ] If accused claimed exception, determine if proved on **preponderance**
- [ ] If **reasonable doubt** exists, **acquit** (benefit of doubt to accused)
- [ ] If guilt proved beyond reasonable doubt AND no exception proved, **convict**
- [ ] Write **reasoned judgment** explaining:
  - Whether each element proved
  - Whether any exception proved
  - Why conviction or acquittal

## Common Errors and Misconceptions

### Errors

1. **Confusing burden of proof with burden of producing evidence**: Burden of proof generally does not shift; burden of producing evidence may shift

2. **Applying "beyond reasonable doubt" standard to accused's defense**: Accused need only prove exception on **preponderance**, not beyond reasonable doubt

3. **Treating Section 106 as shifting burden of proof**: Section 106 shifts only **evidential burden**, not burden of proving guilt (Mir Mohammad Omar case)

4. **Ignoring presumptions**: Failing to consider statutory presumptions (e.g., Section 113B dowry death)

5. **Placing burden on accused to prove innocence**: Violates fundamental principle; burden always on prosecution to prove guilt (except statutory exceptions)

### Misconceptions

**Misconception 1**: "Accused must prove they didn't commit crime"
**Reality**: Accused has **no burden** to prove innocence; prosecution must prove guilt

**Misconception 2**: "If accused doesn't testify, they're guilty"
**Reality**: Accused has **right to silence** (Article 20(3)); silence cannot be basis of conviction

**Misconception 3**: "Circumstantial evidence is weak evidence"
**Reality**: Conviction can be based on circumstantial evidence if **chain is complete** (Sharad Sarda case)

**Misconception 4**: "Section 106 shifts burden of proof"
**Reality**: Shifts only **evidential burden** (duty to produce evidence/explain); burden of proving guilt remains on prosecution

The **burden of proof** is the **cornerstone of criminal justice**, ensuring:
- **Presumption of innocence**: Accused innocent until proven guilty
- **Protection from wrongful conviction**: High standard (beyond reasonable doubt) protects innocent
- **Fair trial**: Burden on state (with greater resources) to prove guilt

**Fundamental Principles**:
1. **Burden on prosecution** to prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt (Section 101-102)
2. **Burden NEVER shifts** except specific statutory exceptions (Section 105, 113A, 113B, etc.)
3. **Standard for prosecution**: **Beyond reasonable doubt**
4. **Standard for accused** (proving exception): **Preponderance of probabilities**
5. **Benefit of doubt** to accused if reasonable doubt exists
6. **Right to silence**: Accused not compelled to testify (Article 20(3))
7. **Section 106** (special knowledge): Shifts **evidential burden**, NOT burden of proof

**Reverse Burden Provisions** (Exceptions):
- Section 105: General exceptions
- Section 113A: Abetment of suicide (dowry)
- Section 113B: Dowry death
- PCA Section 20/24: Corruption
- NDPS Section 35: Culpable mental state

**Evidence Act 1872 vs BSA 2023**:
- **No change** in burden of proof provisions
- Sections 101-114A retained identically
- All judicial precedents continue

**Ultimate Safeguard**: Burden of proof protects individual liberty from arbitrary state power - foundational to rule of law.
