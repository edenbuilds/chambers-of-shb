## Overview

This protocol covers **competency to contract** - who can and cannot enter into legally binding contracts under Indian law.

**Key Principle**: Only persons competent to contract can create valid contracts. Contracts by incompetent persons are either **void** or **voidable**.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 10: Essential of Valid Contract

```
"All agreements are contracts if they are made by the free consent of parties competent to contract..."
```

**Competency is ESSENTIAL** element of valid contract

### Section 11: Who are Competent to Contract

```
"Every person is competent to contract who is of the age of majority according to the law to which he is subject, and who is of sound mind and is not disqualified from contracting by any law to which he is subject."
```

**THREE Categories of Incompetent Persons**:
1. **Minors** (Below 18 years)
2. **Persons of Unsound Mind**
3. **Persons Disqualified by Law** (Alien enemies, insolvents, convicts, etc.)

---

## Category 1: Minors (Section 11)

### Definition of Minor

**Age of Majority**: Governed by **Indian Majority Act 1875**

**Section 3 Indian Majority Act**:
- **General Rule**: 18 years
- **Exception**: If court/guardian appointed → 21 years

**Example**:
- Child born on Jan 1, 2006 → Attains majority on Jan 1, 2024 (18th birthday)
- If guardian appointed by court → Attains majority on Jan 1, 2027 (21st birthday)

### Legal Position: Minor's Agreement is VOID AB INITIO

**Landmark Case: Mohori Bibee v. Dharmodas Ghose (1903)** (Privy Council - Binding on India)

**Facts**:
- Minor (Dharmodas Ghose, 17 years) executed mortgage deed for Rs. 20,000
- Lender (Brahmo Dutt) advanced Rs. 8,000
- Minor's mother sued to set aside mortgage (son still minor)
- Defendant argued: Minor's contract voidable (can be ratified after majority)

**Privy Council Held**:
- **Minor's agreement is VOID AB INITIO** (void from the beginning)
- Not merely voidable
- **Cannot be ratified** after attaining majority
- Minor not liable to return money or property received

**Effect**:
- No contract exists at all
- No obligation on minor
- No question of enforcement or ratification

### Consequences of Mohori Bibee Principle

#### 1. Minor Cannot Be Sued on Contract

**Rule**: Minor cannot be sued for breach of contract or to enforce contractual obligations

**Reason**: Contract void; no legal obligation exists

**Example**: Minor borrows Rs. 1 lakh, spends it, refuses to repay → Cannot be sued (contract void)

#### 2. Minor Cannot Sue on Contract (General Rule)

**Rule**: Since contract void, minor also generally cannot sue

**Exception**: **If minor partly performed** and wants to recover property/money
- **Minority as Shield, Not Sword**: Minor can use minority to escape liability, but cannot enforce void agreement

#### 3. Goods Supplied to Minor - No Liability

**General Rule**: Minor not liable to pay for goods supplied

**Exception: Necessaries** (Section 68)

**Section 68**: "If a person, incapable of entering into a contract, or any one whom he is legally bound to support, is supplied by another person with necessaries suited to his condition in life, the person who has furnished such supplies is entitled to be reimbursed from the property of such incapable person."

**Key Points**:
- **Reimbursement from property**: Can claim from minor's property (not personal liability)
- **Necessaries**: Essential goods (food, clothing, shelter, education, medical)
- **Suited to condition in life**: Luxury car NOT necessity for poor minor

**Landmark Case: Nash v. Inman (1908)** (English case, persuasive):
- Minor (Cambridge undergraduate) bought 11 fancy waistcoats
- Tailor sued for price
- Court held: NOT necessaries (minor already sufficiently supplied with clothes)

**Indian Position** (Section 68):
- Supplier can recover from **minor's property** (not personal obligation)
- Must prove: (1) Necessaries, (2) Suited to minor's condition, (3) Minor has property

#### 4. Money Advanced to Minor - No Recovery

**General Rule**: Money lent to minor cannot be recovered

**Case: Mohori Bibee**: Minor borrowed Rs. 8,000; lender could not recover

**Exception: Necessaries** (Section 68):
- If money advanced for necessaries (food, medicine) and used for that purpose → Can recover from minor's property

#### 5. Minor's Property - No Specific Performance Against Minor

**Specific Performance Act, Section 14(d)**: Court will not enforce specific performance against minor

**Example**: Minor agrees to sell land; later refuses → Court will not order specific performance (minor protected)

#### 6. Minor as Partner

**Indian Partnership Act, Section 30**: Minor cannot be partner in firm

**But**: Minor can be **admitted to benefits of partnership** (shares profits but not liable for losses)

#### 7. Minor's Liability in Tort (Wrongful Act)

**General Rule**: Minor liable for torts (civil wrongs like assault, trespass)

**Exception**: **Cannot convert contract into tort** to make minor liable

**Case: Burnard v. Haggis (1863)**:
- Minor hired horse for riding (contract)
- Minor jumped horse, horse injured
- Owner sued in tort (negligence)
- Court held: Liable (tort separate from contract)

**Case: Jennings v. Rundall (1799)** (Counter):
- Minor hired horse for riding
- Lent horse to friend (breach of contract)
- Owner sued in tort
- Court held: NOT liable (would be enforcing contract indirectly)

**Rule**: Minor liable for tort ONLY if tort independent of contract (not mere breach of contract)

#### 8. Estoppel Against Minor

**Estoppel**: Preventing person from denying previous representation

**General Rule**: Minor can plead minority EVEN IF minor lied about age

**Case: Mohori Bibee**: Minor falsely represented he was major; still could avoid contract

**Rationale**: Estoppel cannot create contract where none exists (contract void)

**BUT**: Minor liable in tort for **deceit/fraud** (independent tort)

#### 9. Restitution (Return of Benefit)

**General Rule**: Minor not obliged to return benefit received under void contract

**Case: Mohori Bibee**: Minor not obliged to return Rs. 8,000

**Equity Doctrine** (Some courts apply):
- If property/money still identifiable in minor's hands → Court may order return on equitable grounds
- **But**: If money spent or property consumed → Cannot be recovered

**Section 64 Indian Contract Act**: When agreement discovered to be void, person receiving advantage must restore it

**Debate**: Whether Section 64 applies to minors (generally NO; Mohori Bibee prevails)

### Ratification After Attaining Majority

**Rule**: Minor's agreement is **VOID**, not voidable

**Consequence**: **Cannot be ratified** after attaining majority

**Case: Mohori Bibee**: Expressly held that minor cannot ratify after majority

**Fresh Contract Needed**: After majority, parties can enter into **fresh contract** (not ratification of old void contract)

## Category 2: Persons of Unsound Mind (Section 12)

### Definition of Sound Mind

**Section 12**: "A person is said to be of sound mind for the purpose of making a contract, if, at the time when he makes it, he is capable of understanding it and of forming a rational judgment as to its effect upon his interests."

**Test**: Capacity to understand AND form rational judgment

**Time Relevant**: At the time of making contract (not before or after)

### Types of Mental Incapacity

#### 1. Permanent Insanity

**Total incapacity**: Person always of unsound mind
- **Effect**: All contracts VOID

**Guardian**: Guardian can contract on behalf of insane person (for necessaries)

#### 2. Temporary Insanity (Lucid Intervals)

**Intermittent insanity**: Person alternates between sanity and insanity

**Section 12 Explanation**: "A person who is usually of unsound mind, but occasionally of sound mind, may make a contract when he is of sound mind."

**Rule**: Contract during lucid interval (sound mind period) → VALID

**Burden of Proof**: Party alleging unsoundness must prove person was of unsound mind at time of contract

**Case: Imperial Loan Co. v. Stone (1892)**:
- Defendant signed guarantee during lucid interval
- Later claimed he was insane
- Court held: Valid contract (signed during lucid interval)

#### 3. Drunkenness / Intoxication

**Section 12 Illustration**: "A patient in a lunatic asylum, who is at intervals of sound mind, may contract during those intervals."

**Intoxication**: Person under influence of alcohol/drugs

**Test**: Was person capable of understanding contract?

**Rule**:
- If so intoxicated as to be incapable of understanding → Contract VOIDABLE
- If capable of understanding despite intoxication → Contract VALID

**Case: Gore v. Gibson (1845)**:
- Defendant signed contract while drunk
- Court held: Voidable if defendant proves (1) so drunk as incapable of understanding, (2) other party knew of drunkenness

**Indian Position**: Contracts by intoxicated persons voidable if incapacity proven

#### 4. Mental Deficiency (Not Total Insanity)

**Low intelligence**: Person not insane but limited mental capacity

**Test**: Could person understand nature and effect of contract?

**Example**: Illiterate person signs complex legal document without explanation
- Not necessarily void (unless fraud/undue influence)
- Court looks at whether person understood

### Effect of Unsoundness of Mind

#### If Unsound at Time of Contract:

**Contract VOID** (no legal effect)

**Exception: Necessaries** (Section 68):
- Like minors, insane person's property liable for necessaries supplied
- **Reimbursement from property**, not personal liability

**Case: In re Rhodes (1890)**:
- Lunatic supplied with necessaries (food, clothing)
- Supplier entitled to reasonable payment from lunatic's estate

#### If Sound at Time of Contract:

**Contract VALID** (no infirmity)

### Burden of Proof

**General Presumption**: Every person is of sound mind

**Burden**: Party alleging unsoundness must prove:
1. Person was of unsound mind at time of contract
2. Incapacity was such that person could not understand contract

**Case: Maneck Cowasjee Vakharia v. Bai Sakina (1910)**:
- Plaintiffs claimed deceased was of unsound mind when executing will
- Burden on plaintiffs to prove unsoundness
- Could not prove → Will upheld

## Category 3: Persons Disqualified by Law

**Section 11**: "...and is not disqualified from contracting by any law to which he is subject."

### Types of Disqualified Persons

#### 1. Alien Enemies (During War)

**Alien Enemy**: Citizen of country at war with India

**Rule**: Contracts with alien enemies during war are **VOID**

**Rationale**: Trading with enemy aids enemy war effort (against public policy)

**Exception**: Contracts made **before war** are merely **suspended** (revived after war ends)

#### 2. Foreign Sovereigns and Ambassadors

**Diplomatic Immunity**: Cannot be sued in Indian courts without consent

**Effect**: Can contract, but cannot be sued for breach (practical bar)

#### 3. Insolvents (Undischarged)

**Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code 2016**

**Undischarged Insolvent**: Person declared insolvent but not yet discharged

**Rule**: Insolvent **CAN contract**, but:
- **Pre-insolvency debts**: Vest in Official Assignee/Trustee
- **Post-insolvency contracts**: Valid, but creditors cannot seize assets (assets vest in Official Assignee)

**After Discharge**: Full capacity restored

#### 4. Convicts (During Sentence)

**Conviction**: Person sentenced to imprisonment

**Rule**: Convict **CAN contract** (no legal bar), but practical difficulties (imprisoned)

**Civil Disabilities**:
- Cannot sue during imprisonment (civil rights suspended)
- After release, rights restored

#### 5. Corporations (Beyond Memorandum Powers)

**Corporation**: Legal person created by law (companies, statutory bodies)

**Doctrine of Ultra Vires**: Corporation cannot contract beyond powers in Memorandum of Association

**Effect**: Ultra vires contracts VOID

**Companies Act 2013**: Object clause broad; ultra vires less common now

#### 6. Married Women (Historical)

**Historical Position**: Married women could not contract (Hindu Law, Muslim Law)

**Current Position**: Married women have **FULL capacity** to contract
- Hindu Women's Rights Act 1937
- General emancipation laws

**No Disability**: Married women fully competent

## Practical Implications

### Contracts with Minors

**Best Practices**:
1. **Verify Age**: Ask for proof (birth certificate, Aadhaar)
2. **Avoid Contracting**: Do not contract with minors (unenforceable)
3. **Guardian's Contract**: Contract with parent/guardian instead
4. **Joint Contract**: If minor + adult jointly sign, adult liable (but minor not)

**Risk**: Entering contract with minor = Complete loss if minor repudiates

### Contracts with Persons of Unsound Mind

**Best Practices**:
1. **Assess Capacity**: Does person understand transaction?
2. **Medical Certificate**: For large transactions, obtain medical fitness certificate
3. **Witnesses**: Have witnesses observe person's demeanor and understanding
4. **Guardian Approval**: For major transactions, involve guardian

### Necessaries (Section 68)

**What Qualifies as Necessaries?**

**Factors**:
1. **Essential for living**: Food, clothing, shelter, medical care
2. **Education**: School fees, books
3. **Suited to condition in life**: What's necessary for rich person may differ from poor
4. **Not already supplied**: If minor already has sufficient clothing, more clothes not necessaries

**Examples**:
- **Necessaries**: School tuition, food, basic clothing, medicine, rent
- **NOT Necessaries**: Luxury car, expensive jewelry, non-essential gadgets

**Practical Tip**: If supplying to minor/insane person, document that:
- Goods are necessaries
- Suited to their condition
- Not already adequately supplied

```
START
  ↓
Is person a MINOR (below 18 years)?
  ├─ YES → Contract VOID
  │         Exception: Reimbursement from property for necessaries (Section 68)
  └─ NO → Proceed

  ↓
Is person of UNSOUND MIND at time of contract?
  ├─ YES → Contract VOID
  │         Exception: Reimbursement from property for necessaries (Section 68)
  └─ NO → Proceed

  ↓
Is person DISQUALIFIED by law?
  - Alien enemy during war
  - Undischarged insolvent
  - Corporation (ultra vires)
  ├─ YES → Contract VOID or LIMITED
  └─ NO → Person COMPETENT to contract ✓
```

## Practice Examples

### Example 1: Minor's Contract

**Facts**:
- 16-year-old boy buys motorcycle for Rs. 80,000 on credit
- After 2 months, minor refuses to pay
- Seller sues for Rs. 80,000

**Analysis**:
- **Minor's agreement**: VOID (Mohori Bibee)
- **Motorcycle**: NOT necessary (luxury item for 16-year-old)
- **Result**: Seller **CANNOT** recover payment (contract void)

### Example 2: Necessaries Supplied to Minor

**Facts**:
- 17-year-old orphan minor living alone
- Landlord rents room for Rs. 5,000/month (market rate)
- Minor occupies room for 6 months, then refuses to pay
- Minor owns property worth Rs. 10 lakhs

**Analysis**:
- **Shelter**: Necessary (Section 68)
- **Suited to condition**: Yes (basic accommodation)
- **Minor's property**: Rs. 10 lakhs available
- **Result**: Landlord can recover Rs. 30,000 (6 months × Rs. 5,000) **from minor's property** (not personal liability)

### Example 3: False Representation of Age

**Facts**:
- 17-year-old lies, says he's 19
- Shopkeeper sells laptop on EMI believing he's major
- Minor defaults on EMI, reveals he's minor

**Analysis**:
- **Contract**: VOID (minor)
- **Estoppel**: Cannot operate against minor (Mohori Bibee)
- **Fraud**: Minor committed fraud, but contract still void
- **Result**: Shopkeeper **CANNOT** recover EMI payments (contract void)
- **Remedy**: Shopkeeper may sue for **tort of deceit** (separate from contract)

### Example 4: Temporary Insanity (Lucid Interval)

**Facts**:
- Person suffering from bipolar disorder
- During stable period (lucid interval), signs sale deed
- Medical records show person was stable at time
- Later, during manic episode, wants to cancel

**Analysis**:
- **Unsound mind?**: Not at time of contract (lucid interval)
- **Section 12**: Contract during lucid interval valid
- **Burden**: Person claiming insanity must prove unsoundness at time of contract
- **Result**: Contract **VALID** (signed during sound mind period)

### Example 5: Intoxication

**Facts**:
- Person heavily drunk at party
- Signs agreement to sell car for Rs. 2 lakhs (worth Rs. 8 lakhs)
- Buyer knew person was drunk
- Next day, seller wants to cancel

**Analysis**:
- **Intoxication**: So drunk as incapable of understanding (Rs. 2L for Rs. 8L car)
- **Buyer's knowledge**: Buyer knew of drunkenness
- **Effect**: Contract **VOIDABLE** (seller can repudiate)
- **Result**: Seller can cancel contract

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Assuming Minor's Contract is Voidable (Not Void)

**Mistake**: Thinking minor can ratify contract after majority

**Correct**: Minor's contract VOID ab initio; cannot be ratified (Mohori Bibee)

### 2. Suing Minor for Breach

**Mistake**: Attempting to sue minor for contractual obligations

**Correct**: Cannot sue minor on void contract (no liability exists)

### 3. Misunderstanding Necessaries Exception

**Mistake**: Thinking minor personally liable for necessaries

**Correct**: Only **minor's property** liable (Section 68); not personal liability

### 4. Estoppel Against Minor

**Mistake**: Believing minor who lied about age is estopped from pleading minority

**Correct**: Estoppel cannot validate void contract (Mohori Bibee); minor can plead minority

### 5. Confusing Tort with Contract (Minor's Liability)

**Mistake**: Suing minor in tort for what is essentially breach of contract

**Correct**: Minor liable only for torts **independent** of contract (Burnard v. Haggis); not for torts that indirectly enforce contract

### 6. Unsound Mind - Permanent vs Temporary

**Mistake**: Assuming person occasionally insane is never competent

**Correct**: Contract during **lucid interval** is valid (Section 12)
