# Contract Formation Protocol (India)

## Protocol Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-CONTRACT-FORMATION
- **Version**: 1.0.0
- **Domain**: Contract Law (India)
- **Subdomain**: Contract Formation
- **Statute**: Indian Contract Act 1872 (Sections 1-30)
- **Last Updated**: January 2025
- **Effective**: Since 1872 (no recent amendments)

---

## Overview

This protocol covers the **essential elements** for formation of a valid contract under the Indian Contract Act 1872. A contract is an agreement enforceable by law.

**Key Principle**: Not all agreements are contracts; only those agreements that meet all essential elements become legally enforceable contracts.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 2(e): Definition of Agreement

```
"Every promise and every set of promises, forming the consideration for each other, is an agreement."
```

**Formula**: Promise + Promise = Agreement

### Section 2(h): Definition of Contract

```
"An agreement enforceable by law is a contract."
```

**Formula**: Agreement + Enforceability = Contract

### Section 10: What Agreements are Contracts

```
"All agreements are contracts if they are made by the free consent of parties competent to contract, for a lawful consideration and with a lawful object, and are not hereby expressly declared to be void."
```

**Essential Elements** (from Section 10):
1. **Offer** (Proposal)
2. **Acceptance**
3. **Consideration**
4. **Capacity** (Competency to contract)
5. **Free Consent**
6. **Lawful Object**
7. **Lawful Consideration**
8. **Not Expressly Void**
9. **Certainty** (Section 29)
10. **Possibility of Performance** (Section 56)

## Element 1: Offer (Proposal)

### Section 2(a): Definition of Proposal (Offer)

```
"When one person signifies to another his willingness to do or to abstain from doing anything, with a view to obtaining the assent of that other to such act or abstinence, he is said to make a proposal."
```

**Components**:
1. **Signification of willingness**: Intention to be bound
2. **To do or abstain**: Positive or negative act
3. **To obtain assent**: Seeking acceptance
4. **Communicated to offeree**: Offer must reach offeree

### Types of Offers

#### 1. Specific Offer vs General Offer

**Specific Offer**: Made to a specific person or group
- Example: A offers to sell car to B for Rs. 5 lakhs
- Only B can accept

**General Offer**: Made to public at large
- Example: Reward for finding lost dog
- Any member of public who knows of offer can accept

**Landmark Case: Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball Co. (1893)** (English case, persuasive in India)
- Advertisement: "£100 reward to anyone who uses smoke ball as directed and still catches flu"
- Court held: General offer to world at large
- Anyone who performs conditions accepts offer
- Consideration: Using product + giving up legal right (to sue for flu)

#### 2. Express vs Implied Offer

**Express Offer**: Made by words (written or oral)
- Example: Written job offer letter

**Implied Offer**: Made by conduct
- Example: Bus conductor stands at bus stop with empty bus → Implied offer to carry passengers
- **Case: Wilkie v. London Passenger Transport Board (1947)**: Bus at stop implies offer to transport

#### 3. Cross Offers

**Cross Offer**: Two parties make identical offers to each other simultaneously without knowledge of other's offer

**Rule**: Cross offers do NOT constitute contract (no acceptance)

**Case: Tinn v. Hoffmann & Co. (1873)**:
- A wrote to B offering to sell iron
- B simultaneously wrote to A offering to buy iron (same terms)
- Letters crossed in post
- Court held: NO contract (no acceptance of either offer)

### Essential Characteristics of Valid Offer

1. **Intention to Create Legal Relations**:
   - Serious offer intended to create binding obligation
   - NOT mere invitation to treat or social invitation
   - **Case: Balfour v. Balfour (1919)**: Domestic agreements generally lack intention to create legal relations

2. **Definite and Certain Terms**:
   - Offer must be clear, not vague
   - **Section 29**: Agreement void if meaning uncertain
   - **Case: Guthing v. Lynn (1831)**: "I will give £5 more if horse is lucky" → Void (uncertain)

3. **Communicated to Offeree**:
   - Offer must reach offeree
   - Cannot accept offer without knowledge
   - **Case: Lalman Shukla v. Gauri Dutt (1913)**: Servant found lost nephew without knowing of reward offer → Cannot claim reward (no knowledge of offer)

4. **Distinguished from Invitation to Treat**:
   
   **Invitation to Treat**: Invitation to others to make offer (not itself an offer)
   
   Examples:
   - **Display of Goods in Shop**: NOT offer; invitation to customer to offer to buy
     - **Case: Pharmaceutical Society v. Boots (1953)**: Goods on self-service display are invitation to treat; customer's taking goods to counter is offer; cashier's acceptance completes contract
   - **Auction**: Auctioneer's request for bids is invitation; bidder's bid is offer; fall of hammer is acceptance
     - **Section 64 Sale of Goods Act**: Sale by auction complete when auctioneer announces completion by fall of hammer
   - **Advertisement**: Generally invitation to treat (unless reward case like Carlill)
   - **Tender Notice**: Invitation to submit bids
   - **Catalogue/Price List**: Invitation to offer

### Termination of Offer

**When does an offer cease to exist?**

#### 1. Revocation (Section 5)

**Section 5**: "A proposal may be revoked at any time before the communication of its acceptance is complete as against the proposer, but not afterwards."

**Rule**: Offer can be revoked BEFORE acceptance, not after

**Communication of Revocation** (Section 6):
- Revocation complete when it comes to knowledge of offeree

**Case: Dickinson v. Dodds (1876)**:
- D offered to sell property to P, offer open till Friday
- Before Friday, D sold to third party
- P learned of sale from friend
- P purported to accept on Friday
- Court held: Offer revoked when P learned of sale; no contract

**Exception: Irrevocable Offer** (Supported by consideration)
- If offeree pays to keep offer open (option contract) → Offer cannot be revoked during option period

#### 2. Lapse of Time (Section 6(3))

**Section 6(3)**: Proposal lapses "by the lapse of the time prescribed in such proposal for its acceptance, or if no time is so prescribed, by the lapse of a reasonable time."

**Reasonable Time**: Depends on:
- Nature of subject matter (perishable goods = short time)
- Trade custom
- Circumstances

**Case: Ramsgate Victoria Hotel v. Montefiore (1866)**:
- M applied for shares in June
- Company allotted shares in November
- Court held: Offer lapsed by reasonable time; shares allotment too late

#### 3. Rejection (Section 6(1))

**Section 6(1)**: Proposal lapses "by the communication of its rejection by the person to whom it is made."

**Rejection Methods**:
- Express rejection
- **Counter-offer**: Rejection of original offer + new offer
  - **Case: Hyde v. Wrench (1840)**: W offered to sell farm for £1000; H counter-offered £950 (rejected by W); H then tried to accept £1000 offer → Court held: Original offer terminated by counter-offer; cannot be revived

#### 4. Death or Insanity (Section 6(4), (5))

**Section 6(4)**: Offer lapses "by the death or insanity of the proposer, if the fact of his death or insanity comes to the knowledge of the acceptor before acceptance."

**Rule**: 
- If offeree knows of death/insanity before acceptance → Offer lapses
- If offeree does NOT know → Acceptance may be valid (uncertain in India; English law suggests valid)

#### 5. Non-fulfillment of Condition Precedent

If offer states a condition that must be fulfilled before acceptance, failure to fulfill condition → Offer lapses

#### 6. Operation of Law

- Change in law making offer illegal → Offer lapses
- Destruction of subject matter → Offer lapses

## Element 2: Acceptance

### Section 2(b): Definition of Acceptance

```
"When the person to whom the proposal is made signifies his assent thereto, the proposal is said to be accepted. A proposal, when accepted, becomes a promise."
```

**Formula**: Offer + Acceptance = Promise (Agreement)

### Essentials of Valid Acceptance

#### 1. Absolute and Unqualified (Mirror Image Rule)

**Section 7(1)**: "In order to convert a proposal into a promise, the acceptance must be absolute and unqualified."

**Rule**: Acceptance must match offer exactly (mirror image)
- Any variation = Counter-offer (not acceptance)
- Counter-offer rejects original offer

**Case: Neale v. Merrett (1930)**:
- Offer to sell land for £280
- Acceptance: "I accept, send me contract"
- Court held: Valid acceptance (request for contract not a condition)

**Conditional Acceptance**: 
- "I accept subject to contract" → NOT acceptance (condition)
- "I accept if you reduce price" → Counter-offer (not acceptance)

#### 2. Communicated to Offeror

**Section 4**: "The communication of an acceptance is complete - as against the proposer, when it is put in a course of transmission to him so as to be out of the power of the acceptor."

**Rule**: Acceptance must be communicated; mental acceptance insufficient

**Methods of Communication**:
- **Oral**: In person, by phone (instantaneous)
- **Written**: Letter, email, WhatsApp
- **Conduct**: Implied acceptance by performing act

**Silence is NOT Acceptance** (General Rule)

**Case: Felthouse v. Bindley (1862)**:
- Nephew negotiating to sell horse to uncle
- Uncle wrote: "If I hear no more, I consider horse mine"
- Nephew did not reply but told auctioneer not to sell horse
- Auctioneer mistakenly sold horse
- Court held: NO contract (silence not acceptance)

**Exception**: Course of dealing may imply acceptance by silence
- Long-standing business relationship where silence always treated as acceptance

#### 3. Mode of Acceptance (Section 7(2))

**Section 7(2)**: "If the proposal prescribes the manner in which it is to be accepted, and the acceptance is not made in such manner, the proposer may, within a reasonable time after the acceptance is communicated to him, insist that his proposal shall be accepted in the prescribed manner, and not otherwise; but if he fails to do so, he accepts the acceptance."

**Rule**:
- If mode prescribed (e.g., "acceptance by registered post") → Must follow mode
- If mode not followed → Offeror can insist on proper mode OR waive requirement by conduct

#### 4. By Person to Whom Offer is Made (or Authorized Agent)

**Rule**: Acceptance must be by offeree (person to whom offer made) or their authorized agent

**Exception: General Offer**: Anyone can accept (e.g., Carlill case)

#### 5. Before Revocation or Lapse

**Section 5**: Acceptance must be before offer is revoked or lapses

#### 6. Within Reasonable Time (If No Time Specified)

**Section 6(3)**: If no time specified, acceptance must be within reasonable time

#### 7. Must Show Intention to Accept

**Acceptance by Conduct**:
- Performing act requested in offer = Acceptance
- **Case: Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball**: Using smoke ball as directed = Acceptance of offer

### Communication of Acceptance

#### Rule for Different Modes

**Section 4: Communication of Acceptance**

```
"The communication of an acceptance is complete—
(1) as against the proposer, when it is put in a course of transmission to him so as to be out of the power of the acceptor;
(2) as against the acceptor, when it comes to the knowledge of the proposer."
```

**Practical Application**:

**Postal Rule** (Instantaneous vs Non-Instantaneous):

1. **Non-Instantaneous Communication** (Letter):
   - **Against Proposer** (Offeror): Complete when letter POSTED (acceptor drops in mailbox)
   - **Against Acceptor**: Complete when letter RECEIVED by proposer
   
   **Consequence**: 
   - Offeror cannot revoke after letter posted (even if not yet received)
   - Acceptor can withdraw acceptance before letter reaches offeror (telegraph to offeror)

2. **Instantaneous Communication** (Phone, Telex, Fax, Email, WhatsApp):
   - Complete when received and read by offeror
   - **Case: Entores v. Miles Far East Corporation (1955)**: Telex acceptance complete when received
   - Modern view: Email complete when received (even if not opened immediately)

**Case: Adams v. Lindsell (1818)** (Leading case on Postal Rule):
- L wrote offer to A on Sept 2
- Letter misdirected, reached A on Sept 5
- A posted acceptance on Sept 5
- Acceptance reached L on Sept 9
- Meanwhile, L sold goods to third party on Sept 8
- Court held: Contract formed on Sept 5 (when A posted acceptance); L's sale on Sept 8 was breach

### Acceptance by Performance (Unilateral Contract)

**Unilateral Contract**: Offer requests performance of act (not promise to perform)

**Acceptance**: Completing the act

**Example**: Reward offer
- Offer: "Rs. 10,000 reward for finding lost dog"
- Acceptance: Finding and returning dog (not promising to find dog)

**No Need to Communicate Intention**: Performance itself is acceptance
- **Case: Carlill**: Mrs. Carlill did not write to company saying "I accept"; using product was acceptance

## Element 3: Consideration

### Section 2(d): Definition of Consideration

```
"When, at the desire of the promisor, the promisee or any other person has done or abstained from doing, or does or abstains from doing, or promises to do or to abstain from doing, something, such act or abstinence or promise is called a consideration for the promise."
```

**Elements**:
1. **At the desire of promisor**: Must be at promisor's request (not voluntary)
2. **By promisee or any other person**: Consideration can move from third party (stranger to consideration allowed)
3. **Past, Present, or Future**: Done, doing, or promises to do

### Types of Consideration

#### 1. **Executed Consideration** (Past)
- Act done BEFORE promise made
- **Indian Law**: Past consideration IS valid (unlike English law)
- **Example**: A saves B's life; later B promises Rs. 1 lakh as reward → Valid contract in India

**Case: Lampleigh v. Braithwait (1615)** (Exception in English law, general rule in India):
- B asked L to seek pardon from king
- L did so
- B later promised to pay £100
- Court held: Valid (implied promise to pay existed from beginning)

#### 2. **Executory Consideration** (Future)
- Promise to do something in future
- Most common type
- **Example**: A promises to deliver goods next month; B promises to pay upon delivery

#### 3. **Present Consideration** (Simultaneous)
- Act and promise simultaneous
- **Example**: Cash sale in shop

### Essentials of Valid Consideration

#### 1. At the Desire of Promisor

**Rule**: Consideration must be at promisor's request, not voluntary

**Case: Durga Prasad v. Baldeo (1880)**:
- D spent money beautifying market at request of Collector
- B (market owner) promised to pay commission on sales
- Later B refused
- Court held: NO contract (D did not act at B's request; acted at Collector's request)

#### 2. May Move from Promisee or Any Other Person (Doctrine of Privity)

**Section 2(d)**: "promisee or any other person"

**Indian Law**: Consideration can move from third party (stranger to consideration)
- Different from English law (consideration must move from promisee)

**Case: Chinnaya v. Ramaya (1882)** (Madras High Court):
- Old lady gifted property to daughter
- Daughter promised to pay annuity to lady's sister
- Later daughter refused
- Court held: Valid contract (consideration moved from mother; sister can enforce even though she gave no consideration)

**BUT: Stranger to Contract Cannot Sue** (Section 2(d) allows stranger to consideration, but Section 2(c) requires privity of contract)

#### 3. Must Be Real, But Need Not Be Adequate

**Section 25 Exception 1**: "An agreement made without consideration is void, unless it is a promise to compensate for something done."

**Adequacy**: Court will not inquire into adequacy (whether fair value)
- Rs. 1 consideration for Rs. 1 crore property → Valid (inadequate but real)
- BUT: Gross inadequacy may indicate fraud/undue influence

**Case: Thomas v. Thomas (1842)**:
- Widow allowed to live in house for £1/year
- Court held: Valid consideration (need not be adequate)

**Real**: Must have some value, not illusory
- "I promise to give you gift if I feel like it" → Illusory (no real obligation)

#### 4. Must Be Lawful (Section 23)

**Section 23**: Consideration unlawful if:
- Forbidden by law
- Defeats provisions of law
- Fraudulent
- Immoral
- Opposed to public policy

**Example**: Agreement to commit crime → Void (unlawful consideration)

#### 5. Must Not Be Illusory or Vague

**Illusory Promise**: No real obligation
- "I will buy if I want to" → Illusory

**Vague**: Uncertain terms
- "I will pay reasonable price" → May be too vague (depends on context)

### Agreements Without Consideration (Section 25)

**General Rule**: Agreement without consideration is VOID

**Exceptions** (Section 25):

#### Exception 1: Love and Affection (Section 25(1))

Agreement without consideration valid if:
1. **In writing and registered**
2. **Between parties standing in near relation to each other**
3. **Made on account of natural love and affection**

**Case: Rajlukhy Dabee v. Bhootnath Mookerjee (1900)**:
- Husband promised to pay wife Rs. 500 as maintenance
- Written and registered
- Court held: Valid (love and affection between spouses)

**Case: Lakshminarayan v. Unknown** (Counter-example):
- Agreement between husband and wife to pay maintenance
- But relationship strained (frequent quarrels)
- Court held: VOID (no natural love and affection despite near relation)

#### Exception 2: Compensation for Past Voluntary Services (Section 25(2))

Agreement to compensate for something done voluntarily in past is valid if:
1. **Act was done voluntarily** (not at promisor's request)
2. **Promisor was benefited** (legally bound to compensate)

**Example**: A saves B's life (voluntarily); B later promises reward → Valid

#### Exception 3: Promise to Pay Time-Barred Debt (Section 25(3))

Promise to pay debt barred by limitation is valid if:
1. **In writing and signed** by debtor or agent
2. **Promise is to pay whole or part** of time-barred debt

**Example**: Debt barred by 3-year limitation; debtor writes promising to pay → Valid (limitation period starts fresh)

#### Exception 4: Agency (Section 185)

Agreement to indemnify agent for acts done in good faith is valid even without consideration

#### Exception 5: Gift (Section 122 Transfer of Property Act)

Gift is valid without consideration if:
- In writing and registered (immovable property)
- Delivery of possession (movable property)

## Formation of Contract: Summary Checklist

```
VALID CONTRACT CHECKLIST:

1. ✓ **Offer**: 
   - Definite, certain terms
   - Communicated to offeree
   - Intention to create legal relations
   - Distinguished from invitation to treat

2. ✓ **Acceptance**:
   - Absolute and unqualified (mirror image)
   - Communicated to offeror
   - By person to whom offer made (or agent)
   - Before revocation/lapse
   - Within reasonable time (if no time specified)

3. ✓ **Consideration**:
   - At desire of promisor
   - May move from third party
   - Real (but need not be adequate)
   - Lawful
   - Not illusory/vague
   - OR: Falls within Section 25 exceptions (love/affection, past services, time-barred debt)

4. ✓ **Capacity** (Competent to contract) - See IL-CONTRACT-CAPACITY protocol

5. ✓ **Free Consent** (No coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, mistake) - See IL-CONTRACT-FREE-CONSENT protocol

6. ✓ **Lawful Object and Consideration** - See IL-CONTRACT-LEGALITY protocol

7. ✓ **Not Expressly Void** - See IL-CONTRACT-VOID-VOIDABLE protocol

8. ✓ **Certainty** (Section 29): Terms certain, not vague

9. ✓ **Possibility of Performance** (Section 56): Performance possible

If ALL elements present → VALID CONTRACT (legally enforceable)
If any element missing → NO CONTRACT (void or voidable)
```

## Practical Flowchart: Is There a Valid Contract?

```
START
  ↓
Was there an OFFER?
  - Definite, certain, communicated, intention to create legal relations
  ├─ YES → Proceed
  └─ NO → NO CONTRACT

  ↓
Was there ACCEPTANCE?
  - Absolute, unqualified, communicated, by offeree, before revocation/lapse
  ├─ YES → Proceed
  └─ NO → NO CONTRACT

  ↓
Is there CONSIDERATION?
  - At promisor's desire, real, lawful, not illusory
  - OR: Section 25 exception (love/affection, past services, time-barred debt, gift)
  ├─ YES → Proceed
  └─ NO → VOID (Section 25)

  ↓
Parties COMPETENT to contract? (Age, sound mind, not disqualified)
  ├─ YES → Proceed
  └─ NO → VOID/VOIDABLE (See IL-CONTRACT-CAPACITY)

  ↓
FREE CONSENT? (No coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, mistake)
  ├─ YES → Proceed
  └─ NO → VOIDABLE (See IL-CONTRACT-FREE-CONSENT)

  ↓
LAWFUL Object and Consideration? (Not forbidden, fraudulent, immoral, against public policy)
  ├─ YES → Proceed
  └─ NO → VOID (Section 23)

  ↓
CERTAIN and NOT VAGUE? (Section 29)
  ├─ YES → Proceed
  └─ NO → VOID

  ↓
POSSIBLE to perform? (Section 56)
  ├─ YES → VALID CONTRACT ✓
  └─ NO → VOID
```

## Practice Examples

### Example 1: Valid Contract Formation

**Facts**:
- A owns car, posts ad: "Selling 2020 Honda City for Rs. 8 lakhs"
- B sees ad, calls A: "I accept your offer, I'll buy for Rs. 8 lakhs"
- A agrees

**Analysis**:
- **Offer**: A's ad (specific offer to sell car for Rs. 8 lakhs)
- **Acceptance**: B's call (absolute, unqualified, communicated)
- **Consideration**: B promises Rs. 8 lakhs (executory consideration)
- **Result**: **VALID CONTRACT** formed when B accepted

### Example 2: Invitation to Treat (Not Offer)

**Facts**:
- Shop displays iPhone with price tag Rs. 50,000
- Customer picks up iPhone, takes to counter
- Cashier refuses to sell, says "Price wrong, should be Rs. 70,000"

**Analysis**:
- **Display of goods**: Invitation to treat (NOT offer)
- **Customer taking to counter**: Offer to buy for Rs. 50,000
- **Cashier's refusal**: Rejection of offer
- **Result**: **NO CONTRACT** (shop not bound by display price)

### Example 3: Counter-Offer (Not Acceptance)

**Facts**:
- A offers to sell land for Rs. 50 lakhs
- B responds: "I'll buy for Rs. 45 lakhs"
- A refuses
- B then says: "OK, I accept Rs. 50 lakhs"

**Analysis**:
- **A's offer**: Rs. 50 lakhs
- **B's response**: Counter-offer of Rs. 45 lakhs (terminates original offer)
- **A's refusal**: Rejection of counter-offer
- **B's purported acceptance**: Cannot accept terminated offer
- **Result**: **NO CONTRACT**

### Example 4: Postal Rule

**Facts**:
- A posts offer letter on Jan 1
- B receives on Jan 5
- B posts acceptance on Jan 7
- A receives acceptance on Jan 10
- A posted revocation on Jan 8 (reaches B on Jan 12)

**Analysis**:
- **Offer communicated**: Jan 5 (when B receives)
- **Acceptance complete (against A)**: Jan 7 (when B posts) - Postal Rule
- **Revocation**: Jan 8 (too late; contract already formed on Jan 7)
- **Result**: **VALID CONTRACT** formed Jan 7

### Example 5: Past Consideration

**Facts**:
- A saves B's child from drowning (voluntary act)
- B, grateful, promises Rs. 1 lakh reward
- Later B refuses to pay

**Analysis**:
- **Offer**: B's promise of Rs. 1 lakh
- **Consideration**: A's past act of saving child
- **Past consideration**: Valid in India (Section 2(d) allows past consideration)
- **Result**: **VALID CONTRACT** (B must pay)

**Note**: In English law, past consideration generally invalid (A could not enforce)

### Example 6: Agreement Without Consideration (Love and Affection)

**Facts**:
- Father promises to give Rs. 10 lakhs to son
- Promise in writing and registered
- Father and son have loving relationship
- Father later refuses

**Analysis**:
- **Consideration**: None (gift promise)
- **Section 25(1) Exception**: Written, registered, near relation, natural love and affection
- **Result**: **VALID CONTRACT** (enforceable despite no consideration)

## Common Pitfalls and Mistakes

### 1. Confusing Invitation to Treat with Offer

**Mistake**: Thinking shop display or advertisement is offer

**Correct**: Display/ad generally invitation to treat; customer's picking goods/responding is offer

### 2. Assuming Silence is Acceptance

**Mistake**: "If I don't hear from you, I assume you accept"

**Correct**: Silence NOT acceptance (Felthouse case); must communicate acceptance

### 3. Counter-Offer Kills Original Offer

**Mistake**: Making counter-offer but thinking can later accept original

**Correct**: Counter-offer terminates original offer; cannot be revived

### 4. Postal Rule Confusion

**Mistake**: Thinking acceptance complete only when received

**Correct**: For posted letter, acceptance complete when posted (against offeror); but can be revoked before receipt (against acceptor)

### 5. Past Consideration Invalid?

**Mistake**: Thinking past consideration always invalid

**Correct**: In India, past consideration IS valid (unlike English law)

### 6. Adequacy of Consideration

**Mistake**: Challenging contract because consideration inadequate (token Rs. 1)

**Correct**: Consideration need not be adequate (court won't inquire into fairness unless fraud/undue influence)
