# Contract Impossibility and Frustration Protocol (India)

## Protocol Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-CONTRACT-IMPOSSIBILITY
- **Version**: 1.0.0
- **Domain**: Contract Law (India)
- **Subdomain**: Contract Performance - Impossibility
- **Statute**: Indian Contract Act 1872 (Section 56, 32-36)
- **Last Updated**: January 2025
- **Effective**: Since 1872

---

## Overview

This protocol covers **impossibility of performance** and **frustration of contract** - circumstances under which parties are excused from performing contractual obligations due to unforeseen events making performance impossible or purposeless.

**Key Principle**: Doctrine of frustration = "If the foundation of a contract is destroyed by an unforeseen event, the contract is discharged."

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 56: Doctrine of Impossibility / Frustration

```
"An agreement to do an act impossible in itself is void.

A contract to do an act which, after the contract is made, becomes impossible, or, by reason of some event which the promisor could not prevent, unlawful, becomes void when the act becomes impossible or unlawful."
```

**Two Parts**:
1. **Initial Impossibility** (at time of formation) → Agreement VOID ab initio
2. **Supervening Impossibility** (after formation) → Contract becomes VOID

---

## Initial Impossibility

**Rule**: Agreement to do impossible act → VOID from beginning

### Types of Initial Impossibility

#### 1. Physical Impossibility

**Impossible by laws of nature**

**Example**: Agreement to make sun rise from west → VOID (physically impossible)

#### 2. Legal Impossibility

**Forbidden by law**

**Example**: Agreement to import goods that are banned → VOID (legally impossible)

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-LEGALITY protocol (unlawful object)

#### 3. Non-Existence of Subject Matter (Unknown to Parties)

**Subject matter doesn't exist at time of contract** (but parties don't know)

**Case: Couturier v. Hastie (1856)** (English case):
- Contract to sell cargo of corn
- Unknown to parties, cargo had perished during voyage (before contract)
- Court held: Contract VOID (subject matter non-existent)

**Section 20 Illustration (Indian Contract Act)**:
- A agrees to buy B's horse
- Unknown to both, horse had died before agreement
- Agreement VOID

---

## Supervening Impossibility / Frustration

**Rule**: If performance becomes impossible after contract formed (due to event beyond parties' control) → Contract becomes VOID

**Doctrine**: **Frustration of Contract** (Taylor v. Caldwell doctrine)

### Landmark Case: Taylor v. Caldwell (1863)

**Facts**:
- Contract to rent music hall for concerts on specific dates
- Before first concert, hall burned down (not fault of either party)
- Plaintiff sued for breach

**Court Held**:
- Contract **implied condition**: Hall's continued existence necessary for performance
- Hall destroyed → Implied condition failed
- Contract **frustrated** (discharged); both parties excused

**Principle**: If foundational assumption destroyed → Contract frustrated

**Indian Law**: Section 56 incorporates Taylor v. Caldwell principle

---

## Grounds for Supervening Impossibility / Frustration

### 1. Destruction / Loss of Subject Matter

**Specific goods perish**

**Example**: Contract to sell specific car; car destroyed before delivery → VOID (Section 56)

**Sale of Goods Act 1930, Section 7**:
"Where there is a contract for the sale of specific goods, the contract is void if the goods, without the knowledge of the seller, have perished at the time when the contract is made."

**Section 8**: If goods perish before risk passes to buyer → Contract VOID

**Case**: Contract to sell specific painting; painting destroyed in fire → VOID

### 2. Death / Permanent Incapacity (Personal Service Contracts)

**Personal performance essential**; person dies or becomes incapable

**Example**: Contract for singer to perform; singer dies → VOID

**Case: Robinson v. Davison (1871)**:
- Contract for pianist to perform at concert
- Pianist fell seriously ill (unable to perform)
- Court held: Contract frustrated (personal incapacity)

**Non-Personal Service**: Death does NOT frustrate
- Example: Contract to supply goods; supplier (company) → Company's obligations pass to successors

### 3. Supervening Illegality / Change of Law

**Performance becomes illegal** after contract formed

**Example**: Contract to export goods; government bans export → VOID

**Example**: Contract during peace; war breaks out (trade with enemy becomes illegal) → VOID

**Case: Baily v. De Crespigny (1869)**:
- Contract to lease land; landlord agreed not to build on adjoining land
- Railway Company acquired land by compulsory statute (landlord had no choice)
- Court held: Contract frustrated (performance became illegal/impossible by law)

### 4. Non-Occurrence / Cancellation of Contemplated Event (Frustration of Purpose)

**Event that was foundation of contract doesn't occur**

**Coronation Cases** (English law, persuasive in India):

**Case: Krell v. Henry (1903)**:
- Contract to rent room for 2 days to view King Edward VII's coronation procession
- Coronation cancelled (King's illness)
- Court held: Contract frustrated (sole purpose was viewing procession; purpose frustrated)

**Contrast: Herne Bay Steamboat Co. v. Hutton (1903)**:
- Contract to hire steamboat to view naval review (King's fleet)
- Naval review cancelled
- Court held: Contract NOT frustrated (viewing fleet not sole purpose; cruise still possible)

**Distinction**: Frustration requires event to be **foundation** of contract (not merely motive)

**Indian Application**:

**Case: Satyabrata Ghose v. Mugneeram Bangur & Co. (1954)** (Supreme Court):
- Contract for sale of land (building plots)
- Government requisitioned land (wartime acquisition; seller lost possession)
- Seller could not transfer land
- Supreme Court held: Contract frustrated (performance impossible due to requisition)

### 5. Excessive Delay / Inordinate Delay

**Performance delayed so long** that it defeats object of contract

**Test**: Would delayed performance be radically different from what was contracted?

**Example**: Contract to ship perishable goods; ship detained for months → Frustrated

**Case: Jackson v. Union Marine Insurance Co. (1874)**:
- Charter party (ship hire) for voyage to load cargo
- Ship damaged; repairs took 8 months
- Court held: Contract frustrated (delay so long that commercial purpose defeated)

**Indian Position**: Delay must be **substantial** and **defeat purpose**

### 6. Government Intervention / Expropriation

**Government action** makes performance impossible

**Examples**:
- **Requisition** of property (Satyabrata Ghose case)
- **Compulsory acquisition** of land
- **Wartime restrictions**

## Limitations on Doctrine of Frustration

**Frustration is NARROW doctrine** - Not available in all hardship cases

### 1. Self-Induced Frustration

**Rule**: If party's own act causes impossibility → NOT frustration

**Example**: Seller contracts to sell specific goods; seller sells goods to someone else → NOT frustration (self-induced)

**Case: Maritime National Fish Ltd. v. Ocean Trawlers Ltd. (1935)**:
- Contract to charter trawler (fishing license required)
- Charterer applied for 3 licenses; got only 1
- Charterer allocated license to own trawler (not chartered one)
- Court held: NOT frustration (charterer's choice caused impossibility)

### 2. Foreseen / Foreseeable Events

**Rule**: If event was foreseeable and should have been provided for → NOT frustration

**Example**: Contract in monsoon season; floods occur (foreseeable) → NOT frustration

**Test**: Would reasonable person have contemplated event and provided for it?

### 3. Mere Hardship / Increased Difficulty / Expense

**Rule**: Frustration ≠ Hardship

**Mere increase in cost or difficulty** does NOT frustrate contract

**Example**: Contract to supply goods at fixed price; cost of raw material increases 50% → NOT frustration (still possible, just less profitable)

**Case: Davis Contractors Ltd. v. Fareham UDC (1956)**:
- Construction contract for houses
- Labor shortage; costs increased; took longer
- Contractor claimed frustration
- Court held: NOT frustration (performance still possible; mere hardship/expense doesn't excuse)

**Indian Position**: Same (Section 56 requires impossibility, not hardship)

**Case: Alopi Parshad v. Union of India (1960)** (Supreme Court):
- Contract to supply food grains
- Government controlled prices; performance became unprofitable
- Court held: NOT frustration (still possible; economic hardship doesn't excuse)

### 4. Partial Impossibility

**Rule**: If part of contract impossible, rest still performs able → NOT frustrated (unless part essential)

**Example**: Contract to supply 1000 units; supplier can supply 800 → NOT frustrated (partial performance possible)

**But**: If essential part impossible → Frustrated

### 5. Alternative Modes of Performance Available

**Rule**: If alternative method exists → NOT frustrated

**Example**: Contract to transport goods by specific ship; ship unavailable but other ships available → NOT frustrated

## Contractual Provisions: Force Majeure Clauses

**Force Majeure**: "Superior force" (unforeseeable event beyond parties' control)

**Force Majeure Clause**: Contractual provision excusing performance if specified events occur

**Example Clause**:
"Neither party shall be liable for failure to perform due to acts of God, war, strikes, floods, government action, or other events beyond reasonable control."

**Effect**:
- **If clause present**: Clause governs (not Section 56)
- **If clause absent**: Section 56 applies

**Key Differences**:

| Aspect | Section 56 (Frustration) | Force Majeure Clause |
|--------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| **Scope** | Narrow (impossibility/unlawfulness) | Can be broader (include hardship) |
| **Effect** | Contract VOID (discharged) | Performance suspended (or excused) |
| **Contract Continues?** | NO (void) | YES (unless clause says terminate) |
| **Burden of Proof** | Party claiming must prove impossibility | Party claiming must prove event within clause |

**Case: Energy Watchdog v. CERC (2017)** (Supreme Court):
- Force majeure clause in Power Purchase Agreement
- Change of law affected economics
- Court held: Must prove event within force majeure clause; economic hardship alone not enough

**COVID-19 Pandemic**:

**Government Notification**: Ministry of Finance (Feb 2020) declared COVID-19 as force majeure event for government contracts

**Private Contracts**: Depends on:
1. **Force majeure clause**: If clause covers pandemic/epidemic → Excused
2. **Section 56**: If performance impossible (not mere hardship) → Frustrated

**Case: Halliburton Offshore Services v. Vedanta Ltd. (2020)** (Bombay High Court):
- COVID-19 lockdown; oil rig services
- Court held: Analyze whether performance impossible or mere difficulty

## Effect of Frustration / Supervening Impossibility

**Section 56**: "A contract to do an act which, after the contract is made, becomes impossible... becomes void..."

**Contract becomes VOID** (not voidable)

### Consequences

#### 1. Parties Discharged from Future Obligations

**No further performance required** by either party

**Example**: Contract frustrated on June 1; neither party obliged to perform after June 1

#### 2. Loss Lies Where It Falls

**General Rule**: No compensation for loss due to frustration

**Reason**: Neither party at fault (supervening event beyond control)

**Example**: Buyer paid advance; goods perish before delivery (frustration)
- **General rule**: Buyer loses advance (loss lies where it falls)
- **Exception**: Sections 64-65 (restitution if benefit received)

#### 3. Restitution (Sections 64-65)

**Section 65**: "When an agreement is discovered to be void, or when a contract becomes void, any person who has received any advantage under such agreement or contract is bound to restore it, or to make compensation for it to the person from whom he received it."

**Mutual Restoration**:
- Money paid → Must be returned
- Goods delivered → Must be returned (or compensate)

**Limit**: Restore benefit actually received (not expected profit)

**Case: Fibrosa Spolka Akcyjna v. Fairbairn (1943)** (English case):
- Buyer paid advance for machinery
- Contract frustrated (war)
- Court held: Buyer can recover advance

**Indian Position**: Section 65 applies (parties restore benefits)

**But**: If benefit consumed/destroyed → May not be recoverable

## Contingent Contracts (Sections 32-36)

**Contingent Contract**: Performance depends on happening or non-happening of uncertain future event

**Section 32**: "If two or more parties enter into a contract, the performance of which is dependent on the happening of an event which may or may not happen, such contract is a contingent contract."

**Examples**:
- Insurance contract (pay if loss occurs)
- "I'll sell my house if I get transfer to another city"

### When Contingent Contract Becomes VOID

#### Section 33: Event Becomes Impossible

```
"Contingent contracts to do or not to do anything if an uncertain future event happens, cannot be enforced by law, unless and until that event has happened. If the event becomes impossible, such contracts become void."
```

**Example**: "I'll pay you Rs. 1 lakh if A comes back from dead" → VOID (impossible event)

#### Section 34: Event Must Happen Within Fixed Time

```
"If the future event on which a contract is contingent is the way in which a person will act at an unspecified time, the event shall be considered to become impossible when such person does anything which renders it impossible that he should so act within any definite time, or otherwise than under further contingencies."
```

**Example**: "I'll pay if ship arrives by Dec 31"; ship sinks before Dec 31 → Contract VOID

#### Section 35: Contingent on Event NOT Happening

```
"Contingent contracts to do or not to do anything if an uncertain future event does not happen, can be enforced when the happening of that event becomes impossible, and not before."
```

**Example**: "I'll pay if ship does NOT arrive" → Enforceable when ship's arrival becomes impossible

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Destruction of Subject Matter

**Facts**: A contracts to sell specific vintage car to B for Rs. 50 lakhs; car destroyed in fire before delivery

**Analysis**:
- **Supervening impossibility**: Subject matter destroyed (Section 56)
- **Effect**: Contract VOID; both parties discharged
- **Restitution**: If B paid advance, A must return

### Example 2: Personal Incapacity

**Facts**: Famous singer contracts to perform at wedding; singer has accident (permanent injury; cannot sing)

**Analysis**:
- **Personal service**: Yes (famous singer)
- **Permanent incapacity**: Cannot perform
- **Effect**: Contract VOID (Section 56)

### Example 3: Frustration of Purpose

**Facts**: A rents hall from B for wedding on Dec 25; wedding cancelled (bride elopes with someone else)

**Analysis**:
- **Cancellation of wedding**: Frustration of purpose?
- **Test**: Was wedding viewing foundation of contract (like Krell v. Henry)?
- **Likely**: NOT frustrated (hall rental not dependent on wedding; A could use for other purpose)

### Example 4: Mere Hardship (NOT Frustration)

**Facts**:
- A contracts to supply steel at Rs. 50/kg (1000 kg = Rs. 50,000)
- Steel prices rise to Rs. 80/kg
- A claims frustration (uneconomical to perform)

**Analysis**:
- **Hardship**: Yes (A will lose money)
- **Impossibility**: NO (A can still supply steel)
- **Section 56**: Does not apply (mere economic hardship)
- **Effect**: Contract NOT frustrated; A must perform (or pay damages for breach)

### Example 5: Force Majeure Clause

**Facts**:
- Construction contract with force majeure clause covering "acts of God, floods, earthquakes"
- Earthquake damages site; 3-month delay
- Force majeure clause suspends performance during event

**Analysis**:
- **Earthquake**: Within force majeure clause
- **Effect**: Performance suspended (not contract void)
- **After earthquake ends**: Parties resume performance

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Confusing Hardship with Impossibility

**Mistake**: Claiming frustration because performance became difficult/expensive

**Correct**: Frustration requires impossibility or unlawfulness (not mere hardship)

### 2. Self-Induced Impossibility

**Mistake**: Claiming frustration when party's own act caused impossibility

**Correct**: Self-induced events do NOT frustrate

### 3. Foreseeable Events

**Mistake**: Claiming frustration for event that should have been anticipated

**Correct**: Foreseeable risks should be allocated in contract (frustration unavailable)

### 4. Confusing Section 56 with Force Majeure

**Mistake**: Thinking Section 56 same as force majeure clause

**Correct**:
- **Section 56**: Statutory doctrine (narrow); contract VOID
- **Force majeure**: Contractual clause (can be broader); performance suspended

### 5. Expecting Compensation After Frustration

**Mistake**: Thinking frustrated party gets damages

**Correct**: No damages (neither party at fault); loss lies where it falls (except restitution under Section 65)
