# Contract Performance Protocol (India)

## Protocol Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-CONTRACT-PERFORMANCE
- **Version**: 1.0.0
- **Domain**: Contract Law (India)
- **Subdomain**: Contract Performance
- **Statute**: Indian Contract Act 1872 (Sections 37-50)
- **Last Updated**: January 2025
- **Effective**: Since 1872

---

## Overview

This protocol covers **performance of contracts** - who must perform, when, where, and how contracts must be performed under Indian law.

**Key Principle**: Parties must perform their contractual obligations exactly as agreed, unless excused by law.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 37: Obligation of Parties

```
"The parties to a contract must either perform, or offer to perform, their respective promises, unless such performance is dispensed with or excused under the provisions of this Act, or of any other law."
```

**General Rule**: **Promises must be performed**

**Exceptions** (Performance dispensed with or excused):
- Mutual agreement to rescind
- Impossibility (Section 56)
- Breach by other party
- Waiver
- Novation, remission, or alteration

---

## Who Must Perform? (Sections 40-41)

### Section 40: Person by Whom Promise is to be Performed

```
"If it appears from the nature of the case that it was the intention of the parties to any contract that any promise contained in it should be performed by the promisor himself, such promise must be performed by the promisor. In other cases, the promisor or his representatives may employ a competent person to perform it."
```

**General Rule**: Promisor OR promisor's agent can perform

**Exception**: If contract requires **personal performance** → Only promisor must perform

#### Personal Performance Required

**When is personal performance essential?**

1. **Contracts involving personal skill/taste**:
   - Portrait painting by famous artist
   - Performance by specific musician
   - Legal services by specific lawyer

**Example**: Contract for Lata Mangeshkar to sing at wedding → Only Lata must perform (cannot send substitute)

2. **Contracts based on personal confidence**:
   - Doctor-patient (patient chose specific doctor)
   - Lawyer-client relationship

**Case: British Waggon Co. v. Lea & Co. (1880)**:
- Contract to hire wagons from specific company (reputable)
- Company assigned contract to another company (not reputable)
- Court held: Personal performance required (contract based on confidence in specific company)

#### Performance by Agent/Representative Allowed

**When can agent perform?**

**General contracts** where personality doesn't matter:
- Sale of goods
- Delivery of commodities
- Payment of money
- Construction work (not requiring specific personal skill)

**Example**: Contract to deliver 100 tons of wheat → Seller can use agent to deliver

### Section 41: Effect of Refusal to Accept Offer of Performance

```
"When a promisor has made an offer of performance to the promisee, and the offer has not been accepted, the promisor is not responsible for non-performance, nor does he thereby lose his rights under the contract."
```

**Tender of Performance**: Offer to perform (even if not accepted) = Discharge of obligation

**Effect**:
1. Promisor not liable for non-performance (if promisee refuses tender)
2. Promisor retains rights under contract (can sue for breach by promisee)

**Illustration (Section 41)**:
- A contracts to deliver goods to B on Jan 1
- A tenders goods on Jan 1
- B refuses to accept
- A not liable for non-performance; A can sue B for breach

**Valid Tender Requirements**:
1. **Unconditional**: No strings attached
2. **At proper time and place**: As per contract
3. **Of proper amount/quantity**: Exact as agreed
4. **To proper person**: Promisee or authorized agent

**Tender of Money**:
- Must be in legal tender (currency notes, coins)
- Exact amount (creditor not bound to give change)

**Case: Dixon v. Clark (1848)**:
- Debtor tendered payment in bank notes (legal tender at that time)
- Creditor refused
- Court held: Valid tender; debtor discharged

## When Must Contract be Performed? (Sections 46-48)

### Section 46: Time for Performance (When No Time Specified)

```
"When a promise is to be performed on a certain day, and the promisor has not performed it at the appointed time, the promisor may perform it at any time during the usual hours of business on such day, unless it appears from the nature of the contract that performance at any particular time was intended."
```

**Rules**:

1. **If specific time mentioned**: Perform at that time
   - Example: "Deliver goods at 10 AM on Jan 5" → Must deliver at 10 AM

2. **If specific day (no time) mentioned**: Perform during usual business hours
   - Example: "Deliver goods on Jan 5" → Can deliver anytime between 9 AM - 6 PM (usual hours)

3. **If no time specified**: Within reasonable time
   - **Section 46**: "If no time is specified, the promisor must perform the promise within a reasonable time."
   - **Reasonable time**: Depends on nature of contract, trade custom, circumstances

**Case: Pantankar v. State of Maharashtra (1975)**:
- Contract to supply goods; no time specified
- Court held: Must perform within reasonable time (determined by nature of goods - perishable vs non-perishable)

### Section 47: Time is of the Essence

```
"When a contract specifies that time is of the essence, failure to perform on time is a breach of condition, and the aggrieved party can terminate the contract."
```

**Time of the Essence**: Punctuality is critical; delay = breach

**When is time of the essence?**

1. **Expressly stated**: "Time is of the essence"
2. **Implied from nature of contract**:
   - Perishable goods (flowers for wedding, food)
   - Market fluctuation goods (shares, commodities)
   - Seasonal goods (Christmas decorations in December)
   - Event-specific (goods for specific event - wedding, conference)

**Effect if Time of Essence**:
- Delay in performance = **Breach of condition**
- Aggrieved party can **terminate contract**

**Effect if Time NOT of Essence**:
- Delay in performance = **Breach of warranty** (minor breach)
- Aggrieved party cannot terminate; can only claim damages

**Case: Henthorn v. Fraser (1892)**:
- Sale of land; time NOT of essence (land not perishable)
- Buyer delayed payment
- Court held: Seller cannot terminate; can only sue for damages

### Section 48: Place of Performance

```
"Where a contract requires performance at a specified place, it must be performed at that place. If no place is specified, the promisor must apply to the promisee to appoint a place."
```

**Rules**:

1. **If place specified**: Perform at that place
   - Example: "Deliver goods to warehouse at [address]" → Must deliver there

2. **If no place specified**: Promisee must designate place
   - Promisor requests promisee to specify
   - If promisee doesn't specify → Promisor's own place (promisor's residence/business)

## How Must Contract be Performed? (Sections 42-45, 49-50)

### Section 42: Devolution of Joint Liabilities

```
"When two or more persons have made a joint promise, then, unless a contrary intention appears from the contract, all such persons, during their joint lives, must fulfill the promise jointly."
```

**Joint Promise**: Multiple promisors

**Rule**: All must perform jointly (during their lives)

**Death of Joint Promisor**:
- **Section 42**: Deceased's estate liable (legal representatives must perform)
- Other surviving promisors continue to be liable

**Illustration**:
- A, B, C jointly promise to pay Rs. 30,000 to X
- A dies
- B, C must pay (A's estate also liable for 1/3 share)

**Joint and Several Liability**:
- If contract states "joint and several" → Each promisor liable for entire performance
- Promisee can sue any one or all promisors

### Section 43: Joint Promisees - Any One Can Demand Performance

```
"When a promise is made to two or more persons jointly, then, unless a contrary intention appears from the contract, the right to claim performance rests, as between the promisees or their representatives, with them all jointly during their joint lives, and, after the death of any one of them, with the representative of such deceased person jointly with the surviving promisees."
```

**Joint Promisees**: Multiple persons to whom promise made

**Rule**: All must demand jointly (during joint lives)

**Death of Joint Promisee**: Right passes to legal representatives + surviving promisees (jointly)

### Section 49: Manner of Performance When Party Undertakes More Than He Promised

```
"When a party to a contract performs, or offers to perform, his part of it in a manner not sanctioned by the contract, the other party may accept such performance, or he may insist upon the performance of the promise in the manner prescribed by the contract."
```

**Excess Performance**: Performing more than agreed

**Promisee's Options**:
1. **Accept excess performance** (waive contract terms)
2. **Reject excess** (insist on exact performance)

**Example**: Contract to deliver 100 kg wheat; promisor delivers 150 kg
- Promisee can accept 150 kg OR reject excess and demand exactly 100 kg

### Section 50: Performance in Instalments

```
"Where a party to a contract promises to perform his obligations in instalments, the promisee may refuse to accept partial performance unless the contract specifically allows it."
```

**General Rule**: **Entire performance required** (not partial)

**Exception**: If contract allows instalments → Partial performance accepted

**Example**:
- Contract to sell 1000 units
- If contract silent → Seller must deliver all 1000 units at once
- If contract states "delivery in 10 instalments of 100 units" → Buyer must accept instalments

**Illustration**: A contracts to sell and deliver 100 bales of goods to B
- A tenders 50 bales
- B can refuse (entitled to entire 100 bales unless contract allows partial delivery)

## Reciprocal Promises (Sections 51-58)

### Definition (Section 2(f))

```
"Promises which form the consideration or part of the consideration for each other are called reciprocal promises."
```

**Reciprocal Promises**: Mutual promises forming consideration for each other

**Example**: Sale contract
- Seller promises to deliver goods
- Buyer promises to pay price
- Both promises are reciprocal (consideration for each other)

### Types of Reciprocal Promises

#### 1. Mutual and Independent Promises (Section 52)

**Section 52**: Promises independent of each other; failure of one does not excuse the other

**Example**: A promises to sell land to B on Jan 1; B promises to pay on Jan 15
- Promises independent
- A must transfer land even if B hasn't paid yet (B's payment date is later)

#### 2. Mutual and Dependent / Conditional Promises (Section 51)

**Section 51**: Performance of one promise depends on prior performance of other

**Order of Performance**:
1. **Promisor must perform first** (then promisee performs)
2. **Simultaneous performance** (both at same time)

**Example**: Cash sale
- Seller delivers goods; buyer pays
- **Simultaneous** (Section 51(1))

**Example**: Land sale with deed execution
- Seller must execute deed first; then buyer pays
- **Conditional** (Section 51(2))

#### 3. Mutual Promises - One Impossible (Section 56)

If one reciprocal promise is impossible → Entire contract void

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-IMPOSSIBILITY protocol

### Section 53: Liability of Party Preventing Performance

```
"When a contract contains reciprocal promises, and one party to the contract prevents the other from performing his promise, the contract becomes voidable at the option of the party so prevented; and he is entitled to compensation from the other party for any loss which he may sustain in consequence of the non-performance of the contract."
```

**Prevention of Performance**: One party hinders other's performance

**Effect**:
1. Contract **voidable** at option of prevented party
2. **Damages** for loss due to non-performance

**Example**: Employer wrongfully dismisses employee before end of contract period
- Employee prevented from performing
- Employee can claim wages for remaining period + damages

**Case: Planche v. Colburn (1831)**:
- Publisher contracted with author to write book
- Publisher abandoned publication (prevented author from completing)
- Court held: Author can recover for work done + damages

## Appropriation of Payments (Sections 59-61)

### Section 59: Application of Payment Where Debt to be Discharged is Indicated

```
"Where a debtor owing several distinct debts to one person, makes a payment to him either with express intimation or under circumstances implying that the payment is to be applied to the discharge of some particular debt, the payment, if accepted, must be applied accordingly."
```

**Debtor's Right**: If debtor specifies which debt to pay → Must be applied accordingly

**Example**: A owes B three debts: Rs. 1,000 (due Jan 1), Rs. 2,000 (due Feb 1), Rs. 3,000 (due Mar 1)
- A pays Rs. 1,000 stating "for Jan 1 debt"
- B must apply payment to Jan 1 debt (cannot apply to other debts)

### Section 60: Application of Payment Where Debt Not Indicated (Creditor's Right)

```
"Where the debtor has omitted to intimate, and there are no circumstances implying to which debt the payment is to be applied, the creditor may apply it at his discretion to any lawful debt actually due and payable to him from the debtor."
```

**Creditor's Right**: If debtor doesn't specify → Creditor can apply to any debt

**Limitations**:
- **Lawful debt** (not time-barred or illegal)
- **Actually due and payable** (not future debt)

### Section 61: Application of Payment Where Neither Party Appropriates (Clayton's Rule)

```
"Where neither party makes any appropriation, the payment shall be applied in discharge of the debts in order of time, whether they are or are not barred by the law of limitation."
```

**Clayton's Rule**: Payment applied to oldest debt first (FIFO - First In, First Out)

**Example**: A owes B three debts: Rs. 1,000 (incurred 2020), Rs. 2,000 (2021), Rs. 3,000 (2022)
- A pays Rs. 1,500 without specifying
- B doesn't specify
- Applied: Rs. 1,000 to 2020 debt (fully paid); Rs. 500 to 2021 debt

**Time-Barred Debts**: Clayton's Rule applies even if debt time-barred

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Personal Performance

**Facts**: A hires famous chef B to cook for wedding; B sends assistant

**Analysis**:
- **Personal skill**: Yes (famous chef)
- **Section 40**: Personal performance required
- **Effect**: A can reject; B in breach

### Example 2: Tender of Performance

**Facts**: Seller tenders delivery of goods on due date; buyer refuses to accept

**Analysis**:
- **Section 41**: Valid tender
- **Effect**: Seller not liable for non-performance; seller can sue buyer for breach

### Example 3: Time of Essence

**Facts**: Contract to deliver flowers for wedding on Dec 25; seller delivers Dec 27

**Analysis**:
- **Time of essence**: Yes (event-specific - flowers for wedding)
- **Delay**: Breach of condition
- **Effect**: Buyer can terminate contract + damages

### Example 4: Joint Promise

**Facts**: A, B, C jointly promise to pay Rs. 30,000 to X; A defaults

**Analysis**:
- **Section 42**: Joint liability
- **Effect**: X can sue A, B, C jointly; or sue any one/all

### Example 5: Reciprocal Promises

**Facts**: A promises to deliver goods to B; B promises to pay on delivery; A doesn't deliver

**Analysis**:
- **Reciprocal promises**: Conditional (Section 51)
- **A's non-performance**: B not obliged to pay (condition precedent not met)
- **Effect**: B can sue A for breach
