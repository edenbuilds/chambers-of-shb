# Sale of Goods Protocol (India)

## Protocol Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-CONTRACT-SALE-OF-GOODS
- **Version**: 1.0.0
- **Domain**: Contract Law (India)
- **Subdomain**: Special Contracts - Sale of Goods
- **Statute**: Sale of Goods Act 1930 (Sections 1-66)
- **Last Updated**: January 2025
- **Effective**: Since 1930

---

## Overview

This protocol covers **sale of goods** contracts under the Sale of Goods Act 1930 - a specialized category of contracts involving transfer of ownership of movable property for a price.

**Key Principle**: Sale of goods is a special contract governed by both the Sale of Goods Act 1930 (specific provisions) and the Indian Contract Act 1872 (general contract law principles).

---

## Nature and Definition

### Section 4: Contract of Sale

```
"(1) A contract of sale of goods is a contract whereby the seller transfers or agrees to transfer the property in goods to the buyer for a price.

(2) There may be a contract of sale between one part-owner and another.

(3) A contract of sale may be absolute or conditional."
```

**Essential Elements**:
1. **At least two parties**: Buyer and seller
2. **Goods**: Movable property
3. **Transfer of ownership**: Property (ownership) must pass
4. **For a price**: Monetary consideration (not barter)
5. **Contract**: Agreement enforceable by law

---

## Contract of Sale vs Sale (Section 4)

**Two Types**:

### 1. Sale (Executed Contract)

**Property passes immediately** (at time of contract)

**Effect**: 
- Ownership transferred to buyer
- Risk passes to buyer
- Seller becomes creditor for price (not owner)

**Example**: A buys book from shop; pays cash; takes book → Sale

### 2. Agreement to Sell (Executory Contract)

**Property to pass in future** (on fulfillment of condition or at future date)

**Effect**:
- Ownership remains with seller
- Risk remains with seller
- Seller retains proprietary rights

**Example**: A contracts to buy car; delivery next month → Agreement to sell

**Section 4(4)**: "An agreement to sell becomes a sale when the time elapses or the conditions are fulfilled subject to which the property in the goods is to be transferred."

---

## Goods (Section 2(7))

**Definition**: "Every kind of movable property other than actionable claims and money"

### Classification of Goods

#### 1. Existing Goods (Section 6)

**Owned or possessed** by seller at time of contract

**Types**:

**a) Specific Goods (Section 2(14))**:
- Identified and agreed upon at contract
- **Example**: "This Honda City car with registration DL-1234"

**b) Ascertained Goods**:
- Initially unascertained; later identified
- **Example**: "100 bags of wheat" (from seller's godown) → Specific 100 bags separated

**c) Unascertained Goods**:
- Not identified at contract
- **Example**: "100 quintals of wheat" (not separated from bulk)

#### 2. Future Goods (Section 2(6))

**To be manufactured or acquired** by seller after contract

**Example**: Contract to sell car to be manufactured next month

**Section 6(2)**: Agreement to sell future goods → "Agreement to sell" (becomes sale when goods acquired/manufactured)

#### 3. Contingent Goods

**Subject to contingency** (may or may not come into existence)

**Example**: Next season's crop (may fail)

---

## Price (Section 2(10))

**Definition**: "Money consideration for sale of goods"

### Determination of Price (Section 9)

**Three Methods**:

#### 1. Fixed by Contract
- Parties agree on specific price
- **Example**: "Rs. 10 lakhs"

#### 2. Agreed Manner of Ascertainment
- Price determined by formula/method agreed
- **Example**: "Market price on delivery date"

#### 3. Determined by Course of Dealing
- Previous transactions between parties
- **Example**: Regular supplier; price as usual

**Section 9(2)**: If price not determined → Buyer pays **reasonable price** (considering circumstances)

**Section 10**: If price to be fixed by third party valuer:
- Valuer fails to fix → Contract VOID
- BUT if goods delivered and buyer uses → Must pay reasonable price

## Conditions and Warranties (Sections 11-17)

**Distinction Critical**: Determines remedy for breach

### Condition (Section 12(2))

**Stipulation essential** to main purpose

**Breach of Condition** → Buyer can:
1. **Reject goods** (treat contract as repudiated), OR
2. **Accept goods** and claim damages (treat as breach of warranty)

**Examples**:
- Sale "by sample" → Goods must correspond to sample (condition)
- Sale "by description" → Goods must match description (condition)

### Warranty (Section 12(3))

**Stipulation collateral** to main purpose

**Breach of Warranty** → Buyer can:
- Only claim **damages** (cannot reject goods)

**Example**: Seller warrants car serviced last month; not serviced → Warranty breach (minor; damages only)

### Section 13: When Condition Becomes Warranty

**Four Situations**:

1. **Acceptance**: Buyer accepts goods (Section 42) → Cannot reject later (condition treated as warranty)
2. **Breach of condition**: Buyer elects to treat as warranty
3. **Fulfillment prevented**: If condition fulfillment prevented by buyer → Treated as warranty
4. **Statute provides**: When Act specifically converts condition to warranty

## Implied Conditions and Warranties

**"Implied"**: Not expressly stated; automatically included by law

### Section 14: Implied Conditions

#### (a) Title (Section 14(a))

**Implied Condition**: Seller has right to sell (owns goods or will own at time of sale)

**Breach**: Buyer can reject + recover full price + damages

**Case: Rowland v. Divall (1923)**:
- Buyer bought car from seller
- Car stolen (seller had no title)
- Police seized car (true owner recovered)
- Court held: Total failure of consideration; buyer recovers full price (even after using car for months)

#### (b) Quiet Possession (Section 14(b))

**Implied Warranty**: Buyer shall have and enjoy quiet possession (no disturbance)

**Breach**: If third party lawfully disturbs → Damages

#### (c) Free from Encumbrances (Section 14(c))

**Implied Warranty**: Goods free from undisclosed charges/encumbrances

**Example**: Car sold with undisclosed lien → Breach of warranty

### Section 15: Sale by Description

**Implied Condition**: Goods must correspond to description

**Applies**: Even if buyer examines goods (if sold by description)

**Example**: "Cambodian rice" → Must be Cambodian (not Vietnamese)

**Case: Varley v. Whipp (1900)**:
- Sale of reaping machine (described as new the previous year)
- Machine was old and worthless
- Court held: Breach of condition (did not correspond to description)

### Section 16: Sale by Sample

**Implied Conditions**:
1. **Bulk corresponds** to sample in quality
2. **Opportunity to compare**: Buyer shall have reasonable opportunity to compare bulk with sample
3. **Free from defects**: Goods free from defects rendering them unmerchantable (not apparent on reasonable examination of sample)

**Example**: Fabric sold by sample; bulk different quality → Breach of condition

**Case: Godley v. Perry (1960)**:
- Sale of catapult (sample tested)
- Bulk defective (elastic broke; injured buyer)
- Defect not discoverable on reasonable examination
- Court held: Breach of implied condition (Section 16(c))

### Section 16A: Merchantable Quality

**Implied Condition** (when goods sold by description):
- Goods of **merchantable quality** (fit for ordinary purposes)
- **Exception**: If defects drawn to buyer's attention OR buyer examines and should have discovered

**Merchantable Quality**: As good as goods ordinarily are in that line of trade

**Example**: Buy "apple juice" → Should be fit for drinking (not spoiled)

## Transfer of Property (Ownership) - Sections 18-26

**"Property"**: Ownership (not physical possession)

**Why Important?**:
- **Risk** passes with property (Section 26)
- **Remedies** depend on whether property passed
- **Insolvency**: If buyer insolvent after property passes, seller may not recover goods

### Section 18: General Rule

**Property passes** when parties intend it to pass

**Ascertain Intent**: From contract terms, conduct, circumstances

### Section 19: Specific or Ascertained Goods

**Rules for when property passes**:

#### Rule 1: Unconditional Contract for Specific Goods in Deliverable State

**Property passes immediately** when contract made (even if delivery/payment later)

**Deliverable State**: Fit to be delivered (no further act needed)

**Example**: 
- Contract to sell specific car (ready for delivery)
- Property passes when contract made
- Even if payment/delivery next week

**Case: Tarling v. Baxter (1827)**:
- Sale of haystack
- Haystack burned before delivery
- Court held: Property passed when contract made; buyer bears loss

#### Rule 2: Specific Goods Not in Deliverable State

**Property passes** when seller does act to put goods in deliverable state + buyer notified

**Example**:
- Sale of timber (to be cut and seasoned)
- Property passes when timber cut, seasoned, and buyer notified

#### Rule 3: Specific Goods in Deliverable State - Measuring/Testing for Price

**Property passes** when measurement/testing done + buyer notified

**Example**:
- Sale of wheat (price per quintal)
- Property passes when weighed and buyer notified

#### Rule 4: Sale or Return / On Approval

**Property passes** when:
1. Buyer **signifies approval/acceptance**, OR
2. Buyer **does act adopting transaction** (e.g., resells), OR
3. Buyer **retains goods** beyond time fixed (or reasonable time if no time fixed)

**Example**:
- Books sent "on approval" for 7 days
- Buyer keeps beyond 7 days → Property passes (deemed acceptance)

#### Rule 5: Unascertained / Future Goods

**Property passes** when goods in **deliverable state** are **unconditionally appropriated** to contract by seller with buyer's **assent** (express or implied)

**Appropriation**: Irrevocable act setting aside specific goods for this contract

**Assent**: Can be before or after appropriation

**Example**:
- Contract to sell 100 bags of rice (unascertained)
- Seller separates 100 bags, marks buyer's name, notifies buyer
- Property passes (unconditional appropriation + implied assent)

**Delivery to Carrier** (Section 23(1)):
- If seller delivers to carrier for transmission to buyer → Deemed appropriation (property passes)

### Section 20: Risk Passes with Property

```
"Unless otherwise agreed, the goods remain at the seller's risk until the property therein is transferred to the buyer, but when the property therein is transferred to the buyer, the goods are at the buyer's risk whether delivery has been made or not."
```

**Rule**: **Risk follows ownership** (not possession)

**Example**:
- Property passed to buyer
- Goods still with seller (awaiting pickup)
- Goods destroyed by fire (no fault of seller)
- **Loss**: Buyer's (must still pay price)

**Exception**: If either party delays delivery and goods deteriorate → Delay-causing party liable

## Performance of Contract (Sections 31-44)

### Section 31: Duties of Seller and Buyer

**Seller's Duty**: Deliver goods

**Buyer's Duty**: Accept and pay

**Concurrent Conditions**: Delivery and payment (unless credit agreed)

### Section 32: Delivery

**Definition**: Voluntary transfer of possession from seller to buyer

**Modes of Delivery**:
1. **Actual Delivery**: Physical handing over
2. **Symbolic Delivery**: Transfer of key, document of title (warehouse receipt, bill of lading)
3. **Constructive Delivery**: 
   - Attornment: Goods with third party; third party acknowledges holds for buyer
   - Transfer of title documents

**Section 35**: Seller must deliver at proper time, place, and manner

### Section 36: Delivery of Wrong Quantity

**Three Scenarios**:

1. **Less than contracted**: Buyer may reject OR accept (pay at contract rate)
2. **More than contracted**: Buyer may:
   - Reject all, OR
   - Accept contract quantity (reject rest), OR
   - Accept all (pay at contract rate for extra)
3. **Mixed with other goods**: Buyer may:
   - Reject all, OR
   - Accept contract goods (reject rest)

### Section 37: Installment Deliveries

**General Rule**: Buyer not bound to accept installments (unless agreed)

**If Agreed**: Each installment is separate contract

**Breach of one installment**: Depends on whether breach goes to whole contract

### Section 38-40: Acceptance

**Acceptance**: Buyer's assent to goods delivered

**When Buyer Deemed to Accept** (Section 42):
1. **Intimates acceptance** to seller
2. **Does act inconsistent** with seller's ownership (e.g., resells)
3. **Retains goods** beyond reasonable time without intimating rejection

**Section 41**: After acceptance, buyer cannot reject (condition becomes warranty; can claim damages only)

**Exception**: Buyer can still reject if:
- Breach goes to root of contract
- Seller concealed latent defect

## Rights of Unpaid Seller (Sections 45-54)

**Unpaid Seller** (Section 45):
1. **Price not paid** AND not received equivalent (e.g., dishonored cheque)
2. **Conditional payment** failed (e.g., bill of exchange dishonored)

### Three Rights

#### 1. Lien (Sections 47-49)

**Right to retain possession** until payment (even if property passed to buyer)

**When Available**:
- Goods sold without credit, OR
- Goods sold on credit (credit period expired), OR
- Buyer becomes insolvent

**Lien Lost**:
- Payment/tender of price
- Waiver (seller agrees buyer may sell/dispose)
- Delivery to carrier without reserving right of disposal

**Nature**: Possessory right (not right to sell)

#### 2. Stoppage in Transit (Sections 50-52)

**Right to stop goods** while in transit (before reaching buyer) if buyer becomes insolvent

**Transit Begins**: Goods delivered to carrier

**Transit Ends**:
- Buyer obtains delivery from carrier, OR
- Carrier acknowledges holds goods for buyer, OR
- Buyer obtains delivery before destination, OR
- Carrier wrongfully refuses to deliver

**How to Exercise**: Notice to carrier; carrier must redeliver to seller (seller bears cost)

#### 3. Right of Resale (Section 54)

**Right to resell goods** in certain circumstances

**When Available**:
1. **Perishable goods** (buyer delays taking delivery), OR
2. **Notice to buyer** (seller gives notice of intention to resell; buyer doesn't pay within reasonable time), OR
3. **Seller reserves right of resale**

**Effect of Resale**:
- Original contract rescinded (BUT buyer liable for damages for breach)
- Second buyer gets good title (even if original buyer paid)

## Remedies

### Buyer's Remedies

#### 1. Damages for Non-Delivery (Section 57)

**Measure**: Difference between contract price and market price (at time of breach)

**Formula**: Market Price - Contract Price = Damages

**Example**:
- Contract to buy at Rs. 100/kg
- Seller doesn't deliver
- Market price Rs. 120/kg
- Damages: Rs. 20/kg

#### 2. Specific Performance (Section 58)

**When**: Goods are **specific or ascertained** (court's discretion)

**Example**: Sale of rare painting; seller refuses → Court may order delivery

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-SPECIFIC-PERFORMANCE protocol

#### 3. Breach of Warranty (Section 59)

**Buyer's options**:
1. **Damages** for breach (diminution in value), OR
2. **Set-off** damages against price (if sued for price)

**Measure**: Difference between value as warranted and actual value

**Example**:
- Car warranted "2020 model"; actually 2018
- Value difference: Rs. 50,000
- Damages: Rs. 50,000

#### 4. Repudiation Before Delivery (Section 60)

**If seller repudiates before delivery** + buyer elects to treat as breach → Buyer may sue for damages (anticipatory breach)

### Seller's Remedies

#### 1. Action for Price (Section 55)

**When**:
1. **Property passed** to buyer + buyer wrongfully refuses to pay, OR
2. **Price payable on day certain** + buyer wrongfully refuses

**Amount**: Full contract price (not damages)

**Example**: Goods delivered; buyer refuses to pay → Seller sues for Rs. 10 lakhs (price)

#### 2. Damages for Non-Acceptance (Section 61)

**When**: Buyer wrongfully refuses to accept + pay

**Measure**: Difference between contract price and market price (at time of breach)

**Formula**: Contract Price - Market Price = Damages

**Alternative**: If no market, seller may resell and claim difference + incidental expenses

**Example**:
- Contract price Rs. 100/kg
- Buyer refuses
- Market price Rs. 80/kg (seller resells)
- Damages: Rs. 20/kg

## Auction Sales (Section 64)

**Section 64: Rules for Auction Sales**

### (1) Sale Complete on Fall of Hammer

**Property passes** when auctioneer's hammer falls (acceptance of bid)

**Before**: Bidder can retract; auctioneer can withdraw goods

### (2) Right to Bid May Be Reserved

**Seller/auctioneer can reserve right** to bid (must be notified)

**If not notified + seller bids** → Sale may be treated as fraudulent by buyer

### (3) Sale Without Reserve

**"Without reserve"**: Goods must be sold to highest bidder (auctioneer cannot withdraw after bidding starts)

### (4) Pretended Bidding / Puffing

**Seller cannot employ person to bid** to increase price (unless notified)

**Effect**: Buyer may avoid contract OR claim damages

## Unpaid Seller vs. Other Rights

### Priority of Rights

**Unpaid Seller's Rights vs.**:

#### 1. Bona Fide Purchaser from Buyer

**Section 27-30**: If buyer resold to innocent third party (before seller exercised rights) → Third party protected (gets good title)

**Exceptions**:
- Seller had stopped goods in transit (third party takes subject to seller's rights)
- Seller reserved right of disposal

#### 2. Official Receiver / Liquidator (Buyer's Insolvency)

**Unpaid seller's rights preserved** in insolvency

**Lien + Stoppage**: Available against buyer's estate

## Comparison: Sale vs. Other Contracts

### Sale vs. Hire-Purchase

| Aspect | Sale | Hire-Purchase |
|--------|------|---------------|
| **Property** | Passes to buyer (immediate or future) | Remains with owner until all installments paid |
| **Right to return** | Generally no (unless condition breached) | Hirer can return (not obliged to buy) |
| **Default** | Buyer liable for full price | Owner can repossess goods |

**Case: Helby v. Matthews (1895)**:
- Hire-purchase agreement (option to buy after installments)
- Hirer pledged goods
- Court held: Property not passed (hire-purchase ≠ sale); pledge invalid

### Sale vs. Bailment

| Aspect | Sale | Bailment |
|--------|------|-----------|
| **Property** | Transfers | Remains with bailor |
| **Consideration** | Price (money) | No price (may be gratuitous or for service) |
| **Return** | No return (ownership transferred) | Goods returned after purpose fulfilled |

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-BAILMENT-PLEDGE protocol

### Sale vs. Contract for Work & Labour

**Distinction**:
- **Sale of Goods**: Transfer of existing goods (or goods to be produced)
- **Work & Labour**: Skill/service applied; goods incidental

**Test**: Substance of contract (goods transfer OR service provision?)

**Example**:
- **Sale**: Buy ready-made furniture → Sale of goods
- **Work**: Commission carpenter to make custom furniture → Work & labour (primarily service)

**Case: Robinson v. Graves (1935)**:
- Contract for artist to paint portrait
- Court held: Work & labour (not sale); substance is skill/service

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Contract of Sale vs. Agreement to Sell

**Facts**: A contracts to buy B's car for Rs. 5 lakhs; delivery and payment next week

**Analysis**:
- **Specific goods**: Yes (identified car)
- **Deliverable state**: Yes (ready for delivery)
- **Rule 1 (Section 19)**: Property should pass immediately
- **BUT**: Parties intended delivery next week (delayed)
- **Result**: Depends on intent; if parties agreed property passes next week → Agreement to sell (until then)

### Example 2: Risk Passes with Property

**Facts**:
- A agrees to sell specific machine to B for Rs. 10 lakhs
- Contract states "property passes when B pays"
- B hasn't paid yet
- Machine destroyed in fire (no fault)

**Analysis**:
- **Property**: Not passed (payment condition not fulfilled)
- **Section 20**: Risk with property → Risk remains with A (seller)
- **Result**: A bears loss; B not liable to pay

### Example 3: Breach of Condition - Buyer's Rights

**Facts**:
- A buys cloth "pure silk" from B for Rs. 50,000
- Cloth delivered; A accepts and uses for 2 months
- A discovers cloth is polyester blend (not pure silk)
- A seeks to reject goods and recover price

**Analysis**:
- **Sale by description** (Section 15): "Pure silk"
- **Breach**: Cloth not pure silk (breach of condition)
- **Acceptance** (Section 42): A used cloth for 2 months (deemed acceptance)
- **Section 13**: After acceptance, condition becomes warranty
- **Result**: A cannot reject; can only claim damages (difference in value)

### Example 4: Unpaid Seller's Lien

**Facts**:
- A (seller) sells goods to B (buyer) for Rs. 1 lakh (no credit)
- A delivered goods to carrier for transmission to B
- B hasn't paid yet
- Goods still in transit

**Analysis**:
- **Property**: Passed when delivered to carrier (Section 23)
- **Unpaid seller**: Yes (price not paid)
- **Lien**: Lost (delivered to carrier; possession lost)
- **Stoppage in transit**: Available (goods in transit + B not paid)
- **Result**: A can stop goods in transit (if B becomes insolvent); otherwise must wait for delivery

### Example 5: Damages for Non-Delivery

**Facts**:
- Contract to buy 1000 kg rice at Rs. 50/kg (total Rs. 50,000)
- Seller doesn't deliver
- Market price Rs. 60/kg at time of breach
- Buyer had resale contract at Rs. 70/kg (seller knew)

**Analysis**:
- **Section 57**: Damages = Market Price - Contract Price
- **Formula**: (Rs. 60 - Rs. 50) × 1000 kg = Rs. 10,000
- **Special damages**: Loss from resale contract = (Rs. 70 - Rs. 60) × 1000 = Rs. 10,000
- **Hadley v. Baxendale Limb 2**: Seller knew of resale → Special damages recoverable
- **Total**: Rs. 10,000 (market difference) + Rs. 10,000 (resale loss) = Rs. 20,000

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Confusing Property with Possession

**Mistake**: Thinking delivery (possession) = ownership (property)

**Correct**: Property (ownership) and possession are separate; property passes per Sections 18-26 (intent/rules); possession passes on delivery

### 2. Assuming Risk Remains with Possessor

**Mistake**: Thinking risk remains with seller until buyer receives goods

**Correct**: Risk follows property (Section 20), not possession; if property passed, buyer bears risk even if seller still has goods

### 3. Rejecting Goods After Acceptance

**Mistake**: Buyer accepts goods, uses them, then tries to reject months later

**Correct**: After acceptance (Section 42), buyer cannot reject (Section 13); condition becomes warranty; damages only

### 4. Ignoring Buyer's Duty to Mitigate (Unpaid Seller)

**Mistake**: Seller keeps goods idle after buyer's breach; claims full damages

**Correct**: Seller must attempt to resell at best price (mitigate loss); damages = difference between contract and resale price (plus reasonable incidentals)

### 5. Applying Sale of Goods Act to Immovables

**Mistake**: Trying to apply Sale of Goods Act to land/property sale

**Correct**: Sale of Goods Act applies only to **movables** (Section 2(7)); immovable property governed by Transfer of Property Act 1882
