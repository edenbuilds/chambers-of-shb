- **Effective**: Since 1872

## Overview

This protocol covers **breach of contract** - the failure to perform contractual obligations without lawful excuse, and the rights and remedies available to the aggrieved party.

**Key Principle**: Breach entitles innocent party to damages and may allow termination of contract.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 39: Effect of Refusal to Accept Offer of Performance

```
"When a party to a contract has refused to perform, or disabled himself from performing, his promise in its entirety, the promisee may put an end to the contract, unless he has signified, by words or conduct, his acquiescence in its continuance."
```

**Breach → Right to Terminate** (if material breach)

## Types of Breach

### 1. Actual Breach

**Definition**: Failure to perform when performance is due

**Elements**:
1. **Performance due**: Time for performance arrived
2. **Failure to perform**: Party does not perform (wholly or partially)
3. **No lawful excuse**: No valid excuse (impossibility, force majeure)

**Example**: Seller fails to deliver goods on agreed delivery date → Actual breach

### 2. Anticipatory Breach

**Definition**: Repudiation of contract before time for performance

**Elements**:
1. **Before performance due**: Repudiation occurs before time for performance
2. **Express or Implied repudiation**:
   - **Express**: Party states they will not perform
   - **Implied**: Party's conduct makes performance impossible

**Landmark Case: Hochster v. De La Tour (1853)**:
- Defendant hired plaintiff as courier for European tour (June 1)
- On May 11, defendant wrote refusing plaintiff's services
- Plaintiff sued on May 22 (before June 1)
- Court held: Plaintiff can sue immediately upon repudiation (anticipatory breach)

**Example - Express**:
- A contracts to deliver goods on Dec 31
- On Dec 1, A writes: "I will not deliver goods"
- Anticipatory breach

**Example - Implied**:
- A contracts to sell specific car to B (delivery Jan 15)
- On Jan 5, A sells same car to C
- A disabled from performing → Anticipatory breach

---

## Rights of Innocent Party (Anticipatory Breach)

**Case: Frost v. Knight (1872)** - Leading case on anticipatory breach

**Innocent Party's Options**:

### Option 1: Treat Contract as Discharged (Accept Repudiation)

**Immediate Action**:
- Treat contract as ended
- Sue immediately for damages
- No need to wait for performance date

**Advantages**:
- Immediate remedy
- Mitigation of losses (can make alternative arrangements)

**Example**: A repudiates on Dec 1; B can sue immediately (no need to wait till Dec 31)

### Option 2: Affirm Contract (Reject Repudiation)

**Wait and See**:
- Keep contract alive
- Wait till performance date
- If party still doesn't perform → Sue then

**Advantages**:
- Party may change mind and perform
- Innocent party can still earn consideration (if needs to complete own performance)

**Risks**:
- Supervening impossibility may occur (frustrating contract)
- Contract may become void before performance date

**Case: Avery v. Bowden (1855)**:
- Charterer repudiated charter party (ship hire)
- Shipowner waited
- Before performance date, Crimean War broke out (charter illegal)
- Court held: Contract frustrated; shipowner cannot sue (should have accepted repudiation immediately)

**Indian Position**: Both options available (affirm or accept repudiation)

## Materiality of Breach

**Not all breaches allow termination**

### Breach of Condition vs Breach of Warranty

**Sale of Goods Act 1930 (Sections 11-13)**: Distinguishes conditions and warranties

**Condition**: **Essential term** of contract
- Breach → Right to terminate + damages

**Warranty**: **Non-essential term** (collateral)
- Breach → Damages only (no right to terminate)

**Section 12 Sale of Goods Act**:

```
"A stipulation in a contract of sale with reference to goods which are the subject thereof may be a condition or a warranty.

A condition is a stipulation essential to the main purpose of the contract, the breach of which gives rise to a right to treat the contract as repudiated.

A warranty is a stipulation collateral to the main purpose of the contract, the breach of which gives rise to a claim for damages but not to a right to reject the goods and treat the contract as repudiated."
```

**Test**: Is term essential to main purpose?
- YES → Condition (breach allows termination)
- NO → Warranty (breach allows damages only)

**Example**:
- **Condition**: Sale of "2024 model car"; delivered 2020 model → Breach of condition (buyer can reject)
- **Warranty**: "Air conditioning works perfectly"; minor AC issue → Breach of warranty (damages only; cannot reject car)

**Case: Bettini v. Gye (1876)**:
- Opera singer contracted to arrive 6 days before performance for rehearsals
- Arrived 4 days before (2 days late for rehearsals)
- Court held: Breach of warranty (rehearsal time not essential; mere collateral); employer cannot terminate

**Case: Poussard v. Spiers (1876)**:
- Opera singer contracted to perform from opening night
- Fell ill; missed opening week
- Court held: Breach of condition (opening performances essential); employer can terminate

### Fundamental / Material Breach

**Fundamental Breach**: Breach going to root of contract

**Effect**: Repudiatory breach (innocent party can terminate)

**Test**: Does breach deprive innocent party of substantially entire benefit of contract?

**Case: Hong Kong Fir Shipping Co. v. Kawasaki Kisen Kaisha (1962)**:
- Ship charter for 24 months
- Ship unseaworthy for 20 weeks (delay)
- Court held: Not fundamental breach (charterer still got substantial benefit for remaining period)

**Innominate Terms**: Terms that can be condition or warranty depending on consequences of breach
- If breach deprives substantial benefit → Condition
- If breach minor → Warranty

## Consequences of Breach

### 1. Damages (Section 73)

**Section 73**: "When a contract has been broken, the party who suffers by such breach is entitled to receive, from the party who has broken the contract, compensation for any loss or damage caused to him thereby, which naturally arose in the usual course of things from such breach, or which the parties knew, when they made the contract, to be likely to result from the breach of it."

**Damages = Compensation** for loss

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-DAMAGES protocol (detailed coverage)

### 2. Specific Performance

**Specific Relief Act 1963**: Court can order specific performance (actual performance of contract)

**When Granted**: If damages inadequate remedy (e.g., sale of unique property, land)

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-SPECIFIC-PERFORMANCE protocol

### 3. Injunction

**Prohibitory Injunction**: Order restraining party from breaching negative covenant

**Example**: Contract not to compete; party competes → Court can grant injunction

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-INJUNCTION protocol

### 4. Quantum Meruit

**Quantum Meruit**: "As much as merited/earned"

**When Applicable**: If contract breached after party has partially performed → Can claim reasonable value of work done

**Example**: Builder contracted to build house for Rs. 50 lakhs; completed 50% when owner breaches → Builder can claim quantum meruit (reasonable value of 50% work)

### 5. Rescission

**Rescission**: Cancellation of contract + restoration to original position

**When Available**: Voidable contracts (fraud, misrepresentation, undue influence)

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-RESCISSION protocol

## Measure of Damages (Section 73)

### General Principle

**Compensatory Damages**: Put innocent party in position they would have been if contract performed

**Not Punitive**: Cannot punish breaching party (unlike criminal law)

### Rule in Hadley v. Baxendale (1854)

**Two-Limb Test** (incorporated in Section 73):

#### Limb 1: Natural Consequences

**Damages that "naturally arose in the usual course of things from such breach"**

**Example**: Contract to deliver goods on Jan 1; seller fails to deliver
- Buyer's loss of profit from resale (foreseeable) → Recoverable
- Buyer's extraordinary loss (unforeseeable) → NOT recoverable

#### Limb 2: Special Damages (Contemplated by Parties)

**Damages "which the parties knew, when they made the contract, to be likely to result from the breach"**

**Example**: Buyer informs seller that goods urgently needed for specific contract; seller fails to deliver
- Buyer's loss from specific contract (seller knew) → Recoverable

**Case: Hadley v. Baxendale (1854)**:
- Mill owner's crankshaft broken; sent for repair
- Carrier delayed delivery of crankshaft to repair shop
- Mill stopped (loss of profit)
- Court held: Loss of profit NOT recoverable (carrier didn't know crankshaft was only one; mill would stop)

**Indian Application**:

**Case: Murlidhar v. Harishchandra (1962)**:
- Contract to supply jaggery (raw sugar)
- Supplier failed
- Buyer claimed loss of profit from reselling jaggery
- Court held: Loss of profit foreseeable; recoverable under Section 73

### Remoteness of Damages

**Proximate Cause**: Damages must flow directly from breach (not too remote)

**Case: Victoria Laundry v. Newman Industries (1949)**:
- Contract to sell boiler to laundry
- Delivery delayed
- Laundry claimed: (1) Normal loss of profit, (2) Loss of lucrative government contract
- Court held:
  - (1) Recoverable (foreseeable)
  - (2) NOT recoverable (seller didn't know about special government contract)

## Duty to Mitigate (Section 73 Proviso)

**Section 73 Proviso**: "Such compensation is not to be given for any remote and indirect loss or damage sustained by reason of the breach. Compensation for breach of contract is subject to the rule that the party complaining of the breach must take reasonable steps to mitigate the loss."

**Duty to Mitigate**: Innocent party must take reasonable steps to minimize loss

**Not Required**: Unreasonable steps or steps involving substantial cost/risk

**Example**: Contract to deliver goods on Jan 1; seller fails
- Buyer should buy goods from alternative source (reasonable mitigation)
- Buyer cannot claim excessive damages if unreasonably failed to mitigate

**Case: British Westinghouse v. Underground Electric Railways (1912)**:
- Defective turbines supplied
- Buyer replaced with more efficient turbines (which saved more than they cost)
- Court held: Damages reduced by savings from new turbines (duty to mitigate)

**Mitigation Examples**:

**Reasonable**:
- Finding alternative supplier
- Accepting similar performance (if offered)
- Re-letting property (if tenant breaches lease)

**Unreasonable** (Not required):
- Accepting substantially different performance
- Incurring substantial costs
- Taking unreasonable risks

## Liquidated Damages vs Penalty (Section 74)

**Section 74**: "When a contract has been broken, if a sum is named in the contract as the amount to be paid in case of such breach, or if the contract contains any other stipulation by way of penalty, the party complaining of the breach is entitled, whether or not actual damage or loss is proved to have been caused thereby, to receive from the party who has broken the contract reasonable compensation not exceeding the amount so named or, as the case may be, the penalty stipulated for."

**Two Concepts**:
1. **Liquidated Damages**: Genuine pre-estimate of loss
2. **Penalty**: Amount intended to penalize (not genuine estimate)

**English Law**: Penalty unenforceable; only liquidated damages enforceable

**Indian Law (Section 74)**: Both enforceable, but court awards **reasonable compensation** (not exceeding stipulated amount)

**Effect**:
- Liquidated damages clause → Ceiling (court can award up to that amount)
- Penalty clause → Ceiling (court can award reasonable compensation up to penalty amount)

**Case: Fateh Chand v. Balkishan Dass (1963)** (Supreme Court):
- Contract stipulated Rs. 10 per day for delay
- Actual loss minimal
- Court held: Can award reasonable compensation (not necessarily full Rs. 10/day if excessive)

**Case: Kailash Nath Associates v. DDA (2015)** (Supreme Court):
- Construction contract with liquidated damages clause
- Contractor delayed
- Supreme Court held: Liquidated damages are compensation (not penalty); court must determine if genuine pre-estimate

**Distinction**:
- **Liquidated damages**: Reasonable estimate of probable loss → Enforceable
- **Penalty**: Extravagant sum (in terrorem) → Enforceable but court reduces to reasonable compensation

## Exclusion / Limitation Clauses

**Exclusion Clause**: Clause excluding or limiting liability for breach

**Example**: "Seller not liable for any consequential damages"

**Indian Position**:
- **Generally valid** (freedom of contract)
- **Exceptions**:
  - **Fundamental breach**: Exclusion clause may not protect if fundamental breach
  - **Unconscionable**: If grossly unfair, court may disregard
  - **Statutory violations**: Cannot exclude liability imposed by statute

**Case: Suisse Atlantique v. NV Rotterdamsche (1967)**:
- Limitation clause in charter party
- Fundamental breach occurred
- Court held: Limitation clause may not apply to fundamental breach

**Indian Approach**: Courts interpret exclusion clauses strictly (against party relying on it)

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Actual Breach

**Facts**: Seller contracts to deliver 1000 units on March 1; fails to deliver

**Analysis**:
- **Type**: Actual breach (performance due; not performed)
- **Buyer's rights**: Terminate + damages
- **Damages**: Loss of profit from resale (if foreseeable)

### Example 2: Anticipatory Breach

**Facts**:
- A contracts to deliver goods to B on Dec 31
- On Dec 1, A states: "I will not deliver"

**Analysis**:
- **Type**: Anticipatory breach (repudiation before due date)
- **B's options**:
  1. Accept repudiation → Sue immediately (Dec 1)
  2. Affirm contract → Wait till Dec 31; sue then if A still doesn't deliver

### Example 3: Breach of Condition vs Warranty

**Facts**:
- Contract to sell "2024 Honda City with leather seats"
- Delivered: 2024 Honda City with cloth seats

**Analysis**:
- **Model (2024 Honda City)**: Condition (essential)
- **Leather seats**: Warranty (collateral)
- **Breach**: Warranty only (seats)
- **Buyer's remedy**: Cannot reject car (model correct); can claim damages for cloth vs leather difference

### Example 4: Liquidated Damages

**Facts**:
- Construction contract: Rs. 50 lakhs; completion by June 1
- Liquidated damages: Rs. 10,000/day for delay
- Contractor completes on Aug 1 (60 days late)
- Actual loss to owner: Rs. 2 lakhs

**Analysis**:
- **Stipulated amount**: Rs. 10,000 × 60 = Rs. 6 lakhs
- **Section 74**: Court awards reasonable compensation (not exceeding Rs. 6 lakhs)
- **Likely award**: Rs. 2 lakhs (actual loss) OR higher if court finds genuine pre-estimate

### Example 5: Duty to Mitigate

**Facts**:
- A contracts to rent office to B for Rs. 1 lakh/month (1-year lease)
- B breaches after 3 months
- A could re-let office immediately for Rs. 90,000/month but doesn't
- A sues for Rs. 9 lakhs (remaining 9 months @ Rs. 1 lakh)

**Analysis**:
- **Duty to mitigate**: A should have re-let office
- **Reasonable mitigation**: Accept Rs. 90,000/month tenant
- **Damages**: Rs. 10,000/month × 9 = Rs. 90,000 (difference) + 3 months vacancy (if reasonable)
- **Cannot claim**: Full Rs. 9 lakhs (failed to mitigate)

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Confusing Condition with Warranty

**Mistake**: Terminating contract for minor breach (warranty)

**Correct**: Only material breach (condition) allows termination; minor breach (warranty) → damages only

### 2. Failing to Mitigate

**Mistake**: Sitting idle after breach, letting losses accumulate

**Correct**: Take reasonable steps to minimize loss (duty to mitigate)

### 3. Assuming Penalty Unenforceable

**Mistake**: Thinking penalty clauses void (English law position)

**Correct**: India (Section 74) - Penalty enforceable as ceiling; court awards reasonable compensation

### 4. Ignoring Anticipatory Breach

**Mistake**: Waiting till performance date when party clearly repudiated

**Correct**: Can accept repudiation and sue immediately (or affirm and wait)

### 5. Remote Damages

**Mistake**: Claiming all losses (even remote, unforeseeable)

**Correct**: Only natural consequences or special damages (known to parties) recoverable (Hadley v. Baxendale)
