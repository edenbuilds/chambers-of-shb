# Agency Protocol (India)

## Protocol Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-CONTRACT-AGENCY
- **Version**: 1.0.0
- **Domain**: Contract Law (India)
- **Subdomain**: Special Contracts - Agency
- **Statute**: Indian Contract Act 1872 (Sections 182-238)
- **Last Updated**: January 2025
- **Effective**: Since 1872

---

## Overview

This protocol covers the **contract of agency** - a relationship where one person (agent) acts on behalf of another (principal) with authority to affect the principal's legal relations with third parties.

**Key Principle**: "Qui facit per alium facit per se" (He who acts through another acts himself) - Principal bound by agent's authorized acts as if principal did them personally.

---

## Section 182: Agent and Principal Defined

```
"An 'agent' is a person employed to do any act for another, or to represent another in dealings with third persons. The person for whom such act is done, or who is so represented, is called the 'principal'."
```

**Essential Elements**:
1. **Two parties**: Principal and agent
2. **Authority**: Agent acts with principal's authority
3. **On behalf**: Agent acts for principal (not for himself)
4. **Legal relations**: Agent creates legal rights/obligations for principal

**Parties**:
1. **Principal**: Person on whose behalf agent acts
2. **Agent**: Person who acts on principal's behalf
3. **Third Party**: Person with whom agent deals on principal's behalf

## Creation of Agency (Sections 183-187)

**Agency created by**:

### 1. Express Agreement (Section 183)

**Express Authority**: Principal expressly appoints agent (oral or written)

**Example**: A appoints B as sales agent (written contract) → Express agency

**No formality required**: Need not be in writing (except power of attorney for immovable property under Registration Act)

### 2. Implied Agreement (Section 187)

**Implied Agency**: Conduct of parties indicates agency relationship

**Example**: 
- Wife purchases household necessaries on credit
- Shopkeeper assumes wife acts as husband's agent
- Husband liable (implied agency)

**Case: Suraj Narain Dube v. Raghunath Prasad (1954)**:
- Manager of joint family property
- Court held: Implied agency from conduct and circumstances

### 3. Necessity (Section 189)

**Agency of Necessity**: In emergencies, person may act as agent to protect another's property

**Requirements**:
1. **Emergency** (impossible to communicate with principal)
2. **Bona fide** (for principal's benefit)
3. **Necessary** (circumstances require immediate action)

**Examples**:
- Ship's captain sells cargo to prevent perishing
- Bailee incurs expense to preserve bailed goods

**Case: Great Northern Railway v. Swaffield (1874)**:
- Railway company fed horse (consignee didn't collect)
- Court held: Agency of necessity; entitled to reimbursement

### 4. Estoppel (Section 237)

**Holding Out**: If principal represents that person is his agent (or allows such representation) → Principal estopped from denying agency

**Requirements**:
1. **Representation** by principal (or principal's conduct allows representation)
2. **Third party relies** on representation
3. **Third party alters position** in good faith

**Example**:
- A (owner) allows B to act as if B is A's agent
- C (third party) contracts with B believing B is agent
- A bound (estopped from denying B's agency)

**Case: Pickering v. Busk (1812)**:
- Broker habitually acted for principal
- Third party relied on apparent authority
- Court held: Principal bound (estoppel)

### 5. Ratification (Sections 196-200)

**Ratification**: Principal retrospectively approves agent's unauthorized act

**Effect**: Unauthorized act becomes authorized from beginning (relates back)

**Section 196**: Ratification of unauthorized act = same effect as if authorized initially

**Requirements**:
1. **Act done on behalf** of principal (agent disclosed he acts for principal)
2. **Principal in existence** at time of act (company not yet incorporated cannot ratify)
3. **Principal competent** at time of act (principal must have capacity when act done)
4. **Full knowledge** of facts
5. **Entire transaction** ratified (cannot ratify part and reject part)
6. **Within reasonable time**
7. **Not illegal** act

**Section 200**: Ratification cannot harm third party who has acted on assumption act was unauthorized

**Example**:
- B (without authority) sells A's car to C for Rs. 5 lakhs
- A later ratifies sale (approves)
- Sale valid from time B sold (relates back)

**Case: Bolton Partners v. Lambert (1889)**:
- Agent accepted offer without authority
- Principal ratified after offer withdrawn
- Court held: Ratification relates back; contract binding from acceptance

## Extent of Agent's Authority (Sections 186-188)

### 1. Express Authority

**Stated in contract** (written or oral)

**Scope**: Agent can do what expressly authorized

### 2. Implied Authority (Section 188)

**Section 188**: "An agent having an authority to do an act has authority to do every lawful thing which is necessary in order to do such act."

**Includes**:
- **Incidental acts**: Necessary for performing express authority
- **Usual and customary acts**: In trade/business (unless principal restricts and third party knows)

**Example**: 
- Agent authorized to sell goods
- **Implied**: Authority to receive payment, give delivery, issue receipt (necessary/incidental)

**Case: Hely-Hutchinson v. Brayhead Ltd. (1968)**:
- Chairman had implied authority from conduct (usual authority for that position)
- Court held: Implied actual authority existed

### 3. Apparent/Ostensible Authority (Section 237)

**Apparent Authority**: Third party reasonably believes agent has authority (based on principal's conduct)

**Principal bound** even if agent lacks actual authority (if third party reasonably relied)

**Example**:
- A (principal) allows B to act as if B is sales manager
- C (customer) orders from B (believing B has authority)
- A bound (apparent authority)

**Case: Freeman & Lockyer v. Buckhurst Park Properties (1964)**:
- De facto director (not formally appointed)
- Company allowed him to act as managing director
- Court held: Apparent authority existed; company bound

### 4. Emergency Authority (Section 189)

**Agent can exceed authority in emergency** (if necessary to protect principal from loss)

**Requirements**:
- Cannot communicate with principal
- Bona fide for principal's benefit

## Duties of Agent to Principal

### 1. Duty to Follow Instructions (Section 211)

**Agent must conduct business** according to principal's directions

**Breach**: Liable for loss from non-compliance

**Example**: Principal instructs agent to sell at minimum Rs. 10 lakhs; agent sells at Rs. 8 lakhs → Agent liable for Rs. 2 lakhs loss

**Exception**: Emergency (Section 189)

### 2. Duty of Skill and Diligence (Section 212)

**Section 212**: "An agent is bound to conduct the business of his principal with as much skill as is generally possessed by persons engaged in similar business, unless the principal has notice of his want of skill."

**Standard**: Reasonable skill and care (as would possess by persons in that business)

**Professional Agent**: Higher standard (professional skill expected)

**Example**: Lawyer agent must exercise lawyer's skill; architect must exercise architect's skill

**Case: Turpin v. Bilton (1843)**:
- Stockbroker agent failed to obtain proper security
- Court held: Liable (breach of duty to exercise skill)

### 3. Duty to Render Accounts (Section 213)

**Agent must maintain** proper accounts; render on demand

**Duty**: Keep principal's money/property separate from own

**Example**: Sales agent must account for all collections; provide statements

### 4. Duty to Communicate (Section 214)

**Agent must communicate** material information acquired in course of agency

**"Material"**: Information affecting principal's decision

**Example**: Agent buying property for principal learns of defect → Must inform principal

**Case: Greenwood v. Martins Bank (1933)**:
- Bank (agent) knew of forgeries by customer's wife
- Failed to inform customer (principal)
- Court held: Breach of duty to communicate

### 5. Duty Not to Deal on Own Account (Section 215)

**Agent cannot deal on own account** (without principal's consent + full disclosure)

**Reason**: Conflict of interest

**Example**: Agent authorized to buy land; buys from self → Must disclose and get consent

**Breach**: Principal can:
- Rescind transaction, OR
- Claim profit from agent

**Case: Armstrong v. Jackson (1917)**:
- Stockbroker sold own shares to principal (disguised as market purchase)
- Court held: Breach of fiduciary duty; contract voidable

### 6. Duty Not to Make Secret Profit (Section 216)

**Agent must account** for all profits/benefits arising from agency

**Secret Profit**: Profit not disclosed to principal

**Examples**:
- Commission from third party (without principal's knowledge)
- Discount obtained (not shared with principal)

**Breach**: Principal can claim secret profit

**Case: Mahesan v. Malaysia Government Officers' Co-op Housing Society (1979)** (Privy Council):
- Director received bribe to award contract at inflated price
- Court held: Must account for bribe; principal can claim

### 7. Duty Not to Delegate (Section 190-194)

**Section 190**: "An agent cannot lawfully employ another to perform acts which he has expressly or impliedly undertaken to perform personally, unless by the ordinary custom of trade a sub-agent may, or from the nature of the agency, a sub-agent must, be employed."

**General Rule**: "Delegatus non potest delegare" (A delegate cannot delegate)

**Agent must act personally** (unless authorized to delegate)

**Exceptions** (when delegation allowed):
1. **Express authority** to delegate
2. **Trade custom** permits
3. **Nature of business** requires (ministerial acts)
4. **Emergency**

**Sub-agent**: Person appointed by agent (with authority)

**Liability**:
- **For sub-agent**: Agent liable if unauthorized delegation
- **Sub-agent's acts**: Principal bound if delegation authorized; otherwise agent liable (Section 191-192)

### 8. Duty Not to Put Himself in Position of Conflict (Fiduciary Duty)

**Fiduciary Relationship**: Agent owes loyalty to principal

**Cannot**:
- Act for opposite party (without disclosure + consent)
- Compete with principal
- Use confidential information for own benefit

## Rights of Agent

### 1. Right to Remuneration (Section 219)

**Agent entitled to agreed remuneration** (commission, fees)

**If no rate fixed**: Reasonable remuneration (quantum meruit)

**When Payable**:
- After completion of work (unless agreed otherwise)
- Effective cause: Agent's efforts caused result

**Section 220**: Agent not entitled if misconduct (breach of duty)

**Example**: Sales agent entitled to commission when sale completed (contract executed)

**Case: Luxor Ltd. v. Cooper (1941)**:
- Estate agent introduced buyer; sale fell through (not agent's fault)
- Contract said "commission on completion"
- Court held: No commission (sale didn't complete)

### 2. Right to Retainer (Section 217)

**Agent can retain** from principal's money:
- All sums due to agent (remuneration, advances, expenses)

**Retainer = Right to deduct** from principal's funds in agent's hands

**Example**: Agent collects Rs. 10 lakhs for principal; entitled to Rs. 1 lakh commission → Can retain Rs. 1 lakh; remit Rs. 9 lakhs

### 3. Right of Lien (Section 221)

**Particular Lien**: Agent can retain principal's goods/property until dues paid

**General Lien**: Certain agents (bankers, factors, wharfingers, attorneys) have general lien (for all dues, not just in respect of those goods)

**Lost if**: Agent parts with possession

**Example**: Commission agent (factor) can retain goods until all commissions/charges paid

### 4. Right to Indemnity (Section 222-225)

**Section 222**: "The employer of an agent is bound to indemnify him against the consequences of all lawful acts done by such agent in exercise of the authority conferred upon him."

**Indemnity covers**:
- Lawful acts within authority
- Acts in emergencies (exceeding authority for principal's benefit)
- Loss from following principal's instructions (even if acts result in liability)

**Example**:
- Principal instructs agent to sell goods
- Goods belong to third party (agent doesn't know)
- True owner sues agent
- Principal must indemnify agent

**Section 223**: Agent acting without authority (but principal ratifies) → Entitled to indemnity

**No Indemnity**: If agent's criminal/illegal act (Section 225)

### 5. Right to Compensation (Section 225)

**Agent entitled to compensation** for:
- Principal's breach of contract (wrongful termination)
- Principal's neglect/misconduct

**Example**: Principal terminates fixed-term agency before expiry (without cause) → Agent can claim damages

## Duties of Principal to Agent

### 1. Duty to Indemnify (Section 222-225)

As above (agent's right to indemnity)

### 2. Duty to Pay Remuneration (Section 219)

**Pay agreed remuneration** (or reasonable remuneration if no agreement)

### 3. Duty to Compensate for Injury (Section 225)

**Compensate agent** for injury caused by principal's:
- Neglect
- Want of skill

**Example**: Principal gives wrong information; agent acts on it and suffers loss → Principal must compensate

---

## Rights of Principal

### 1. Against Agent

**Principal can**:
- Sue for breach of duty (negligence, fraud, unauthorized acts)
- Claim secret profits
- Rescind contract (if agent dealt on own account)
- Claim damages for loss

### 2. Against Third Party

**Principal can sue/be sued** by third party (for contracts made by agent within authority)

**Exception**: Undisclosed principal (see below)

---

## Liability of Principal and Agent to Third Parties

### 1. Disclosed Principal

**Agent acts for known principal** (third party knows agent acts for someone)

**Liability**: **Principal liable** to third party (not agent)

**Example**: B (agent) contracts with C "on behalf of A (principal)" → A liable; B not liable

**Section 230**: Representative properly acting (within authority) not personally liable

### 2. Undisclosed Principal

**Agent acts for principal** but **doesn't disclose** principal's existence (third party thinks agent is principal)

**Liability**: 
- **Agent liable** (third party can sue agent)
- **Principal also liable** (third party can sue principal when discovered)

**Third party's election**: Can sue agent OR principal (not both)

**Section 232**: Third party can hold either agent or undisclosed principal liable

**Exception**: If third party specifically contracted with agent personally (no substitution of principal allowed)

**Example**:
- B buys goods from C (C thinks B is principal)
- B actually buying for A (undisclosed principal)
- C can sue B or A (when A discovered)

**Case: Sims v. Bond (1833)**:
- Agent contracted without disclosing principal
- Principal discovered later
- Court held: Third party can elect to sue agent or principal

### 3. Agent Exceeding Authority (Section 235)

**Agent exceeds authority** (or acts without authority) → **Agent personally liable**

**Principal not liable** (unless ratifies)

**Agent's liability**: Breach of warranty of authority

**Example**: Agent sells principal's car (without authority); principal refuses to honor → Agent liable to buyer (breach of warranty)

### 4. Foreign Principal (Section 234)

**Agent for foreign principal** → **Agent personally liable** (unless contract provides otherwise)

**Reason**: Difficult to enforce against foreign principal

### 5. Agent Acting for Principal with No Capacity (Section 235)

**If principal lacks capacity** (minor, unsound mind) → **Agent personally liable**

**Example**: Agent contracts on behalf of minor → Agent liable (minor's contract void)

## Termination of Agency (Sections 201-210)

**Agency terminates by**:

### 1. Agreement (Section 202)

**Mutual agreement** to terminate

**Revocation by principal** (Section 203):
- Can revoke anytime (if agency at will)
- Must compensate if revocation before end of fixed term

**Renunciation by agent** (Section 206):
- Can renounce anytime (if agency at will)
- Must give reasonable notice
- Liable for damages if renounces without notice or before fixed term

### 2. Performance (Section 202)

**Completion of business** for which agency created

**Example**: Agent hired to sell property; property sold → Agency ends

### 3. Lapse of Time (Section 202)

**Expiry of period** (if agency for fixed term)

**Example**: Agency for 1 year; 1 year passes → Agency ends

### 4. Death or Insanity (Section 209)

**Death or insanity** of principal or agent → Agency terminates

**Reason**: Personal nature of relationship

**Exception**: Coupled with interest (see below)

### 5. Insolvency of Principal (Section 209)

**Principal becomes insolvent** → Agency terminates

**Reason**: Principal's property vests in official receiver

### 6. Destruction of Subject Matter (Section 202)

**Subject matter** of agency destroyed → Agency terminates

**Example**: Agency to sell specific car; car destroyed → Agency ends

### 7. Principal Becoming Alien Enemy (Section 210)

**War breaks out** → Agency with alien enemy terminates

**Reason**: Illegal to contract with enemy

## Irrevocable Agency (Section 202 Proviso, Section 203)

**General Rule**: Principal can revoke agency anytime

**Exception**: **Agency coupled with interest** = Irrevocable

**"Coupled with Interest"**: Agent has interest in subject matter (not just interest in remuneration)

**Requirements**:
1. **Agent has proprietary interest** in subject matter
2. **Interest is to secure debt** or pre-existing obligation

**Example**:
- A borrows Rs. 10 lakhs from B
- A gives B power to sell A's property to recover loan
- B has interest in property (security for loan)
- Agency irrevocable

**NOT Coupled with Interest**:
- Mere right to commission (interest in remuneration only)

**Case: Smart v. Sandars (1848)**:
- Agent had interest in subject matter to secure debt
- Court held: Agency irrevocable (coupled with interest)

**Section 202 Proviso**: Agency cannot be terminated to agent's prejudice if coupled with interest

**Section 203**: Principal cannot revoke if agency coupled with interest (unless agent consents)

## Del Credere Agent (Section 204)

**Del Credere Agent**: Agent who guarantees that third parties (customers) will pay

**For Guarantee**: Gets extra commission (del credere commission)

**Liability**: If customer defaults → Del credere agent pays principal

**Example**: Sales agent guarantees customer will pay for goods sold on credit; customer defaults → Agent pays

**Nature**: Agent + Surety (guarantee)

**Section 204**: Del credere agent liable to principal if third party defaults

---

## Sub-Agent (Sections 190-195)

**Sub-Agent**: Person appointed by agent (with authority)

### Named Sub-Agent (Section 194)

**Agent nominates** person to act under principal's direction → **Principal and nominated person = principal-agent** (original agent drops out)

**Example**: A appoints B as agent; B nominates C (with A's consent); C acts under A's direction → A-C is principal-agent

### Substituted Agent (Section 191)

**Agent substitutes** another person (with authority) → **Principal and substituted agent = principal-agent** (original agent responsible for selection)

**Original agent's liability**: For selection (if incompetent/dishonest); not for substituted agent's acts (if properly selected)

### Sub-Agent Proper (Section 190-192)

**Agent delegates** with authority → **Sub-agent is agent's agent** (not principal's agent directly)

**Liability**:
- **Agent liable** to principal for sub-agent's acts
- **Sub-agent liable** to agent (not principal) - Section 193

**Exception**: Section 192 - Sub-agent represents principal for acts in ordinary course (in respect of third parties)

**Example**: Lawyer appoints junior lawyer (sub-agent) to draft document → Lawyer liable to principal for sub-agent's negligence

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Ratification

**Facts**:
- B (without authority) sells A's property to C for Rs. 50 lakhs
- Market value Rs. 55 lakhs (good price)
- A approves sale (ratifies) after 2 days
- C tries to withdraw (claims no contract)

**Analysis**:
- **Section 196**: Ratification relates back to time of act
- **B acted on behalf of A**: Yes (disclosed he acted for A)
- **A's knowledge**: Full knowledge (knew terms)
- **Entire transaction**: A ratified entire sale
- **Reasonable time**: 2 days reasonable
- **Result**: Contract valid from time B sold (C bound; cannot withdraw)

### Example 2: Agent Exceeding Authority

**Facts**:
- A authorizes B to sell car for minimum Rs. 5 lakhs
- B sells to C for Rs. 4.5 lakhs (exceeded authority)
- A refuses to deliver car
- C sues

**Analysis**:
- **B exceeded authority**: Sold below minimum
- **A not bound**: No ratification; transaction outside authority (Section 235)
- **B's liability**: Personally liable to C (breach of warranty of authority)
- **C's remedy**: Sue B for damages (cannot enforce against A)

### Example 3: Secret Profit

**Facts**:
- A (principal) instructs B (agent) to buy property
- B negotiates with C (seller); obtains 10% discount
- B buys at full price (keeps 10% discount as secret profit)

**Analysis**:
- **Section 216**: Agent must account for all benefits
- **Secret profit**: 10% discount not disclosed
- **Breach**: Fiduciary duty violated
- **A's remedies**:
  - Claim 10% discount from B
  - Dismiss B (terminate agency)
  - Sue for damages
- **Result**: B must pay A the secret profit

### Example 4: Undisclosed Principal

**Facts**:
- A instructs B to buy goods from C (don't disclose A's identity)
- B buys from C (C thinks B is principal)
- A later revealed as true principal
- Goods defective; who can sue C?

**Analysis**:
- **Undisclosed principal**: C didn't know A existed
- **Section 232**: Third party can sue agent or undisclosed principal (when discovered)
- **A's right**: Can sue C (even though undisclosed)
- **B's right**: Also can sue C (as apparent principal)
- **C's right**: Elect to sue A or B (not both)

### Example 5: Irrevocable Agency

**Facts**:
- A borrows Rs. 20 lakhs from B (bank)
- A gives B power of attorney to sell A's property (if A defaults)
- Loan secured by property
- A tries to revoke power of attorney before loan repaid

**Analysis**:
- **Agency coupled with interest**: Yes (B has security interest in property)
- **Section 203**: Cannot revoke if coupled with interest
- **Result**: A cannot revoke (power of attorney irrevocable until loan repaid)

### Example 6: Del Credere Agent

**Facts**:
- A (manufacturer) appoints B (sales agent)
- B guarantees customer payments (del credere agent)
- Extra 5% commission for guarantee
- B sells Rs. 10 lakhs goods to C (customer)
- C defaults; doesn't pay

**Analysis**:
- **Del credere agent**: B guaranteed C's payment
- **Section 204**: B liable to pay A (if C defaults)
- **Result**: B must pay A Rs. 10 lakhs (then B can sue C)

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Assuming Agent Always Personally Liable

**Mistake**: Thinking agent liable to third party for contracts made on principal's behalf

**Correct**: 
- **Disclosed principal**: Agent NOT liable (principal liable)
- **Undisclosed principal**: Agent liable (third party's election)
- **Exceeded authority**: Agent liable

### 2. Delegating Without Authority

**Mistake**: Agent delegates to sub-agent without principal's consent

**Correct**: Cannot delegate unless authorized (Section 190); agent liable for unauthorized sub-agent's acts

### 3. Not Disclosing Secret Profits

**Mistake**: Agent thinks can keep commission from third party (if helps principal get good deal)

**Correct**: Must disclose ALL benefits/profits to principal (Section 216); secret profit = breach

### 4. Principal Revoking Agency Coupled with Interest

**Mistake**: Principal tries to revoke power of attorney given as security

**Correct**: Cannot revoke if coupled with interest (Section 203) unless agent consents or debt paid

### 5. Confusing Del Credere with Surety

**Mistake**: Treating del credere agent same as guarantor

**Correct**: 
- **Del credere**: Agent + guarantor (one person; one contract)
- **Guarantee**: Three parties (creditor, debtor, surety; separate contract)
