# Partnership Protocol (India)

## Protocol Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-CONTRACT-PARTNERSHIP
- **Version**: 1.0.0
- **Domain**: Contract Law (India)
- **Subdomain**: Special Contracts - Partnership
- **Statute**: Indian Partnership Act 1932 (Sections 4-73)
- **Last Updated**: January 2025
- **Effective**: Since 1932

---

## Overview

This protocol covers **partnership** - a relationship between persons who agree to share profits of a business carried on by all or any of them acting for all.

**Key Principle**: Partnership is based on mutual agency; each partner is agent of the firm and other partners for business purposes.

---

## Section 4: Partnership Defined

```
"Partnership is the relation between persons who have agreed to share the profits of a business carried on by all or any of them acting for all."
```

**Essential Elements**:
1. **Association of two or more persons**: Minimum 2 partners (maximum 50 for ordinary business, 20 for banking - Companies Act 2013)
2. **Agreement**: Express or implied contract
3. **Business**: Lawful business (trade, occupation, profession)
4. **Carried on by all or any of them acting for all**: Mutual agency
5. **Sharing of profits**: Agreement to share profits (loss sharing implied)

**Persons**: Individuals, companies, other partnerships (but NOT HUF as partner)

---

## Section 5: Partnership vs. Other Relationships

**Partnership Essentials vs. Similar Relationships**:

### NOT Partnership If:

#### 1. Co-ownership (Section 5(a))

**Joint/co-ownership** ≠ Partnership

**Mere co-ownership**: No partnership (even if share profits from property)

**Example**: A and B jointly own building; share rent → NOT partnership (no business carried on)

**But**: If actively manage and rent out multiple properties as business → May be partnership

#### 2. Sharing of Gross Returns (Section 5(b))

**Sharing gross receipts** ≠ Partnership

**Must share profits** (not just revenue)

**Example**: A and B share stage performance income equally → NOT partnership (unless they run entertainment business)

#### 3. Receipt of Share of Profits (Section 6)

**Receiving profit share** does NOT alone prove partnership

**Share of profits can be**:
- Debt payment (installments calculated from profits)
- Employee remuneration (profit-linked bonus)
- Widow/child of deceased partner receiving annuity
- Lender receiving interest varying with profits
- Previous owner receiving price by installments (calculated from profits)

**None above = Partnership** (unless other elements present)

**Example**: Employee receives 10% of profits as bonus → NOT partner (employee)

## Types of Partners

### 1. Actual/Active Partner (Section 12(a))

**Takes active part** in business management

**Liable**: To third parties (known to be partner)

### 2. Sleeping/Dormant Partner (Section 12(b))

**Does not participate** in management (contributes capital; shares profit)

**Liable**: To third parties (even if not known) from date of joining

**Retirement**: Must give public notice (Section 32) to avoid future liability

### 3. Nominal Partner (Section 12(c))

**Lends name only** (no capital contribution; no profit share usually)

**Liable**: To third parties (holding out as partner)

**Example**: Famous person lends name to business (for prestige)

### 4. Partner by Estoppel / Holding Out (Section 28)

**Represents self as partner** OR allows such representation

**Liable**: To third parties who rely on representation (even if not actually partner)

**Section 28**: "Anyone who by words or conduct represents himself, or knowingly permits himself to be represented, to be a partner in a firm, is liable as a partner to anyone who has on the faith of any such representation given credit to the firm."

**Example**: A allows business to use name on letterhead as partner (but not actually partner) → A liable to third parties who relied

### 5. Minor Partner (Section 30)

**Minor admitted to benefits** of partnership (with all partners' consent)

**Rights**: Share profits, access accounts, sue for accounts (on leaving)

**Liabilities**: 
- **NOT personally liable** for firm debts (minor's share in firm liable)
- **On majority**: Can elect within 6 months to become full partner OR leave (Section 30(5))

**If elects to continue**: Becomes full partner (liable for all debts from date admitted as minor)

**If elects to leave**: Liable only for acts when minor (not personally liable for firm debts)

## Formation of Partnership

### Partnership Agreement/Deed

**Not mandatory**: Can be oral or written

**But advisable**: Written agreement (partnership deed) to avoid disputes

**Usual Contents**:
1. Name and address of firm and partners
2. Nature of business
3. Capital contribution by each partner
4. Profit/loss sharing ratio
5. Partners' duties and powers
6. Salaries/remuneration (if any)
7. Interest on capital, drawings, loans (if any)
8. Duration (if fixed-term)
9. Accounts and audit
10. Admission, retirement, expulsion of partners
11. Dissolution terms
12. Arbitration clause (for disputes)

### Registration (Section 58-60)

**Optional** (NOT mandatory)

**Registered Firm**: Firm registered with Registrar of Firms

**Advantages of Registration**:
1. **Can sue**: Firm/partner can sue third party or other partner (Section 69)
2. **Claim set-off**: Can claim set-off in suit against firm

**Unregistered Firm Disabilities** (Section 69):
- **Cannot sue**: Firm cannot sue third party for enforcement of contract
- **Partner cannot sue**: Another partner or firm for enforcement of rights

**Can be sued**: Third party can sue unregistered firm (Section 69)

**When to Register**: Anytime during existence of firm

## Mutual Rights and Duties of Partners (Sections 9-13)

### Rights of Partners

#### 1. Right to Take Part in Business (Section 12(a))

**Every partner entitled** to participate in management (unless agreed otherwise)

**Majority decision**: Ordinary business matters decided by majority (Section 13(1))

**Unanimous decision**: Required for:
- Change in nature of business
- Admission of new partner
- Expulsion of partner
- Major alterations

#### 2. Right to Share Profits (Section 13(b))

**Default**: Equal sharing (even if capital unequal)

**Can agree**: Different profit-sharing ratio

**Loss sharing**: Same ratio as profit (Section 13(b)) unless agreed otherwise

#### 3. Right to Interest on Capital (Section 13(c))

**No interest on capital** (unless agreed)

**If agreed**: Rate and terms specified in partnership deed

#### 4. Right to Interest on Advances (Section 13(d))

**Partner advances money** (beyond agreed capital) → Entitled to **6% interest** (Section 13(d))

**From date of advance** until repayment

#### 5. Right to Indemnity (Section 13(e))

**Partner entitled to indemnity** from firm for:
- Acts done in ordinary course of business
- Acts done in emergency to protect firm from loss

**Example**: Partner pays firm's debt from own pocket (in emergency) → Firm must reimburse

#### 6. Right to Access Books (Section 12(b))

**Every partner** entitled to access and inspect partnership books

**Must be kept at principal place** of business

### Duties of Partners

#### 1. Duty to Carry On Business (Section 9)

**Business of firm** carried on by all or any partners acting for all

**Mutual agency**: Each partner is agent of firm (for business purposes)

#### 2. Duty to Act in Good Faith (Section 15-16)

**Fiduciary Duty**: Partner must act in good faith towards other partners

**Section 15**: Render true accounts; full information of all matters affecting firm

**Section 16**: Duty to indemnify for willful neglect/fraud

#### 3. Duty to Disclose (Section 15)

**Duty to render true accounts** and provide full information

**Material information**: Must disclose to other partners

#### 4. Duty to Account for Personal Profits (Section 16(a))

**Section 16(a)**: "Subject to contract between the partners, every partner must account to the firm for any benefit derived by him without the consent of the other partners from any transaction of the firm or from the use of the property or business connection of the firm or the firm name."

**Secret Profit**: Must account to firm

**Example**: Partner uses firm's name to get contract for self → Must account for profit

**Case: Ajaib Singh v. Shamsher Singh (1934)**:
- Partner obtained land allotment using firm's name
- Court held: Must transfer land to firm (profit from firm connection)

#### 5. Duty to Account for Competing Business (Section 16(b))

**Section 16(b)**: "If a partner carries on any business of the same nature as and competing with that of the firm, he must account for and pay to the firm all profits made by him in that business."

**Competing business**: Without other partners' consent

**Remedy**: Other partners can claim profits

**Example**: Partner in textile firm starts own textile business (competing) → Must pay profits to firm

#### 6. Duty Not to Assign Interest Without Consent (Section 29)

**Partner cannot transfer** his partnership interest to outsider (without other partners' consent)

**Can assign**: Right to share of profits (assignee doesn't become partner; no management rights)

## Implied Authority of Partner (Section 19)

**Section 19**: "Subject to the provisions of section 22, the act of a partner which is done to carry on, in the usual way, business of the kind carried on by the firm, binds the firm."

**Implied Authority**: Partner can bind firm by acts in ordinary course of business

**Usual Way**: Customary in that type of business

**Examples of Implied Authority**:
- Buy goods for firm
- Sell goods of firm
- Receive payment
- Settle accounts
- Employ staff
- Draw, accept, endorse negotiable instruments (if firm deals in such)

**NOT Implied Authority** (Section 19(2)):
1. Submit dispute to arbitration
2. Open bank account in firm's name (unless expressly authorized)
3. Compromise/relinquish claim
4. Withdraw suit/proceeding
5. Admit liability in suit
6. Acquire immovable property on firm's behalf
7. Transfer immovable property of firm
8. Enter partnership on firm's behalf

**Third Party Protection**: If third party believed partner had authority (reasonable belief) → Firm bound

---

## Restrictions on Partner's Authority (Section 22)

**Section 22**: Majority of partners can restrict partner's authority (if in good faith)

**Restriction binding** only if third party had notice

**Example**: Partners restrict one partner from buying above Rs. 1 lakh; third party doesn't know → Firm bound (no notice of restriction)

## Liability of Partners

### 1. Joint and Several Liability (Section 25)

**Every partner jointly and severally liable** for all acts of firm done while a partner

**Joint**: All partners liable together

**Several**: Each partner individually liable for entire debt

**Creditor can sue**:
- All partners jointly, OR
- Any partner individually for full amount

**Example**: Firm owes Rs. 10 lakhs to creditor; creditor can sue any one partner for full Rs. 10 lakhs

### 2. Liability of Incoming Partner (Section 31)

**New partner admitted** → Liable for firm acts **from date of admission** (not prior debts)

**Can agree**: To be liable for prior debts (novation with creditors)

### 3. Liability of Retiring Partner (Section 32)

**Retiring partner** → Liable for firm debts **up to date of retirement**

**After retirement**: NOT liable for new debts (if proper notice given)

**Public Notice Required** (Section 32(3)):
- Advertisement in Official Gazette, AND
- Notice to persons who dealt with firm (individually)

**If no notice**: Retiring partner liable to third parties who had no notice (Section 32(3))

**Agreement with creditors**: Retiring partner can get novation (creditors agree to release)

### 4. Liability of Deceased Partner's Estate (Section 34)

**Deceased partner's estate** → Liable for firm debts up to death

**NOT liable** for subsequent debts

### 5. Liability for Wrongful Acts (Section 26)

**Firm liable** for partner's wrongful act/omission (if in ordinary course of business)

**All partners liable** (joint and several)

**Example**: Partner commits fraud while conducting firm business → Firm and all partners liable

**Case: Hamlyn v. Houston & Co. (1903)**:
- Partner bribed clerk to get competitor's information
- Court held: Firm liable (act in course of business)

### 6. Liability for Misapplication (Section 27)

**Firm liable** if:
- Partner receives money/property from third party
- Partner misapplies it (while acting within apparent authority)

**Example**: Partner receives client's money for investment; misappropriates → Firm liable

## Partnership Property (Section 14)

**Partnership Property**: Property brought in or acquired for firm purposes

**Section 14**: "Subject to contract between the partners, the property of the firm includes all property and rights and interests in property originally brought into the stock of the firm, or acquired, by purchase or otherwise, by or for the firm, or for the purposes and in the course of the business of the firm."

**Includes**:
- Capital contributions
- Property purchased with firm money
- Goodwill of business
- Property held/used for firm business

**NOT Partnership Property**:
- Partner's personal property (even if used by firm under license)

**Co-ownership**: Partners are co-owners of partnership property (cannot transfer individual share without consent)

**On Dissolution**: Partnership property applied first to pay firm debts; surplus distributed to partners

## Dissolution of Partnership (Sections 39-55)

**Dissolution Types**:

### 1. Dissolution of Partnership (Between Partners)

**One or more partners leave/join** → Partnership changes (but firm may continue)

**Example**: 3-partner firm; 1 retires → Partnership dissolved (but 2 remaining partners can continue firm)

### 2. Dissolution of Firm

**Firm ceases to exist**; business wound up

**All partners leave** OR firm business ends

### Modes of Dissolution

#### A. Dissolution by Agreement (Section 40)

**Partners agree** to dissolve (any time)

**Partnership deed**: May specify dissolution events

#### B. Compulsory Dissolution (Section 41)

**Automatic dissolution** if:
1. **All but one partner insolvent**, OR
2. **Business becomes illegal** (all partners or carrying on business becomes unlawful)

**Example**: Partnership in gambling (gambling becomes illegal) → Firm dissolved

#### C. Dissolution on Contingency (Section 42)

**If partnership deed provides**, firm dissolves on:
- Death of partner
- Insolvency of partner
- Happening of specified event

**Example**: Deed says "firm dissolves on X's death" → X dies → Firm dissolved

**If deed silent**: Death/insolvency does NOT automatically dissolve (continuing partners can continue)

#### D. Dissolution by Notice (Section 43)

**Partnership at will**: Any partner can dissolve by giving notice

**Notice to all other partners**

**Effective**: From date in notice OR date notice communicated (if no date)

**Example**: 3 partners; no fixed term; A gives notice → Partnership dissolves from date in notice

#### E. Dissolution by Court (Section 44)

**Court can dissolve** on suit by partner if:

1. **Partner of unsound mind** (or permanently incapable)
2. **Partner guilty of misconduct** (prejudicial to business)
3. **Partner persistently breaches** agreement
4. **Partner transfers interest** (whole/part) to third party
5. **Business carried on at loss** (cannot be carried on except at loss)
6. **Court satisfied just and equitable** to dissolve

**Case: Ananth Pai v. Shanker Shenoy (1986)**:
- Loss of confidence between partners
- Court held: Just and equitable to dissolve

**Example: Misconduct**: Partner misappropriates firm funds → Court can dissolve

## Consequences of Dissolution (Sections 46-55)

### 1. Settlement of Accounts (Section 46-55)

**On dissolution**, accounts settled:

**Priority Order** (Section 48):
1. **Outside creditors** (third parties)
2. **Partner's advances** (beyond capital)
3. **Partner's capital**
4. **Surplus** (if any) → Distributed in profit-sharing ratio

**Losses**: Applied in order:
1. From **profits** (if any)
2. From **capital**
3. From **partners personally** (in profit-sharing ratio)

**Example**:
- Assets: Rs. 100 lakhs
- Outside debts: Rs. 60 lakhs
- Partner A's advance: Rs. 10 lakhs
- Capital (A: Rs. 15 lakhs, B: Rs. 15 lakhs)
- **Settlement**:
  - Pay creditors Rs. 60 lakhs
  - Repay A's advance Rs. 10 lakhs
  - Remaining Rs. 30 lakhs: Return capital (A Rs. 15 lakhs, B Rs. 15 lakhs)
  - No surplus

### 2. Authority of Partners After Dissolution (Section 46)

**Continuing authority**: Only for winding up (not new business)

**Partners can**:
- Complete pending transactions
- Realize assets
- Pay debts
- Distribute surplus

**Cannot**: Start new transactions

### 3. Rights of Partners After Dissolution (Section 46-47)

**Each partner or representative** can:
- Apply partnership property to pay debts/liabilities
- Sell goodwill (unless agreed otherwise)
- Require accounts from date of dissolution

**Section 47**: After dissolution, goodwill sold (unless agreed otherwise)

### 4. Return of Premium on Premature Dissolution (Section 51)

**Premium paid** by partner on joining (for fixed term)

**If dissolved prematurely** (not partner's fault) → Partner entitled to **refund of premium** (or proportionate part)

**Exceptions**: If dissolved due to:
- Partner's death
- Partner's misconduct
- Agreement excluding premium return

### 5. Private Partnership Property (Section 53)

**On partner's insolvency**: Partner's separate property applied:
1. First to **separate debts** (personal creditors)
2. Then to **firm debts**

**Firm property** applied:
1. First to **firm debts**
2. Then to **partner's separate debts** (if any claim)

## Limited Liability Partnership (LLP Act 2008)

**LLP**: Hybrid (partnership flexibility + company's limited liability)

**Features**:
1. **Separate legal entity** (like company)
2. **Limited liability**: Partners not personally liable (except own wrongful acts)
3. **No minimum capital**
4. **Flexible management** (like partnership)
5. **Perpetual succession** (doesn't dissolve on partner's death)

**Registration**: Mandatory (with Registrar of Companies)

**Governed by**: Limited Liability Partnership Act 2008 (NOT Partnership Act 1932)

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Partner by Holding Out

**Facts**:
- A, B, C are partners
- D (not partner) allows name on firm's letterhead as partner
- E (third party) gives credit to firm (relying on D's name)
- Firm defaults

**Analysis**:
- **Section 28**: D represented self as partner (holding out)
- **E's reliance**: E gave credit on faith of D's representation
- **Result**: D liable to E as partner (even though not actually partner)

### Example 2: Competing Business

**Facts**:
- A and B are partners in software firm
- A starts own software company (competing)
- A makes Rs. 10 lakhs profit from own company
- B discovers; demands share

**Analysis**:
- **Section 16(b)**: Partner cannot carry on competing business without consent
- **Duty to account**: A must pay Rs. 10 lakhs to firm
- **Result**: B can claim Rs. 10 lakhs profit (or proportionate share per partnership ratio)

### Example 3: Minor Electing on Majority

**Facts**:
- X (minor) admitted to benefits of partnership at age 16
- On attaining 18, X continues for 8 months (doesn't elect)
- X then wants to leave; claims not liable for firm debts

**Analysis**:
- **Section 30(5)**: Minor must elect within 6 months of majority
- **8 months passed**: No election within 6 months
- **Deemed election**: Deemed to have elected to continue (become full partner)
- **Result**: X is full partner; liable for all firm debts (including during minority period)

### Example 4: Retiring Partner Liability

**Facts**:
- A, B, C are partners
- A retires on June 1
- A gives notice in Gazette but not to individual customers
- On July 1, firm (B, C) borrows Rs. 5 lakhs from D (old customer)
- D had no notice of A's retirement
- Firm defaults

**Analysis**:
- **Section 32(3)**: Retiring partner liable unless notice given
- **A gave Gazette notice**: Yes (but not enough for old customers)
- **D (old customer)**: No individual notice
- **Result**: A liable to D (no proper notice; D didn't know A retired)

### Example 5: Dissolution Settlement

**Facts**:
- Partnership dissolved
- Assets: Rs. 80 lakhs
- Liabilities: Outside debts Rs. 50 lakhs
- Partner A's advance: Rs. 15 lakhs (beyond capital)
- Capital: A Rs. 10 lakhs, B Rs. 10 lakhs
- Profit ratio: A:B = 60:40

**Analysis (Section 48)**:
1. **Pay outside creditors**: Rs. 50 lakhs
2. **Remaining**: Rs. 30 lakhs
3. **Repay A's advance**: Rs. 15 lakhs
4. **Remaining**: Rs. 15 lakhs
5. **Return capital**: A Rs. 10 lakhs, B Rs. 10 lakhs = Rs. 20 lakhs
6. **Shortfall**: Rs. 5 lakhs (not enough for full capital)
7. **Loss of Rs. 5 lakhs** shared in profit ratio (60:40):
   - A loses Rs. 3 lakhs (60%)
   - B loses Rs. 2 lakhs (40%)
8. **Final distribution**:
   - A gets: Rs. 15 lakhs (advance) + Rs. 7 lakhs (Rs. 10L capital - Rs. 3L loss) = Rs. 22 lakhs
   - B gets: Rs. 8 lakhs (Rs. 10L capital - Rs. 2L loss)

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Assuming Profit Sharing = Partnership

**Mistake**: Thinking anyone receiving profit share is partner

**Correct**: Profit sharing alone doesn't prove partnership (Section 6); must have all essential elements (agreement, business, mutual agency)

### 2. Not Giving Notice on Retirement

**Mistake**: Retiring partner thinks retirement ends liability (without notice)

**Correct**: Must give public notice (Gazette + individual to known parties) to avoid future liability (Section 32)

### 3. Expelling Partner Without Deed Provision

**Mistake**: Majority partners expel one partner (no provision in deed)

**Correct**: Cannot expel unless partnership deed expressly permits (must be in good faith + bona fide)

### 4. Minor Thinking Can Become Partner

**Mistake**: Admitting minor as full partner

**Correct**: Minor can only be admitted to benefits (not full partner - Section 30); no personal liability

### 5. Unregistered Firm Trying to Sue

**Mistake**: Unregistered firm sues third party for contract enforcement

**Correct**: Unregistered firm cannot sue (Section 69); must register first (though can be sued by third parties)
