## Overview

This protocol covers **damages** for breach of contract - the monetary compensation awarded to the innocent party for loss suffered due to breach.

**Key Principle**: Damages aim to compensate, not punish. The goal is to put the innocent party in the position they would have been in had the contract been performed.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 73: Compensation for Loss or Damage

```
"When a contract has been broken, the party who suffers by such breach is entitled to receive, from the party who has broken the contract, compensation for any loss or damage caused to him thereby, which naturally arose in the usual course of things from such breach, or which the parties knew, when they made the contract, to be likely to result from the breach of it.

Such compensation is not to be given for any remote and indirect loss or damage sustained by reason of the breach.

When a contract has been broken, the party complaining of the breach must take reasonable steps to mitigate the loss consequent on the breach."
```

**Key Elements**:
1. **Compensation** for loss/damage
2. **Natural consequences** OR **contemplated consequences**
3. **NOT remote/indirect** damages
4. **Duty to mitigate** losses

### Section 74: Liquidated Damages and Penalty

```
"When a contract has been broken, if a sum is named in the contract as the amount to be paid in case of such breach, or if the contract contains any other stipulation by way of penalty, the party complaining of the breach is entitled, whether or not actual damage or loss is proved to have been caused thereby, to receive from the party who has broken the contract reasonable compensation not exceeding the amount so named or, as the case may be, the penalty stipulated for."
```

**Liquidated damages clause** = Ceiling on compensation

### Section 75: Compensation for Breach of Contract to Indemnify

```
"A person who rightfully rescinds a contract is entitled to compensation for any damage which he has sustained through the non-fulfillment of the contract."
```

**Rescission + Damages** available

## Types of Damages

### 1. Compensatory Damages (General Damages)

**Purpose**: Compensate for actual loss suffered

**Rule**: Put innocent party in position they would have been if contract performed

**Measure**: Difference between contract value and actual position

**Example**: Contract to deliver goods worth Rs. 10 lakhs; seller breaches; buyer buys from market for Rs. 12 lakhs
- **Damages**: Rs. 2 lakhs (additional cost incurred)

### 2. Nominal Damages

**Purpose**: Vindicate legal right (when breach proved but no actual loss)

**Award**: Token amount (Rs. 1 or nominal sum)

**Example**: Seller breaches delivery contract; buyer already bought same goods elsewhere at same price (no actual loss)
- **Damages**: Nominal (legal right vindicated)

**Indian Position**: Generally awarded in breach cases even if no actual loss

### 3. Special / Consequential Damages

**Purpose**: Compensate for foreseeable consequential losses

**Section 73**: "...or which the parties knew, when they made the contract, to be likely to result from the breach..."

**Requirement**: Loss must be in **contemplation of parties** at time of contract

**Example**: Buyer informs seller that goods needed for specific contract; seller breaches
- **Special damages**: Loss from buyer's specific contract (seller knew purpose)

**See**: Hadley v. Baxendale rule (below)

### 4. Reliance Damages / Wasted Expenditure

**Purpose**: Reimburse expenses incurred in reliance on contract

**Example**: Contract to supply machines; buyer spent Rs. 1 lakh preparing factory; seller breaches
- **Reliance damages**: Rs. 1 lakh (wasted preparation costs)

**Alternative to Expectation Damages**: When expectation loss difficult to prove

### 5. Liquidated Damages

**Purpose**: Pre-agreed compensation amount

**Section 74**: Amount named in contract as compensation for breach

**Effect**: Ceiling (court awards reasonable compensation not exceeding stipulated amount)

**See**: Liquidated Damages vs Penalty (below)

## Measure of Damages: Hadley v. Baxendale Rule

**Landmark Case: Hadley v. Baxendale (1854)** (English case, incorporated in Section 73)

**Facts**:
- Mill owner's crankshaft broke; mill stopped
- Owner sent crankshaft to carrier for delivery to repair shop
- Carrier delayed delivery
- Mill remained idle (loss of profit)

**Court Held**: Loss of profit NOT recoverable

**Reasoning**: Carrier didn't know crankshaft was only one; mill would stop entirely

**Two-Limb Test** for Recoverable Damages:

#### Limb 1: Natural Consequences

**Damages "which naturally arose in the usual course of things from such breach"**

**Test**: Would reasonable person foresee this loss as probable consequence?

**Example**: Seller fails to deliver goods
- **Natural consequence**: Buyer's loss from inability to fulfill resale contract (foreseeable)

#### Limb 2: Special Damages (Contemplated)

**Damages "which the parties knew, when they made the contract, to be likely to result from the breach"**

**Test**: Did both parties have **actual knowledge** of special circumstances?

**Example**: Buyer tells seller "I need goods urgently for government contract worth Rs. 1 crore"
- Seller knows buyer will lose Rs. 1 crore if breach
- **Special damages**: Loss from government contract (within contemplation)

**Indian Application**:

**Case: Murlidhar Chiranjilal v. Harishchandra (1962)** (Supreme Court):
- Contract to supply jaggery for resale
- Supplier breached
- Court held: Loss of profit from resale recoverable (naturally foreseeable)

## Remoteness of Damages

**Section 73 Proviso**: "Such compensation is not to be given for any remote and indirect loss or damage sustained by reason of the breach."

**Remote Damages**: Loss too far removed from breach (indirect consequence)

**NOT Recoverable**: If loss not reasonably foreseeable or not in contemplation

**Proximate Cause Test**: Loss must flow directly from breach

**Case: Victoria Laundry (Windsor) Ltd. v. Newman Industries Ltd. (1949)**:
- Contract to sell boiler to laundry; delivery delayed
- Laundry claimed: (1) Normal loss of profit, (2) Loss of lucrative dyeing contract with government
- Court held:
  - (1) **Recoverable** (foreseeable - laundry needs boiler to operate)
  - (2) **NOT recoverable** (seller didn't know about special government contract)

## Duty to Mitigate (Section 73 Proviso)

**Section 73**: "...the party complaining of the breach must take reasonable steps to mitigate the loss consequent on the breach."

**Rule**: Innocent party must minimize loss after breach

### What is Reasonable Mitigation?

**Reasonable Steps**:
- Finding alternative supplier
- Re-letting property (if tenant breaches)
- Selling goods at best available price
- Accepting substitute performance (if reasonable)

**NOT Required**:
- Unreasonable expense or risk
- Substantially different performance
- Litigation or complicated action

**Example**: Seller breaches; buyer must buy from alternative source (reasonable)
- Buyer cannot sit idle and claim damages for extended period

**Case: British Westinghouse Electric v. Underground Electric Railways (1912)**:
- Defective turbines supplied
- Buyer replaced with more efficient turbines
- New turbines saved more than cost
- Court held: Savings offset damages (duty to mitigate)

**Indian Application**:

**Case: Sohanlal v. Charan Das (1970)**:
- Buyer breached property purchase; seller kept property idle
- Court held: Seller should have sold property to mitigate loss

## Liquidated Damages vs Penalty (Section 74)

### Distinction

**Liquidated Damages**: Genuine pre-estimate of loss

**Penalty**: Amount intended to compel performance (punitive; not genuine estimate)

### English Law Position

**Penalty**: Unenforceable; only liquidated damages enforceable

**Test (Dunlop v. Selfridge)**: Is amount genuine pre-estimate? OR Extravagant sum (in terrorem)?

### Indian Law Position (Section 74)

**Both Liquidated Damages and Penalty Enforceable** - But as ceiling

**Section 74**: Court awards **reasonable compensation** not exceeding stipulated amount

**Effect**:
- Stipulated amount = Ceiling
- Court can award less (if reasonable compensation less than stipulated)
- Court can award full amount (if reasonable compensation equals or exceeds stipulated)

**Difference from English Law**:
- **English**: Penalty not enforceable at all
- **Indian**: Penalty enforceable as ceiling

**Case: Fateh Chand v. Balkishan Dass (1963)** (Supreme Court):
- Contract stipulated Rs. 200/day liquidated damages for delay
- Actual loss minimal
- Court held: Award reasonable compensation (can be less than stipulated)

**Case: Oil and Natural Gas Corporation v. Saw Pipes (2003)** (Supreme Court):
- Large liquidated damages clause in contract
- Court held: Section 74 treats liquidated damages and penalty alike; court awards reasonable compensation up to stipulated ceiling

**Case: Kailash Nath Associates v. DDA (2015)** (Supreme Court):
- Construction contract; liquidated damages for delay
- Supreme Court clarified: If liquidated damages are genuine pre-estimate, court should generally award stipulated amount (not reduce arbitrarily)
- Burden on party claiming less to show stipulated amount unreasonable

### Proof of Actual Loss (Section 74 Proviso)

**Section 74**: "...whether or not actual damage or loss is proved to have been caused thereby..."

**Rule**: Need NOT prove actual loss if liquidated damages clause exists

**Benefit**: Saves innocent party from proving exact quantum of loss

**Example**: Delay in construction; liquidated damages Rs. 10,000/day
- Innocent party need not prove exact Rs. 10,000 loss per day
- Court awards reasonable compensation up to Rs. 10,000/day

## Damages in Specific Contracts

### A. Sale of Goods Act 1930 (Sections 55-61)

**Buyer's Damages** (Seller's Breach):

**Section 57**: Non-delivery by seller
- **Measure**: Difference between contract price and market price (at time of breach)
- **Example**: Contract price Rs. 1 lakh; market price Rs. 1.2 lakh → Damages Rs. 20,000

**Section 58**: Breach of warranty by seller
- **Measure**: Difference between value as warranted and actual value
- **Plus**: Consequential damages (if foreseeable)

**Seller's Damages** (Buyer's Breach):

**Section 61**: Non-acceptance by buyer
- **Measure**: Difference between contract price and market price (or resale price)
- **Example**: Contract price Rs. 1 lakh; seller sells in market for Rs. 80,000 → Damages Rs. 20,000

### B. Specific Performance Contracts (Specific Relief Act 1963)

**Alternative Remedy**: Specific performance (actual performance) instead of damages

**When Damages Inadequate**:
- Sale of unique property (land, artwork)
- Specific goods with special value

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-SPECIFIC-PERFORMANCE protocol

### C. Indemnity Contracts (Section 124-125)

**Section 125**: Indemnity-holder can recover:
- Damages paid in suit
- Costs incurred
- Sums paid under compromise

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-INDEMNITY-GUARANTEE protocol

## Damages NOT Recoverable

### 1. Remote / Indirect Damages

**Example**: Seller breaches delivery; buyer misses meeting and loses job (too remote)

### 2. Speculative / Uncertain Damages

**Example**: Claim for "potential future profits" with no basis

### 3. Damages Due to Failure to Mitigate

**Example**: Buyer doesn't buy from alternative source; claims extended losses

### 4. Punitive / Exemplary Damages

**General Rule**: Punitive damages NOT awarded in contract breach (only compensatory)

**Exception**: Tort damages (fraud, deceit) may include punitive component

**Indian Position**: Generally no punitive damages for pure contract breach

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Hadley v. Baxendale Application

**Facts**:
- A contracts to supply machine part to B for Rs. 50,000 (delivery Jan 1)
- A breaches
- B's factory stops; loses Rs. 5 lakhs profit

**Analysis**:
- **Direct loss**: Cost of buying from market (if higher than Rs. 50,000) → Recoverable
- **Loss of profit**: Foreseeable? Did A know factory would stop?
  - If YES (A knew) → Recoverable (special damages)
  - If NO (A didn't know) → NOT recoverable (remote)

### Example 2: Duty to Mitigate

**Facts**:
- A leases office to B for Rs. 1 lakh/month (1 year)
- B breaches after 3 months
- A could re-let for Rs. 90,000/month but doesn't
- A sues for Rs. 9 lakhs (9 months × Rs. 1 lakh)

**Analysis**:
- **Duty to mitigate**: A should re-let
- **Reasonable mitigation**: Accept Rs. 90,000 tenant
- **Damages**: Rs. 10,000/month × 9 = Rs. 90,000 + reasonable vacancy period

### Example 3: Liquidated Damages

**Facts**:
- Construction contract: Rs. 50 lakhs; completion June 1
- Liquidated damages: Rs. 10,000/day delay
- Contractor completes Aug 1 (60 days late)
- Actual loss: Rs. 3 lakhs

**Analysis**:
- **Stipulated amount**: Rs. 10,000 × 60 = Rs. 6 lakhs
- **Section 74**: Court awards reasonable compensation (not exceeding Rs. 6 lakhs)
- **Likely award**: Rs. 3 lakhs (actual loss) to Rs. 6 lakhs (depending on whether genuine pre-estimate)

### Example 4: Sale of Goods (Non-Delivery)

**Facts**:
- Contract to buy 100 units at Rs. 1,000/unit (total Rs. 1 lakh)
- Seller doesn't deliver
- Market price at breach: Rs. 1,200/unit

**Analysis**:
- **Section 57 Sale of Goods Act**: Difference between contract and market price
- **Damages**: (Rs. 1,200 - Rs. 1,000) × 100 = Rs. 20,000

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Expecting Profit as Damages (When Remote)

**Mistake**: Claiming lost profits without proving foreseeability

**Correct**: Lost profits recoverable only if foreseeable or in parties' contemplation

### 2. Failing to Mitigate

**Mistake**: Sitting idle after breach, letting losses accumulate

**Correct**: Take reasonable steps to minimize loss

### 3. Confusing Penalty with Liquidated Damages

**Mistake** (English law mindset): Thinking penalty unenforceable

**Correct** (Indian law): Both enforceable as ceiling under Section 74

### 4. Claiming Punitive Damages

**Mistake**: Requesting punitive damages for contract breach

**Correct**: Contract damages are compensatory (not punitive)

### 5. Remote Damages

**Mistake**: Claiming all losses (even indirectly connected)

**Correct**: Only proximate, foreseeable damages recoverable
