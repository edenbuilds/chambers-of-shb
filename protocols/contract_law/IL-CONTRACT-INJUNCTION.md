## Overview

This protocol covers **injunctions** - court orders restraining a party from doing (prohibitory injunction) or compelling a party to do (mandatory injunction) a specific act, typically to prevent breach of contract or continuing wrong.

**Key Principle**: Injunction is equitable remedy to prevent irreparable harm where damages are inadequate.

---

## Types of Injunctions

### 1. Temporary / Interim Injunction (Order 39 Rule 1-2 CPC)

**Purpose**: Preserve status quo during pendency of suit

**Duration**: Until final judgment

**Example**: Preventing defendant from selling disputed property during lawsuit

### 2. Perpetual / Permanent Injunction (Sections 36-42 Specific Relief Act)

**Purpose**: Final relief after trial

**Duration**: Permanent (enforceable indefinitely)

**Example**: Permanently restraining defendant from breaching non-compete clause

### 3. Prohibitory Injunction

**Restrains**: Prevents party from doing an act

**Example**: "Defendant restrained from operating competing business within 5 km"

### 4. Mandatory Injunction

**Compels**: Requires party to do an act (undo wrong)

**Example**: "Defendant directed to remove encroachment from plaintiff's land"

**Note**: Mandatory injunction granted sparingly (more intrusive)

## Statutory Framework

### Section 36: Preventive Relief - When Injunction Granted

```
"(1) A perpetual injunction may be granted to the plaintiff to prevent the breach of an obligation existing in his favour, whether expressly or by implication.

(2) When such obligation arises from contract, the court shall be guided by the rules and provisions contained in Chapter II."
```

**Conditions**:
1. **Obligation** exists in plaintiff's favor
2. **Breach** of obligation (actual or threatened)
3. **Damages inadequate** remedy

### Section 37: Injunction When Refused

**Section 37**: Injunction cannot be granted:

#### (a) To Restrain Judicial/Legislative Proceedings

Cannot prevent court proceedings or legislative acts

**Example**: Cannot restrain someone from filing suit

#### (b) To Restrain Criminal Proceedings

Cannot prevent prosecution for criminal offense

**Exception**: Can restrain if prosecution malicious/vexatious AND no adequate remedy

#### (c) When Equally Efficacious Relief Possible

If plaintiff can get adequate remedy otherwise → Injunction refused

**Example**: If damages sufficient → Injunction not needed

#### (d) Breach of Contract Not Specifically Enforceable

**If specific performance not available** (e.g., personal service) → Injunction for positive covenant also not available

**But**: Negative covenant can be enforced by injunction (see below)

#### (e) Multiplicity of Proceedings

If injunction would cause multiple/complex legal proceedings → May be refused

#### (f) Against Government for Non-Performance of Contract

Generally cannot enjoin government from breaching contract (sovereign immunity)

### Section 38: Damages in Addition to Injunction

**Section 38(1)**: "The plaintiff in a suit for perpetual injunction may claim damages, either in addition to, or in substitution for, such injunction."

**Options**:
1. Injunction + Damages
2. Damages instead of injunction (if injunction refused)

## Injunctions in Contract Law

### A. Negative Covenants (Section 42)

**Section 42**: "Where a contract comprises an affirmative agreement to do a certain act, coupled with a negative agreement, express or implied, not to do a certain act, the circumstance that the court is unable to compel specific performance of the affirmative agreement shall not preclude it from granting an injunction to perform the negative agreement."

**Rule**: **Negative covenant** enforceable by injunction **even if positive covenant not specifically enforceable**

**Landmark Case: Lumley v. Wagner (1852)** (English case, persuasive in India):
- Opera singer contracted to sing at plaintiff's theater
- Negative covenant: "Will not sing at other theaters during contract period"
- Singer breached; agreed to sing at competitor's theater
- Court held: Cannot compel singer to sing (personal service), BUT can grant injunction restraining from singing elsewhere

**Application**:
- **Positive covenant**: "Employee will work for employer" → NOT specifically enforceable (personal service)
- **Negative covenant**: "Employee will not work for competitor" → Enforceable by injunction

**Example**: Employment contract with non-compete clause
- Cannot force employee to work (specific performance not available)
- Can restrain employee from working for competitor (injunction available)

**Case: Niranjan Shankar Golikari v. Century Spinning (1967)** (Supreme Court):
- Post-employment non-compete clause (5 years)
- Court held: Unreasonable restraint (Section 27 Contract Act); injunction refused
- **But**: Reasonable restraint during employment → Injunction possible

**Reasonable Negative Covenants**:
- Non-disclosure of trade secrets
- Non-solicitation of clients (reasonable period)
- Non-compete during employment

**Unreasonable Negative Covenants**:
- Permanent non-compete
- Geographically unlimited restraint
- Post-employment non-compete (generally; Section 27)

### B. Breach of Confidence / Trade Secrets

**Example**: Employee about to disclose confidential information
- Injunction to prevent disclosure (protect trade secrets)

**Requirements**:
1. Information confidential
2. Obligation of confidence exists
3. Threatened breach
4. Damages inadequate

### C. Breach of Exclusive Dealing Agreements

**Example**: Distributor agrees to sell only plaintiff's products; starts selling competitor's
- Injunction to restrain selling competitor's products

## Temporary Injunction (Order 39 Rule 1-2 CPC)

**Purpose**: Interim relief during pendency of suit

### Order 39 Rule 1: When Temporary Injunction Granted

```
"Where in any suit it is proved by affidavit or otherwise—

(a) that any property in dispute in a suit is in danger of being wasted, damaged or alienated by any party to the suit, or wrongfully sold in execution of a decree, or
(b) that the defendant threatens, or intends to remove or dispose of his property with a view to defrauding his creditors, or
(c) that the defendant threatens to dispossess the plaintiff or otherwise cause injury to the plaintiff in relation to any property in dispute in the suit,

the Court may... grant a temporary injunction to restrain such act, or make such other order for the purpose of staying and preventing the wasting, damaging, alienation, sale, removal or disposition of the property or dispossession of the plaintiff, or otherwise causing injury to the plaintiff in relation to any property in dispute in the suit..."
```

**Conditions**:
1. **Prima facie case**: Plaintiff shows arguable case
2. **Balance of convenience**: In plaintiff's favor
3. **Irreparable injury**: Plaintiff will suffer irreparable harm if injunction not granted

**Test (Three-Fold)**:

#### 1. Prima Facie Case

**Question**: Does plaintiff show serious question to be tried?

**Standard**: Not conclusive proof; just arguable case

**Example**: Plaintiff shows contract exists and breach threatened

#### 2. Balance of Convenience

**Question**: Would plaintiff or defendant suffer greater hardship?

**Balancing**:
- Hardship to plaintiff if injunction NOT granted
- vs Hardship to defendant if injunction granted

**Example**: If plaintiff's business destroyed vs defendant's minor inconvenience → Balance favors plaintiff

#### 3. Irreparable Injury

**Question**: Can damages adequately compensate plaintiff?

**Irreparable**: Cannot be compensated by money

**Examples**:
- Loss of goodwill
- Disclosure of trade secrets
- Destruction of unique property

**Case: Dorab Cawasji Warden v. Coomi Sorab Warden (1990)** (Supreme Court):
- Temporary injunction application
- Court held: Must satisfy all three conditions (prima facie case, balance of convenience, irreparable injury)

## Quia Timet Injunction (Anticipatory)

**Quia Timet**: "Because he fears"

**Preventive Injunction**: To prevent anticipated future wrong

**Requirements**:
1. **Strong probability** of future injury (not mere speculation)
2. **Imminent** danger
3. **Irreparable** harm if not prevented

**Example**: Neighbor building structure that will cause damage to plaintiff's property
- Injunction to prevent construction (before actual damage occurs)

**Case: Redland Bricks Ltd. v. Morris (1970)**:
- Defendant's excavation threatened plaintiff's land (subsidence risk)
- Court held: Quia timet injunction granted (prevent future damage)

## Mandatory Injunction

**Compels Positive Act**: Requires defendant to do something (undo wrong)

**Standard**: **Higher** than prohibitory injunction (more intrusive)

**When Granted**:
1. **Clear legal right** violated
2. **Defendant caused wrongful change** in status quo
3. **Restoration necessary** (cannot be compensated by damages)

**Example**: Defendant encroached on plaintiff's land
- Mandatory injunction to remove encroachment

**Case: Mahadeolal Kanodia v. Administrator General of West Bengal (1960)**:
- Defendant wrongfully occupied plaintiff's property
- Court held: Mandatory injunction to vacate (restore status quo ante)

**Contrast**: Prohibitory injunction preserves existing state; mandatory injunction restores previous state

## Defenses to Injunction

### 1. Plaintiff's Delay / Laches

**Unreasonable Delay**: Plaintiff delayed in seeking relief

**Example**: Breach in 2020; plaintiff seeks injunction in 2024 → May be refused (laches)

**Acquiescence**: Plaintiff silently permitted act → Cannot later object

### 2. Clean Hands Doctrine

**"He who seeks equity must do equity"**

**Plaintiff's misconduct**: If plaintiff breached or acted inequitably → Injunction refused

**Example**: Plaintiff also breached contract → May be estopped from seeking injunction

### 3. Impossibility / Hardship

**Extreme hardship** to defendant with minimal benefit to plaintiff → May be refused

**Balance**: Court weighs hardship to both parties

### 4. Public Interest

**If injunction against public interest** → Refused

**Example**: Injunction would shut down factory (thousands unemployed) for minor breach → May be refused

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Non-Compete Clause (Negative Covenant)

**Facts**:
- A (employee) signs employment contract with non-compete clause: "Will not work for competitor for 1 year after leaving"
- A resigns; joins competitor immediately

**Analysis**:
- **Negative covenant**: Section 42
- **Personal service**: Cannot compel A to work (specific performance unavailable)
- **But**: Can restrain A from working for competitor (injunction possible if reasonable)
- **Reasonableness**: 1 year, competitor only → Likely reasonable during employment; post-employment may violate Section 27
- **Result**: Injunction likely REFUSED (post-employment non-compete generally void in India under Section 27 Contract Act)

### Example 2: Trade Secret Protection

**Facts**:
- A (employee) has access to company's secret formula
- A about to join competitor; threatens to disclose formula

**Analysis**:
- **Confidential information**: Trade secret
- **Obligation**: Duty of confidence exists
- **Irreparable harm**: Disclosure cannot be undone; damages inadequate
- **Prima facie case**: Yes
- **Result**: Temporary injunction granted (prevent disclosure)

### Example 3: Exclusive Distribution Agreement

**Facts**:
- A (distributor) agrees to sell only B's (manufacturer) products in territory
- A starts selling competitor C's products

**Analysis**:
- **Negative covenant**: "Will not sell competitor's products"
- **Breach**: A selling C's products
- **Section 42**: Negative covenant enforceable
- **Result**: Injunction granted (restrain A from selling C's products)

### Example 4: Mandatory Injunction (Encroachment)

**Facts**:
- A owns land; B encroaches and builds shed on A's land
- A seeks injunction

**Analysis**:
- **Wrongful change**: B encroached (changed status quo)
- **Clear right**: A's ownership
- **Mandatory injunction**: To remove encroachment (restore status quo)
- **Result**: Mandatory injunction granted (B must remove shed)

### Example 5: Temporary Injunction (Property Sale)

**Facts**:
- A sues B for specific performance of land sale
- B about to sell land to C (third party)
- A seeks temporary injunction

**Analysis**:
- **Prima facie case**: Land sale contract exists
- **Irreparable injury**: If sold to C, A cannot get specific land
- **Balance of convenience**: Favors A (B can sell after trial if A loses)
- **Result**: Temporary injunction granted (B restrained from selling pending trial)

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Seeking Injunction for Personal Service

**Mistake**: Trying to compel employee to work via injunction

**Correct**: Cannot compel personal service (positive covenant); only negative covenant (non-compete, confidentiality) enforceable

### 2. Post-Employment Non-Compete in India

**Mistake**: Assuming post-employment non-compete enforceable

**Correct**: Generally VOID under Section 27 (Niranjan Shankar case); only reasonable restraints during employment valid

### 3. Delay in Seeking Injunction

**Mistake**: Waiting months/years before seeking injunction

**Correct**: Apply promptly; delay/laches → Injunction refused

### 4. Assuming Injunction Always Available

**Mistake**: Expecting injunction for all breaches

**Correct**: Only when damages inadequate; irreparable harm shown

### 5. Mandatory vs Prohibitory

**Mistake**: Treating mandatory injunction same as prohibitory

**Correct**: Mandatory injunction harder to get (more intrusive); requires clear legal right
