# Free Consent Protocol (India)

## Protocol Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-CONTRACT-FREE-CONSENT
- **Version**: 1.0.0
- **Domain**: Contract Law (India)
- **Subdomain**: Contract Formation - Free Consent
- **Statute**: Indian Contract Act 1872 (Sections 13-22)
- **Last Updated**: January 2025
- **Effective**: Since 1872

---

## Overview

This protocol covers **free consent** - an essential element of valid contracts. Consent obtained through coercion, undue influence, fraud, misrepresentation, or mistake is NOT free consent, making the contract **voidable** or **void**.

**Key Principle**: Consent must be genuine and voluntary for a valid contract.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 10: Free Consent Required

```
"All agreements are contracts if they are made by the free consent of parties..."
```

**Free Consent** is ESSENTIAL element

### Section 13: Definition of Consent

```
"Two or more persons are said to consent when they agree upon the same thing in the same sense."
```

**Consensus ad idem**: Meeting of minds (agreement on same thing in same sense)

### Section 14: What is Free Consent

```
"Consent is said to be free when it is not caused by—
(1) coercion, as defined in Section 15, or
(2) undue influence, as defined in Section 16, or
(3) fraud, as defined in Section 17, or
(4) misrepresentation, as defined in Section 18, or
(5) mistake, subject to the provisions of Sections 20, 21 and 22."
```

**Five Vitiating Factors**:
1. Coercion
2. Undue influence
3. Fraud
4. Misrepresentation
5. Mistake

**Effect**: If consent obtained by any of these → Contract VOIDABLE (can be cancelled by aggrieved party)

## 1. Coercion (Section 15)

### Definition

**Section 15**: "Coercion is the committing, or threatening to commit, any act forbidden by the Indian Penal Code, or the unlawful detaining, or threatening to detain, any property, to the prejudice of any person whatever, with the intention of causing any person to enter into an agreement."

**Elements**:
1. **Committing or threatening** to commit an act forbidden by IPC
2. **OR**: Unlawful detaining or threatening to detain property
3. **Intention**: To cause person to enter into agreement
4. **Prejudice**: To any person whatever (not necessarily party to contract)

### Essential Characteristics

#### 1. Act Forbidden by IPC (Criminal Act)

**Examples of Coercion**:
- **Threat of violence**: "Sign this contract or I'll kill you"
- **Threat of criminal prosecution**: "Sign or I'll file false police complaint"
- **Threat of harm to family**: "Sign or I'll hurt your child"
- **Kidnapping**: Detaining person until they sign
- **Assault**: Forcing person to sign under physical force

**NOT Coercion**:
- **Threat of civil suit**: "Sign or I'll sue you" (NOT coercion; legitimate right)
- **Economic pressure**: "Sign or I won't do business with you" (NOT coercion unless threat to do illegal act)

**Case: Chikham Amiraju v. Chikham Seshamma (1918)**:
- Threat to commit suicide unless property transferred
- Court held: Coercion (attempt to suicide was crime under IPC 309 at that time)
- Note: IPC 309 now repealed; attempt to suicide decriminalized; this case may not apply today

#### 2. Unlawful Detention of Property

**Example**: A wrongfully seizes B's goods; refuses to return unless B signs contract → Coercion

**Lawful Detention**: NOT coercion
- Creditor retaining debtor's property under lien (lawful) → NOT coercion

#### 3. Prejudice to Any Person Whatever

**Unique Feature**: Prejudice need not be to party to contract

**Example**: A threatens to harm B's son C unless B signs contract with A → Coercion (prejudice to C)

**Section 15 Explanation**: "It is immaterial whether the Indian Penal Code is or is not in force in the place where the coercion is employed."

#### 4. Intention to Cause Person to Enter Agreement

**Causal Connection**: Coercion must induce consent

**Burden**: Party alleging coercion must prove:
1. Coercive act occurred
2. Consent given because of coercion (would not have consented otherwise)

### Effect of Coercion

**Section 19**: Contract voidable at option of party whose consent obtained by coercion

**Options for Aggrieved Party**:
1. **Rescind (cancel) contract**: Avoid contract and demand return of any benefit
2. **Affirm contract**: Choose to continue with contract

**Section 19A**: Contract induced by coercion voidable; party not bound to perform

### Burden of Proof

**General Rule**: Party alleging coercion must prove coercion

**Exception: Unconscionable Bargain**: If contract grossly unfair + relationship of dominance → Burden shifts to dominant party to prove no coercion/undue influence

## 2. Undue Influence (Section 16)

### Definition

**Section 16(1)**: "A contract is said to be induced by 'undue influence' where the relations subsisting between the parties are such that one of the parties is in a position to dominate the will of the other and uses that position to obtain an unfair advantage over the other."

**Elements**:
1. **Dominant position**: One party can dominate will of other
2. **Use of dominant position**: To obtain unfair advantage

### When Does Dominant Position Exist?

**Section 16(2)**: Dominant position exists where:

#### (a) Real or Apparent Authority

**Examples**:
- **Employer-employee**
- **Government official-citizen**
- **Police officer-accused**

**Case: Ranganayakamma v. Alwar Setti (1889)**:
- Village headman (authority figure) obtained sale deed
- Court held: Dominant position exists (undue influence presumed)

#### (b) Fiduciary Relationship

**Fiduciary**: Relationship of trust and confidence

**Examples**:
- **Doctor-patient**
- **Lawyer-client**
- **Guardian-ward**
- **Trustee-beneficiary**
- **Spiritual advisor-devotee**

**Case: Inche Noriah v. Shaik Allie Bin Omar (1929)** (Privy Council):
- Old illiterate woman gifted property to nephew (her advisor)
- Court held: Fiduciary relationship; gift set aside (undue influence presumed)

#### (c) Mental Capacity Diminished

**Weakened mental state**: Temporary or permanent

**Examples**:
- **Old age with senility**
- **Illness affecting judgment**
- **Grief/distress** (e.g., death of loved one)

**Case: Raghunath Prasad v. Sarju Prasad (1924)**:
- Pardanashin woman (in purdah, secluded)
- Sold property without independent advice
- Court held: Weakened state (seclusion); undue influence presumed

### Presumption of Undue Influence (Section 16(3))

**Section 16(3)**: "Where a person who is in a position to dominate the will of another, enters into a contract with him, and the transaction appears, on the face of it or on the evidence adduced, to be unconscionable, the burden of proving that such contract was not induced by undue influence shall lie upon the person in a position to dominate the will of the other."

**When Presumption Arises**:
1. **Dominant position** exists (Section 16(2) relationships)
2. **Transaction unconscionable** (grossly unfair)

**Burden Shifts**: Dominant party must prove NO undue influence

**Unconscionable Transaction**: Unfair, one-sided, against natural justice

**Examples**:
- Property worth Rs. 50 lakhs sold for Rs. 5 lakhs
- Gift of entire property by elderly patient to doctor
- Transfer of property by client to lawyer for no consideration

**Case: Mannu Singh v. Umadat Pandey (1890)**:
- Zamindar (landlord) sold property to money-lender for inadequate price
- Money-lender in dominant position
- Court held: Undue influence presumed; money-lender failed to rebut

### Undue Influence vs Coercion

| Aspect | Coercion | Undue Influence |
|--------|----------|-----------------|
| **Nature** | Physical force/threat | Mental/moral pressure |
| **Relationship** | Need not exist | Usually exists (dominant-subservient) |
| **Act** | Criminal act (IPC) | Use of dominant position (not criminal) |
| **Burden** | On party alleging coercion | Shifts to dominant party if transaction unconscionable |
| **Example** | "Sign or I'll shoot you" | Doctor persuading patient to gift property |

### Effect of Undue Influence

**Section 19A**: Contract induced by undue influence voidable at option of aggrieved party

**Section 19A Proviso**: Court has discretion:
- **Set aside contract** entirely, OR
- **Uphold contract** with modified terms (rescind unfair clauses, adjust consideration)

**Example**: Property sold for Rs. 5 lakhs (worth Rs. 50 lakhs) under undue influence → Court may order: (1) Complete rescission, OR (2) Sale upheld but price increased to fair market value

## 3. Fraud (Section 17)

### Definition

**Section 17**: "'Fraud' means and includes any of the following acts committed by a party to a contract, or with his connivance, or by his agent, with intent to deceive another party thereto or his agent, or to induce him to enter into the contract—

(1) the suggestion, as a fact, of that which is not true by one who does not believe it to be true;
(2) the active concealment of a fact by one having knowledge or belief of the fact;
(3) a promise made without any intention of performing it;
(4) any other act fitted to deceive;
(5) any such act or omission as the law specially declares to be fraudulent."

**Elements of Fraud**:
1. **False representation** of fact (knowingly false)
2. **Intent to deceive** or induce contract
3. **Aggrieved party relied** on false representation
4. **Aggrieved party suffered** (or would suffer) loss

### Types of Fraud (Five Acts)

#### (1) Suggestion of Fact That is Not True (False Statement)

**Classic Fraud**: Stating something false as fact

**Elements**:
- Statement of fact (not opinion)
- False
- Maker knows it's false OR does not believe it's true

**Examples**:
- Selling car, stating "This car never met with accident" when car was in major accident
- Selling land, stating "This land has clear title" when land subject to mortgage

**NOT Fraud**:
- **Opinion**: "This is the best car in the market" (opinion, not fact; puffery allowed)
- **Belief**: "I believe this land will become valuable" (future prediction; not fact)

**Case: Derry v. Peek (1889)** (English case, persuasive):
- Company prospectus stated tramway would be steam-powered (pending Board approval)
- Board denied approval
- Court held: NOT fraud (honest belief; no intent to deceive)

#### (2) Active Concealment of Fact

**Active Concealment**: Deliberately hiding material fact

**Example**: 
- Seller paints over termite damage in house
- Seller hides defect in machinery

**Mere Silence**: Generally NOT fraud (caveat emptor - buyer beware)

**Exception: Duty to Speak** (Section 19):
- Fiduciary relationship (lawyer-client, doctor-patient)
- Relationship of trust (partners, trustees)
- Contract of utmost good faith (uberrimae fidei) - Insurance contracts

**Case: Keates v. Cadogan (1851)**:
- Landlord rented house knowing it was uninhabitable
- Did not disclose
- Court held: NO fraud (mere silence; no duty to disclose in lease contract)

**But**: If landlord **actively concealed** defects (plastered over cracks) → Fraud

#### (3) Promise Without Intention to Perform

**False Promise**: Promising to do something with NO intention of doing it

**Example**:
- Borrowing money promising to repay, knowing you cannot repay
- Promising to deliver goods, knowing you cannot deliver

**Difficulty**: Proving intent at time of promise

**Evidence of No Intent**:
- Immediate breach after contract
- Impossibility known to promisor
- Pattern of making promises without performing

**Case: Pollock Principles**: "If at time of making promise, promisor had no intention to perform → Fraud"

#### (4) Any Other Act Fitted to Deceive

**Catch-All Provision**: Any deceptive act

**Examples**:
- Rigging auction (false bidders to inflate price)
- Creating false documents
- Impersonation

#### (5) Act Declared Fraudulent by Law

**Statutory Fraud**: Acts specifically declared fraudulent by law

**Examples**:
- Fraudulent preference (insolvency law)
- Insider trading (securities law)

### Essentials of Fraud

#### 1. Representation of Fact (Not Opinion or Future Promise)

**Fact**: Statement about existing or past circumstances
- "This car has 50,000 km mileage" (fact)

**Opinion**: Subjective belief or judgment
- "This is a good car" (opinion)

**Future Promise**: Statement about future event
- "This land will appreciate" (future prediction; not fraud unless promisor knows it's false AND has no intention to make it happen)

**Exception: Expert Opinion**:
- Opinion by expert may be treated as fact
- Example: Doctor saying "This medicine is safe" when doctor knows it's dangerous → Fraud

**Case: Bisset v. Wilkinson (1927)**:
- Seller stated land could support 2000 sheep (opinion based on estimate)
- Buyer relied; land could only support 1000
- Court held: NOT fraud (honest opinion; buyer should have verified)

#### 2. False to Knowledge of Representor

**Scienter** (guilty knowledge): Representor must KNOW it's false

**Three States of Mind**:
1. **Knows it's false**: Fraud
2. **Believes it's true but careless**: NOT fraud (may be negligent misrepresentation)
3. **Does not believe it's true** (uncertain but represents as fact): Fraud

**Case: Derry v. Peek**: Honest belief (even if unreasonable) → NOT fraud

#### 3. Intent to Deceive or Induce Contract

**Mens Rea**: Fraudulent intent required

**Burden**: Party alleging fraud must prove intent to deceive

#### 4. Reliance by Aggrieved Party

**Inducement**: Fraud must have induced contract
- Aggrieved party relied on false representation
- Would not have entered contract but for fraud

**If No Reliance**: No fraud

**Example**: A lies about car's mileage; B buys car without caring about mileage → No fraud (no reliance)

### Effect of Fraud

**Section 19**: Contract voidable at option of aggrieved party

**Section 19 Proviso**: "If a party whose consent was obtained by fraud had the means of discovering the truth with ordinary diligence, the contract is not voidable."

**Exception to Voidability**: If victim could have discovered truth with ordinary diligence → Contract NOT voidable

**Rationale**: Negligent victim should not be rewarded

**Case: Central Inland Water Transport Corporation v. Brojo Nath (1986)**:
- Supreme Court: Even if party could have discovered fraud, contract still voidable if fraud practiced

**Current Position**: Courts reluctant to deny relief merely because victim was careless

**Remedies**:
1. **Rescission**: Cancel contract and restore parties to original position
2. **Damages**: Sue for tort of deceit (fraud in tort)

**Section 64**: Party rescinding contract must restore any benefit received

## 4. Misrepresentation (Section 18)

### Definition

**Section 18**: "Misrepresentation means and includes—

(1) the positive assertion, in a manner not warranted by the information of the person making it, of that which is not true, though he believes it to be true;
(2) any breach of duty which, without an intent to deceive, gains an advantage to the person committing it, or any one claiming under him, by misleading another to his prejudice or to the prejudice of any one claiming under him;
(3) causing, however innocently, a party to an agreement to make a mistake as to the substance of the thing which is the subject of the agreement."

**Key Difference from Fraud**: NO intent to deceive (innocent misrepresentation)

### Types of Misrepresentation

#### (1) Unwarranted Positive Assertion

**Innocent False Statement**: Person believes it's true but it's false

**Example**: Seller says "This car never met with accident" honestly believing it's true, but car was in accident (seller didn't know)

#### (2) Breach of Duty Gaining Advantage

**Example**: Agent fails to disclose material fact to principal (breach of fiduciary duty)

#### (3) Innocent Mistake as to Subject Matter

**Example**: Both parties believe painting is by Picasso; turns out it's fake → Innocent misrepresentation

### Misrepresentation vs Fraud

| Aspect | Fraud | Misrepresentation |
|--------|-------|-------------------|
| **Knowledge** | Knows it's false OR doesn't believe it's true | Believes it's true (but it's false) |
| **Intent** | Intent to deceive | No intent to deceive (innocent) |
| **Remedy** | Rescission + Damages (tort of deceit) | Rescission only (no damages) |
| **Burden** | On party alleging fraud | On party alleging misrepresentation |

**Case: Peek v. Gurney (1873)**:
- Prospectus contained false statements made in honest belief
- Court held: Misrepresentation (not fraud); no damages (but rescission available)

### Effect of Misrepresentation

**Section 19**: Contract voidable at option of aggrieved party

**But**: If aggrieved party had means to discover truth with ordinary diligence → Contract NOT voidable (Section 19 proviso)

**Remedies**:
1. **Rescission**: Cancel contract
2. **NO Damages**: Cannot sue for damages (no fraud; no tort of deceit)

## 5. Mistake (Sections 20-22)

### Introduction

**Mistake**: Erroneous belief about fact or law

**Effect**: Depends on type of mistake
- **Bilateral mistake of fact**: Void (Section 20)
- **Unilateral mistake**: Generally no effect (contract valid)
- **Mistake of law**: Generally no effect (ignorantia juris non excusat)

### Section 20: Bilateral Mistake of Fact

**Section 20**: "Where both the parties to an agreement are under a mistake as to a matter of fact essential to the agreement, the agreement is void."

**Elements**:
1. **Bilateral** (both parties mistaken)
2. **Mistake of fact** (not law)
3. **Essential to agreement** (goes to root of contract)

#### What is "Essential to Agreement"?

**Examples of Essential Mistakes** (Agreement VOID):

1. **Mistake as to Subject Matter Existence**:
   - **Case: Couturier v. Hastie (1856)**: Contract to sell cargo of corn; unknown to parties, cargo already perished at sea → Void (subject matter non-existent)
   - **Indian Analogy - Section 20 Illustration**: A agrees to buy B's horse; unknown to both, horse died → Void

2. **Mistake as to Identity of Subject Matter**:
   - A thinks selling horse X; B thinks buying horse Y → Void (no consensus ad idem)

3. **Mistake as to Quality**:
   - **If quality goes to root of contract** → Void
   - **Case: Raffles v. Wichelhaus (1864)** (Peerless case): Contract to buy cotton arriving on ship "Peerless"; two ships named "Peerless" (one in Oct, one in Dec) → Void (ambiguity as to which ship)

**Examples of NON-Essential Mistakes** (Agreement VALID):

1. **Mistake as to Peripheral Quality**:
   - A buys painting thinking it's valuable; turns out it's not → Valid (quality not essential; buyer's mistake on value)

2. **Mistake as to Motive**:
   - A buys land intending to build factory; later discovers zoning doesn't allow factory → Valid (mistake as to motive/future use; not essential to agreement)

### Section 21: Mistake as to Law

**Section 21**: "A contract is not voidable because it was caused by a mistake as to any law in force in India..."

**General Rule**: Mistake of law is NO excuse

**Ignorantia juris non excusat**: Ignorance of law is no excuse

**Example**: A pays money to B under mistake of law (thinking legally obliged); A cannot recover (mistake of law)

**Exception**: Mistake of **foreign law** treated as mistake of fact → Agreement voidable

### Section 22: Unilateral Mistake

**Section 22**: "A contract is not voidable merely because it was caused by one of the parties to it being under a mistake as to a matter of fact."

**General Rule**: Unilateral mistake (one party mistaken) → Contract VALID

**Exception**: Mistake as to **identity** or **nature of contract** → Void

#### Exception 1: Mistake as to Identity of Person

**Mistake as to identity** (Not mere creditworthiness) → Void

**Case: Cundy v. Lindsay (1878)**:
- L received order from "Blenkarn" who pretended to be reputable firm "Blenkiron & Co."
- L sold goods on credit to "Blenkarn" thinking it was "Blenkiron"
- Court held: Void (mistake as to identity)

**Contrast - Mistake as to Creditworthiness** (Contract Valid):
- A thinks B is creditworthy; B is not → Valid (mistake as to attribute, not identity)

**Case: King's Norton Metal Co. v. Edridge (1897)**:
- Order from "Hallam & Co." (fictitious firm)
- Seller thought it was large firm; actually one person
- Court held: Valid contract (mistake as to attributes, not identity)

#### Exception 2: Mistake as to Nature of Contract (Non Est Factum)

**Non est factum**: "It is not my deed"

**Rule**: If person signs document fundamentally different from what they thought → Void

**Requirements**:
1. **Fundamental difference** (not just terms)
2. **Not negligent** (reasonable care taken)

**Example**: Illiterate person asked to sign "receipt"; actually mortgage deed → Void (non est factum)

**Case: Saunders v. Anglia Building Society (1971)**:
- Elderly woman signed document thinking it was gift to nephew; actually sale to third party
- Court held: Valid (not fundamentally different; both were dispositions of property)

## Remedies for Vitiated Consent

### Voidable Contracts (Coercion, Undue Influence, Fraud, Misrepresentation)

**Section 19**: Voidable at option of aggrieved party

**Options**:
1. **Rescission**: Cancel contract and restore original position
2. **Affirmation**: Continue with contract
3. **Damages**: Only for fraud (tort of deceit); not for others

**Section 64**: When party rescinds voidable contract, must restore any benefit received (or compensate if cannot restore)

**Loss of Right to Rescind**:
- **Affirmation**: Aggrieved party affirms contract (expressly or by conduct)
- **Lapse of time**: Unreasonable delay
- **Third party rights**: Innocent third party acquired rights in subject matter

### Void Contracts (Bilateral Mistake)

**Effect**: Void ab initio (no contract exists)

**Consequences**:
- No obligations on either party
- Any property/money transferred can be recovered
- Third party acquiring rights in good faith protected

## Burden of Proof

| Vitiating Factor | Burden of Proof |
|------------------|-----------------|
| **Coercion** | On party alleging coercion |
| **Undue Influence** | On party alleging; BUT if dominant position + unconscionable transaction, shifts to dominant party |
| **Fraud** | On party alleging fraud |
| **Misrepresentation** | On party alleging misrepresentation |
| **Mistake** | On party alleging mistake |

## Practical Flowchart: Was Consent Free?

```
START
  ↓
Was consent obtained by COERCION?
(Threat of criminal act OR unlawful detention)
  ├─ YES → Contract VOIDABLE (Section 19)
  └─ NO → Proceed

  ↓
Was consent obtained by UNDUE INFLUENCE?
(Dominant position + unfair advantage)
  ├─ YES → Contract VOIDABLE (Section 19A)
  └─ NO → Proceed

  ↓
Was consent obtained by FRAUD?
(False representation + intent to deceive + reliance)
  ├─ YES → Contract VOIDABLE (Section 19) + Damages
  └─ NO → Proceed

  ↓
Was consent obtained by MISREPRESENTATION?
(Innocent false representation + reliance)
  ├─ YES → Contract VOIDABLE (Section 19) (No damages)
  └─ NO → Proceed

  ↓
Was there MISTAKE?
  ├─ Bilateral mistake of fact (essential) → VOID (Section 20)
  ├─ Unilateral mistake (identity/non est factum) → VOID (Section 22 exception)
  ├─ Mistake of law → VALID (Section 21)
  └─ Other mistakes → VALID

  ↓
CONSENT IS FREE ✓
```

## Practice Examples

### Example 1: Coercion

**Facts**:
- X threatens to file false criminal case against Y unless Y sells land for Rs. 10 lakhs
- Y, in fear, sells land (worth Rs. 50 lakhs)

**Analysis**:
- **Coercion**: Threat to commit act forbidden by IPC (false case = IPC 182, 211)
- **Intention**: To induce contract
- **Effect**: Contract VOIDABLE at Y's option (Section 19)
- **Remedy**: Y can rescind and recover land

### Example 2: Undue Influence

**Facts**:
- Elderly patient gifts Rs. 20 lakhs to doctor who treated her
- Doctor-patient relationship
- Gift made without independent advice

**Analysis**:
- **Fiduciary relationship**: Doctor-patient (Section 16(2)(b))
- **Unconscionable**: Large gift to doctor (unfair)
- **Presumption**: Undue influence presumed (Section 16(3))
- **Burden**: On doctor to prove no undue influence
- **Effect**: Contract VOIDABLE (likely rescinded)

### Example 3: Fraud

**Facts**:
- Seller shows car, says "No accident, 50,000 km"
- Actually: Major accident, 1,50,000 km (odometer tampered)
- Buyer relies, buys for Rs. 5 lakhs

**Analysis**:
- **False representation**: Accident history, mileage
- **Knowledge**: Seller knows it's false
- **Intent to deceive**: To induce sale
- **Reliance**: Buyer relied
- **Effect**: Contract VOIDABLE + Damages (tort of deceit)

### Example 4: Misrepresentation

**Facts**:
- Seller honestly believes car never had accident (previous owner lied)
- Tells buyer "No accident"
- Actually car was in accident
- Buyer discovers after purchase

**Analysis**:
- **Innocent false statement**: Seller believed it was true
- **No intent to deceive**: Misrepresentation (not fraud)
- **Effect**: Contract VOIDABLE (but NO damages)
- **Remedy**: Rescission only

### Example 5: Bilateral Mistake

**Facts**:
- A agrees to sell B a painting believed to be by Picasso for Rs. 1 crore
- Both genuinely believe it's Picasso original
- Later discovered to be fake (worth Rs. 10,000)

**Analysis**:
- **Bilateral mistake**: Both parties mistaken
- **Essential to agreement**: Identity/authenticity of painting (goes to root)
- **Effect**: Contract VOID (Section 20)
- **Result**: A must return Rs. 1 crore; B returns painting

### Example 6: Unilateral Mistake (Not Voidable)

**Facts**:
- A sells land to B for Rs. 50 lakhs
- A thinks land cannot be used for commercial purposes
- B knows land has commercial zoning (worth Rs. 2 crores)
- A later discovers mistake

**Analysis**:
- **Unilateral mistake**: Only A mistaken (about commercial use)
- **Not as to identity**: Parties agree on same land
- **General rule**: Unilateral mistake → Contract VALID
- **Effect**: Contract VALID (A cannot rescind)

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Coercion vs Undue Influence

**Mistake**: Confusing the two

**Correct**:
- **Coercion**: Physical/criminal threat
- **Undue Influence**: Mental/moral pressure using dominant position

### 2. Fraud vs Misrepresentation

**Mistake**: Thinking they're the same

**Correct**:
- **Fraud**: Intentional deception → Rescission + Damages
- **Misrepresentation**: Innocent false statement → Rescission only (no damages)

### 3. Mere Silence ≠ Fraud

**Mistake**: Thinking failure to disclose is always fraud

**Correct**: Mere silence NOT fraud (caveat emptor) UNLESS duty to disclose (fiduciary relationship, insurance contracts)

### 4. Opinion vs Fact

**Mistake**: Treating all statements as factual representations

**Correct**: 
- **Opinion/Puffery**: "Best car in town" → Not fraud
- **Fact**: "50,000 km mileage" → Can be fraud if false

### 5. Bilateral vs Unilateral Mistake

**Mistake**: Thinking all mistakes void contract

**Correct**:
- **Bilateral mistake (essential)**: VOID
- **Unilateral mistake**: Generally VALID (exceptions: identity, non est factum)

### 6. Mistake of Law vs Fact

**Mistake**: Thinking mistake of law voids contract

**Correct**: Mistake of law NO excuse (Section 21); contract VALID
