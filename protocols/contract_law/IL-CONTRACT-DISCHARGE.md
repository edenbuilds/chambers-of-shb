# Contract Discharge Protocol (India)

## Protocol Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-CONTRACT-DISCHARGE
- **Version**: 1.0.0
- **Domain**: Contract Law (India)
- **Subdomain**: Contract Performance - Discharge
- **Statute**: Indian Contract Act 1872 (Sections 62-67)
- **Last Updated**: January 2025
- **Effective**: Since 1872

---

## Overview

This protocol covers **discharge of contracts** - the ways in which contractual obligations come to an end, releasing parties from further performance.

**Key Principle**: Contracts end when obligations are fulfilled, mutually cancelled, or excused by law.

---

## Methods of Discharge

**Six Methods**:
1. **Performance** (Section 37)
2. **Agreement** (Sections 62-63)
3. **Impossibility** (Section 56)
4. **Breach** (Section 39)
5. **Lapse of Time** (Limitation Act)
6. **Operation of Law** (Death, insolvency, merger)

---

## 1. Discharge by Performance

### Actual Performance (Section 37)

**Rule**: Contract discharged when parties perform all promises

**Requirements**:
- **Complete performance** (not partial)
- **Exact performance** (as per contract terms)
- **By proper person** (promisor or agent if allowed)
- **At proper time and place**

**Example**: Seller delivers 100 units; buyer pays Rs. 10,000 → Contract fully performed and discharged

### Attempted Performance / Tender (Section 38)

**Section 38**: "Where a promisor has made an offer of performance to the promisee, and the offer has not been accepted, the promisor is not responsible for non-performance, nor does he thereby lose his rights under the contract."

**Tender of Performance**: Unconditional offer to perform

**Effect**: Discharges promisor's obligation (even if not accepted)

**Example**: Seller tenders delivery on due date; buyer refuses → Seller discharged from obligation; can sue buyer

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-PERFORMANCE protocol (Section 41)

## 2. Discharge by Agreement

**Maxim**: "As parties can make contract, they can unmake it"

**Four Types**:
- Novation (Section 62)
- Rescission (Section 62)
- Alteration (Section 62)
- Remission (Section 63)
- Waiver

### A. Novation (Section 62)

**Section 62**: "If the parties to a contract agree to substitute a new contract for it, or to rescind or alter it, the original contract need not be performed."

**Novation**: Substitution of new contract for old contract

**Elements**:
1. **Mutual agreement** of all parties
2. **New contract** with different terms OR different parties
3. **Consideration**: New contract itself is consideration

**Types of Novation**:

#### 1. Change of Parties

**Example**: A owes Rs. 10,000 to B; B, A, and C agree that C will pay Rs. 10,000 to B (A released)
- Old contract: A → B
- New contract: C → B
- A discharged from obligation

#### 2. Change of Terms

**Example**: A agrees to deliver goods on Jan 1; parties agree to change to Jan 15
- Old contract: Delivery Jan 1
- New contract: Delivery Jan 15
- Original obligation discharged

**Case: Scarf v. Jardine (1882)**:
- Original contract to deliver wheat
- Parties agreed to substitute barley
- Court held: Novation; original contract discharged

### B. Rescission (Section 62)

**Rescission**: Mutual cancellation of contract

**Elements**:
1. **Mutual agreement** to cancel
2. **Before performance** (or partial performance with agreement)
3. **Consideration**: Mutual release is consideration

**Example**: A and B agree to cancel contract before delivery
- Both released from obligations
- No further performance required

**Bilateral Rescission**: Both parties release each other

**Unilateral Rescission**: One party releases other (requires consideration)

### C. Alteration (Section 62)

**Alteration**: Material change in one or more terms

**Requirements**:
1. **Mutual consent** to alter terms
2. **Material change** (substantial, not minor)
3. **Consideration**: Mutual promises for alteration

**Example**: Contract for Rs. 10,000; parties agree to change to Rs. 12,000
- Original contract discharged
- New contract for Rs. 12,000 formed

**Minor vs Material Change**:
- **Minor**: Change of payment mode (cash → cheque) → Amendment (not discharge)
- **Material**: Change of price, quantity, delivery date → Alteration (original contract discharged)

### D. Remission (Section 63)

**Section 63**: "The promisee may dispense with or remit, wholly or in part, the performance of the promise made to him, or extend the time for such performance, or accept instead of it any satisfaction which he thinks fit."

**Remission**: Acceptance of less than agreed performance

**Types**:

#### 1. Remission of Part Performance

**Example**: A owes Rs. 10,000 to B; B accepts Rs. 8,000 in full settlement
- B **remits** Rs. 2,000
- A discharged from Rs. 10,000 debt

**No Consideration Required**: Section 63 exception (promisee voluntarily accepts less)

#### 2. Extension of Time

**Example**: Payment due Jan 1; promisee extends to Jan 15
- Original obligation (Jan 1) discharged
- New obligation (Jan 15) created

#### 3. Acceptance of Different Satisfaction

**Example**: A owes Rs. 10,000 cash to B; B accepts car in settlement
- B accepts car **instead of** cash
- A's cash obligation discharged

**Case: Pinnel's Case (1602)** (English law):
- General rule: Part payment not satisfaction of whole debt (creditor can sue for balance)
- Exception: If part payment **plus something additional** (e.g., early payment, different goods) → Satisfaction

**Indian Law (Section 63)**: Remission valid even without consideration (promisee's voluntary acceptance sufficient)

### E. Waiver

**Waiver**: Voluntary relinquishment of right

**Example**: Seller has right to sue for delay; seller waives right
- Seller cannot later sue for delay damages

**Requirements**:
- **Voluntary**: Must be intentional
- **With knowledge**: Party knows of right being waived
- **Unequivocal**: Clear intention to waive

**No Consideration Required**: Waiver is gratuitous abandonment of right

## 3. Discharge by Impossibility

**Section 56**: "An agreement to do an act impossible in itself is void... A contract to do an act which, after the contract is made, becomes impossible, or, by reason of some event which the promisor could not prevent, unlawful, becomes void when the act becomes impossible or unlawful."

**Two Types**:
1. **Initial Impossibility** (at time of formation) → Void ab initio
2. **Supervening impossibility** (after formation) → Contract becomes void

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-IMPOSSIBILITY protocol (detailed coverage)

**Supervening Impossibility** (Section 56):

### A. Destruction of Subject Matter

**Example**: Contract to sell specific car; car destroyed in fire before delivery → Void

**Case: Taylor v. Caldwell (1863)**:
- Contract to rent music hall
- Hall burned down
- Court held: Contract void (impossibility)

### B. Death or Incapacity (Personal Service Contracts)

**Example**: Contract for artist to paint portrait; artist dies → Void

**Case: Robinson v. Davison (1871)**:
- Contract for pianist to perform
- Pianist fell ill (could not perform)
- Court held: Contract void (impossibility)

### C. Change of Law / Supervening Illegality

**Example**: Contract to export goods; government bans export → Void

**Example**: Contract during peacetime becomes illegal during war (trading with enemy) → Void

### D. Non-Occurrence of Contemplated Event

**Frustration of Purpose**: Event that was foundation of contract doesn't occur

**Case: Krell v. Henry (1903)** (Coronation case):
- Contract to rent room to view coronation procession
- Coronation cancelled
- Court held: Contract void (frustration of purpose)

**Indian Application**:

**Case: Satyabrata Ghose v. Mugneeram Bangur (1954)** (Supreme Court):
- Land sale agreement
- Government requisitioned land
- Seller could not transfer
- Court held: Contract void (supervening impossibility)

## 4. Discharge by Breach

**Breach**: Failure to perform contractual obligations

**Two Types**:
1. **Actual Breach**: Breach at time performance due
2. **Anticipatory Breach**: Breach before performance due (repudiation)

**Effect**: Innocent party can treat contract as discharged + sue for damages

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-BREACH protocol (detailed coverage)

### Actual Breach

**Example**: Seller fails to deliver goods on delivery date → Actual breach

**Innocent Party's Rights**:
1. Treat contract as discharged (terminate)
2. Sue for damages

### Anticipatory Breach

**Example**: Seller declares (before delivery date) that they will not deliver → Anticipatory breach

**Innocent Party's Options** (Hochster v. De La Tour):
1. **Immediately sue** for breach (before performance date)
2. **Wait** till performance date and sue then

**Case: Frost v. Knight (1872)**:
- Defendant engaged to marry plaintiff
- Defendant repudiated engagement before marriage date (father still alive; marriage conditional on father's death)
- Court held: Plaintiff can sue immediately (anticipatory breach)

## 5. Discharge by Lapse of Time

**Limitation Act 1963**: Prescribes time limits for suing on contracts

**General Limitation Periods**:
- **Contracts**: 3 years from breach
- **Sale of goods**: 3 years from delivery
- **Negotiable instruments**: 3 years from maturity
- **Suits on judgments**: 12 years

**Effect of Limitation**: Right to sue barred (contract not performed but cannot enforce in court)

**Debt Still Exists**: Limitation bars remedy, not right
- Debtor can voluntarily pay time-barred debt (payment valid)
- Promise to pay time-barred debt (written, signed) → Revives debt (Section 25(3))

**See**: Limitation Act 1963

## 6. Discharge by Operation of Law

### A. Merger

**Merger**: Lower right merges into higher right

**Example**: Simple contract debt merged into judgment decree
- Creditor sues; obtains court judgment
- Original contract merged into judgment
- Cannot sue on original contract (must execute judgment)

### B. Death

**General Rule**: Death does NOT discharge contract (obligations pass to legal representatives)

**Exception**: Personal service contracts
- Contract for specific person's services
- Death → Contract discharged (cannot be performed by representatives)

**Example**: Contract for famous singer to perform → Singer dies → Discharged

### C. Insolvency

**Adjudication of Insolvency**: Insolvent discharged from pre-insolvency debts (after discharge order)

**Effect**: Contractual debts incurred before insolvency → Discharged

**But**: Creditors can claim from assets vested in Official Assignee

**See**: Insolvency and Bankruptcy Code 2016

### D. Material Alteration

**Unilateral Material Alteration**: If one party materially alters contract without consent → Contract voidable

**Material Alteration**: Substantial change (price, quantity, date)

**Example**: Creditor alters promissory note (increases amount) without debtor's consent → Note voidable

**Case: Pigot's Case (1614)**:
- Material alteration without consent vitiates instrument
- Debtor discharged

**Indian Position**: Material alteration without consent → Contract voidable at option of non-altering party

## Comparison Table: Discharge Methods

| Method | Basis | Mutual Consent Required? | Effect |
|--------|-------|--------------------------|--------|
| **Performance** | Completion of obligations | No | Contract fulfilled |
| **Novation** | New contract substituted | Yes | Original discharged; new contract formed |
| **Rescission** | Mutual cancellation | Yes | Both parties released |
| **Alteration** | Material change in terms | Yes | Original discharged; altered contract formed |
| **Remission** | Acceptance of less | No (promisee's unilateral act) | Promisor discharged to extent remitted |
| **Waiver** | Voluntary relinquishment | No (unilateral) | Right waived |
| **Impossibility** | Supervening event | No (operation of law) | Contract void |
| **Breach** | Failure to perform | No | Innocent party can terminate + damages |
| **Lapse of Time** | Limitation period expires | No | Right to sue barred |
| **Operation of Law** | Death, insolvency, merger | No | Contract discharged by law |

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Novation

**Facts**:
- A owes Rs. 50,000 to B
- A, B, and C agree: C will pay Rs. 50,000 to B; A released

**Analysis**:
- **Novation**: Change of parties
- **Effect**: Original contract (A → B) discharged; new contract (C → B) formed

### Example 2: Rescission

**Facts**:
- A contracts to sell car to B for Rs. 5 lakhs
- Before delivery, both agree to cancel contract

**Analysis**:
- **Rescission**: Mutual cancellation
- **Effect**: Both released; no further obligations

### Example 3: Remission

**Facts**:
- A owes Rs. 10,000 to B (due Jan 1)
- B accepts Rs. 7,000 in full settlement

**Analysis**:
- **Remission**: B accepts less (Section 63)
- **Effect**: A discharged from entire Rs. 10,000 debt

### Example 4: Impossibility

**Facts**:
- A contracts to deliver specific vintage car to B on June 1
- Car destroyed in fire on May 15

**Analysis**:
- **Supervening impossibility**: Subject matter destroyed (Section 56)
- **Effect**: Contract void; both parties discharged

### Example 5: Anticipatory Breach

**Facts**:
- A contracts to deliver goods to B on Dec 31
- On Dec 1, A informs B: "I will not deliver goods"

**Analysis**:
- **Anticipatory breach**: Repudiation before due date
- **B's options**: (1) Sue immediately, OR (2) Wait till Dec 31 and sue then
- **Effect**: If B sues, contract discharged + damages

### Example 6: Lapse of Time

**Facts**:
- A lends Rs. 1 lakh to B in 2020
- B defaults
- A sues in 2025 (5 years later)

**Analysis**:
- **Limitation**: 3 years for contracts (Limitation Act)
- **Effect**: A's suit barred (time-barred); cannot recover in court

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Confusing Novation with Amendment

**Mistake**: Treating minor changes as novation

**Correct**:
- **Amendment**: Minor change (doesn't discharge original)
- **Novation**: Substantial change (discharges original; creates new)

### 2. Assuming Part Payment Discharges Debt

**Mistake**: Thinking partial payment = full discharge

**Correct**:
- **General rule**: Part payment NOT discharge (creditor can sue for balance)
- **Exception**: Remission under Section 63 (creditor accepts less in full settlement)

### 3. Thinking Impossibility Always Discharges

**Mistake**: Claiming impossibility for every difficulty

**Correct**:
- **Impossibility**: Objective impossibility (cannot be performed by anyone)
- **Difficulty/Hardship**: Not impossibility (contract still binding)

### 4. Forgetting Limitation Periods

**Mistake**: Delaying suit beyond limitation period

**Correct**: Sue within 3 years of breach (general contracts)

### 5. Unilateral Alteration

**Mistake**: One party alters contract assuming it's valid

**Correct**: Material alteration without consent → Voidable (other party can avoid)
