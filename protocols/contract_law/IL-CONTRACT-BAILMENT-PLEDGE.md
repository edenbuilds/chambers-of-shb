# Bailment and Pledge Protocol (India)

- **Domain**: Contract Law (India)
- **Subdomain**: Special Contracts - Bailment & Pledge
- **Statute**: Indian Contract Act 1872 (Sections 148-181)
- **Last Updated**: January 2025
- **Effective**: Since 1872

## Overview

This protocol covers two related special contracts:
1. **Bailment** (Sections 148-171): Temporary transfer of possession of goods for a purpose
2. **Pledge** (Sections 172-181): Special type of bailment as security for payment of debt/performance of promise

**Key Principle**: Bailment involves **possession transfer** (not ownership); ownership remains with bailor.

---

## PART A: CONTRACT OF BAILMENT

### Section 148: Bailment Defined

```
"A 'bailment' is the delivery of goods by one person to another for some purpose, upon a contract that they shall, when the purpose is accomplished, be returned or otherwise disposed of according to the directions of the person delivering them. The person delivering the goods is called the 'bailor'. The person to whom they are delivered is called the 'bailee'."
```

**Essential Elements**:
1. **Delivery of possession**: Goods transferred from bailor to bailee
2. **Purpose**: Specific purpose for bailment
3. **Contract**: Agreement between parties (express or implied)
4. **Return**: Goods to be returned when purpose accomplished (or disposed per bailor's direction)
5. **Same goods**: Identical goods returned (not equivalent)

**Parties**:
1. **Bailor**: Person delivering goods (owner or authorized person)
2. **Bailee**: Person receiving goods (takes possession, not ownership)

---

### Types of Delivery

#### 1. Actual Delivery

**Physical transfer** of possession

**Example**: A hands car keys to B (valet parking) → Actual delivery

#### 2. Constructive Delivery

**Symbolic transfer** without physical handing over

**Examples**:
- Transfer of keys (when goods not movable)
- Transfer of documents of title (warehouse receipt, bill of lading)
- Attornment: Third party holding goods acknowledges holds for bailee

**Section 149**: "The delivery to the bailee may be made by doing anything which has the effect of putting the goods in the possession of the intended bailee or of any person authorized to hold them on his behalf."

---

### Types of Bailment

#### 1. Based on Consideration

##### a) Gratuitous Bailment

**No consideration** (free service)

**Example**: A lends book to friend B (no charge) → Gratuitous bailment

**Bailee's Duty**: Take care as would of own goods (Section 151)

##### b) Bailment for Reward (Non-Gratuitous)

**Consideration present** (payment/benefit)

**Example**: A parks car at paid parking (B) → Bailment for reward

**Bailee's Duty**: Higher standard of care (Section 152)

#### 2. Based on Benefit

##### a) Sole Benefit of Bailor

**Example**: Gratuitous custody (A deposits goods with B for safekeeping; no charge)

**Bailee's liability**: Minimal (only gross negligence)

##### b) Sole Benefit of Bailee

**Example**: Gratuitous loan (A lends car to B free)

**Bailee's liability**: Higher (must take extraordinary care)

##### c) Mutual Benefit

**Example**: Paid storage, rental, transport

**Bailee's liability**: Reasonable care (as prudent person)

#### 3. Based on Purpose

**Six Classes** (Section 148 Explanation):

1. **Deposit** (safekeeping): A deposits jewelry with B (bank locker)
2. **Commodatum** (gratuitous loan): A lends book to B
3. **Hire** (paid use): A rents car from B
4. **Pawn/Pledge** (security): A pledges gold to B (for loan)
5. **Delivery for work** (services): A gives watch to B (for repair)
6. **Gratuitous services**: A delivers goods to B for free transport

---

### Duties of Bailee

#### 1. Duty to Take Reasonable Care (Sections 151-152)

**Section 151**: Gratuitous bailment
```
"In all cases of bailment the bailee is bound to take as much care of the goods bailed to him as a man of ordinary prudence would, under similar circumstances, take of his own goods of the same bulk, quality and value as the goods bailed."
```

**Section 152**: Bailment for reward
```
"The bailee, in the absence of any special contract, is not responsible for the loss, destruction or deterioration of the thing bailed, if he has taken the amount of care of it described in section 151."
```

**Standard**: **Reasonable care** (man of ordinary prudence)

**NOT insurer**: Bailee not liable for loss despite care (force majeure, etc.)

**Exception**: Liability even with care if:
- Special contract (bailee guaranteed safety)
- Bailee's deliberate act/negligence

**Case: Chaudhary Dharampal v. Allahabad Bank (1957)**:
- Bank locker; theft by breaking bank wall
- Court held: Bank not liable (took reasonable care; theft by extraordinary means)

**But**: If inadequate security → Bank liable

#### 2. Not to Make Unauthorized Use (Section 154)

**Bailee must use goods only for agreed purpose**

**Breach**: Unauthorized use → Bailee liable for all loss (even without negligence)

**Example**: 
- A hires car to B for city use
- B drives to mountains (unauthorized)
- Car damaged (no negligence)
- B liable (unauthorized use)

**Case: Lilley v. Doubleday (1881)**:
- Horse hired for ride; bailee used for racing
- Horse injured during race
- Court held: Bailee liable (unauthorized use)

#### 3. Not to Mix Goods (Section 155-157)

**Section 155**: **Without consent**
- If bailee mixes goods with own goods → Liable for loss
- If goods separable → Bailee bears separation cost + compensates loss

**Section 156**: **With consent**
- If mixed with consent → Both have proportionate share in mixture
- Loss borne proportionately

**Section 157**: Difficulty in separation
- Bailee must compensate bailor for loss from separation difficulty

#### 4. Return Goods (Section 160)

**Bailee must return goods** when:
- Purpose accomplished, OR
- Time period expired, OR
- Bailor demands (if gratuitous bailment)

**Identical Goods**: Must return same goods (not equivalent - except fungible goods with consent)

**Section 161**: If bailee doesn't return on demand → Liable for loss even by unavoidable accident

#### 5. Return to Right Person (Section 160)

**Return to bailor** OR person authorized by bailor

**Section 164**: Bailee's liability if delivers to wrong person (even if in good faith)

**Exception**: Delivery to person with better title (bailee protected)

#### 6. Return Accretion/Profit (Section 163)

**Any increase/profit** from goods belongs to bailor

**Example**: Cow bailed; calf born → Calf belongs to bailor

---

### Rights of Bailee

#### 1. Right to Expenses and Remuneration (Section 158)

**Bailment for reward**: Bailee entitled to remuneration agreed

**Extraordinary expenses** (preserving goods, defending bailor's title): Bailee can claim from bailor

**Example**: Goods deposited with B; third party claims ownership; B incurs legal costs defending A's title → B can claim costs from A

#### 2. Right of Lien (Section 170)

**Particular Lien**: Bailee can retain goods until:
- Remuneration paid, AND
- Expenses incurred reimbursed

**Only for amount due** in respect of those goods (not other debts)

**Example**: A repairs B's car; B doesn't pay → A can retain car (lien for repair charges)

**Lost if**: 
- Bailee parts with possession (voluntarily)
- Payment/tender of dues

#### 3. Right to Sue Third Party (Section 180)

**If third party wrongfully deprives bailee of use/damages goods** → Bailee can sue

**Bailee's right**: Sue in own name (as if bailee were owner)

**Example**: Car bailed to A; C negligently damages car → A (bailee) can sue C for damages

#### 4. Right to Compensation for Breach (Section 168)

**If bailor breaches contract** (e.g., terminates bailment wrongfully) → Bailee can claim damages

**Example**: Goods hired for 3 months; bailor demands return after 1 month → Bailee can claim damages for remaining 2 months

---

### Duties of Bailor

#### 1. Disclosure of Known Defects (Section 150)

**Gratuitous bailment**: Bailor must disclose faults known to bailor (causing injury to bailee)

**Bailment for reward**: Bailor must disclose faults **whether known or not** (should have known with ordinary diligence)

**Liability**: Damages for loss arising from non-disclosure

**Example**:
- A lends defective car to B (gratuitous)
- A knows brakes defective; doesn't disclose
- B injured due to brake failure
- A liable (non-disclosure of known defect)

**For Reward**:
- A rents car to B
- Brakes defective (A didn't check; reasonable inspection would reveal)
- B injured
- A liable (should have known)

#### 2. Bear Extraordinary Expenses (Section 158)

**Bailor must reimburse** bailee for extraordinary expenses incurred for preservation of goods

#### 3. Indemnify Bailee (Section 164)

**If bailor has no right to bail goods** OR gives wrong instructions → Must indemnify bailee for loss

**Example**: 
- A bails B's goods to C (A not authorized)
- B (true owner) sues C
- A must indemnify C for loss

#### 4. Receive Back Goods (Gratuitous Bailment)

**Gratuitous bailment**: Bailor must take back goods when bailment terminates

**If bailor doesn't**: Liable for loss to bailee

---

### Rights of Bailor

#### 1. Enforce Bailee's Duties

**Sue bailee** for breach of duty (negligence, unauthorized use, etc.)

#### 2. Terminate Gratuitous Bailment (Section 159)

**Bailor can demand return** anytime (if gratuitous bailment)

**But**: Liable for damages if termination causes loss to bailee

**Example**: A lends B machine (gratuitous); B using for important project; A demands return midway → A liable for B's project loss

#### 3. Claim Damages from Third Party (Section 180)

**If third party wrongfully damages bailed goods** → Bailor can sue for damages (to ownership interest)

---

### Termination of Bailment

**Bailment ends when**:

1. **Purpose accomplished**: Task completed
2. **Time expired**: Period ended
3. **Destruction of goods**: Subject matter perished
4. **Act inconsistent with bailment**: Bailee acts as owner (sells goods)
5. **Death** (if personal skill/confidence essential - Section 162)
6. **Bailor's demand** (gratuitous bailment - Section 159)

**Section 162**: Death terminates if contract personal; otherwise, legal representatives succeed

---

### Finder of Goods (Sections 168-169)

**Finder of lost goods** = Bailee

**Section 168**: Finder has:
- **Duty**: Same as bailee (take reasonable care; restore to true owner)
- **Right**: Particular lien for expenses + compensation for preserving/finding owner

**Section 169**: Finder can sell if:
1. **Goods perishable**, OR
2. **Owner cannot be found with reasonable effort**, OR
3. **Owner refuses to pay** finder's lawful charges (and charges ≥ 2/3 of goods' value)

**Sale Procedure**: Public notice required (if possible)

**Case: Parker v. British Airways Board (1982)**:
- Finder of lost bracelet at airport
- Court held: Finder has rights against all (except true owner)
- Better title than possessor of premises (airline)

## PART B: CONTRACT OF PLEDGE

### Section 172: Pledge Defined

```
"The bailment of goods as security for payment of a debt or performance of a promise is called 'pledge'. The bailor is in this case called the 'pawnor', and the bailee is called the 'pawnee'."
```

**Pledge = Special bailment** (security purpose)

**Parties**:
1. **Pawnor**: Person delivering goods (borrower)
2. **Pawnee**: Person receiving goods as security (lender)

**Purpose**: Security for debt/promise

**Example**: A borrows Rs. 1 lakh from B; pledges gold ornaments as security → A is pawnor; B is pawnee

---

### Essential Elements of Pledge

1. **Delivery of possession**: Pawnor to pawnee (actual or constructive)
2. **Security**: For debt or performance of promise
3. **Return**: Goods returned when debt paid/promise performed
4. **Same goods**: Specific goods pledged (not fungible substitutes)

**Section 173**: Constructive delivery sufficient

**Case: Official Assignee of Bombay v. Shri Suraj Mal Ram Nath (1961)** (Supreme Court):
- Pledge by delivery of document of title valid
- Constructive possession sufficient

---

### Who Can Pledge?

**General Rule**: **Owner** or person with authority

**Section 178**: **Non-owner can pledge** if:

#### 1. Mercantile Agent (Section 178)

**Mercantile agent** in possession with owner's consent → Can pledge (if in ordinary course of business)

**Pawnee's protection**: If pawnee acts in good faith + no notice of agent's lack of authority

**Example**: A (owner) gives goods to B (agent) for sale; B pledges to C (lender) → C gets good title (if in good faith)

#### 2. Person with Limited Interest (Section 179)

**Person with limited ownership** (co-owner, joint owner) → Can pledge (to extent of his interest)

**Example**: A and B co-own goods; A pledges his 50% share → Valid pledge of A's interest

#### 3. Seller/Buyer in Possession (Section 178A)

**Seller in possession after sale** OR **buyer in possession before property passes** → Can pledge

**Pawnee protected** if bona fide and no notice

**Case: Lallan Prasad v. Rahmat Ali (1967)** (Supreme Court):
- Buyer obtained possession before paying
- Buyer pledged goods
- Court held: Pledge valid (buyer in possession with seller's consent)

---

### Duties of Pawnee

#### 1. Take Reasonable Care (Section 150-152)

**Same as bailee**: Reasonable care of pledged goods

**Liable for loss** due to negligence

#### 2. Not Make Unauthorized Use (Section 154)

**No use of pledged goods** (unless authorized)

**Example**: Pawnee pledges car; cannot drive car (unless pawnor permits)

#### 3. Return on Payment (Section 177)

**Pawnee must return goods** when:
- Debt paid, OR
- Promise performed

**If pawnee refuses** after payment → Liable for damages

#### 4. Account for Surplus on Sale (Section 176)

**If pawnee sells pledged goods** (on default) → Must account for surplus (after deducting debt + expenses)

**Example**:
- Debt Rs. 1 lakh
- Pledged goods sold for Rs. 1.5 lakhs
- Pawnee deducts Rs. 1 lakh (debt) + Rs. 10,000 (sale expenses)
- Must return Rs. 40,000 (surplus) to pawnor

#### 5. Give Reasonable Notice Before Sale (Section 176)

**Pawnee must give reasonable notice** before selling pledged goods

**Purpose**: Allow pawnor to redeem

---

### Rights of Pawnee

#### 1. Right of Retainer (Section 174)

**Pawnee can retain goods** until:
- Debt paid, AND
- Interest paid, AND
- Expenses incurred reimbursed

**Particular lien**: For amount due in respect of pledged goods

#### 2. Right of Retainer for Subsequent Advances (Section 175)

**If pawnor makes subsequent borrowings** from same pawnee → Pawnee can retain for all debts (if agreed)

**Example**: 
- A pledges goods for Rs. 1 lakh loan (Loan 1)
- Later borrows Rs. 50,000 more (Loan 2) from same pawnee
- If agreed, pawnee can retain until Rs. 1.5 lakhs paid

#### 3. Right to Extraordinary Expenses (Section 175A)

**Pawnee entitled to reimbursement** for extraordinary expenses (preserving pledged goods)

#### 4. Right of Sale (Section 176)

**On pawnor's default**, pawnee can:
1. **Sue for debt** (retain goods as collateral), OR
2. **Sell goods** (after reasonable notice)

**Sale Procedure**:
- Reasonable notice to pawnor
- Public sale (unless contract provides otherwise)
- Account for surplus

**Pawnee's right**: Choose between suit or sale (cannot do both for same debt)

**Example**:
- Debt Rs. 1 lakh; pawnor defaults
- Pawnee can:
  - (a) Sue for Rs. 1 lakh (keep goods as security), OR
  - (b) Sell goods (after notice); apply proceeds to debt

#### 5. Sue Third Party (Section 180)

**If third party wrongfully damages pledged goods** → Pawnee can sue (as bailee)

---

### Rights of Pawnor

#### 1. Right to Redeem (Section 177)

**Pawnor can redeem** pledged goods by:
- Paying debt, AND
- Interest, AND
- Expenses

**Right survives default** (as long as goods not sold)

**Example**: 
- Loan due June 1; pawnor defaults
- Pawnee gives notice of sale (to be sold July 1)
- Pawnor can redeem anytime before sale (by paying all dues)

#### 2. Right to Surplus (Section 176)

**After sale**, pawnor entitled to surplus (sale proceeds - debt - expenses)

#### 3. Pawnor's Right to Limit Pawnee's Sale (Section 176 Proviso)

**If default is only part payment** → Pawnee cannot sell entire goods (only proportionate part)

**Example**:
- Debt Rs. 1 lakh; pledged goods worth Rs. 2 lakhs
- Pawnor pays Rs. 50,000; defaults on Rs. 50,000
- Pawnee cannot sell entire goods (worth Rs. 2 lakhs); can sell only proportionate part (worth Rs. 1 lakh after accounting for Rs. 50,000 paid)

**Interpretation**: Controversial; some courts allow full sale with accounting to pawnor

---

### Pledge vs. Mortgage

| Aspect | Pledge | Mortgage |
|--------|--------|----------|
| **Subject** | Movable goods only | Immovable property (land, buildings) |
| **Possession** | Pawnee gets possession | Mortgagee usually doesn't get possession |
| **Transfer of Ownership** | No (only possession) | Limited ownership transferred |
| **Right to Use** | Pawnee cannot use (unless authorized) | Mortgagee cannot use (unless possession mortgage) |
| **Sale on Default** | Can sell after notice (Section 176) | Must file suit for foreclosure/sale (unless document permits) |
| **Statute** | Contract Act (Sections 172-181) | Transfer of Property Act 1882 |

---

### Pledge vs. Hypothecation

| Aspect | Pledge | Hypothecation |
|--------|--------|---------------|
| **Possession** | Pawnee gets possession | Borrower retains possession |
| **Nature** | Bailment (actual security) | Equitable charge (no bailment) |
| **Sale Rights** | Pawnee can sell (Section 176) | Creditor cannot sell directly (must take possession first) |
| **Example** | Gold loan (gold deposited with bank) | Vehicle loan (vehicle with borrower) |

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Bailment - Unauthorized Use

**Facts**:
- A rents car to B for city use (within 50 km radius)
- B drives to hill station (200 km away; unauthorized use)
- Car damaged in accident (no negligence of B)
- Repair cost Rs. 1 lakh

**Analysis**:
- **Section 154**: Unauthorized use → Bailee liable for all loss (even without negligence)
- **Result**: B liable for Rs. 1 lakh (even though accident not due to B's fault)

### Example 2: Bailment - Mixing Goods

**Facts**:
- A deposits 100 kg rice (premium quality) with B for storage
- B mixes A's rice with own rice (ordinary quality) without A's consent
- Mixture impossible to separate

**Analysis**:
- **Section 155**: Mixing without consent → Bailee liable for loss
- **Inseparable mixture**: A can claim compensation for premium rice value
- **Result**: B must compensate A (difference between premium and ordinary rice value)

### Example 3: Finder of Goods

**Facts**:
- A finds lost mobile phone at railway station
- A spends Rs. 5,000 (advertising, storage, attempting to find owner)
- True owner C comes forward after 3 months
- C refuses to pay A's expenses

**Analysis**:
- **Section 168**: Finder = bailee; has particular lien for expenses
- **A's rights**: 
  - Retain phone until C pays Rs. 5,000 (expenses)
  - If C refuses + expenses ≥ 2/3 of phone value → A can sell (Section 169)
- **Result**: A has lien; can retain phone or sell if conditions met

### Example 4: Pledge - Sale on Default

**Facts**:
- A pledges gold ornaments (value Rs. 5 lakhs) to B for loan of Rs. 2 lakhs
- Loan due June 1; A defaults
- B gives notice (30 days) of intention to sell
- A doesn't pay; B sells ornaments for Rs. 4.8 lakhs (after public auction)
- Sale expenses: Rs. 20,000

**Analysis**:
- **Section 176**: Pawnee can sell after notice
- **Notice given**: 30 days (reasonable)
- **Sale proceeds**: Rs. 4.8 lakhs
- **Deductions**: Rs. 2 lakhs (debt) + Rs. 20,000 (expenses) = Rs. 2.2 lakhs
- **Surplus**: Rs. 4.8 lakhs - Rs. 2.2 lakhs = Rs. 2.6 lakhs
- **Result**: B must pay Rs. 2.6 lakhs to A (pawnor)

### Example 5: Pledge by Non-Owner

**Facts**:
- A (owner) gives goods to B (mercantile agent) for sale
- B pledges goods to C (lender) for personal loan (without A's knowledge)
- C acts in good faith; no notice of B's lack of authority

**Analysis**:
- **Section 178**: Mercantile agent in possession with owner's consent can pledge
- **C's protection**: If (1) B is mercantile agent, (2) in possession with A's consent, (3) C in good faith, (4) ordinary course of business
- **Result**: C gets valid pledge (good title); A cannot reclaim goods from C (but can sue B for breach)

### Example 6: Bailment for Reward - Reasonable Care

**Facts**:
- A deposits jewelry in B's bank locker (paid service)
- Thieves break into bank; cut locker wall with gas cutter; steal jewelry
- Bank had: (1) Security guards, (2) CCTV, (3) Alarm system
- Theft highly sophisticated (gas cutter; avoided alarms)

**Analysis**:
- **Section 152**: Bailee for reward must take reasonable care
- **Not insurer**: Not liable for loss despite care
- **Reasonable care**: Bank had adequate security (guards, CCTV, alarm)
- **Extraordinary means**: Thieves used sophisticated method
- **Result**: Bank NOT liable (took reasonable care; loss due to extraordinary means beyond prevention)

**But**: If inadequate security (e.g., no guard, broken locks) → Bank liable (negligence)

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Confusing Bailment with Sale

**Mistake**: Treating transfer of possession as transfer of ownership

**Correct**: 
- **Bailment**: Possession transfers (ownership remains with bailor)
- **Sale**: Ownership transfers (Section 4 Sale of Goods Act)

### 2. Assuming Bailee is Insurer

**Mistake**: Expecting bailee liable for all loss (even if bailee took care)

**Correct**: Bailee liable only for negligence (Section 152); not liable for unavoidable loss despite care

### 3. Using Bailed Goods Without Permission

**Mistake**: Bailee uses goods for different purpose (thinking minor change acceptable)

**Correct**: Unauthorized use = liable for all loss (even without negligence - Section 154)

### 4. Pawnee Selling Without Notice

**Mistake**: Pawnee sells pledged goods immediately on default (without notice to pawnor)

**Correct**: Must give reasonable notice (Section 176); pawnor has right to redeem until sale

### 5. Pawnor Assuming Cannot Redeem After Default

**Mistake**: Thinking once defaulted, pledge is lost (no redemption possible)

**Correct**: Pawnor can redeem anytime before sale (by paying all dues - Section 177)
