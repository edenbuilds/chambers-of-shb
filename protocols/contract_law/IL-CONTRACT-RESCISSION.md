# Contract Rescission Protocol (India)

## Protocol Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-CONTRACT-RESCISSION
- **Version**: 1.0.0
- **Domain**: Contract Law (India)
- **Subdomain**: Remedies - Rescission
- **Statute**: Indian Contract Act 1872 (Sections 19, 19A, 39, 64-67), Specific Relief Act 1963 (Section 27)
- **Last Updated**: January 2025
- **Effective**: Since 1872

---

## Overview

This protocol covers **rescission** (cancellation) of contracts - the equitable remedy of terminating a contract and restoring parties to their pre-contract position when contract is voidable due to vitiated consent, misrepresentation, breach, or other grounds.

**Key Principle**: Rescission aims to undo contract and restore status quo ante (position before contract).

---

## Nature of Rescission

**Rescission**: Cancellation of contract + Restoration to original position

**Effect**:
- Contract treated as if never existed
- Parties restored to pre-contract position (restitutio in integrum)
- No further obligations

**Equitable Remedy**: Court's discretion (not absolute right)

---

## Grounds for Rescission

### 1. Voidable Contracts (Sections 19, 19A)

**Vitiated Consent**: Contract voidable at option of aggrieved party

#### A. Coercion (Section 15)

**Section 19**: "When consent to an agreement is caused by coercion, fraud or misrepresentation, the agreement is a contract voidable at the option of the party whose consent was so caused."

**Example**: Threat of violence to sign contract → Victim can rescind

#### B. Undue Influence (Section 16)

**Section 19A**: "When consent to an agreement is caused by undue influence, the agreement is a contract voidable at the option of the party whose consent was so caused..."

**Example**: Doctor persuades patient to gift property → Patient can rescind

#### C. Fraud (Section 17)

**Fraud**: Intentional false representation inducing contract

**Example**: Seller falsely states car never had accident → Buyer can rescind + damages

#### D. Misrepresentation (Section 18)

**Innocent Misrepresentation**: False statement without intent to deceive

**Example**: Seller honestly but mistakenly states property boundary → Buyer can rescind (no damages)

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-FREE-CONSENT protocol

### 2. Breach of Contract (Section 39, 75)

**Section 39**: "When a party to a contract has refused to perform, or disabled himself from performing, his promise in its entirety, the promisee may put an end to the contract..."

**Material Breach**: Breach going to root of contract

**Repudiation**: Party refuses to perform (anticipatory or actual breach)

**Effect**: Innocent party can rescind + damages

**Example**: Seller repudiates sale before delivery date → Buyer can rescind + damages

### 3. Mutual Mistake (Section 20)

**Bilateral Mistake**: Both parties mistaken about essential fact

**Effect**: Contract VOID (not voidable; automatically rescinded)

**Example**: Both believe painting is original; it's fake → Contract void

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-FREE-CONSENT protocol

### 4. Failure of Consideration

**Total Failure**: Consideration promised but not provided

**Example**: A pays advance for goods; B doesn't deliver → A can rescind + recover advance

### 5. Impossibility / Frustration (Section 56)

**Supervening Impossibility**: Performance becomes impossible after contract

**Effect**: Contract VOID (automatic rescission)

**Example**: Subject matter destroyed → Contract void

**See**: IL-CONTRACT-IMPOSSIBILITY protocol

## Rescission ab initio vs Rescission for Breach

### Rescission ab initio (From Beginning)

**Ground**: Defect in formation (fraud, misrepresentation, coercion, undue influence)

**Effect**: Contract treated as never existed

**Restitution**: Section 64 - Restore benefits received

### Rescission for Breach

**Ground**: Breach during performance

**Effect**: Contract terminated from breach date (not from beginning)

**Damages**: Innocent party can claim damages for breach + rescind

**Section 75**: "A person who rightfully rescinds a contract is entitled to compensation for any damage which he has sustained through the non-fulfillment of the contract."

---

## Effects of Rescission (Section 64)

### Section 64: Consequences of Rescission

```
"When a person at whose option a contract is voidable rescinds it, the other party thereto need not perform any promise therein contained in which he is promisor. The party rescinding a voidable contract shall, if he has received any benefit thereunder from another party to such contract, restore such benefit, so far as may be, to the person from whom it was received."
```

**Mutual Restoration**:
1. **Rescinding party**: Must return benefit received
2. **Other party**: Released from obligations; must return benefit

**"So far as may be"**: Exact restoration if possible; if not, compensate

**Example**: 
- Buyer paid Rs. 10 lakhs; received car
- Buyer rescinds (fraud)
- Buyer returns car; Seller returns Rs. 10 lakhs

## Loss of Right to Rescind

**When can aggrieved party NOT rescind?**

### 1. Affirmation (Express or Implied)

**Affirmation**: Party with right to rescind chooses to continue contract

**Express Affirmation**: Clearly states will continue with contract

**Implied Affirmation**: Conduct indicating acceptance

**Example**: Buyer discovers fraud; continues using goods for 6 months → Implied affirmation (lost right to rescind)

**Case: Long v. Lloyd (1958)**:
- Buyer purchased lorry based on misrepresentation
- Buyer used lorry, attempted repairs
- Court held: Implied affirmation; cannot rescind

### 2. Lapse of Time

**Unreasonable Delay**: Aggrieved party delays rescission unreasonably

**Reasonable Time**: Depends on circumstances

**Fraud**: Time runs from **discovery** of fraud (not contract date)

**Misrepresentation**: Time generally runs from contract date

**Example**: Misrepresentation discovered 5 years after contract → Too late to rescind (laches)

**Case: Leaf v. International Galleries (1950)**:
- Painting misrepresented as by famous artist
- Buyer discovered mistake 5 years later
- Court held: Too late to rescind (lapse of time)

### 3. Third Party Rights

**Bona Fide Purchaser**: Innocent third party acquires rights

**Rule**: If third party acquired rights in good faith for value → Cannot rescind against third party

**Example**:
- A sells car to B (induced by fraud)
- B sells car to C (innocent buyer)
- A discovers fraud
- A cannot rescind and recover car from C (C protected)
- **Remedy**: A can sue B for damages (tort of deceit)

**Exception**: If third party had notice of fraud → Not protected

### 4. Impossibility of Restitution

**Cannot Restore**: If benefit cannot be returned → May lose right to rescind

**Example**: Aggrieved party consumed goods → Cannot return

**But**: Court may allow rescission with compensation for consumed goods (Section 64: "so far as may be")

**Substantial Alteration**: If subject matter substantially altered → Restitution impossible

**Example**: Buyer bought cloth, made suits → Cannot return cloth as is (substantial alteration)

## Rescission Procedure

### 1. Extra-Judicial Rescission

**Without Court**: Party can rescind by notice to other party

**Requirements**:
1. **Clear communication** of rescission
2. **Return** or offer to return benefit received
3. **Immediate** (no delay)

**Advantage**: Quick; no court involvement

**Risk**: If wrongfully rescinded → Liable for breach

### 2. Judicial Rescission (Section 27 Specific Relief Act)

**Through Court**: File suit for rescission

**Specific Relief Act, Section 27**: Court can rescind contract

**When Necessary**:
- Dispute about right to rescind
- Restitution complex
- Other party denies rescission

**Procedure**:
1. File suit for rescission
2. Plead grounds (fraud, misrepresentation, breach)
3. Offer to restore benefit
4. Court orders rescission + restitution

## Rescission vs Other Remedies

### Rescission vs Damages

| Aspect | Rescission | Damages |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **Nature** | Equitable (cancel contract) | Legal (monetary compensation) |
| **Effect** | Contract undone; restitution | Contract continues; money paid |
| **When** | Voidable contracts, material breach | Any breach |
| **Plus** | Can claim damages too (fraud, breach) | Cannot rescind |

**Combined**: Rescission + Damages available (fraud, breach)

### Rescission vs Specific Performance

**Opposite Remedies**:
- **Specific Performance**: Compel performance (enforce contract)
- **Rescission**: Cancel contract (avoid performance)

**Cannot Seek Both**: Choose one (but can plead in alternative)

### Rescission vs Termination

**Rescission** (Voidable contracts):
- Undo from beginning (ab initio)
- Restore status quo ante
- Equitable remedy

**Termination** (Breach):
- End contract from breach date (not retroactive)
- No performance from breach onwards
- Damages for breach

---

## Partial Rescission

**General Rule**: Rescission is total (entire contract cancelled)

**Exception (Section 19A)**: Undue influence cases

**Section 19A**: Court may:
- Set aside contract entirely, OR
- Require return of benefit, OR
- **Set aside only part** (retain rest)

**Example**: Sale of property under undue influence at Rs. 10 lakhs (worth Rs. 50 lakhs)
- Court may: (1) Rescind entirely, OR (2) Uphold sale but increase price to Rs. 50 lakhs

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Rescission for Fraud

**Facts**:
- A sells car to B for Rs. 5 lakhs
- A falsely states "never in accident" (major accident occurred)
- B discovers fraud after 2 months

**Analysis**:
- **Fraud**: Intentional false representation (Section 17)
- **Voidable**: Section 19
- **B's options**: (1) Rescind + return car + recover Rs. 5 lakhs + damages (tort of deceit), OR (2) Affirm + claim damages only
- **Result**: B can rescind (discovered recently; no affirmation)

### Example 2: Loss of Right (Third Party)

**Facts**:
- A sells car to B (fraud)
- B sells car to C (innocent buyer for value)
- A discovers fraud; seeks to rescind and recover car from C

**Analysis**:
- **Third party rights**: C is bona fide purchaser
- **Protection**: C protected (acquired in good faith for value)
- **Result**: A cannot rescind against C; can only sue B for damages

### Example 3: Affirmation (Implied)

**Facts**:
- A buys property from B based on misrepresentation
- A discovers misrepresentation after 6 months
- A continues living in property for 2 more years
- A then seeks rescission

**Analysis**:
- **Lapse of time**: 2 years after discovery
- **Implied affirmation**: Continued use
- **Result**: Right to rescind LOST (affirmation by conduct + lapse)

### Example 4: Rescission for Breach

**Facts**:
- A (seller) contracts to deliver goods to B on June 1
- On May 15, A repudiates (says won't deliver)
- B rescinds immediately

**Analysis**:
- **Anticipatory breach**: Section 39
- **Material breach**: Total refusal (repudiation)
- **B's rights**: Rescind + damages
- **Result**: Contract rescinded; B can sue for damages (buy from market; claim difference + consequential loss)

### Example 5: Impossibility of Restitution

**Facts**:
- A buys cloth from B (100 meters) based on misrepresentation
- A makes suits from cloth (all consumed)
- A discovers misrepresentation; seeks rescission

**Analysis**:
- **Restitution impossible**: Cloth consumed/substantially altered
- **General rule**: Cannot rescind if cannot restore
- **Section 64**: "So far as may be"
- **Possible outcome**: Court may allow rescission with compensation for cloth value (not return of cloth itself)

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Delay in Rescinding

**Mistake**: Discovering fraud/misrepresentation but delaying rescission for years

**Correct**: Rescind promptly after discovery; unreasonable delay = loss of right (laches)

### 2. Affirming After Knowledge

**Mistake**: Continuing to use benefit after discovering fraud, then trying to rescind

**Correct**: Rescind immediately upon discovery; continued use = implied affirmation

### 3. Not Offering Restitution

**Mistake**: Seeking rescission without offering to return benefit

**Correct**: Must restore benefit (or offer to restore) when rescinding (Section 64)

### 4. Forgetting Third Party Rights

**Mistake**: Assuming can always rescind and recover property

**Correct**: If bona fide third party acquired rights → Cannot rescind against them

### 5. Confusing Rescission with Termination

**Mistake**: Using terms interchangeably

**Correct**:
- **Rescission**: Undo from beginning (voidable contracts)
- **Termination**: End from breach date (breach cases)
