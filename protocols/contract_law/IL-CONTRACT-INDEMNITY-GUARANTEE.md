# Indemnity and Guarantee Protocol (India)

## Protocol Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-CONTRACT-INDEMNITY-GUARANTEE
- **Version**: 1.0.0
- **Domain**: Contract Law (India)
- **Subdomain**: Special Contracts - Indemnity & Guarantee
- **Statute**: Indian Contract Act 1872 (Sections 124-147)
- **Last Updated**: January 2025
- **Effective**: Since 1872

---

## Overview

This protocol covers two distinct but related special contracts:
1. **Contract of Indemnity** (Sections 124-125): Promise to compensate for loss
2. **Contract of Guarantee** (Sections 126-147): Promise to answer for another's debt/default

**Key Distinction**: Indemnity has TWO parties (indemnifier, indemnity-holder); Guarantee has THREE parties (creditor, principal debtor, surety).

---

## PART A: CONTRACT OF INDEMNITY

### Section 124: Contract of Indemnity Defined

```
"A contract by which one party promises to save the other from loss caused to him by the conduct of the promisor himself, or by the conduct of any other person, is called a 'contract of indemnity'."
```

**Parties**:
1. **Indemnifier**: Person who promises to indemnify (make good the loss)
2. **Indemnity-holder**: Person whose loss is to be made good

**Essential Elements**:
1. Promise to make good loss
2. Loss caused by indemnifier's conduct OR any other person's conduct
3. Consideration (must be present like any contract)

**Example**: A promises to compensate B for any loss B may suffer if B lends money to C. A is indemnifier; B is indemnity-holder.

---

### Types of Indemnity

#### 1. Express Indemnity

**Explicitly stated** in contract

**Example**: Insurance contracts (insurer indemnifies insured)

#### 2. Implied Indemnity

**Implied by law** from circumstances

**Example**: 
- Principal-agent relationship (principal indemnifies agent for lawful acts)
- Director acts on company's behalf (company indemnifies)

---

### Section 125: Rights of Indemnity-Holder

**Indemnity-holder can recover** from indemnifier:

```
"The promisee in a contract of indemnity, acting within the scope of his authority, is entitled to recover from the promisor—

(1) all damages which he may be compelled to pay in any suit in respect of any matter to which the promise to indemnify applies;

(2) all costs which he may be compelled to pay in any such suit if, in bringing or defending it, he did not contravene the orders of the promisor, and acted as it would have been prudent for him to act in the absence of any contract of indemnity, or if the promisor authorized him to bring or defend the suit;

(3) all sums which he may have paid under the terms of any compromise of any such suit, if the compromise was not contrary to the orders of the promisor, and was one which it would have been prudent for the promisee to make in the absence of any contract of indemnity, or if the promisor authorized him to compromise the suit."
```

**Three Categories of Recoverable Amounts**:

#### 1. Damages Compelled to Pay

**All damages** paid in suit relating to indemnified matter

**Example**: 
- A indemnifies B against loss from C
- C sues B; court awards Rs. 10 lakhs to C
- B can recover Rs. 10 lakhs from A (indemnifier)

#### 2. Costs of Suit

**Legal costs** incurred in bringing/defending suit

**Conditions**:
- Did not contravene indemnifier's orders, AND
- Acted prudently (as would have acted without indemnity), OR
- Indemnifier authorized suit

**Example**: B incurs Rs. 2 lakhs legal fees defending against C → B can recover from A (if prudent/authorized)

#### 3. Compromise Amounts

**Sums paid** in settlement/compromise

**Conditions**:
- Not contrary to indemnifier's orders, AND
- Prudent compromise, OR
- Indemnifier authorized compromise

**Example**: B settles with C for Rs. 5 lakhs (instead of fighting for Rs. 10 lakhs claim) → B can recover Rs. 5 lakhs from A (if prudent)

---

### When Does Indemnity-Holder's Right Arise?

**Indian Law** (Gajanan Moreshwar v. Moreshwar Madan - 1942, Bombay HC):

**ONLY after indemnity-holder suffers actual loss** (not when liability arises)

**Example**:
- C sues B for Rs. 10 lakhs
- A (indemnifier) promises to indemnify B
- **Indian view**: B can claim from A only after court orders B to pay (not when suit filed)

**English Law** (contrast): Indemnity-holder can claim when liability arises (even before actual payment)

**Criticism**: Indian view may leave indemnity-holder without funds to pay; some courts now allow claim when liability definite (even if not yet paid)

---

### Indemnity in Insurance Contracts

**Insurance = Contract of Indemnity** (except life insurance)

**Insurer indemnifies insured** against specified losses

**Principles**:
1. **Insurable Interest**: Insured must have interest in subject matter
2. **Utmost Good Faith** (uberrima fides): Full disclosure required
3. **Indemnity**: Compensate actual loss (not profit)
4. **Subrogation**: Insurer steps into insured's shoes (can sue wrongdoer)
5. **Contribution**: If multiple insurers, share loss proportionately

**Example**: Fire insurance on house (Rs. 50 lakhs insured value)
- House burns; actual loss Rs. 40 lakhs
- Insurer pays Rs. 40 lakhs (indemnity for actual loss)

## PART B: CONTRACT OF GUARANTEE

### Section 126: Contract of Guarantee Defined

```
"A 'contract of guarantee' is a contract to perform the promise, or discharge the liability, of a third person in case of his default. The person who gives the guarantee is called the 'surety'; the person in respect of whose default the guarantee is given is called the 'principal debtor', and the person to whom the guarantee is given is called the 'creditor'. A guarantee may be either oral or written."
```

**Three Parties**:
1. **Creditor**: Person to whom guarantee given (lender, seller)
2. **Principal Debtor**: Person whose debt/default guaranteed (borrower, buyer)
3. **Surety**: Person who gives guarantee (guarantor)

**Nature**: **Surety promises creditor** to pay/perform if principal debtor defaults

**Example**: 
- C (bank) lends Rs. 10 lakhs to B (borrower)
- A signs as guarantor (surety)
- If B defaults, A must pay C

---

### Essential Features

#### 1. Three Parties Required

**Unlike indemnity** (two parties)

#### 2. Concurrence of Three Parties

**All three must agree** (creditor, principal debtor, surety)

#### 3. Primary Liability on Principal Debtor

**Surety's liability = Secondary** (only if principal debtor defaults)

**Primary obligation**: Principal debtor must pay

**Surety's obligation**: Contingent on default

#### 4. Existing or Future Debt

**Can guarantee existing debt** OR future debt

**Example**: Guarantee for running account (future transactions)

#### 5. Oral or Written

**Section 126**: May be oral or written (BUT some statutes require writing, e.g., Statute of Frauds in some jurisdictions)

**Consideration**: Must be present (BUT consideration from creditor to principal debtor sufficient - Section 127)

---

### Section 127: Consideration for Guarantee

```
"Anything done, or any promise made, for the benefit of the principal debtor, may be a sufficient consideration to the surety for giving the guarantee."
```

**Rule**: **Consideration to principal debtor** is sufficient consideration for surety's guarantee

**Surety need not receive direct benefit**

**Example**:
- Bank lends Rs. 10 lakhs to B (principal debtor)
- A guarantees loan (surety)
- **Consideration**: Loan to B (not to A) is sufficient consideration for A's guarantee

**Case: Kashiba v. Shripat (1894)**:
- C lent money to B
- A guaranteed repayment
- Court held: Loan to B is consideration for A's guarantee (A need not receive anything)

---

### Section 128: Surety's Liability

```
"The liability of the surety is co-extensive with that of the principal debtor, unless it is otherwise provided by the contract."
```

**Co-extensive Liability**: Surety liable for **same amount** and **same extent** as principal debtor

**Example**: 
- Principal debtor owes Rs. 10 lakhs + interest
- Surety liable for Rs. 10 lakhs + interest (same extent)

**But**: Contract can **limit** surety's liability

**Example**: "Guarantee limited to Rs. 5 lakhs" → Surety liable only up to Rs. 5 lakhs (even if debt Rs. 10 lakhs)

---

### Types of Guarantee

#### 1. Specific Guarantee (Section 129)

**For single transaction** or specific debt

**Discharge**: When guaranteed debt paid or contract performed

**Example**: A guarantees B's loan of Rs. 5 lakhs from C (specific loan) → Surety's liability ends when Rs. 5 lakhs repaid

#### 2. Continuing Guarantee (Section 129)

**For series of transactions** (running account, multiple debts)

**Extends to**: All transactions until revoked

**Example**: A guarantees B's account with C (supplier) up to Rs. 10 lakhs → Covers all transactions until A revokes

**Section 130**: Continuing guarantee for future transactions revocable (as to future transactions) by:
- Notice to creditor (by surety), OR
- Death of surety (for future transactions; existing liabilities continue)

---

### Rights of Surety

#### 1. Rights Against Creditor

##### a) Section 141: Creditor Must Sue Principal Debtor First?

**General Rule**: **NO** - Creditor can sue surety directly (without suing principal debtor first)

**Surety's liability = Joint with principal debtor** (creditor's choice whom to sue)

**But**: Contract may require creditor to first proceed against principal debtor

##### b) Section 133: Release by Creditor

**If creditor releases principal debtor** → Surety also discharged

**Reason**: Surety's right of subrogation (sue principal debtor) would be affected

**Example**: C agrees to release B from Rs. 10 lakhs debt → A (surety) also discharged

**Exception**: If release is with surety's consent OR "without prejudice to surety's rights"

##### c) Section 134: Material Variance

**If contract between creditor and principal debtor varied** (without surety's consent) → Surety discharged (for transactions after variance)

**Material Variance**: Significant change affecting surety's risk

**Example**: 
- Original loan: Rs. 5 lakhs at 10% interest
- Varied to: Rs. 10 lakhs at 15% interest (without A's consent)
- A (surety) discharged

**Case: Bank of Bihar v. Damodar Prasad (1969)** (Supreme Court):
- Bank agreed to reduce interest (variation)
- Court held: Material alteration without surety's consent → Surety discharged

##### d) Section 135: Creditor's Conduct

**If creditor does any act** (or omits to do act) that impairs surety's remedy against principal debtor → Surety discharged

**Examples**:
- Creditor releases security provided by principal debtor
- Creditor fails to register charge (loses priority)

**Section 139**: Creditor's release of co-surety (without consent) → Other sureties discharged (to extent of released surety's share)

##### e) Section 142: Guarantee Obtained by Misrepresentation/Concealment

**If guarantee obtained by**:
- **Misrepresentation** by creditor, OR
- **Concealment of material facts** (relating to principal debtor's credit/affairs)

**Effect**: Guarantee INVALID

**Example**: Bank conceals principal debtor's prior defaults → Surety's guarantee invalid

**But**: If surety knew facts OR creditor had no knowledge → Guarantee valid

#### 2. Rights Against Principal Debtor

##### a) Section 140: Right of Subrogation

**After paying creditor**, surety is **subrogated** to all creditor's rights against principal debtor

**Subrogation**: Surety steps into creditor's shoes

**Surety can**:
1. Sue principal debtor for amount paid
2. Enforce all securities held by creditor
3. Stand in creditor's place (as if surety paid his own debt and is now creditor)

**Example**:
- A (surety) pays Rs. 10 lakhs to C (creditor) on B's default
- A now has all C's rights:
  - Sue B for Rs. 10 lakhs
  - Enforce mortgage held by C
  - Claim interest, costs (as C could)

**Section 140 applies even if surety pays part** (subrogated pro rata)

##### b) Section 145: Right to Indemnity

**Surety has right** to be indemnified by principal debtor

**Implied contract**: Principal debtor must indemnify surety for all amounts paid + costs

**Example**: A pays Rs. 10 lakhs + Rs. 1 lakh legal fees → B must indemnify A for Rs. 11 lakhs

#### 3. Rights Against Co-Sureties

##### a) Section 146: Contribution Among Co-Sureties

**If multiple sureties** guarantee same debt → Liable to contribute equally (unless contract provides otherwise)

**Equal Contribution** (in absence of agreement)

**Example**:
- Principal debtor defaults on Rs. 30 lakhs
- Three sureties: A, B, C (no limit specified)
- Each liable for Rs. 10 lakhs (equal contribution)

**Unequal Amounts Guaranteed**:
- A guarantees Rs. 20 lakhs
- B guarantees Rs. 10 lakhs
- Principal debtor defaults on Rs. 30 lakhs
- **Contribution**: Pro rata to guaranteed amounts
  - A pays Rs. 20 lakhs (max)
  - B pays Rs. 10 lakhs (max)
  - Total Rs. 30 lakhs covered

**If one surety pays more than his share** → Can claim contribution from co-sureties

---

### Discharge of Surety

**Surety's liability ends** in following cases:

#### 1. Payment / Performance (Section 143)

**Discharge**: When principal debtor pays debt or performs obligation

**Complete satisfaction** of creditor → Surety discharged

#### 2. Release by Creditor (Section 133)

**If creditor releases principal debtor** → Surety discharged

**If creditor releases co-surety** (Section 138) → Other sureties discharged to extent of released surety's share

#### 3. Variation in Contract (Section 133)

**Material alteration** without surety's consent → Surety discharged

#### 4. Creditor's Act/Omission (Section 135)

**Creditor does act** impairing surety's remedy → Surety discharged

**Examples**:
- Releases security
- Fails to sue within limitation period
- Gives time to principal debtor without surety's consent (Section 136)

**Section 136**: Giving time to principal debtor = Variance (surety discharged)

**Exception**: Agreement "without prejudice to surety"

#### 5. Revocation (Continuing Guarantee - Section 130)

**Continuing guarantee**: Revocable by notice (for future transactions)

**Death of surety**: Continuing guarantee terminates (for future transactions)

#### 6. Loss of Security (Section 141)

**If creditor loses or parts with security** (without surety's consent) → Surety discharged (to extent of value of security lost)

#### 7. Composition or Promise Not to Sue (Section 143)

**If creditor compounds with principal debtor** OR agrees not to sue → Surety discharged (unless surety consents)

## Indemnity vs. Guarantee: Key Differences

| Aspect | Indemnity | Guarantee |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Parties** | TWO (indemnifier, indemnity-holder) | THREE (creditor, principal debtor, surety) |
| **Nature of Liability** | Primary (indemnifier's own act or another's act) | Secondary (contingent on principal debtor's default) |
| **Liability Arises** | When loss occurs | When principal debtor defaults |
| **Request** | No request from third party (indemnifier promises to save from loss) | Surety at request of principal debtor (or creditor) |
| **Right to Sue Third Party** | Indemnity-holder can sue if loss by third party | Surety can sue principal debtor after payment |
| **Discharge** | By payment or performance | By payment, release, variance, creditor's act, etc. (Sections 133-141) |
| **Number of Contracts** | ONE (between indemnifier and indemnity-holder) | THREE (creditor-debtor, creditor-surety, surety-debtor) |
| **Examples** | Insurance, principal-agent indemnity | Loan guarantees, performance bonds |

**Case: Bank of Bihar v. Damodar Prasad (1969)** (Supreme Court):
- Distinguished indemnity and guarantee
- Key: If liability primary (indemnifier's own promise to pay) → Indemnity
- If liability secondary (pay if another defaults) → Guarantee

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Indemnity - Section 125 Rights

**Facts**:
- A (landlord) indemnifies B (tenant) against loss from C (third party claiming ownership)
- C sues B for possession
- B defends; incurs Rs. 2 lakhs legal costs
- Court orders B to pay C Rs. 10 lakhs (C proved ownership)

**Analysis**:
- **Contract of indemnity**: A promised to save B from loss
- **Section 125(1)**: Damages compelled to pay → Rs. 10 lakhs recoverable from A
- **Section 125(2)**: Costs of defending → Rs. 2 lakhs recoverable (if prudent defense)
- **Total**: B can recover Rs. 12 lakhs from A (indemnifier)

### Example 2: Guarantee - Co-extensive Liability

**Facts**:
- C (bank) lends Rs. 10 lakhs to B (borrower) at 12% interest
- A signs as surety (guarantees repayment)
- B defaults after paying Rs. 3 lakhs; owes Rs. 7 lakhs + interest Rs. 1 lakh

**Analysis**:
- **Section 128**: Surety's liability co-extensive with principal debtor
- **A's liability**: Rs. 7 lakhs (principal) + Rs. 1 lakh (interest) = Rs. 8 lakhs
- **Creditor's choice**: C can sue A directly (need not sue B first - Section 141)

### Example 3: Material Variance - Surety Discharged

**Facts**:
- A guarantees B's loan of Rs. 5 lakhs from C at 10% interest (3-year term)
- After 1 year, C and B agree: Increase to Rs. 8 lakhs at 15% interest (without A's consent)
- B defaults on Rs. 8 lakhs

**Analysis**:
- **Material variance** (Section 134): Amount increased, interest increased
- **Without surety's consent**: A not consulted
- **Effect**: A discharged (for variation part)
- **Result**: A liable only for original Rs. 5 lakhs at 10% (not Rs. 8 lakhs at 15%)

### Example 4: Continuing Guarantee - Revocation

**Facts**:
- A guarantees B's account with C (supplier) up to Rs. 20 lakhs (continuing guarantee)
- Transactions: Month 1 (Rs. 5 lakhs), Month 2 (Rs. 3 lakhs), Month 3 (Rs. 4 lakhs)
- After Month 3, A revokes guarantee by notice to C
- Month 4: B buys Rs. 6 lakhs goods; defaults on entire Rs. 18 lakhs

**Analysis**:
- **Section 130**: Continuing guarantee revocable by notice
- **Revocation effect**: Applies to future transactions only
- **A's liability**: Months 1-3 (Rs. 12 lakhs) + any transaction before revocation notice
- **Not liable**: Month 4 (Rs. 6 lakhs) if transaction after revocation notice received by C
- **Result**: A liable up to Rs. 12 lakhs (transactions before revocation)

### Example 5: Surety's Right of Subrogation

**Facts**:
- C lends Rs. 10 lakhs to B (secured by mortgage on B's property)
- A (surety) guarantees loan
- B defaults; A pays Rs. 10 lakhs to C

**Analysis**:
- **Section 140**: Surety subrogated to creditor's rights
- **A's rights after payment**:
  1. Sue B for Rs. 10 lakhs (right of indemnity - Section 145)
  2. Enforce mortgage on B's property (subrogation to C's security)
  3. Claim interest, costs as C could have
- **Result**: A has all remedies C had against B

### Example 6: Contribution Among Co-Sureties

**Facts**:
- Principal debtor owes Rs. 30 lakhs
- Three sureties: A (guaranteed Rs. 15 lakhs), B (guaranteed Rs. 10 lakhs), C (guaranteed Rs. 5 lakhs)
- Creditor sues A; A pays full Rs. 30 lakhs

**Analysis**:
- **Section 146**: Co-sureties contribute equally (or pro rata if unequal guarantees)
- **Guaranteed amounts total**: Rs. 30 lakhs (Rs. 15L + Rs. 10L + Rs. 5L)
- **Each liable**: Pro rata to guaranteed amount
  - A: (15/30) × Rs. 30L = Rs. 15 lakhs
  - B: (10/30) × Rs. 30L = Rs. 10 lakhs
  - C: (5/30) × Rs. 30L = Rs. 5 lakhs
- **A paid Rs. 30 lakhs**: Can claim contribution:
  - From B: Rs. 10 lakhs
  - From C: Rs. 5 lakhs
- **A's final liability**: Rs. 15 lakhs (after contribution)

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Confusing Indemnity with Guarantee

**Mistake**: Treating two-party promise to pay as guarantee

**Correct**: 
- **Two parties** → Indemnity
- **Three parties** (creditor, debtor, surety) → Guarantee

### 2. Assuming Creditor Must Sue Principal Debtor First

**Mistake**: Thinking surety liable only after creditor exhausts remedies against principal debtor

**Correct**: Creditor can sue surety directly (Section 141); surety's liability joint with principal debtor

### 3. Not Getting Surety's Consent for Variations

**Mistake**: Creditor and principal debtor vary contract (increase amount, extend time) without informing surety

**Correct**: Material variation without surety's consent discharges surety (Section 134)

### 4. Releasing Principal Debtor Without Considering Surety

**Mistake**: Creditor releases principal debtor, thinking can still claim from surety

**Correct**: Releasing principal debtor discharges surety (Section 133) unless release "without prejudice to surety"

### 5. Continuing Guarantee After Surety's Death

**Mistake**: Creditor continues extending credit after surety's death, expecting surety's estate liable

**Correct**: Death revokes continuing guarantee (for future transactions - Section 130); estate liable only for debts up to death
