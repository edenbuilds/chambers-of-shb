# Lawful Object and Consideration Protocol (India)

## Protocol Metadata
- **Protocol ID**: IL-CONTRACT-LEGALITY
- **Version**: 1.0.0
- **Domain**: Contract Law (India)
- **Subdomain**: Contract Formation - Legality
- **Statute**: Indian Contract Act 1872 (Sections 23-24, 26-30)
- **Last Updated**: January 2025
- **Effective**: Since 1872

---

## Overview

This protocol covers **legality of object and consideration** - essential elements for valid contracts. Agreements with unlawful object or unlawful consideration are **VOID** (not merely voidable).

**Key Principle**: Contracts must serve lawful purposes and not contravene law, morality, or public policy.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 10: Lawfulness Required

```
"All agreements are contracts if they are made... for a lawful consideration and with a lawful object..."
```

**Lawful Object AND Lawful Consideration** both required

### Section 23: Unlawful Consideration and Object

```
"The consideration or object of an agreement is lawful, unless—
(1) it is forbidden by law; or
(2) is of such a nature that, if permitted, it would defeat the provisions of any law; or
(3) is fraudulent; or
(4) involves or implies injury to the person or property of another; or
(5) the Court regards it as immoral, or opposed to public policy.

In each of these cases, the consideration or object of an agreement is said to be unlawful. Every agreement of which the object or consideration is unlawful is void."
```

**Five Grounds of Unlawfulness**:
1. Forbidden by law
2. Defeats provisions of law
3. Fraudulent
4. Injurious to person/property
5. Immoral or opposed to public policy

---

## Ground 1: Forbidden by Law

### Definition

**Forbidden by Law**: Prohibited by statute or regulation

**Effect**: Agreement VOID

### Examples

#### 1. Agreements to Commit Crime

**Example**: Contract to murder someone for payment → VOID

**Case: Gherulal Parekh v. Mahadeodas (1959)**:
- Agreement to smuggle goods (prohibited under Customs Act)
- Court held: VOID (forbidden by law)

#### 2. Wagering Agreements (Section 30)

**Section 30**: "Agreements by way of wager are void..."

**Wager**: Bet or gamble where parties have opposite interests; one wins, other loses

**Elements of Wager**:
1. **Future uncertain event** (outcome unknown)
2. **Mutual chance** of gain or loss
3. **No control** over event
4. **No other interest** except winning bet

**Example**: Bet on cricket match outcome → VOID

**Exceptions** (NOT wagering):
- **Insurance contracts**: Insurable interest exists (not wager)
- **Prize competitions**: Skill-based competitions (not wager)
- **Horse racing**: Expressly permitted by statute

**Case: Carlill v. Carbolic Smoke Ball**: Reward for using product NOT wager (unilateral offer; consideration given)

#### 3. Unlicensed Business Agreements

**Example**: Contract by unlicensed money-lender (where license required by law) → VOID

**Case: Smith v. Mawhood (1845)**:
- Contract by unlicensed tobacco seller (license required)
- Court held: VOID (prohibited by law)

#### 4. Agreements for Prohibited Goods/Services

**Examples**:
- Sale of narcotics (prohibited under NDPS Act)
- Sale of human organs (prohibited under Transplantation of Human Organs Act)
- Child labor contracts (prohibited under Child Labour Act)

## Ground 2: Defeats Provisions of Law

### Definition

**Defeats Provisions of Law**: Agreement that, if enforced, would nullify or circumvent statutory provisions

**Effect**: Agreement VOID

**Rationale**: Cannot do indirectly what cannot be done directly

### Examples

#### 1. Agreements to Defeat Stamp Duty

**Example**: Two documents executed - one genuine (understating price to avoid stamp duty), one collateral (actual price) → Both VOID

**Case: Smt. Bibi Sayeeda v. Nawab Mohd. Najmul Ikram Khan (1940)**:
- Sale deed understated price to evade stamp duty
- Collateral agreement for balance payment
- Court held: Both documents VOID (defeat stamp law)

#### 2. Agreements to Defeat Insolvency Laws

**Example**: Insolvent transfers property to friend to hide from creditors → VOID

**Fraudulent preference**: Transfer to defeat creditors' rights

#### 3. Agreements in Restraint of Marriage (Section 26)

**Section 26**: "Every agreement in restraint of the marriage of any person, other than a minor, is void."

**Rule**: Cannot prevent adult from marrying

**Example**: Father's will: "Daughter gets property only if she never marries" → Condition VOID

**Exception**: Minor's marriage can be restricted (parent's right)

**Case: Lowe v. Peers (1768)**:
- Promise by man not to marry in return for payment
- Court held: VOID (restraint of marriage)

#### 4. Agreements in Restraint of Parental Rights

**Example**: Agreement that parent will not have custody of child → VOID (defeats family law)

---

## Ground 3: Fraudulent

### Definition

**Fraudulent Agreement**: Agreement itself is fraudulent scheme

**Effect**: Agreement VOID

**Distinction**: 
- **Fraud inducing contract**: Contract voidable (Section 17)
- **Fraudulent agreement**: Agreement itself fraud → VOID (Section 23)

### Examples

#### 1. Agreement to Defraud Third Party

**Example**: A and B agree to falsely claim C owes them money → VOID

**Case: Alexander v. Rayson (1936)**:
- Landlord and tenant agreed to split rent into two agreements (one actual, one false)
- Purpose: Reduce tenant's apparent liability (to defraud rating authority)
- Court held: VOID (fraudulent agreement)

#### 2. Conspiracy to Defraud

**Example**: Bidders at auction agree not to bid against each other (to buy cheap, defraud seller) → VOID

#### 3. Ponzi Schemes / Pyramid Schemes

**Example**: Agreement to run Ponzi scheme (pay old investors with new investors' money) → VOID

---

## Ground 4: Injurious to Person or Property

### Definition

**Injurious Agreement**: Agreement involves harm to person or property

**Effect**: Agreement VOID

### Examples

#### 1. Agreement to Injure Person

**Example**: Contract to assault someone → VOID

#### 2. Agreement to Damage Property

**Example**: Agreement to burn someone's house → VOID

#### 3. Agreement Endangering Life

**Example**: Agreement to participate in dangerous stunt without safety (endangering life) → May be VOID

**Case: Everett v. Williams (1725)** (Highwayman's case):
- Two highwaymen (robbers) agreed to share robbery proceeds
- Later disputed sharing
- Court held: VOID (illegal partnership to injure property of others)

## Ground 5: Immoral or Opposed to Public Policy

### A. Immoral Agreements

**Definition**: Agreements contrary to accepted moral standards

**Effect**: Agreement VOID

#### Examples of Immoral Agreements

**1. Agreements for Immoral Purposes**:
- Contract for prostitution
- Agreement to promote adultery

**Case: Pearce v. Brooks (1866)**:
- Contract to supply fancy carriage to prostitute (knowing purpose)
- Court held: VOID (immoral purpose)

**2. Agreements for Sale of Public Offices**:
- Agreement to buy/sell government position → VOID

**Case: Parkinson v. College of Ambulance (1925)**:
- Agreement to procure knighthood for payment
- Court held: VOID (immoral - sale of honors)

**3. Marriage Brokerage Contracts**:
- Agreement to arrange marriage for fee → VOID

**Rationale**: Marriage should be based on consent, not commercial transaction

### B. Agreements Opposed to Public Policy

**Definition**: Agreements harmful to public interest, even if not expressly forbidden

**Court's Discretion**: Court determines what violates public policy

**Categories**:

#### 1. Agreements in Restraint of Trade (Section 27)

**Section 27**: "Every agreement by which any one is restrained from exercising a lawful profession, trade or business of any kind, is to that extent void."

**General Rule**: Restraint of trade VOID

**Rationale**: Free competition benefits society; individual's right to work

**Example**: A agrees never to practice medicine → VOID

**EXCEPTIONS** (Restraints that are VALID):

**a) Sale of Goodwill (Section 27 Exception 1)**

**Section 27 Exception**: Buyer of goodwill can restrain seller from competing

**Requirements**:
1. **Sale of goodwill** of business
2. **Reasonable restraint** (time, geography, scope)

**Example**: A sells bakery to B; agrees not to open bakery within 5 km for 3 years → VALID

**Reasonableness Test**:
- **Time**: Not perpetual (3-5 years reasonable)
- **Geography**: Not worldwide (local area reasonable)
- **Scope**: Related business only

**Case: Nordenfelt v. Maxim Nordenfelt (1894)**:
- Seller of armaments business agreed not to compete worldwide for 25 years
- Court held: VALID (armaments business global; buyer needed worldwide protection)

**Indian Position**: More restrictive than English law; courts strictly scrutinize reasonableness

**Case: Madhub Chunder v. Raj Coomar Doss (1874)**:
- Attorney agreed not to practice in Calcutta after leaving firm
- Court held: VOID (unreasonable restraint)

**b) Partnership Agreement (Section 27 Exception 2)**

Partners can agree that some will not carry on business during partnership

**Example**: Partnership deed: "Partner A will not run separate business during partnership" → VALID

**c) Employment Contracts - Limited Validity**

**General Rule**: Cannot prevent employee from working after leaving

**Exception: Negative Covenants** (Limited enforceability):
- **Non-solicitation**: Cannot solicit former employer's clients (reasonable period) → May be valid
- **Non-compete**: Cannot work for competitor → Generally VOID in India (Section 27 strict)
- **Confidentiality**: Cannot disclose trade secrets → VALID (protects proprietary info)

**Case: Niranjan Shankar Golikari v. Century Spinning (1967)** (Supreme Court):
- Employment contract: Employee cannot work in similar business for 5 years after leaving
- Court held: VOID (violates Section 27)
- **BUT**: Reasonable restraint during employment valid

**Case: Gujarat Bottling Co. v. Coca Cola Co. (1995)**:
- Franchise agreement: Cannot sell competing products during agreement → VALID
- After termination: Cannot sell competing products → VOID

**b) Agreements in Restraint of Legal Proceedings (Section 28)

**Section 28**: "Every agreement, by which any party thereto is restricted absolutely from enforcing his rights under or in respect of any contract, by the usual legal proceedings in the ordinary tribunals, or which limits the time within which he may thus enforce his rights, is void to that extent."

**General Rule**: Cannot oust jurisdiction of courts

**Effect**: Clauses preventing party from suing → VOID

**Example**: Contract clause: "Parties cannot sue in court for any dispute" → VOID

**EXCEPTIONS**:

**i) Arbitration Clause (Section 28 Exception 1)**

**Arbitration Agreement**: Valid alternative dispute resolution

**Requirements**:
1. **After dispute arises** OR specific arbitration clause
2. **Does not bar courts** ultimately (arbitration award can be challenged in court)

**Example**: "All disputes will be resolved by arbitration" → VALID

**Arbitration and Conciliation Act 1996**: Governs arbitration

**ii) Limitation of Time (Section 28 Exception 2)**

**Reasonable time limit**: Can shorten limitation period (but not extinguish right)

**Example**: "Claims must be made within 1 year (instead of 3 years)" → VALID (if reasonable)

**iii) Agreements on Uncertain Matters (Section 29)

**Section 29**: "Agreements, the meaning of which is not certain, or capable of being made certain, are void."

**Certainty Requirement**: Terms must be definite

**Example**: "A agrees to sell goods to B at fair price" → May be VOID (vague - what is "fair price"?)

**But**: If market standard exists or method to determine price → Valid

**Case: Guthing v. Lynn (1831)**:
- "I will give £5 more if horse is lucky"
- Court held: VOID (uncertain - what is "lucky"?)

**Case: May & Butcher v. The King (1934)**:
- Agreement to sell at price "to be agreed later"
- Court held: VOID (price essential; not agreed)

**Indian Position**: Courts try to save contracts if some mechanism to ascertain terms

#### 3. Agreements Interfering with Administration of Justice

**Examples**:
- Agreement to suppress prosecution
- Agreement to procure false evidence
- Agreement to pervert justice

**Case: Keir v. Leeman (1846)**:
- Agreement to compound (settle) criminal prosecution for theft
- Court held: VOID (interferes with justice)

#### 4. Agreements for Trading with Enemy

**During war**: Trading with enemy country VOID

**Rationale**: Aids enemy (against public policy)

#### 5. Agreements Promoting Corruption

**Example**: Agreement to bribe public official → VOID

**Case**: Any agreement involving corruption under Prevention of Corruption Act → VOID

#### 6. Champertous Agreements

**Champerty**: Agreement to finance litigation in return for share of proceeds

**Example**: Lawyer agrees to fund client's case; take 50% of winnings → VOID (champerty)

**Rationale**: Promotes unnecessary litigation

**Exception: Contingency fees** (Some jurisdictions allow; India generally prohibits)

## Section 24: Void Agreements if Consideration or Object Unlawful in Part

**Section 24**: "If any part of a single consideration for one or more objects, or any one or any part of any one of several considerations for a single object, is unlawful, the agreement is void."

**Rule**: If any part of consideration or object unlawful → Entire agreement VOID

**Indivisible Agreement**: Cannot sever unlawful part

### Example 1: Part Consideration Unlawful

**Facts**: A agrees to B: "I'll pay you Rs. 10,000 to (1) deliver goods (lawful), and (2) kill my enemy (unlawful)"

**Analysis**:
- **Single consideration**: Rs. 10,000
- **Multiple objects**: (1) Lawful, (2) Unlawful
- **Effect**: Entire agreement VOID (cannot separate unlawful part)

### Example 2: Severable Agreement

**Facts**: Two separate agreements: (1) Pay Rs. 5,000 for delivering goods, (2) Pay Rs. 5,000 for killing enemy

**Analysis**:
- **Severable**: Two distinct agreements
- **Effect**: Agreement (1) VALID; Agreement (2) VOID

**Severability Test**: Can lawful part stand independently?

## Doctrine of "In Pari Delicto"

**Maxim**: "In pari delicto potior est conditio defendentis"

**Translation**: "In equal wrongdoing, the position of the defendant is stronger"

**Rule**: If both parties equally guilty (in pari delicto), court will not assist either party

**Effect**: Money/property advanced under illegal contract cannot be recovered

**Case: Holman v. Johnson (1775)**:
- Smuggling agreement
- Buyer paid; seller failed to deliver smuggled goods
- Court held: Cannot recover payment (both parties guilty)

### Exception: Not In Pari Delicto

**If parties NOT equally guilty**, innocent party can recover

**Examples**:
1. **Repentance before performance**: Party withdraws before illegal act → Can recover
2. **Oppression**: Weaker party forced into illegal contract → Can recover
3. **Statute protects one class**: Law protecting specific class of persons

**Case: Hermann v. Jeuchner (1885)**:
- Contract for future prostitution
- Woman repented before performing
- Court held: Can recover payment (not in pari delicto; repented)

## Practical Implications

### Due Diligence Checklist

**Before entering contract, verify**:

1. ✓ **Lawful object**: Purpose lawful under all applicable laws?
2. ✓ **Lawful consideration**: Payment/exchange lawful?
3. ✓ **Licenses/permissions**: Required licenses obtained?
4. ✓ **Restraint of trade**: Any non-compete clauses reasonable? (Sale of goodwill exception?)
5. ✓ **Arbitration clause**: Properly drafted? (Not ousting court jurisdiction)
6. ✓ **Certainty**: All terms certain and ascertainable?
7. ✓ **No fraud**: Agreement not scheme to defraud third party?
8. ✓ **Moral standards**: Agreement not immoral?
9. ✓ **Public policy**: Agreement not against public interest?

### Drafting Tips

#### 1. Severability Clause

**Purpose**: If part of contract void, rest remains valid

**Example Clause**: "If any provision of this Agreement is held invalid or unenforceable, such provision shall be severed and the remaining provisions shall continue in full force."

**Effectiveness**: Works ONLY if provisions are truly severable (not if entire object unlawful)

#### 2. Arbitration Clause (Section 28 Exception)

**Valid Clause**: "All disputes arising out of this Agreement shall be referred to arbitration in accordance with the Arbitration and Conciliation Act 1996. Subject to arbitration, the courts at [City] shall have exclusive jurisdiction."

**Invalid Clause**: "Parties waive all rights to sue in any court" → VOID (ousts jurisdiction)

#### 3. Non-Compete (Restrictive Covenants)

**For Sale of Goodwill**:
- **Geographic limit**: "within 10 km radius of business"
- **Time limit**: "for period of 3 years from sale"
- **Scope limit**: "not carry on similar bakery business"

**For Employment** (Limited validity in India):
- **During employment**: Valid
- **Post-employment**: Generally VOID (Niranjan Shankar case)
- **Confidentiality/Trade Secrets**: Valid (protects proprietary info)

## Flowchart: Is Object/Consideration Lawful?

```
START
  ↓
Is object or consideration FORBIDDEN BY LAW?
(Prohibited by statute - crime, unlicensed business, prohibited goods)
  ├─ YES → Agreement VOID
  └─ NO → Proceed

  ↓
Does it DEFEAT PROVISIONS OF LAW?
(Evade stamp duty, restraint of marriage, insolvency fraud)
  ├─ YES → Agreement VOID
  └─ NO → Proceed

  ↓
Is it FRAUDULENT?
(Scheme to defraud third party, conspiracy)
  ├─ YES → Agreement VOID
  └─ NO → Proceed

  ↓
Is it INJURIOUS to person or property?
(Assault, damage property, endanger life)
  ├─ YES → Agreement VOID
  └─ NO → Proceed

  ↓
Is it IMMORAL?
(Prostitution, adultery, sale of public office)
  ├─ YES → Agreement VOID
  └─ NO → Proceed

  ↓
Is it OPPOSED TO PUBLIC POLICY?
  ├─ Restraint of trade (Section 27) → VOID (unless sale of goodwill exception)
  ├─ Oust jurisdiction of courts (Section 28) → VOID (unless arbitration exception)
  ├─ Uncertain terms (Section 29) → VOID
  ├─ Trading with enemy, corruption, champerty → VOID
  └─ NO → Object and Consideration LAWFUL ✓
```

## Practice Examples

### Example 1: Wagering Agreement

**Facts**:
- A and B bet Rs. 1 lakh on cricket match outcome
- A wins bet; B refuses to pay

**Analysis**:
- **Wager**: Bet on uncertain event (Section 30)
- **Effect**: Agreement VOID
- **Result**: A cannot recover Rs. 1 lakh (void agreement)

### Example 2: Sale of Goodwill

**Facts**:
- A sells bakery business to B for Rs. 50 lakhs
- Agreement: A will not open bakery within 5 km for 3 years
- After 6 months, A opens bakery 2 km away

**Analysis**:
- **Sale of goodwill**: Yes (bakery business sold)
- **Restraint**: 5 km, 3 years (reasonable in geography and time)
- **Exception to Section 27**: Valid (sale of goodwill exception)
- **Result**: B can seek injunction to prevent A from operating

### Example 3: Employment Non-Compete

**Facts**:
- A employed as software engineer at Company X
- Employment contract: "After leaving, cannot work for competing company for 2 years"
- A leaves; joins Company Y (competitor)

**Analysis**:
- **Post-employment non-compete**: Restraint of trade
- **Section 27**: VOID (Niranjan Shankar precedent)
- **Result**: Clause unenforceable; A can join Company Y

### Example 4: Part Consideration Unlawful

**Facts**:
- A agrees to B: "Pay Rs. 1 lakh for (1) delivering goods, and (2) bribing official"
- Single payment for two objects

**Analysis**:
- **Section 24**: Part of object unlawful (bribery)
- **Indivisible**: Single consideration for multiple objects
- **Effect**: Entire agreement VOID
- **Result**: Cannot enforce even lawful part (delivery of goods)

### Example 5: Arbitration Clause

**Facts**:
- Contract clause: "All disputes shall be resolved by arbitration; parties waive right to approach courts"

**Analysis**:
- **Arbitration**: Valid (Section 28 exception)
- **Waive right to courts**: VOID (ousts jurisdiction)
- **Severability**: Arbitration clause valid; waiver clause void
- **Result**: Arbitration valid; but arbitration award can be challenged in court

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Confusing Void with Voidable

**Mistake**: Thinking unlawful agreement is voidable (can be affirmed)

**Correct**: Unlawful agreement is VOID (no contract exists; cannot be affirmed)

### 2. Assuming All Restraints of Trade Void

**Mistake**: Thinking all non-compete clauses void

**Correct**: 
- **Sale of goodwill**: Valid if reasonable
- **Employment post-termination**: Generally void
- **During employment**: Valid

### 3. Arbitration Clause Ousting Jurisdiction

**Mistake**: Drafting clause that completely bars courts

**Correct**: Arbitration valid; but courts retain supervisory jurisdiction (can challenge award)

### 4. Recovering Under Illegal Contract

**Mistake**: Thinking can sue to enforce illegal contract or recover money paid

**Correct**: In pari delicto → Cannot recover (except if not equally guilty)

### 5. Severability of Unlawful Provisions

**Mistake**: Assuming unlawful part can always be severed

**Correct**: 
- **Section 24**: If entire object or consideration unlawful → Entire agreement void (cannot sever)
- **Severability**: Only if provisions truly independent
