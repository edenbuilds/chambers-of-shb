## Overview

This protocol covers **specific performance** - an equitable remedy compelling the defaulting party to actually perform the contract (instead of paying damages).

**Key Principle**: Specific performance is granted when damages are inadequate remedy, typically for unique property or where monetary compensation cannot adequately compensate the injured party.

---

## Statutory Framework

### Section 10: Cases Where Specific Performance Enforceable

```
"Except as otherwise provided in this Chapter, the specific performance of any contract may, in the discretion of the court, be enforced—

(a) when there exists no standard for ascertaining the actual damage caused by the non-performance of the act agreed to be done; or
(b) when the act agreed to be done is such that compensation in money for its non-performance would not afford adequate relief."
```

**Discretionary Remedy**: Court decides whether to grant

**When Granted**:
1. **No standard** for ascertaining damages, OR
2. **Damages inadequate** remedy

## When Specific Performance Granted

### 1. Contracts for Sale of Immovable Property

**General Rule**: Specific performance routinely granted for land/property

**Reason**: Every piece of land is unique; damages inadequate

**Section 10**: Sale of immovable property → Specific performance available

**Example**: A contracts to sell house to B; A refuses → Court can order A to transfer house

**Case: Rajendra Prasad v. Urmila Devi (1980)** (Supreme Court):
- Contract for sale of land
- Seller refused to transfer
- Court held: Specific performance granted (land unique; damages inadequate)

### 2. Contracts for Unique Movables

**Unique/Rare Goods**: Articles with special value (not just monetary)

**Examples**:
- Antique furniture
- Rare paintings/artwork
- Heirlooms with sentimental value
- Limited edition items

**Example**: Contract to sell rare vintage car; seller refuses → Specific performance may be granted

**Case: Behnke v. Bede Shipping Co. (1927)**:
- Contract to sell specific ship
- Court held: Specific performance granted (ship unique; specific tonnage and specifications)

### 3. Partnership Agreements

**Section 14(b)**: Specific performance of partnership contract may be granted

**Reason**: Personal relationship; damages inadequate for breach of partnership terms

### 4. Contracts Where Damages Not Ascertainable

**No Standard**: If impossible to calculate loss in monetary terms

**Example**: Contract for specific performance by artist (unique service)

## When Specific Performance NOT Granted

### Section 14: Contracts Not Specifically Enforceable

#### (a) Contracts for Non-Pecuniary Nature

**Section 14(1)(a)**: Not enforceable if "the act agreed to be done is such that the court cannot supervise the execution thereof"

**Example**: Contract for personal services requiring continuous supervision

**Reason**: Courts cannot supervise day-to-day performance

#### (b) Contracts for Personal Service / Trust

**Section 14(1)(c)**: "Contracts involving the performance of personal acts which require personal skill or confidence"

**Examples**:
- Employment contracts (cannot force employee to work)
- Artist to paint portrait (personal skill)
- Doctor to perform surgery (personal service)

**Reason**: Violates personal liberty (Article 21); akin to involuntary servitude

**Case: Chitti Babu v. Sreeramula (1970)**:
- Employment contract
- Court held: Cannot grant specific performance (personal service)

**Exception**: Negative covenants enforceable by injunction
- "Employee will not work for competitor" → Injunction possible (prohibitory)
- "Employee must work for employer" → Specific performance NOT possible (mandatory)

#### (c) Contracts for Standard Goods (Readily Available)

**Section 14(1)(b)**: "Contracts which can be adequately relieved by compensation in money"

**Standard/Generic Goods**: Available in market at determinable price

**Example**: Contract to buy 100 tons of wheat → Specific performance NOT granted (wheat available in market; damages adequate)

**Case: Jai Gopal v. Gumani Ram (1965)**:
- Contract to supply timber
- Court held: Specific performance not granted (timber available in market)

#### (d) Determinable Contracts at Plaintiff's Option

**Section 14(1)(d)**: If contract determinable at plaintiff's option → Not specifically enforceable

**Reason**: Inequitable (plaintiff can exit anytime but force defendant to perform)

#### (e) Performance Involves Multiple Acts

**Section 14(1)(e)**: Contract requiring continuous performance over time

**Example**: Long-term supply contracts requiring daily deliveries

**Reason**: Court cannot supervise ongoing performance

#### (f) Contracts in Nature of Revocable or Terminable

**Section 14(1)(f)**: Contracts which parties could lawfully terminate

**Example**: Partnership at will (can be terminated anytime)

## Discretion of Court (Section 20)

**Section 20**: "The jurisdiction to decree specific performance is discretionary, and the court is not bound to grant such relief merely because it is lawful to do so; but the discretion of the court is not arbitrary but sound and reasonable, guided by judicial principles and capable of correction by a court of appeal."

**Discretionary Factors**:

### 1. Conduct of Plaintiff (Clean Hands Doctrine)

**Maxim**: "He who seeks equity must do equity"

**Rule**: Plaintiff's conduct must be fair

**Case: Ameer Bee v. Mohideen (1971)**:
- Plaintiff delayed performance
- Court refused specific performance (plaintiff's conduct)

**Example**: Plaintiff defaulted on payment; later seeks specific performance → May be refused

### 2. Hardship to Defendant

**Section 20(2)(b)**: Court may refuse if performance causes hardship "which the plaintiff either could have avoided or adequately compensated"

**Example**: Defendant mortgaged property to third party in good faith (believing contract cancelled) → Specific performance may cause hardship

**Balance**: Court balances plaintiff's loss vs defendant's hardship

### 3. Adequacy of Damages

**Even if land/unique goods**: If damages adequate in particular case → May refuse specific performance

### 4. Delay / Laches

**Unreasonable Delay**: Plaintiff delayed in seeking relief

**Example**: Breach in 2020; plaintiff sues in 2025 → Court may refuse (laches)

**Limitation**: 3 years from breach (Limitation Act)

### 5. Impossibility / Illegality

**If performance impossible or illegal** → Cannot grant specific performance

**Example**: Property destroyed after contract → Specific performance impossible

## Substituted Performance (Section 20A)

**Section 20A** (Added by Amendment Act 2018): If defendant fails to perform decree → Plaintiff can get substituted performance

**Substituted Performance**: Court allows plaintiff to get performance from third party at defendant's cost

**Example**: Defendant to construct building; doesn't perform → Court allows plaintiff to hire contractor; cost recovered from defendant

**Objective**: Ensure actual performance (not just monetary compensation)

---

## Part Performance (Section 53A Transfer of Property Act)

**Section 53A**: Part performance doctrine

**Rule**: If buyer partly performed (took possession, made improvements) → Seller cannot evict

**Protection**: Buyer who partially performed in good faith protected from eviction

**Requirements**:
1. **Contract in writing**
2. **Part performance** by buyer (took possession, paid part price, made improvements)
3. **Good faith**

**Example**: Buyer pays 50%, takes possession, renovates; seller refuses to transfer → Buyer protected from eviction; can seek specific performance

---

## Compensation in Addition to Specific Performance (Section 21)

**Section 21**: "In a suit for specific performance, the plaintiff may also claim compensation for its breach, either in addition to, or in substitution for, such performance."

**Options**:
1. **Specific performance + Compensation** for delay/partial breach
2. **Compensation instead of specific performance** (if court refuses specific performance)

**Example**: Contract to transfer property by June 1; defendant delays till August 1
- Court grants specific performance (transfer now)
- Plus compensation for 2-month delay (loss of use/rental income)

---

## Mutuality Principle (Historical)

**Historical Rule**: Specific performance granted only if remedy available to **both** parties (mutuality)

**Abolished**: Mutuality principle no longer strictly applied in India

**Current Position**: Court grants if appropriate, even if remedy not available to both parties

**Example**: Contract with minor (minor cannot be compelled) → But specific performance may be granted against adult party if equitable

---

## Damages in Lieu of Specific Performance

**If specific performance refused** → Plaintiff can claim damages

**Section 21**: Compensation in substitution for specific performance

**Example**: Court refuses specific performance (defendant's hardship) → Plaintiff awarded damages instead

## Practical Examples

### Example 1: Sale of Land (Specific Performance Granted)

**Facts**: A agrees to sell land to B for Rs. 50 lakhs; A refuses to transfer

**Analysis**:
- **Immovable property**: Land unique (Section 10)
- **Damages inadequate**: Cannot replace this specific land
- **Result**: Court grants specific performance (A must transfer land)

### Example 2: Standard Goods (Specific Performance Refused)

**Facts**: A contracts to supply 1000 kg rice to B; A refuses

**Analysis**:
- **Standard goods**: Rice available in market
- **Damages adequate**: B can buy from market; claim difference
- **Section 14(1)(b)**: Damages adequate remedy
- **Result**: Specific performance NOT granted; B gets damages

### Example 3: Personal Service (Refused)

**Facts**: A (employee) contracts to work for B (employer) for 2 years; A quits after 6 months

**Analysis**:
- **Personal service**: Employment contract (Section 14(1)(c))
- **Cannot compel**: Forcing work violates personal liberty
- **Result**: Specific performance NOT granted
- **Alternative**: B can seek injunction (prevent A from working for competitor if negative covenant exists)

### Example 4: Unique Movable (Granted)

**Facts**: A contracts to sell rare antique painting to B; A refuses

**Analysis**:
- **Unique good**: Rare painting (cannot be replaced)
- **Damages inadequate**: Monetary compensation cannot replace unique artwork
- **Section 10**: Adequate relief not possible by damages
- **Result**: Specific performance MAY be granted (court's discretion)

### Example 5: Plaintiff's Conduct (Refused)

**Facts**:
- A (buyer) contracts to buy property from B for Rs. 1 crore
- A pays Rs. 10 lakhs advance; balance due in 3 months
- A delays payment by 2 years
- A now seeks specific performance

**Analysis**:
- **Plaintiff's delay**: Unreasonable delay (2 years)
- **Clean hands doctrine**: A's conduct not equitable
- **Section 20**: Court's discretion
- **Result**: Specific performance likely REFUSED (plaintiff's laches)

## Comparison: Specific Performance vs Damages

| Aspect | Specific Performance | Damages |
|--------|---------------------|---------|
| **Nature** | Equitable remedy | Legal remedy (common law) |
| **Effect** | Actual performance compelled | Monetary compensation |
| **Availability** | Discretionary (court decides) | Right (if breach proved) |
| **When Granted** | Damages inadequate (unique property) | Always available (if breach) |
| **Examples** | Sale of land, unique goods | Standard goods, calculable loss |

## Common Mistakes and Pitfalls

### 1. Assuming Specific Performance Always Available

**Mistake**: Expecting specific performance for all breaches

**Correct**: Only when damages inadequate (unique property, no standard for damages)

### 2. Personal Service Contracts

**Mistake**: Seeking specific performance of employment/personal service contracts

**Correct**: Cannot compel personal service (Section 14(1)(c)); only damages or injunction (negative covenant)

### 3. Delay in Filing Suit

**Mistake**: Waiting years before seeking specific performance

**Correct**: File promptly; unreasonable delay (laches) → Court may refuse

### 4. Unclean Hands

**Mistake**: Plaintiff breached or delayed, then seeks specific performance

**Correct**: Plaintiff must have equitable conduct (clean hands doctrine)

### 5. Standard Goods

**Mistake**: Seeking specific performance for readily available market goods

**Correct**: Specific performance not available if goods available in market (damages adequate)
