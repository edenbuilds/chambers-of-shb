## Purpose
This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on the Payment of Wages Act 1936, covering timely wage payment, permissible and prohibited deductions, wage payment methods, delayed payment penalties, claims procedures, and enforcement mechanisms.

---

## Core Framework

### 1. APPLICABILITY (Section 1)

#### 1.1 Coverage
```
PAYMENT OF WAGES ACT 1936:

APPLIES TO:
✓ Factories (under Factories Act 1948)
✓ Industrial establishments (employing ≥10 workers with power, ≥20 without power)
✓ Railways
✓ Tramways, motor transport service
✓ Air transport service
✓ Docks, wharves, inland vessels
✓ Plantations
✓ Workshops, establishments notified by State Government

WAGE LIMIT (originally):
Act applied only to workers earning ≤₹24,000/month
Amendment 2017: Wage ceiling REMOVED
Now applies to ALL employees (regardless of wage level)

EMPLOYEE DEFINITION (Section 2(1)):
Person employed for hire/reward to do skilled, unskilled, manual, clerical, supervisory work
Includes: Workmen, clerks, supervisors (earning salary, not managerial)
Excludes: Managerial, administrative staff (not on wage/salary basis, partnership, directors)
```

### 2. WAGE PAYMENT TIMELINES (Section 5)

#### 2.1 Payment Frequency & Deadlines
```
ESTABLISHMENTS WITH <1,000 EMPLOYEES:
Must pay wages by 7th of following month

EXAMPLE:
Factory with 500 workers
Salary month: January 2025 (1st Jan to 31st Jan)
Payment deadline: 7th February 2025 (latest)

If paid on 8th Feb → VIOLATION (delay penalty applicable)

ESTABLISHMENTS WITH ≥1,000 EMPLOYEES:
Must pay wages by 10th of following month

EXAMPLE:
IT company with 2,500 employees
Salary month: March 2025
Payment deadline: 10th April 2025

WEEKLY/DAILY WAGE WORKERS:
If wages calculated weekly/daily (not monthly)
→ Must pay before expiry of 7th day from end of wage period

EXAMPLE:
Construction workers (weekly wages, Monday to Sunday)
Week ending: 15th December 2024 (Sunday)
Payment deadline: 22nd December 2024 (7th day from 15th)

TERMINATION/RESIGNATION:
Wages due on exit (Full & Final settlement)
→ Must pay within 2 working days from termination date

EXAMPLE:
Employee resigns: 20th January 2025 (last working day)
F&F settlement: Salary (20 days of Jan) + Leave encashment + Notice pay (if applicable)
Payment deadline: 22nd January 2025 (2 working days)
```

### 3. MODE OF WAGE PAYMENT (Section 6)

#### 3.1 Currency & Methods
```
PRIMARY RULE (Section 6(1)):
Wages shall be paid in CURRENCY COINS or CURRENCY NOTES (cash)

MODERN AMENDMENT (2017):
Wages can be paid by:
1. Cash (physical currency)
2. Cheque (bank cheque, drawn on worker's bank)
3. Direct bank credit (NEFT, RTGS, IMPS to worker's account)

MANDATORY FOR WAGES ≥₹1,000/month (or as notified):
Payment through bank account COMPULSORY
(Anti-corruption measure, digital India initiative)

WORKER'S CONSENT:
For electronic payment (cheque/bank transfer), worker's written consent required
Employer must obtain: Bank account details, authorization to credit salary

WAGE SLIP MANDATORY (not in Act, but standard practice + some state rules):
Employer must provide wage slip showing:
- Gross salary (Basic + DA + HRA + Allowances + Bonus)
- Deductions (PF, ESI, PT, TDS, Loans, Advances)
- Net salary (credited to bank)
- Payment month

EXAMPLE WAGE SLIP:
Employee: XYZ
Month: January 2025
EARNINGS:
Basic Salary: ₹18,000
DA: ₹5,400 (30%)
HRA: ₹9,000 (50%)
Transport: ₹2,500
GROSS: ₹34,900

DEDUCTIONS:
PF (Employee 12%): ₹1,800 (on ₹15,000 Basic, capped)
ESI (Employee 0.75%): ₹262
Professional Tax: ₹200
TDS: ₹150
Loan EMI: ₹2,000
TOTAL DEDUCTIONS: ₹4,412

NET SALARY: ₹30,488 (credited to bank on 7th Feb)
```

### 4. PERMISSIBLE DEDUCTIONS (Section 7)

#### 4.1 Categories of Deductions
```
ONLY FOLLOWING DEDUCTIONS ALLOWED (Section 7):

1. FINES (Section 7(2)(a)):
   - For misconduct, rule violation (as per Standing Orders or employment contract)
   - Conditions:
     - Fine must be mentioned in Standing Orders (approved by Labour Dept)
     - Worker given opportunity to explain (show cause notice)
     - Fine ≤3% of wages per month (maximum cap)
     - Fine credited to welfare fund (not employer's pocket)

   EXAMPLE:
   Worker late for 5 days (fine: ₹100/day per Standing Orders)
   Monthly wages: ₹25,000
   Maximum fine allowed: 3% × ₹25,000 = ₹750
   Actual fine: 5 × ₹100 = ₹500 (within limit, permissible)

2. ABSENCE FROM DUTY (Section 7(2)(b)):
   - Deduction for days absent (proportionate to wages)
   - Formula: (Monthly wages ÷ 26 days) × Absent days

   EXAMPLE:
   Monthly salary: ₹30,000
   Absent: 3 days (unauthorized leave)
   Deduction: (₹30,000 ÷ 26) × 3 = ₹3,462

3. ACCOMMODATION PROVIDED BY EMPLOYER (Section 7(2)(c)):
   - If employer provides housing (quarters, dormitory)
   - Deduction for rent (but ≤ fair market rent, not excessive)

   EXAMPLE:
   Company provides 2BHK flat (market rent ₹15,000/month)
   Deduction from salary: ₹10,000/month (subsidized, within fair value)

4. AMENITIES & SERVICES (Section 7(2)(d)):
   - Electricity, water, conservancy (if employer provides)
   - Transport (if company bus provided)
   - Medical facilities (if company clinic/insurance)
   - Canteen (subsidized meals, worker pays part)

   CONDITION: Deduction ≤ actual cost to employer (no profit)

5. DAMAGE OR LOSS (Section 7(2)(e)):
   - Damage to goods, tools, machinery (due to worker's negligence)
   - Lost items (tools, materials entrusted to worker)

   CONDITIONS:
   - Employer must prove negligence (not normal wear & tear)
   - Worker given opportunity to explain (domestic inquiry)
   - Deduction ≤ actual loss amount
   - Cannot exceed 50% of wages in any month (to ensure living wage)

   EXAMPLE:
   Worker damages machine (negligence, not operational failure)
   Repair cost: ₹25,000
   Worker's monthly wage: ₹20,000
   Deduction: ₹10,000/month (50% max) × 2.5 months = ₹25,000 recovered over time

6. HOUSE-BUILDING, FAMILY PENSION FUND (Section 7(2)(f)):
   - Contribution to approved funds (EPF, insurance, pension)
   - Worker's written authorization required

7. COOPERATIVE SOCIETIES (Section 7(2)(g)):
   - Loan repayment to cooperative society (if worker is member)
   - Deduction as per society rules
   - Written authorization

8. INSURANCE PREMIUM (Section 7(2)(h)):
   - Group insurance, medical insurance (if employer provides)
   - Worker's consent + benefit accrues to worker/family

9. ADVANCES & LOANS (Section 7(2)(j)):
   - Salary advance repayment (taken by worker)
   - Loan EMI (from employer or bank, authorized by worker)

   EXAMPLE:
   Worker takes ₹50,000 salary advance (personal emergency)
   Repayment: ₹5,000/month × 10 months (deducted from salary)

10. INCOME TAX (Section 7(2)(i)):
    - TDS (Tax Deducted at Source)
    - As per Income Tax Act (employer's statutory obligation)
    - Deposited to govt, Form 16 issued to worker

11. PROFESSIONAL TAX (state-specific):
    - State government levy (Maharashtra, Karnataka, etc.)
    - ₹200-₹2,500/month (based on salary slab)
    - Employer deducts, deposits to state

12. COURT ORDERS (Section 7(2)(k)):
    - Attachment of wages (court order in civil suit)
    - Maintenance to spouse/children (family court order)
    - Employer must comply (legal obligation)

    LIMIT: Maximum 50% of wages can be attached (protect worker's subsistence)
```

#### 4.2 Prohibited Deductions
```
FOLLOWING DEDUCTIONS NOT ALLOWED:

✗ Deduction for employer's business loss (market downturn, profit loss)
✗ Deduction for tools, machinery (employer must provide free)
✗ Deduction for safety equipment (helmets, gloves, shoes - employer provides)
✗ Deduction for recruitment expenses (agent fees, training cost before joining)
✗ Deduction for employer's charity, donations (without worker's voluntary consent)
✗ Arbitrary deductions (without written agreement, standing orders, or statutory basis)

IF EMPLOYER MAKES PROHIBITED DEDUCTION:
Worker files complaint → Inspector investigates
If proved: Employer must refund amount + penalty (fine ₹7,500-₹50,000)

CASE EXAMPLE (Supreme Court - People's Union for Democratic Rights, 1982):
Employer deducted ₹500/month for "welfare fund" (not defined, no benefit shown)
Court: Illegal deduction, refund all workers, fine ₹5 lakh (at that time, significant)
```

### 5. DELAYED PAYMENT - COMPENSATION (Section 15, Rule 26)

#### 5.1 Penalty for Delay
```
EMPLOYER'S DUTY:
Pay wages by due date (7th/10th of following month, or 2 days on termination)

IF DELAYED:
Employer liable to pay COMPENSATION to worker

COMPENSATION RATE (Central Rules, varies by state):
- Delhi, Maharashtra: 0.05% per day of delayed amount
- Karnataka: 0.5% per day (more stringent)
- Generally: Interest-like compensation (not fixed in Act, rules specify)

CALCULATION EXAMPLE (Maharashtra):
Salary: ₹30,000
Due date: 7th Feb 2025
Actual payment: 25th Feb 2025 (18 days delay)
Delay compensation: ₹30,000 × 0.05% × 18 days = ₹270

Worker entitled to: ₹30,000 + ₹270 = ₹30,270

CRIMINAL LIABILITY (Section 20):
Willful delay (without reasonable cause)
→ Fine ₹7,500-₹50,000 (on employer)
→ Prosecution in magistrate court

REASONABLE CAUSE (defense):
- Bank strike, system failure (force majeure)
- Worker's bank account closed/frozen (not employer's fault)
- Natural calamity (floods, earthquake affecting operations)

If reasonable cause proved: No penalty (but wages still due)
```

### 6. CLAIMS & ENFORCEMENT

#### 6.1 Authority Under Act (Section 15)
```
AUTHORITY:
Appointed by State Government (usually Labour Commissioner or Inspector)

JURISDICTION:
Territorial (each district has Authority)
Worker files claim in district where employed
```

#### 6.2 Claims Procedure (Section 15)
```
WHO CAN FILE:
- Worker (individual)
- Legal heir (if worker deceased, unpaid wages)
- Assignee (if worker assigned claim rights to union/legal aid)
- Inspector (on behalf of workers, if systemic non-payment)

TIME LIMIT (Section 15(2)):
Within 12 months from date wage became due

EXAMPLE:
Salary for Jan 2025, due on 7th Feb 2025 (not paid)
Claim can be filed till 7th Feb 2026 (12 months from due date)

After 12 months: Claim time-barred (Authority cannot entertain)
Exception: If delay condoned (reasonable cause, Authority's discretion)

PROCEDURE:

STEP 1: APPLICATION (Form A or as per state rules)
Details:
- Worker's name, address, designation
- Employer's name, establishment details
- Wage period, amount due
- Date wage became due, date of claim
- Reasons for non-payment (if known)

STEP 2: NOTICE TO EMPLOYER
Authority issues notice (15-30 days to reply)

STEP 3: EMPLOYER'S REPLY
Defenses:
- "Wage already paid" (proof: bank statement, payroll, acknowledgment)
- "Worker not entitled" (terminated before wage period, suspended)
- "Calculation wrong" (disputes gross/deductions)

STEP 4: INQUIRY (if disputed)
- Evidence: Wage slips, bank statements, employment contract
- Witnesses: Supervisor, HR manager, fellow workers
- Documents: Attendance records, resignation letter, F&F statement

STEP 5: ORDER (Section 15(4))
Authority passes order:
- "Employer shall pay ₹[X] to worker within 30 days"
- Interest/compensation for delay (if applicable)
- Costs (₹[Y], if employer's resistance frivolous)

STEP 6: EXECUTION (if employer does not pay)
Order sent to Labour Court → Executed as decree
- Attachment of bank accounts
- Recovery from employer's assets

TIMELINE:
Application → Order: 3-6 months (if no complications)
```

### 7. REGISTERS & RECORDS (Section 13)

#### 7.1 Mandatory Registers
```
EVERY EMPLOYER MUST MAINTAIN:

1. REGISTER OF WAGES (Form I or equivalent):
   Columns:
   - Employee name, designation, employee ID
   - Wage period (monthly, weekly, daily)
   - Gross wages (Basic + DA + HRA + Allowances)
   - Deductions (PF, ESI, PT, TDS, Fines, Advances, Loans)
   - Net wages paid
   - Date of payment
   - Mode of payment (cash/cheque no./bank credit)
   - Employee's signature or thumb impression (acknowledging receipt)

2. REGISTER OF FINES (if fines imposed):
   - Employee name
   - Date of offense, nature of offense
   - Show cause notice date, employee's explanation
   - Fine amount imposed
   - Date fine deducted from wages
   - Disposal of fine (welfare fund, not employer's income)

3. REGISTER OF DEDUCTIONS FOR DAMAGE/LOSS:
   - Employee name
   - Nature of damage/loss (machine broken, material lost)
   - Amount of damage (assessed value)
   - Inquiry conducted (domestic inquiry report)
   - Employee's explanation
   - Deduction amount, installments

4. MUSTER ROLL (if daily/weekly wages):
   - Employee name
   - Daily attendance (present, absent, leave, half-day)
   - Hours worked (if hourly wages)
   - Overtime hours (if applicable)

RECORD RETENTION:
Maintain for 3 years from last entry
Must be produced to Inspector on demand

DIGITAL REGISTERS:
Permitted (Excel, HRMS software, cloud-based)
Provided: Easily accessible, printable, tamper-proof (audit trail)
```

### 8. INSPECTIONS & PENALTIES

#### 8.1 Inspector's Powers (Section 13)
```
INSPECTOR (appointed by State Government):

POWERS:
1. Enter establishment (during working hours, without prior notice)
2. Examine wage registers, muster rolls, deduction registers
3. Interview employees (about wages, deductions, delays)
4. Seize documents (if violations suspected, false records)
5. File prosecution (if systematic violations, willful non-payment)

EMPLOYER'S DUTY:
Cooperate (provide registers, answer questions, allow entry)

OBSTRUCTION:
Refusing entry, destroying records → Fine ₹50,000 + Imprisonment up to 6 months
```

#### 8.2 Penalties (Section 20-22)
```
DELAYED PAYMENT (without reasonable cause):
Fine: ₹7,500 to ₹50,000 (per violation)
Prosecution: In magistrate court
Imprisonment: Not specified (only fine in most cases)

UNAUTHORIZED DEDUCTIONS:
Fine: ₹7,500 to ₹50,000
Refund amount deducted illegally (to worker)

NOT MAINTAINING REGISTERS:
Fine: ₹3,000 to ₹25,000 (first offense)
Fine: ₹5,000 to ₹50,000 (repeat offense within 5 years)

OBSTRUCTION OF INSPECTOR:
Fine: ₹25,000 to ₹75,000
Imprisonment: Up to 6 months (if serious obstruction, evidence destruction)

CONTINUING OFFENSE (Section 22):
If violation continues despite notice:
Additional fine: ₹500 per day for each day violation continues
(After conviction, if employer still does not comply)

COMPOUNDING (settle without trial):
Minor violations: Can be compounded (fine paid, case closed)
Inspector's discretion, Amount: ₹10,000-₹50,000 (negotiated)
```

## Landmark Cases

### 1. **Mackinnon Mackenzie & Co. v. Audrey D'Costa (1987) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Employer deducted ₹500/month for "welfare fund" without specifying benefits or maintaining fund.

**Held**:
- Deduction for "welfare" without defined scheme = Illegal (not covered by Section 7)
- Employer must refund all deducted amounts to workers
- Vague deductions not permissible (only Section 7 categories allowed)

**Impact**: Strengthened worker protection against arbitrary deductions.

---

### 2. **People's Union for Democratic Rights v. Union of India (1982) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Construction workers not paid minimum wages, delayed payments for months.

**Held**:
- Non-payment/delayed payment of minimum wages = Violation of constitutional rights (Article 23 - forced labour)
- Employers prosecuted under Payment of Wages Act + Minimum Wages Act
- Workers entitled to compensation for delayed payment

**Impact**: Linked wage payment to fundamental rights, enhanced enforcement.

---

### 3. **Bridge & Roof Co. v. Union of India (1963) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Employer delayed wage payment citing financial difficulties.

**Held**:
- Financial difficulty is NOT reasonable cause for delayed payment
- Employer must arrange finances (loans, asset sale) to pay wages on time
- Workers' livelihood cannot be jeopardized due to employer's business losses

**Impact**: Established that wages are sacrosanct, not deferrable for business reasons.

## Compliance Checklist

### For Employers

**Timely Payment:**
- [ ] Pay monthly wages by 7th (if <1,000 employees) or 10th (if ≥1,000 employees)
- [ ] Pay weekly/daily wages within 7 days of wage period end
- [ ] Pay F&F settlement within 2 working days of termination
- [ ] If delayed: Pay compensation (0.05%-0.5% per day, state rules)

**Mode of Payment:**
- [ ] If wage ≥₹1,000/month: Mandatory bank transfer (NEFT/cheque)
- [ ] Obtain worker's written consent for electronic payment
- [ ] Bank account details, authorization (one-time, on joining)
- [ ] Issue wage slip (gross, deductions, net, payment mode, month)

**Permissible Deductions Only (Section 7):**
- [ ] Fines (≤3% monthly wages, as per Standing Orders, credited to welfare fund)
- [ ] Absence (proportionate, ÷26 days × absent days)
- [ ] PF, ESI, PT, TDS (statutory, worker's authorization for PF)
- [ ] Advances, loans (written authorization, repayment schedule)
- [ ] Amenities (rent, transport, canteen - ≤actual cost, no profit)
- [ ] Damage/loss (inquiry, worker's explanation, ≤50% monthly wages)
- [ ] No arbitrary deductions (must fit Section 7 categories)

**Registers (maintain for 3 years):**
- [ ] Wage register (Form I: gross, deductions, net, signature, payment date)
- [ ] Fine register (if fines imposed: offense, inquiry, amount, disposal)
- [ ] Damage/loss register (if deductions for damage: inquiry, assessment, installments)
- [ ] Muster roll (if daily/weekly wages: attendance, hours, overtime)

**Display:**
- [ ] Abstract of Payment of Wages Act (at notice board, in local language + English)
- [ ] Wage period, payment dates (so workers know when to expect salary)

**Inspector Cooperation:**
- [ ] Allow entry during working hours (no obstruction)
- [ ] Provide registers, documents when requested
- [ ] Answer questions honestly (about wage payment, deductions)

---

### For Employees

**Know Your Rights:**
- [ ] Salary due by 7th/10th of month (check employment contract, company policy)
- [ ] F&F due within 2 days of last working day (resignation, termination)
- [ ] Only Section 7 deductions allowed (verify wage slip, question unauthorized deductions)
- [ ] Delayed payment → Entitled to compensation (0.05%-0.5% per day)

**Verify Wage Slip Monthly:**
- [ ] Gross salary correct (Basic, DA, HRA, Allowances as per offer letter)
- [ ] Deductions authorized (PF, ESI, PT, TDS statutory; loans/advances with your consent)
- [ ] Net salary matches bank credit (check bank statement)
- [ ] If discrepancy: Raise with HR immediately (written email, documented)

**File Claim if Non-Payment:**
- [ ] Within 12 months of due date (time limit, don't delay)
- [ ] Application to Authority (district Labour Commissioner, Form A or state form)
- [ ] Evidence: Wage slips, bank statements, employment contract, resignation letter, email trail
- [ ] Follow up hearings (attend, provide testimony if required)

**Termination F&F:**
- [ ] Calculate dues: Salary (pro-rata for month), Leave encashment, Notice pay (if applicable), Gratuity (if eligible)
- [ ] Demand within 2 days (written email to HR, citing Payment of Wages Act Section 5)
- [ ] If not paid: File claim with Authority + complaint to Inspector (dual action)

## Common Pitfalls

### For Employers
1. **Delaying salary payment**: Beyond 7th/10th = compensation + penalty + prosecution risk. Automate payroll, ensure funds availability before month-end.

2. **Arbitrary deductions**: Deducting for "shrinkage", "wastage", "training cost" without Section 7 basis = Illegal. Workers file claims, you refund + fine.

3. **Not maintaining wage register**: Inspector's first check. No register = fine ₹3,000-₹25,000. Maintain digitally (Excel/HRMS), print when needed.

4. **Delaying F&F settlement**: Must pay within 2 days of termination. Cite "pending approval", "exit interview pending" = Not reasonable cause. Pay first, settle formalities later.

5. **Fines exceeding 3%**: Standing Orders allow ₹200/day fine, impose for 10 days = ₹2,000. If monthly wage ₹20,000, 3% = ₹600 max. Excess ₹1,400 illegal.

### For Employees
1. **Not checking wage slip monthly**: Unauthorized deductions continue for months/years. Verify each month, raise issues immediately (easier to resolve recent deductions).

2. **Accepting verbal assurances**: "F&F will be paid in 15 days" (verbal). Get written email (HR/manager confirmation). If not paid, written evidence for claim.

3. **Missing 12-month claim deadline**: Salary not paid for Jan 2024 (due 7th Feb 2024). File claim before 7th Feb 2025. After that, time-barred (Authority rejects).

4. **Not demanding wage slip**: Employer pays cash, no slip. Cannot prove wages/deductions later. Insist on wage slip (even if cash payment, written receipt/slip mandatory).

## Conclusion

The Payment of Wages Act 1936 is one of India's oldest labour laws, ensuring that workers receive their hard-earned wages without delay or unauthorized deductions. Key takeaways:

1. **Timely Payment**: By 7th (if <1,000 employees) or 10th (if ≥1,000) of following month. F&F within 2 days of termination. Delays attract compensation + penalties.

2. **Mode**: Wages ≥₹1,000/month must be paid via bank (cheque/NEFT). Cash allowed for lower wages. Worker's consent required for electronic payment.

3. **Permissible Deductions (Section 7 only)**: Fines (≤3%), Absence, Statutory (PF/ESI/PT/TDS), Advances/Loans, Amenities (rent, transport), Damage/loss (with inquiry). Total deductions ≤50% wages (protect subsistence).

4. **Prohibited**: Arbitrary deductions (employer's loss, tools, safety equipment, charity) = Illegal. Refund + penalty.

5. **Compensation for Delay**: 0.05%-0.5% per day (state rules). Not high, but symbolic + criminal prosecution risk (₹7,500-₹50,000 fine) is deterrent.

6. **Claims**: Within 12 months, to Authority (Labour Commissioner). Quick resolution (3-6 months), executable as decree.

7. **Registers**: Wage register (Form I) mandatory for 3 years. Digital acceptable (Excel, HRMS).

8. **Penalties**: ₹7,500-₹50,000 (delayed payment, unauthorized deductions), ₹3,000-₹50,000 (no registers), ₹25,000-₹75,000 + imprisonment (obstruction).

Compliance is straightforward: Pay on time (automate payroll, bank transfers), deduct only authorized amounts (Section 7 categories), maintain wage register, provide wage slips. The cost of compliance is minimal (payroll software, admin time), while cost of non-compliance (claims, penalties, prosecution, reputational damage, worker attrition) is significant. Workers' wages fund their families' survival—prompt, full payment is not a favor, it is a legal obligation and moral imperative.
