## Purpose
This protocol provides detailed guidance on the collective bargaining process, negotiation strategies, drafting collective bargaining agreements (CBA), implementation, monitoring, and legal framework governing bargaining in good faith under Indian labour law.

---

## Core Framework

### 1. COLLECTIVE BARGAINING - DEFINITION & SCOPE

#### 1.1 Definition
```
COLLECTIVE BARGAINING:
"Process of negotiation between employer(s) and workers' representatives (trade union)
to determine wages, working conditions, benefits, and other employment terms through
mutual agreement."

NOT STATUTORILY DEFINED in any Central Act (unlike US Labor Relations Act)

CONSTITUTIONAL BASIS:
- Article 19(1)(c): Right to form associations/unions
- Implied right to bargain collectively (essential component of union formation)

ILO CONVENTION 98 (Right to Organise and Collective Bargaining):
India has not ratified, but principles recognized in practice
```

#### 1.2 Subjects of Collective Bargaining
```
MANDATORY SUBJECTS (must bargain if raised):
✓ Wages, basic pay, allowances (HRA, DA, transport)
✓ Working hours, shifts, rest intervals
✓ Leave entitlements (earned, casual, sick, maternity)
✓ Bonus (above statutory minimum 8.33%)
✓ Gratuity (beyond statutory formula)
✓ Welfare facilities (canteen, restrooms, medical)
✓ Retrenchment compensation, VRS packages
✓ Grievance procedures, disciplinary processes

PERMISSIVE SUBJECTS (can negotiate, but not mandatory):
✓ Profit-sharing schemes
✓ Employee stock options (ESOPs)
✓ Housing loans, education assistance
✓ Retirement benefits (beyond PF/pension)
✓ Recreation facilities, club memberships

PROHIBITED SUBJECTS (outside bargaining scope):
✗ Illegal demands (violation of law)
✗ Discrimination (based on caste, religion, gender)
✗ Management core functions:
  - Appointment of senior management
  - Business strategy, product pricing
  - Technology adoption (unless affects employment directly)
  - Merger, acquisition decisions (commercial matters)
```

### 2. COLLECTIVE BARGAINING MODELS

#### 2.1 Distributive Bargaining (Win-Lose)
```
DEFINITION:
Fixed-pie approach: One party's gain is other's loss
Example: Wage negotiation (union wants 15% increase, employer offers 8%)

CHARACTERISTICS:
- Competitive, adversarial
- Positional bargaining (each side stakes claim, defends)
- Compromise in middle ground
- Used when interests conflict directly

TACTICS:
- Union: Strike threat, productivity data, industry benchmarks
- Employer: Financial constraints, competitive pressures, profit margins

OUTCOME:
Settlement point between union's aspiration and employer's bottom line
Example: Union demands 15%, employer offers 8% → Settle at 11%
```

#### 2.2 Integrative Bargaining (Win-Win)
```
DEFINITION:
Expanding-pie approach: Mutual gains, creative solutions

EXAMPLE:
Union: Wage increase (15%)
Employer: Cannot afford 15% immediately

INTEGRATIVE SOLUTION:
- 10% wage increase now
- + Skill development program (increases productivity, future earning potential)
- + Flexible work arrangements (work-life balance, reduces absenteeism)
- + Performance bonuses (aligns incentives)

BOTH BENEFIT:
- Workers: 10% now + long-term growth + quality of life
- Employer: Affordable now + productivity gains + lower attrition

CHARACTERISTICS:
- Collaborative, problem-solving
- Interest-based (not positional)
- Creative options, mutual gains
- Long-term relationship focus

WHEN TO USE:
- Long-term employment relationships
- Stable industries (not crisis/downturn)
- Progressive management culture
```

### 3. COLLECTIVE BARGAINING PROCESS (DETAILED)

#### 3.1 PHASE 1: PREPARATION (2-4 weeks)

**For Union:**
```
STEP 1: MEMBER SURVEY
- Identify priorities (wages, benefits, conditions)
- Rank demands (must-have vs nice-to-have)
- Gauge willingness to strike (if negotiations fail)

STEP 2: DATA COLLECTION
- Industry wage benchmarks (similar companies, region)
- Company financials (profit, EBITDA, if publicly available)
- Inflation data (CPI, WPI for DA calculations)
- Legal minimums (minimum wages, bonus, PF rates)

STEP 3: DRAFT CHARTER OF DEMANDS
Example:
1. Wage increase: 12% on Basic (justified by 6% inflation + 4% productivity gain)
2. DA neutralization: 100% (currently 85%)
3. Bonus: 15% (company profits increased 20% last year)
4. Medical insurance: Increase from ₹3L to ₹5L family floater
5. Maternity leave: 26 weeks paid (statutory) + 4 weeks WFH option
6. Grievance committee: Add 1 more worker representative (currently 2:2, make 3:2)

STEP 4: MANDATE FROM MEMBERS
- General body meeting (present charter)
- Vote on charter (approval)
- Strike authorization (if needed for leverage)

STEP 5: IDENTIFY BATNA (Best Alternative to Negotiated Agreement)
- If no settlement: Strike, conciliation, adjudication
- Cost-benefit: Strike losses vs gains from settlement
- Realistic fallback position
```

**For Employer:**
```
STEP 1: REVIEW DEMANDS
- Assess each demand (financial impact, feasibility, legal compliance)
- Prioritize (which demands acceptable, which negotiable, which reject)

STEP 2: FINANCIAL ANALYSIS
- Calculate cost of demands:
  12% wage increase × 800 workers × ₹30,000 avg wage = ₹2.88cr annual cost
  DA 100% (from 85%) × 800 × ₹8,000 = ₹96L annual cost
  Medical insurance (₹3L→₹5L) × 800 = ₹1.6cr annual cost
  TOTAL: ₹5.44cr additional cost

- Assess affordability:
  EBITDA last year: ₹25cr
  New wage bill: ₹5.44cr (21.7% of EBITDA)
  Sustainable? (if EBITDA margins sufficient, yes)

STEP 3: COMPETITIVE BENCHMARKING
- Industry wage rates (region, skill level)
- Competitor benefits (retention risk if below market)

STEP 4: PREPARE COUNTER-OFFER
Example:
1. Wage increase: 9% (not 12%, but above inflation)
2. DA neutralization: 90% (compromise from 85% to 100%)
3. Bonus: 12% (not 15%, but above statutory 8.33%)
4. Medical insurance: ₹4L (not ₹5L, but increase from ₹3L)
5. Maternity WFH: Accept (low cost, high morale value)
6. Grievance committee: Maintain 2:2 (balanced representation)

STEP 5: IDENTIFY BATNA
- If no settlement: Conciliation, potential adjudication
- Cost of adjudication: Legal fees, time, uncertainty
- Cost of strike: Production loss, customer dissatisfaction
- Bottom line: Maximum affordable offer
```

#### 3.2 PHASE 2: NEGOTIATION (4-8 weeks, multiple rounds)

**Round 1: Opening Positions**
```
UNION:
- Presents charter of demands (formal submission)
- Justifications (inflation, productivity, industry benchmarks)
- Emphasizes worker contributions (no strikes in 3 years, flexibility during COVID)

EMPLOYER:
- Acknowledges receipt, appreciates worker contributions
- Presents counter-offer (initial position)
- Justifications (financial constraints, competitive pressures, market conditions)

GAP ANALYSIS:
Union demands ₹5.44cr cost
Employer offers ₹3.2cr cost
Gap: ₹2.24cr (41% difference)

OUTCOME:
No settlement (expected in Round 1)
Agree to next meeting date
```

**Round 2-3: Exploration**
```
TECHNIQUES:

1. QUESTIONING:
   Union: "What financial constraint prevents 12% wage increase?"
   Employer: "Our EBITDA margin is 8%, industry average is 12%. If we increase wages
   12%, our margin becomes 5%, making us uncompetitive for future investments."

2. UNBUNDLING:
   Break down demands into components
   Example: 12% wage increase
   - 6% inflation adjustment (justified, employer may accept)
   - 4% productivity increase (employer wants proof, data)
   - 2% retention premium (employer: attrition is low 5%, not needed)

3. CONDITIONAL OFFERS:
   Union: "If you accept 11% wage increase, we'll agree to productivity targets
   (10% output increase). If targets met, additional 2% bonus."

   Employer: "If you accept 9% wage increase, we'll provide ₹5L medical insurance
   (better coverage, addresses family security concern)."

4. CAUCUS (private sessions):
   Union team meets separately (reassess position, consult members)
   Employer team meets (CFO reviews financials, HR checks market data)

OUTCOME (Round 2-3):
Narrowing gap
Union: Willing to move from 12% to 11% (if other demands met)
Employer: Willing to move from 9% to 10% (if productivity clause added)
```

**Round 4-5: Bargaining**
```
TACTICS:

1. TRADEOFFS:
   Union: "We'll drop grievance committee demand (3:2), if you accept 11% wage increase."
   Employer: "We'll accept ₹5L medical insurance, if you accept 9.5% wage (not 11%)."

2. PACKAGE DEALS:
   Instead of item-by-item, negotiate holistic package:

   PACKAGE A (Union preference):
   - 10.5% wage increase
   - DA 95%
   - Bonus 13%
   - Medical ₹4.5L
   - Maternity WFH: Yes
   Total cost: ₹4.8cr

   PACKAGE B (Employer preference):
   - 9.5% wage increase
   - DA 92%
   - Bonus 12%
   - Medical ₹5L
   - Maternity WFH: Yes
   Total cost: ₹4.2cr

3. MEDIATOR (if deadlock):
   Invite senior HR consultant, retired judge, labour commissioner (informal mediation)
   Mediator suggests middle ground (Package C):
   - 10% wage increase
   - DA 93%
   - Bonus 12.5%
   - Medical ₹4.75L
   - Maternity WFH: Yes
   Total cost: ₹4.5cr (midpoint)

OUTCOME (Round 4-5):
Tentative agreement on most items
Remaining sticking points: Wage (10% vs 10.5%), DA (92% vs 95%)
```

**Round 6: Final Offer & Settlement**
```
FINALIZATION:

Union: "We can accept 10% wage increase IF DA is 94% (split difference 92-95%)
AND bonus 13% (not 12.5%, as company profits are strong)."

Employer: "We can accept DA 94% and bonus 13% IF wage increase is 10% (not 10.5%)
AND productivity clause: If production increases by 8% in next year, additional
1% wage increase retrospectively applied."

FINAL PACKAGE:
- Wage increase: 10% (compromise from 12% demand, 9% initial offer)
- DA neutralization: 94% (compromise from 85% current, 100% demand)
- Bonus: 13% (subject to profitability, min 10%, max 15%)
- Medical insurance: ₹4.75L family floater (compromise from ₹3L, ₹5L demand)
- Maternity WFH: 4 weeks post-leave (accepted, low cost high value)
- Productivity clause: 8% production increase → +1% wage retrospective
- Grievance committee: Maintain 2:2 (union withdrew demand)
- Validity: 3 years (1st April 2025 to 31st March 2028)

TOTAL COST: ₹4.6cr annually (acceptable to employer, good for workers)

OUTCOME:
SETTLEMENT REACHED
Draft agreement prepared
Subject to ratification (union members, employer board)
```

#### 3.3 PHASE 3: RATIFICATION (1-2 weeks)

**Union Side:**
```
STEP 1: DRAFT REVIEW
Negotiating committee reviews draft CBA
Ensure all agreed terms accurately captured
Legal review (if complex clauses)

STEP 2: GENERAL BODY MEETING
Present settlement to all members
Explain each clause (wage, benefits, validity, productivity)
Q&A session (address member concerns)

STEP 3: VOTE
Secret ballot (recommended for transparency)
Majority required (as per union rules, typically >50%)

POSSIBLE OUTCOMES:
- APPROVED: Proceed to signing
- REJECTED: Back to negotiation (rare, negotiating committee usually has mandate)
- APPROVED WITH CONDITIONS: Renegotiate specific clauses

EXAMPLE (Rejection scenario):
Members reject 10% wage increase, demand 11%
Negotiating committee goes back to employer
Employer: "We can increase to 10.25% (final offer), but DA comes down to 93%"
Union members vote again: APPROVED
```

**Employer Side:**
```
STEP 1: MANAGEMENT REVIEW
HR finalizes draft, CFO reviews financial impact
Legal review (compliance, enforceability)

STEP 2: BOARD APPROVAL (if required)
For large companies: Board of Directors approval needed
For SMEs: Owner/CEO approval sufficient

STEP 3: AUTHORIZATION
Signing authority designated (typically HR Head, CEO)

NOTE:
Employer's ratification is usually smoother (negotiating team has management authority)
Unlike union, employer doesn't need membership vote
```

#### 3.4 PHASE 4: EXECUTION & REGISTRATION (1 week)

**Signing Ceremony:**
```
DATE: Mutually agreed (symbolic date, e.g., May Day, company anniversary)

SIGNATORIES:
- Union: General Secretary, President (authorized by general body resolution)
- Employer: CEO/HR Head (authorized by board resolution)

WITNESSES:
- Union executive members
- Senior management
- Optional: Labour Commissioner representative (adds weight)

DOCUMENT:
- Printed on stamp paper (state-specific stamp duty, typically ₹100-₹500)
- Multiple originals (union copy, employer copy, government copy)
- Signed on each page (initials) + full signature on last page
```

**Registration (Section 12(3), Industrial Disputes Act):**
```
STEP 1: PREPARE APPLICATION
Form: Application for registration of settlement
Attachments:
- Certified copy of settlement (signed)
- List of parties (union registration certificate, employer details)
- Proof of ratification (general body meeting minutes for union)

STEP 2: SUBMIT TO LABOUR COMMISSIONER
- State Labour Commissioner (if state jurisdiction)
- Regional Labour Commissioner (Central) (if central jurisdiction)

STEP 3: SCRUTINY
Labour Commissioner verifies:
- Settlement is voluntary (not under duress)
- Signatories are authorized
- Terms are legal (no violation of labour laws, minimum wage, etc.)

STEP 4: REGISTRATION ORDER
If satisfied: Settlement registered (Ref No., Date)
Certificate issued to parties

TIMELINE:
Usually 7-15 days from submission

LEGAL EFFECT (Section 18(1), 18(3)):
- BINDING on all workers (even non-union members)
- BINDING for period specified (3 years in example)
- Cannot raise industrial dispute on settled issues during validity
- ENFORCEABLE as industrial award (Section 33C execution if breach)
```

### 4. COLLECTIVE BARGAINING AGREEMENT (CBA) - STRUCTURE

#### 4.1 Standard CBA Clauses
```
PREAMBLE:
"This Collective Bargaining Agreement (CBA) is entered into on [DATE] between
[EMPLOYER NAME], hereinafter referred to as 'the Company', and [UNION NAME],
hereinafter referred to as 'the Union', representing the workmen employed in
[ESTABLISHMENT NAME], for the purpose of regulating terms and conditions of
employment, promoting industrial peace, and ensuring mutual cooperation."

ARTICLE 1: RECOGNITION
"The Company recognizes the Union as the sole and exclusive bargaining representative
for all workmen employed in the categories listed in Schedule A attached hereto."

ARTICLE 2: SCOPE
"This Agreement covers all workmen in the bargaining unit, whether members of the
Union or not, and shall govern all terms and conditions of employment during the
validity period."

ARTICLE 3: WAGES & ALLOWANCES
3.1 Basic Salary:
    All workmen shall receive a 10% increase in Basic Salary effective 1st April 2025.

    ILLUSTRATION:
    Category I (Unskilled): Current Basic ₹15,000 → New Basic ₹16,500
    Category II (Semi-skilled): Current Basic ₹20,000 → New Basic ₹22,000
    Category III (Skilled): Current Basic ₹28,000 → New Basic ₹30,800

3.2 Dearness Allowance (DA):
    DA shall be paid at 94% neutralization of CPI-IW (Base Year 2016=100).
    Revision: Quarterly (1st Apr, 1st Jul, 1st Oct, 1st Jan)

    FORMULA:
    DA = (CPI Current Quarter - CPI Base 100) × Neutralization Rate × Basic/100

    Example:
    If CPI = 140 (April 2025), Basic = ₹16,500
    DA = (140 - 100) × 94% × ₹16,500 / 100 = ₹6,204

3.3 House Rent Allowance (HRA):
    - Metro cities (Mumbai, Delhi, Bangalore): 50% of Basic
    - Non-metro: 40% of Basic

3.4 Transport Allowance: ₹2,500/month (fixed)

3.5 Special Allowance: 15% of Basic (to make up CTC structure)

ARTICLE 4: BONUS
4.1 Statutory Bonus: Minimum 8.33% as per Payment of Bonus Act 1965
4.2 Performance Bonus: 13% of (Basic + DA, capped at ₹7,000/month × 12)
4.3 Calculation Ceiling: ₹7,000/month (as per Bonus Act)
4.4 Profitability Clause: If EBITDA <5%, bonus = 10% (minimum statutory + 1.67%)
                          If EBITDA ≥5%, bonus = 13%
                          If EBITDA ≥10%, bonus = 15% (maximum incentive)

ARTICLE 5: WORKING HOURS & OVERTIME
5.1 Normal Working Hours: 8 hours/day, 48 hours/week (as per Factories Act)
5.2 Shifts: 3 shifts (6 AM-2 PM, 2 PM-10 PM, 10 PM-6 AM), rotational
5.3 Rest Intervals: 30 minutes after 5 hours (unpaid)
5.4 Weekly Off: One day per week (rotating, compensatory off if worked)
5.5 Overtime: Double the ordinary rate (OT = Basic + DA × 2 ÷ 26 days ÷ 8 hours)

    ILLUSTRATION:
    Worker: Basic ₹16,500, DA ₹6,204
    Hourly rate = (₹16,500 + ₹6,204) ÷ 26 days ÷ 8 hours = ₹109/hour
    OT rate = ₹109 × 2 = ₹218/hour
    If 10 hours OT in month: ₹218 × 10 = ₹2,180

ARTICLE 6: LEAVE ENTITLEMENTS
6.1 Earned Leave: 18 days/year (1 day per 20 worked, Factories Act)
    Accumulation: Maximum 45 days
    Encashment: On resignation/retirement (all accumulated leave)

6.2 Casual Leave: 10 days/year (non-accumulating, non-encashable)

6.3 Sick Leave: 12 days/year (medical certificate required for >2 consecutive days)
    Accumulation: Maximum 60 days
    Encashment: On exit at 50% of daily wage

6.4 Maternity Leave: 26 weeks (as per Maternity Benefit Act)
    + 4 weeks work-from-home option (new benefit under CBA)

6.5 Paternity Leave: 5 days (within 3 months of child's birth)

ARTICLE 7: WELFARE & BENEFITS
7.1 Medical Insurance: ₹4.75 lakh family floater (employee + spouse + 2 children)
    Cashless facility at network hospitals
    Annual health checkup included

7.2 Group Term Life Insurance: ₹10 lakh coverage (premium paid by employer)

7.3 Canteen: Subsidized meals (₹20/meal, subsidy ₹50/meal by employer)

7.4 Uniform & Safety Equipment: Provided free (2 sets/year)

7.5 Education Assistance: ₹10,000/year for children's education (up to Class 12)

ARTICLE 8: PRODUCTIVITY CLAUSE
8.1 Target: 8% increase in production output (Year 1: April 2025 to March 2026)
8.2 Measurement: Tonnes/man-hour (baseline: current year 2024-25)
8.3 Incentive: If target achieved, additional 1% wage increase (retrospective from April 2025)
8.4 Payout: June 2026 (after verification of annual production data)

ARTICLE 9: GRIEVANCE PROCEDURE
9.1 Step 1: Worker → Immediate Supervisor (resolution within 48 hours)
9.2 Step 2: If unresolved → Department Head (within 5 working days)
9.3 Step 3: If unresolved → Grievance Committee (bipartite, 2 management + 2 union)
            Meeting within 10 working days, decision within 7 days
9.4 Step 4: If unresolved → HR Head + Union General Secretary (within 15 days)
9.5 Final: If still unresolved → Conciliation/Adjudication (as per ID Act)

ARTICLE 10: DISCIPLINARY PROCEDURE
10.1 Minor Misconduct: Verbal warning → Written warning → 3-day suspension
10.2 Major Misconduct: Domestic inquiry (as per Standing Orders) → Dismissal
10.3 Union Representation: Worker entitled to union representative during inquiry
10.4 Natural Justice: Charge sheet, opportunity to explain, impartial inquiry officer

ARTICLE 11: NO-STRIKE / NO-LOCKOUT CLAUSE
11.1 During the validity of this Agreement (3 years), the Union agrees not to
     call or support any strike, go-slow, work-to-rule, or other industrial action.
11.2 The Company agrees not to declare lockout during the validity period.
11.3 Exception: In case of emergency (safety hazard, mass retrenchment), parties
     may invoke dispute resolution (conciliation) before resorting to strike/lockout.

ARTICLE 12: MANAGEMENT RIGHTS
12.1 The Company retains exclusive rights on:
     - Appointment, promotion, transfer of employees (subject to fair practice)
     - Business decisions (product mix, technology, pricing)
     - Work allocation, job assignments
     - Disciplinary action (as per Standing Orders & this Agreement)

12.2 Union may raise grievance if management rights exercised in discriminatory
     or victimizing manner.

ARTICLE 13: DURATION & RENEWAL
13.1 Effective Date: 1st April 2025
13.2 Expiry Date: 31st March 2028 (3 years)
13.3 Renewal: Parties shall commence negotiations 90 days before expiry (1st Jan 2028)
13.4 Status Quo: If renewal negotiations ongoing, existing terms continue till
     new CBA signed (maximum 6 months beyond expiry)

ARTICLE 14: AMENDMENT
14.1 This Agreement may be amended only by mutual written consent.
14.2 Any amendment shall be registered with Labour Commissioner (Section 12(3))

ARTICLE 15: TERMINATION
15.1 Either party may terminate this Agreement by giving 90 days written notice
     (only after completing minimum 2 years, i.e., from 1st April 2027 onwards)
15.2 Upon termination, status quo ante (previous CBA terms) applies till new agreement

ARTICLE 16: DISPUTE RESOLUTION
16.1 Any dispute arising from interpretation/implementation of this Agreement
     shall be referred to bipartite committee (HR Head + Union Gen Secy).
16.2 If bipartite fails, voluntary arbitration (mutually agreed arbitrator).
16.3 Arbitrator's decision shall be final and binding.

ARTICLE 17: SIGNATURES & WITNESSES
IN WITNESS WHEREOF, the parties have executed this Agreement on [DATE] at [PLACE].

FOR THE COMPANY:                      FOR THE UNION:
[CEO Name]                            [General Secretary Name]
Chief Executive Officer               General Secretary
[Signature]                           [Signature]

[HR Head Name]                        [President Name]
Head - Human Resources                President
[Signature]                           [Signature]

WITNESSES:
1. [Name, Designation, Signature]
2. [Name, Designation, Signature]
```

## Landmark Cases

### 1. **Sangli Bank Ltd. v. Workmen (1973) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Bank refused to implement settlement alleging it was signed under duress (strike threat).

**Held**:
- Settlement under strike pressure is NOT per se invalid
- Strike is legitimate bargaining tactic (creates pressure for settlement)
- Duress must be illegal coercion (violence, criminal intimidation), not legitimate industrial action

**Impact**: Legitimized strike threat as bargaining leverage. Settlement valid even if reached to avoid/end strike.

---

### 2. **Management of Reserve Bank of India v. Workmen (1966) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: RBI challenged union's demand for wage increase, arguing it's discretionary, not mandatory subject.

**Held**:
- Wages are MANDATORY subject of collective bargaining
- Employer cannot unilaterally refuse to negotiate on wages
- "Collective bargaining is the very heart of labour-management relations"

**Impact**: Established wages as non-negotiable subject (must bargain in good faith).

---

###3. **Indian Railways v. Shiv Nath (1976) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Dispute over applicability of settlement to non-union members.

**Held**:
- Registered settlement (Section 12(3)) binds ALL workers in bargaining unit
- Even non-union members bound (collective agreement for collective good)
- Cannot opt out claiming non-membership

**Impact**: Strengthened CBA binding effect beyond union membership.

## Conclusion

Collective bargaining is the cornerstone of industrial democracy, enabling workers and employers to mutually determine employment terms through negotiation rather than conflict. Key takeaways:

1. **Preparation is Critical**: Data-driven demands (industry benchmarks, financial analysis) strengthen bargaining position. BATNA clarity prevents bad settlements.

2. **Integrative Preferred**: Win-win solutions (skill development, productivity clauses, flexible benefits) create long-term value beyond wage increases.

3. **Good Faith Bargaining**: Though not explicitly mandated by statute, courts recognize duty to negotiate seriously on mandatory subjects (wages, conditions).

4. **Settlement = Industrial Award**: CBA registered under Section 12(3) is binding on all workers (even non-members) and enforceable as award.

5. **No-Strike Clause**: Common provision during CBA validity (3 years), ensures industrial peace while agreement operative.

6. **Periodic Renewal**: CBA typically valid 2-3 years. Renegotiation begins 90 days before expiry to avoid gap.

Both parties benefit: Workers gain better wages/conditions through collective voice; Employers gain industrial peace, predictability, and cooperative workforce. The alternative—adjudication—is adversarial, uncertain, and damages relationships. Collective bargaining, when conducted in good faith, is superior for both.
