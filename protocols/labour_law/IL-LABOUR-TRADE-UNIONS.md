# IL-LABOUR-TRADE-UNIONS

## Purpose
This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on trade union formation, registration, recognition, rights, and collective bargaining under Indian labour law, primarily governed by the Trade Unions Act 1926 and Industrial Relations Code 2020.

---

## Core Framework

### 1. TRADE UNIONS ACT 1926 - LEGAL FRAMEWORK

#### 1.1 Definition & Scope
```
TRADE UNION (Section 2(h)):
"Any combination of workers or employers, formed primarily for regulating
relations between:
- Workers and employers
- Workers and workers
- Employers and employers
- Imposing restrictive conditions on conduct of trade/business"

TYPES OF UNIONS:
1. Workers' Union (employees)
2. Employers' Union (employers' associations)

APPLICABILITY:
✓ All industries, trades, businesses (public and private sector)
✓ Central and state government employees (with restrictions)
✗ Armed forces (prohibited from forming unions)
✗ Police (some states allow associations, not unions)
```

#### 1.2 Objects of Trade Union
```
LAWFUL OBJECTS (Section 15):
✓ Regulating relations between workers and employers
✓ Regulating relations between workers and workers
✓ Imposing restrictive conditions on trade/business
✓ Representing workers in disputes
✓ Negotiating wages, working conditions
✓ Providing benefits to members (education, medical, insurance)
✓ Political fund activities (with separate accounting)

UNLAWFUL OBJECTS:
✗ Criminal activities
✗ Intimidation, violence, coercion
✗ Activities unrelated to industrial relations
```

### 2. REGISTRATION OF TRADE UNIONS

#### 2.1 Eligibility Requirements (Section 4-6)
```
MINIMUM MEMBERSHIP:
- GENERAL RULE: 7 members minimum (Section 4)
  - At least 10% of workers in establishment, OR
  - 100 workers, whichever is LESS

EXAMPLE 1 (Small establishment):
Establishment with 50 workers
Minimum required: 10% × 50 = 5 workers
But statutory minimum = 7 workers
THEREFORE: 7 workers required

EXAMPLE 2 (Medium establishment):
Establishment with 800 workers
10% × 800 = 80 workers
80 < 100 (whichever is less)
THEREFORE: 80 workers required

EXAMPLE 3 (Large establishment):
Establishment with 2,000 workers
10% × 2,000 = 200 workers
But cap is 100 workers
THEREFORE: 100 workers required (maximum cap)

EXECUTIVE MEMBERSHIP:
- At least 50% of executive must be engaged in industry
- Outsiders allowed (lawyers, social workers) but ≤50%
```

#### 2.2 Registration Process
```
STEP 1: APPLICATION (Section 5)
Submit to Registrar of Trade Unions with:
1. Application in Form A
2. Copy of union rules
3. Statement of particulars (Form B):
   - Names, addresses, occupations of members
   - Name and address of union
   - Executive committee details

STEP 2: DOCUMENTS TO ATTACH
- Constitution/rules of union
- List of members (minimum 7 or as calculated)
- Minutes of meeting where decision to register was taken
- Treasurer's consent (in writing)

STEP 3: SCRUTINY BY REGISTRAR
Registrar examines within 3 months:
- Compliance with Act provisions
- Minimum membership met
- Objects are lawful
- Rules comply with Section 6

STEP 4: CERTIFICATE OF REGISTRATION
If satisfied: Certificate issued (Section 8)
If defects: Notice to rectify within specified time
If serious defects: Registration refused (with reasons)

REGISTRATION FEE:
No fee (free registration under Trade Unions Act 1926)

TIMELINE:
Application → Registrar's decision within 3 months
```

#### 2.3 Union Rules (Section 6)
```
MANDATORY PROVISIONS in union rules:

1. NAME & ADDRESS:
   - Name of union (not identical to existing union)
   - Registered office address

2. OBJECTS:
   - Purposes for which union is established
   - Must be lawful objects

3. MEMBERSHIP:
   - Conditions of admission
   - Membership fees, dues
   - Termination of membership

4. EXECUTIVE:
   - Composition, number of members
   - Election procedure, tenure
   - Powers and duties

5. FUNDS:
   - Sources of income (subscription, donations)
   - Application of funds
   - Annual audit requirements
   - Separate political fund (if any)

6. MEETINGS:
   - Frequency (general body, executive)
   - Quorum requirements
   - Voting procedures

7. DISSOLUTION:
   - Procedure for winding up
   - Distribution of assets

8. AMENDMENTS:
   - Procedure for rule changes
   - Notice requirements
```

### 3. RECOGNITION OF TRADE UNIONS

#### 3.1 Types of Recognition
```
INDIA DOES NOT HAVE STATUTORY RECOGNITION (unlike UK, USA)

RECOGNITION METHODS:

1. VOLUNTARY RECOGNITION (most common):
   - Employer voluntarily recognizes union
   - Enters into collective bargaining
   - No legal compulsion

2. CODE OF DISCIPLINE (tripartite):
   - Indian Labour Conference (1958) Code
   - Principles for recognition (not legally binding)
   - Criteria: Majority membership in establishment

3. STATE-SPECIFIC LAWS:
   - Maharashtra: Maharashtra Recognition of Trade Unions and Prevention
     of Unfair Labour Practices Act 1971 (MRTU & PULP Act)
   - Gujarat: Similar provisions
   - Most states: No statutory recognition

4. ESSENTIAL SERVICES:
   - Banks, ports, electricity, transport
   - Majority union gets preferential treatment
   - Representativeness determines bargaining rights
```

#### 3.2 Maharashtra Model (MRTU & PULP Act 1971)
```
STATUTORY RECOGNITION in Maharashtra:

REPRESENTATIVENESS CRITERIA:
Union must have membership of:
- ≥30% of workers in establishment (qualification)
- Highest membership among competing unions (representative union)

RECOGNITION PROCESS:

STEP 1: APPLICATION
Union applies to Industrial Court (within 6 months of registration)

STEP 2: VERIFICATION
Industrial Court verifies:
- Membership strength (≥30%)
- No multiplicity (only one union can be recognized)

STEP 3: RECOGNITION ORDER
If qualified: Industrial Court grants recognition (for 5 years)

RIGHTS OF RECOGNIZED UNION:
✓ Exclusive collective bargaining rights
✓ Represent workers in disputes (under Industrial Disputes Act)
✓ Access to employer premises (for union activities)
✓ Checkoff system (employer deducts dues from wages)

WITHDRAWAL OF RECOGNITION:
- Membership falls below 30%
- Union indulges in unfair labour practices
- Non-functioning for continuous period
```

#### 3.3 Code of Discipline Criteria (All-India)
```
INDIAN LABOUR CONFERENCE CODE (1958):
Not legally binding, but widely followed

RECOGNITION CRITERIA:

1. SINGLE UNION (preferred):
   If one union has majority (>50%) membership → Recognize

2. MULTIPLE UNIONS (if no majority):
   - Union with largest membership gets recognition
   - Others may get representation proportional to strength

3. VERIFICATION:
   - Membership count by independent agency
   - Check-off system verification (union dues deducted)
   - Secret ballot (rare)

EXAMPLE:
Establishment with 1,000 workers
Union A: 550 members (55%)
Union B: 300 members (30%)
Union C: 150 members (15%)

OUTCOME: Union A recognized (majority >50%)
Unions B & C may get consultative rights (depending on employer)
```

### 4. RIGHTS & IMMUNITIES OF REGISTERED UNIONS

#### 4.1 Legal Immunities (Section 17-18)
```
CIVIL IMMUNITY (Section 17):
"No suit or civil proceeding against registered trade union or office-bearer
for any act in contemplation or furtherance of trade dispute."

PROTECTED ACTIVITIES:
✓ Calling lawful strike
✓ Picketing (peaceful)
✓ Inducing breach of contract (for lawful strike)
✓ Collective bargaining

NOT PROTECTED:
✗ Violence, intimidation
✗ Criminal activities
✗ Breach of contract for unlawful purposes

CRIMINAL IMMUNITY (Section 18):
Registered union members not liable for conspiracy if:
- Act is in contemplation/furtherance of trade dispute
- Act would not be punishable if done by one person
```

#### 4.2 Rights of Trade Unions
```
STATUTORY RIGHTS:

1. COLLECTIVE BARGAINING (primary right):
   - Negotiate wages, working conditions
   - Enter into settlements (binding on members)
   - Represent workers in conciliation, adjudication

2. RIGHT TO STRIKE (with limitations):
   - Section 23 Industrial Disputes Act
   - Subject to notice requirements (14 days in public utility)
   - Cannot strike during conciliation/adjudication

3. ACCESS TO PREMISES:
   - Union representatives can enter workplace
   - For legitimate union activities (meetings, grievances)
   - Subject to reasonable employer restrictions

4. REPRESENTATION IN DISPUTES:
   - Represent workers before Labour Court, Industrial Tribunal
   - File disputes on behalf of members
   - Participate in conciliation proceedings

5. POLITICAL FUND:
   - Maintain separate political fund (Section 16)
   - Contribute to political parties (with member consent)
   - Separate accounting required

6. PROPERTY OWNERSHIP:
   - Own property in union's name
   - Sue and be sued in registered name
   - Contract in union's name
```

#### 4.3 Obligations of Trade Unions
```
STATUTORY OBLIGATIONS:

1. ANNUAL RETURNS (Section 28):
   - Submit to Registrar by 31st May (for preceding year ending 31st March)
   - Form D: General statement of income, assets, liabilities
   - List of changes in executive, office-bearers

2. MAINTENANCE OF ACCOUNTS (Section 27):
   - Proper books of account
   - Annual audit by qualified auditor
   - Balance sheet, income & expenditure statement

3. SEPARATE POLITICAL FUND (Section 16):
   - If contributing to political parties
   - Members can opt out (must not be penalized)
   - Separate audit and accounting

4. NOTICE OF CHANGES (Section 23-24):
   - Change in name, address → Notice to Registrar within 14 days
   - Change in rules → Notice within 15 days (approval required)
   - Change in executive → Annual return

5. DUTY TO MEMBERS:
   - Act in members' interest
   - Democratic functioning
   - Transparent accounting
   - No misappropriation of funds

6. CODE OF DISCIPLINE (voluntary but expected):
   - No strike without notice
   - No inter-union rivalry
   - Peaceful settlement of disputes
   - No unfair labour practices
```

### 5. COLLECTIVE BARGAINING

#### 5.1 Definition & Scope
```
COLLECTIVE BARGAINING:
"Process of negotiation between employer(s) and workers' representatives
(trade union) to determine wages, working conditions, and other employment terms."

NOT DEFINED IN ANY CENTRAL ACT (but recognized as fundamental right)

CONSTITUTIONAL BASIS:
- Article 19(1)(c): Right to form associations/unions
- Implied right to collective bargaining

LEVELS OF BARGAINING:
1. PLANT LEVEL: Single factory/establishment
2. INDUSTRY LEVEL: All units in particular industry (e.g., textiles)
3. NATIONAL LEVEL: Tripartite (Government + Employers + Unions)
```

#### 5.2 Process of Collective Bargaining
```
STAGE 1: PREPARATION
Union: Survey members, identify demands (wages, benefits, conditions)
Employer: Review financial capacity, industry standards

STAGE 2: NEGOTIATION
- Union submits charter of demands
- Series of meetings between union leaders and management
- Give and take (compromise)
- Focus on mutual gains

STAGE 3: SETTLEMENT
- Agreement reached → Draft settlement
- Executive committees approve (both sides)
- Signed by authorized representatives

STAGE 4: REGISTRATION (optional but recommended)
- Submit settlement to Labour Commissioner
- Registered under Section 12(3) Industrial Disputes Act
- Becomes binding on all workers (not just union members)

STAGE 5: IMPLEMENTATION
- Employer implements agreed terms
- Union monitors compliance
- Grievance mechanism for violations
```

#### 5.3 Collective Bargaining Agreement (CBA)
```
TYPICAL CONTENTS OF CBA:

1. WAGES & ALLOWANCES:
   - Basic pay revision (percentage or absolute)
   - Dearness allowance (DA) formula
   - HRA, transport, other allowances
   - Annual increment structure

2. WORKING CONDITIONS:
   - Working hours, shifts, overtime
   - Rest intervals, weekly offs
   - Safety equipment, canteen facilities

3. BENEFITS:
   - Leave entitlements (beyond statutory)
   - Medical insurance, group insurance
   - Bonus (beyond Payment of Bonus Act minimum)
   - Gratuity (if more generous than statute)

4. EMPLOYMENT SECURITY:
   - Retrenchment compensation
   - Lay-off compensation
   - VRS packages
   - Job security clauses (no termination without cause)

5. GRIEVANCE REDRESSAL:
   - Grievance procedure (steps, timeline)
   - Bipartite committee composition
   - Arbitration clause (for deadlocks)

6. UNION RIGHTS:
   - Check-off system (dues deduction)
   - Union office space on premises
   - Paid leave for union activities

7. DURATION & RENEWAL:
   - Validity period (usually 2-3 years)
   - Automatic renewal or renegotiation clause
   - Termination notice period

EXAMPLE CLAUSE (Wage Revision):
"All workers in Category I shall receive a wage increase of 12% on basic
salary effective 1st April 2025. DA shall be revised quarterly based on
CPI-IW, with neutralization at 100%. Annual increment of 5% on basic
guaranteed for workers with satisfactory performance."
```

### 6. UNFAIR LABOUR PRACTICES

#### 6.1 By Employers (Schedule to MRTU & PULP Act, adopted widely)
```
UNFAIR PRACTICES BY EMPLOYER:

1. INTERFERENCE WITH UNION FORMATION:
   ✗ Threatening workers for joining union
   ✗ Offering incentives to refrain from union membership
   ✗ Discrimination against union members

2. DOMINATION OF UNION:
   ✗ Employer-sponsored union ("yellow union")
   ✗ Financing union activities to control
   ✗ Interference in union elections

3. VICTIMIZATION:
   ✗ Dismissal for union activities
   ✗ Denial of promotion, transfer to unfavorable location
   ✗ Discriminatory treatment

4. REFUSAL TO BARGAIN:
   ✗ Refusing to recognize/negotiate with recognized union
   ✗ Refusing to sign agreed settlement
   ✗ Unilateral change in employment conditions during negotiation

5. EMPLOYING WORKERS DURING STRIKE:
   ✗ Employing new workers during lawful strike (to replace strikers)
   ✗ Threatening permanent replacement

REMEDIES:
- Complaint to Industrial Court/Labour Court
- Reinstatement with back wages (if dismissed)
- Compensation to workers
- Prosecution of employer (fine, imprisonment)
```

#### 6.2 By Trade Unions
```
UNFAIR PRACTICES BY UNION:

1. COERCION OF WORKERS:
   ✗ Forcing workers to join union (closed shop illegal in India)
   ✗ Threatening violence for non-participation in strike
   ✗ Obstruction of workers willing to work

2. COERCION OF EMPLOYER:
   ✗ Violence, intimidation during negotiations
   ✗ Gherao (unlawful confinement of management)
   ✗ Damage to employer property

3. ILLEGAL STRIKES:
   ✗ Strike during conciliation/adjudication (Section 23 ID Act)
   ✗ Strike without 14 days notice (in public utility services)
   ✗ Wildcat strike (without union approval)

4. MISREPRESENTATION:
   ✗ False claims of membership strength
   ✗ Forged signatures on membership forms
   ✗ Collecting dues from non-members

CONSEQUENCES:
- Cancellation of union registration (extreme cases)
- Withdrawal of recognition (if state has recognition law)
- Civil/criminal liability for office-bearers
- Damages to employer for losses
```

### 7. UNION MEMBERSHIP & GOVERNANCE

#### 7.1 Membership Rights
```
RIGHTS OF MEMBERS:

1. PARTICIPATION:
   ✓ Vote in union elections (executive committee)
   ✓ Attend general body meetings
   ✓ Access to union accounts (annual report)

2. REPRESENTATION:
   ✓ Grievance representation by union
   ✓ Legal aid in employment disputes
   ✓ Collective bargaining benefits

3. DISSENT:
   ✓ Oppose union decisions (through democratic process)
   ✓ Contest elections
   ✓ Opt out of political fund contributions

OBLIGATIONS OF MEMBERS:
- Pay membership dues (as per union rules)
- Abide by union constitution
- Support lawful union activities
- No indiscipline (internal disputes resolved democratically)
```

#### 7.2 Union Elections
```
ELECTION OF EXECUTIVE COMMITTEE:

FREQUENCY:
Trade Unions Act 1926: Silent (usually every 2-3 years as per union rules)

ELIGIBILITY TO VOTE:
All members in good standing (dues paid up to date)

ELIGIBILITY TO CONTEST:
- Member for minimum period (union rules, typically 6-12 months)
- Not disqualified (due to misconduct, misappropriation)

ELECTION PROCESS (typical):
1. Election Committee formed (independent members)
2. Notice of elections (30 days advance)
3. Nomination period (7-15 days)
4. Scrutiny of nominations
5. Campaigning period (if contested)
6. Secret ballot voting
7. Counting and declaration
8. Oath of office

CHALLENGES:
- Election disputes resolved internally (appeal to general body)
- If internal remedies exhausted → Civil court (rare)
- Registrar of Trade Unions has no jurisdiction over internal elections
```

#### 7.3 Financial Management
```
SOURCES OF INCOME:

1. MEMBERSHIP DUES:
   - Monthly/annual subscription (as per union rules)
   - Typically ₹10-₹100 per month (workmen)
   - Professional unions: ₹500-₹5,000 per year

2. DONATIONS:
   - From members, sympathizers
   - Must be accounted separately

3. FUND-RAISING EVENTS:
   - Cultural programs, sports, seminars

4. INVESTMENT INCOME:
   - Interest on fixed deposits
   - Dividends (if union owns securities)

APPLICATION OF FUNDS (Section 15):
PERMITTED:
✓ Salary of office-bearers, staff
✓ Legal expenses (disputes, representation)
✓ Welfare of members (education, medical, funeral)
✓ Conduct of trade disputes (strike pay, legal fees)
✓ Political fund (separate accounting, with member consent)
✓ Affiliation fees (to federations, international unions)

PROHIBITED:
✗ Personal benefit of office-bearers (beyond reasonable salary)
✗ Speculative investments
✗ Loans to office-bearers (without general body approval)
```

## Landmark Cases

### 1. **Kameshwar Prasad v. State of Bihar (1962) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Challenge to Bihar govt order prohibiting govt employees from joining certain political parties/unions.

**Held**:
- Right to form associations (Article 19(1)(c)) includes right to form trade unions
- Collective bargaining is implicit in right to form unions
- State can impose reasonable restrictions (Article 19(4))

**Impact**: Established constitutional basis for trade union rights in India.

---

### 2. **B.R. Singh v. Union of India (1989) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Central government employees challenged prohibition on strikes.

**Held**:
- Right to form union does NOT include right to strike
- Strike is not a fundamental right
- Government can prohibit strikes in essential services

**Impact**: Clarified that while union formation is protected, strike is not an absolute right.

---

### 3. **Indian Hume Pipe Co. Ltd. v. Workmen (1960) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Employer questioned union's authority to raise dispute on behalf of dismissed worker.

**Held**:
- Registered union has locus standi to raise industrial disputes
- Even for individual workers (if affects employment relationship)
- Union acts as representative of workers' collective interests

**Impact**: Strengthened unions' representative capacity in disputes.

---

### 4. **Western India Match Co. v. Workmen (1974) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Employer refused to recognize union claiming majority membership.

**Held**:
- In absence of statutory recognition law (Central Act), employer not obligated to recognize
- Recognition is voluntary (except in states like Maharashtra with MRTU Act)
- However, refusing to negotiate may be unfair labour practice

**Impact**: Highlighted gap in Central law (no compulsory recognition), but moral/practical obligation to recognize majority union.

---

### 5. **AITUC v. Hindustan Vacuum Brake Ltd. (1968) - Karnataka High Court**
**Facts**: Employer favored minority union over majority union for negotiations.

**Held**:
- Employer should recognize union representing majority
- Even without statutory compulsion, principles of industrial democracy require it
- Dealing with minority union when majority exists is unfair labour practice

**Impact**: Strengthened case for recognition based on representativeness.

## Compliance Checklist

### For Trade Unions

**Registration:**
- [ ] Apply with minimum 7 members (or 10%/100, whichever less)
- [ ] Draft union rules (Section 6 mandatory provisions)
- [ ] Obtain members' consent and signatures (Form B)
- [ ] Submit application to Registrar (Form A)
- [ ] Obtain Certificate of Registration within 3 months

**Annual Compliance:**
- [ ] Submit annual returns by 31st May (Form D)
- [ ] Conduct annual audit (qualified auditor)
- [ ] Publish balance sheet, income & expenditure statement
- [ ] Convene annual general meeting (present accounts)
- [ ] Update executive committee changes (within 14 days)

**Financial Management:**
- [ ] Maintain proper books of account
- [ ] Separate political fund (if applicable)
- [ ] Issue receipts for membership dues, donations
- [ ] Application of funds as per Section 15 (lawful objects only)
- [ ] No misappropriation by office-bearers

**Collective Bargaining:**
- [ ] Prepare charter of demands (based on member survey)
- [ ] Conduct negotiations in good faith
- [ ] Execute settlement in writing (signatures of authorized representatives)
- [ ] Register settlement with Labour Commissioner (Section 12(3) ID Act)
- [ ] Communicate CBA terms to all members

**Member Relations:**
- [ ] Conduct timely elections (as per union rules)
- [ ] Democratic functioning (general body approval for major decisions)
- [ ] Transparent accounting (members' right to information)
- [ ] Grievance redressal mechanism for members

---

### For Employers

**Recognition (if applicable):**
- [ ] Verify union's representativeness (membership strength)
- [ ] Check registration status (certificate from Registrar)
- [ ] In Maharashtra/Gujarat: Comply with statutory recognition (Industrial Court order)
- [ ] Enter into recognition agreement (rights, obligations)

**Collective Bargaining:**
- [ ] Negotiate in good faith (with recognized/representative union)
- [ ] Provide relevant information (financial statements, if requested)
- [ ] Execute settlement in writing
- [ ] Register settlement with Labour Commissioner
- [ ] Implement CBA terms (wages, benefits, conditions)

**Unfair Labour Practices (avoid):**
- [ ] No interference with union formation/activities
- [ ] No victimization of union members/office-bearers
- [ ] No employer-dominated "yellow unions"
- [ ] No refusal to bargain with recognized union
- [ ] No discrimination in promotions, transfers due to union membership

**Check-Off System (if agreed):**
- [ ] Deduct union dues from wages (with written consent of worker)
- [ ] Remit to union within specified period (usually monthly)
- [ ] Provide statement of deductions (names, amounts)
- [ ] Allow workers to opt out (cannot be compulsory)

**Union Access:**
- [ ] Permit union representatives to enter premises (for legitimate activities)
- [ ] Provide space for union office (if agreed in CBA)
- [ ] Allow notice boards for union communications
- [ ] Grant leave to union office-bearers (for union work, as per CBA)

## Common Pitfalls

### For Trade Unions
1. **Non-filing of annual returns**: Registrar can cancel registration (Section 10). Ensure timely filing by 31st May.

2. **Misappropriation of funds**: Office-bearers liable criminally + civilly. Maintain transparency, conduct audits.

3. **Indulging in unfair labour practices**: Coercion, violence → Loss of recognition, criminal liability. Stick to lawful methods.

4. **Claiming false membership**: Verification exposes fraud → Loss of credibility, recognition. Maintain genuine membership records.

5. **Violating strike laws**: Illegal strikes (during conciliation, without notice) → Loss of civil immunity, dismissal of strikers.

### For Employers
1. **Refusing to recognize majority union**: Unfair labour practice, industrial unrest. Pragmatically, recognize representative union.

2. **Victimizing union members**: Reinstatement with back wages, compensation. Strict adherence to non-discrimination.

3. **Unilateral change in conditions during CBA negotiation**: Unfair practice. Maintain status quo during bargaining.

4. **Creating employer-dominated union**: "Yellow union" tactics backfire. Genuine unions emerge stronger. Be fair.

5. **Refusing to implement registered settlement**: Breach of statutory obligation (Section 12(3) ID Act binding). Legal enforcement + contempt.

## Calculation Examples

### Example 1: Minimum Membership for Registration
```
SCENARIO 1: Factory with 65 workers
Calculation: 10% × 65 = 6.5 workers
But statutory minimum = 7 workers
RESULT: 7 workers required for registration

SCENARIO 2: Manufacturing unit with 450 workers
Calculation: 10% × 450 = 45 workers
45 < 100 (cap)
RESULT: 45 workers required

SCENARIO 3: Large textile mill with 5,000 workers
Calculation: 10% × 5,000 = 500 workers
But cap = 100 workers (maximum)
RESULT: 100 workers required (not 500)
```

### Example 2: Union Membership Verification (Recognition)
```
SCENARIO:
ABC Manufacturing Ltd., 1,200 workers
Three unions claim representation:

Union A (Bharatiya Mazdoor Sangh): 680 members (56.67%)
Union B (Hind Mazdoor Sabha): 350 members (29.17%)
Union C (Independent Workers Union): 170 members (14.17%)

TOTAL: 1,200 (all workers unionized)

VERIFICATION PROCESS:
1. Check registration certificates (all 3 registered)
2. Membership forms verification (signatures, consent)
3. Check-off records (union dues deduction from wages)
4. Cross-check duplicates (worker cannot be member of 2 unions in same industry)

OUTCOME:
Union A has MAJORITY (>50%) → RECOGNIZED UNION
(In Maharashtra: Industrial Court would issue recognition order for 5 years)

Union B & C: May get consultative rights (proportional representation in joint committees)
```

### Example 3: Collective Bargaining Agreement - Wage Revision
```
SCENARIO:
XYZ Ltd. (800 workers), CBA negotiations for 2025-2027

UNION'S DEMAND:
- 15% wage increase on Basic
- DA neutralization 100% (currently 85%)
- Bonus 15% (currently 10%)
- Annual increment 6% (currently 4%)

EMPLOYER'S COUNTER:
- 8% wage increase
- DA neutralization 90%
- Bonus 12%
- Annual increment 5%

NEGOTIATION (5 rounds over 2 months):
Round 1-3: Positions stated, justifications (union: inflation 6%, productivity increased 12%)
            (employer: profitability constraints, industry benchmark 9% wage increase)
Round 4: Mediator from Labour Commissioner's office
Round 5: Agreement reached

FINAL SETTLEMENT:
- 12% wage increase on Basic (effective 1st April 2025)
- DA neutralization 95% (phased: 90% Year 1, 95% Year 2-3)
- Bonus 13% (subject to profitability, minimum 10%)
- Annual increment 5.5%
- Additional: Medical insurance coverage increased from ₹3L to ₹5L family floater
- Validity: 3 years (1st April 2025 to 31st March 2028)

IMPACT:
Worker earning ₹25,000/month Basic:
Old: Basic ₹25,000 + DA ₹8,500 (85% × ₹10,000) = ₹33,500
New: Basic ₹28,000 (12% increase) + DA ₹10,640 (95% × ₹11,200) = ₹38,640
Monthly increase: ₹5,140 (15.3% overall increase in take-home)

Annual cost to company:
₹5,140 × 800 workers × 12 months = ₹4.93 crore additional wage bill per year
```

## Conclusion

Trade unions are fundamental institutions of industrial democracy in India, balancing power between employers and workers. Key takeaways:

1. **Registration**: 7 members minimum (or 10%/100 workers, whichever less), free registration, Certificate within 3 months.

2. **Recognition**: No central law for compulsory recognition (except Maharashtra MRTU Act). Voluntary recognition based on majority membership is industrial best practice.

3. **Rights**: Collective bargaining (primary right), civil & criminal immunity for lawful activities, representation in disputes, property ownership in union name.

4. **Collective Bargaining**: Negotiation between union and employer, settlement on wages/conditions, registration under Section 12(3) ID Act makes it binding on all workers.

5. **Unfair Practices**: Prohibited for both employers (victimization, refusal to bargain) and unions (coercion, illegal strikes). Remedies through Industrial/Labour Courts.

6. **Compliance**: Annual returns by 31st May, annual audit, democratic elections, transparent accounting.

Employers benefit from stable industrial relations through genuine recognition and good-faith bargaining. Workers gain collective voice and improved terms through organized unions. The law facilitates this balance while prohibiting coercion and violence from either side.
