# Termination for Cause - Misconduct & Domestic Inquiry Framework

## Protocol ID
**IL-LABOUR-TERMINATION-FOR-CAUSE** (v1.0)

---

## Purpose
Guide employers and HR professionals through the legal framework, procedural requirements, and best practices for terminating employees for misconduct or poor performance under Indian labour law, covering grounds for termination, domestic inquiry process following Principles of Natural Justice, charge sheet drafting, inquiry procedures, proportionate punishment, payment entitlements, and common pitfalls.

---

## Legal Framework

### 1. Statutory Provisions

**Industrial Employment (Standing Orders) Act 1946 - Standing Order 25**:
```
"Acts and omissions which constitute misconduct:
(a) Willful insubordination or disobedience
(b) Theft, fraud, or dishonesty
(c) Taking or giving bribes
(d) Habitual absence or late attendance without leave
(e) Absence without leave for more than 10 days
(f) Damage to property through negligence
(g) Disorderly or riotous behavior
(h) Habitual breach of rules
(i) Striking work in contravention of the Act
[15-20 misconduct categories total in Model Standing Orders]
```

**Industrial Disputes Act 1947 - Section 25F** (Retrenchment):
```
No workman shall be retrenched unless:
(a) One month's written notice OR wages in lieu given
(b) Compensation paid (15 days average wages × years of service)
(c) Notice served on appropriate government

NOTE: Section 25F does NOT apply to termination for misconduct (cause-based
termination). Only applies to termination without cause (retrenchment).
```

**Payment of Gratuity Act 1972 - Section 4(6)** (Forfeiture):
```
Gratuity may be wholly or partially forfeited if the services of such employee
have been terminated for any act, willful omission or negligence causing any
damage or loss to, or destruction of, property belonging to the employer, OR
for riotous or disorderly conduct or any other act of violence, OR for any act
constituting an offence involving moral turpitude committed by him in the course
of his employment.
```

### 2. Principles of Natural Justice

**Mandatory Requirements** (Per Supreme Court Rulings):

**Audi Alteram Partem** (Hear the Other Side):
1. **Notice of Charges**: Employee must be informed of specific allegations (charge sheet)
2. **Opportunity to Explain**: Employee must be given chance to present defense
3. **Right to Hearing**: Employee must be heard before adverse decision taken
4. **Right to Cross-Examine**: Employee can cross-examine witnesses against him
5. **Right to Representation**: Employee can engage representative (union leader, lawyer)

**Nemo Judex in Causa Sua** (No One Judge in Own Cause):
1. **Impartial Inquiry Officer**: Person conducting inquiry must be unbiased (not complainant, not reporting manager if he's witness)
2. **No Predetermined Conclusion**: Inquiry must be genuine investigation, not formality to rubber-stamp management decision

**Landmark Case**: Indian Bank v. M. Sadananda Bhat (SC 1994)
- **Held**: Domestic inquiry MUST follow Principles of Natural Justice. Even if misconduct proven, if inquiry vitiated by procedural lapses (no opportunity to cross-examine, biased inquiry officer), termination order will be set aside by Labour Court.

## Grounds for Termination

### 1. Misconduct Categories

**A. THEFT, FRAUD, EMBEZZLEMENT**:
- Stealing company property (raw materials, finished goods, equipment, cash)
- Misappropriation of funds (expense fraud, vendor kickbacks)
- Forgery of documents (fake medical certificates, attendance records)

**Example**:
```
SCENARIO: Warehouse Manager Theft

Facts:
- Warehouse manager (5 years service) caught on CCTV removing 10 cartons of
  finished goods (worth ₹2 lakh) from warehouse at night
- Security guard detained him at gate, recovered goods from his car
- Manager admitted theft during initial questioning

Domestic Inquiry:
- Charge: Theft of company property (10 cartons, ₹2L value) on 15th Jan 2025
- Evidence: CCTV footage, security log, recovered goods, manager's written statement
- Manager's defense: "Financial emergency due to wife's medical treatment, intended
  to return goods after selling"
- Inquiry finding: GUILTY (intent to permanently deprive company of property = theft,
  financial emergency not valid defense)

Punishment: DISMISSAL (termination without notice)

Consequences:
- No notice pay
- Gratuity FORFEITED (Section 4(6)(a) - theft constitutes moral turpitude)
- Leave encashment: Paid (for earned leave already accrued, but company may
  deduct value of stolen goods ₹2L from final settlement)
- Police complaint: Company files criminal case (IPC Section 381 - theft by employee)
```

**B. WILLFUL INSUBORDINATION**:
- Refusal to obey lawful orders from superior
- Disrespectful behavior towards management
- Challenging authority in front of others

**Example**:
```
SCENARIO: Refusal to Accept Transfer

Facts:
- Senior Manager (8 years service) given transfer order (Mumbai to Delhi, 60 days
  notice, relocation assistance ₹3L)
- Employee agreement states: "Company may transfer employee to any India location
  with 60 days notice"
- Manager refuses transfer (cites personal reasons - children's education, wife's job)
- Sends email to CEO copying entire company: "This transfer order is harassment,
  I will not comply"

Domestic Inquiry:
- Charge: Willful insubordination (refusal to comply with lawful transfer order)
- Evidence: Transfer order (60 days notice given, relocation assistance offered),
  employee's refusal email, employment agreement clause 4
- Employee's defense: "Transfer is victimization, I am being singled out, my
  performance is excellent, no valid business reason for transfer"
- Inquiry finding: GUILTY (employment agreement clearly permits transfers, 60 days
  notice given, relocation assistance provided = lawful order, employee's refusal
  is insubordination)

Punishment: DISMISSAL

Proportionality Check:
- Courts may consider: Is dismissal too harsh? Could lesser punishment suffice
  (demotion, transfer with warning)?
- Factor: Public defiance (email to CEO copying entire company) aggravates misconduct
  → Dismissal proportionate
```

**C. HABITUAL ABSENCE / UNAUTHORIZED LEAVE**:
- Repeated unauthorized absences
- Absence without leave >10 days (Standing Order 25(e) - deemed abandonment)
- Persistent late-coming despite warnings

**Example**:
```
SCENARIO: Abandonment of Duty

Facts:
- Software engineer (2 years service) absent from work for 15 consecutive days
  (1st to 15th March) without intimation
- No leave application, no response to manager's calls/emails
- HR sends letter to registered address: "Report within 3 days or explain absence,
  else deemed abandonment"
- Employee doesn't respond

Termination Process:
- Charge sheet not required (abandonment = voluntary resignation, not misconduct
  requiring inquiry)
- Termination letter issued (18th March): "You are deemed to have abandoned
  employment by remaining absent for 15 days without leave/intimation"

Payment:
- Salary till 15th March (last date worked)
- Leave encashment for accrued leave
- Gratuity: NOT forfeited (abandonment ≠ moral turpitude)

NOTE: If employee later claims (in Labour Court) that absence was due to medical
emergency/family crisis (submits hospital records), Court may order reinstatement.
BETTER PRACTICE: Conduct inquiry even for abandonment cases (to establish whether
absence was willful or genuine emergency).
```

**D. SEXUAL HARASSMENT**:
- Sexual harassment (unwelcome sexual advances, requests for sexual favors, sexually
  colored remarks)
- Governed by POSH Act 2013 (Prevention of Sexual Harassment at Workplace Act)

**Example**:
```
SCENARIO: Sexual Harassment by Manager

Facts:
- Female employee (junior executive) files complaint with Internal Complaints
  Committee (ICC) under POSH Act
- Allegation: Male manager (her direct superior) sent inappropriate WhatsApp
  messages, made sexual comments during office hours, touched her inappropriately
- ICC investigation: Statements recorded (complainant, respondent, 3 witnesses),
  WhatsApp messages submitted as evidence

ICC Findings:
- GUILTY of sexual harassment (WhatsApp messages clearly inappropriate, witness
  corroborates inappropriate comments)

ICC Recommendation:
- Terminate respondent's employment (Section 13(3)(i) POSH Act - dismissal for
  proven sexual harassment)

Company Action:
- Accept ICC recommendation → Issue termination order (no separate domestic inquiry
  required, ICC inquiry satisfies Principles of Natural Justice)

Punishment: DISMISSAL

Consequences:
- No notice pay
- Gratuity: FORFEITED (sexual harassment = moral turpitude per Section 4(6)(a))
- Police complaint: Company may file FIR (IPC Section 354A - sexual harassment)
- Blacklisted: Company shares ICC findings with industry associations (if permitted
  by POSH Act)
```

**E. POOR PERFORMANCE** (Not Misconduct, But Grounds for Termination):

**Important Distinction**:
- Misconduct = Willful wrongdoing (theft, insubordination, violence)
- Poor Performance = Inability to meet job requirements (not willful, but incompetence)

**Termination for Poor Performance**:
1. **Documentation Required**: Performance appraisals showing consistent underperformance (2-3 cycles)
2. **Performance Improvement Plan (PIP)**: Give employee opportunity to improve (3-6 months PIP with clear targets)
3. **Warnings**: Written warnings (first warning, final warning)
4. **Domestic Inquiry**: Some courts require inquiry even for performance termination (to establish poor performance objectively)

**Example**:
```
SCENARIO: Sales Manager Consistent Underperformance

Facts:
- Sales manager (4 years service) consistently misses targets
  - FY 2022-23: 60% of target achieved (target ₹1 crore, achieved ₹60 lakh)
  - FY 2023-24: 55% of target (target ₹1.2 crore, achieved ₹66 lakh)
  - Q1 FY 2024-25: 45% of target (on track for worst year)

Process:
- Month 1-3: PIP issued (clear targets: ₹30 lakh sales per quarter, 15 new
  customers, 80% customer retention)
- Month 4-6: PIP review - Only 50% of targets met
- Month 7: Final warning issued (achieve ₹35 lakh in next quarter or termination)
- Month 10: Still 50% achievement

Termination:
- Charge: Persistent failure to meet performance standards despite warnings and PIP
- Inquiry: Manager cannot show valid reasons for underperformance (market conditions
  affected all sales team, but others met 80% targets)
- Inquiry finding: GUILTY of poor performance
- Punishment: TERMINATION with notice (60 days notice OR payment in lieu)

Payment:
- Notice pay (if not required to serve notice)
- Leave encashment
- Gratuity: NOT forfeited (poor performance ≠ moral turpitude)
- Severance: 1 month per year of service (company policy, ₹2.4L for 4 years service)
```

## Domestic Inquiry Process - Step by Step

### Step 1: Preliminary Investigation

**Before Issuing Charge Sheet**:
1. **Fact-Finding**: Gather evidence (CCTV footage, witness statements, documents, emails)
2. **Prima Facie Case**: Verify whether there's sufficient evidence to proceed (if evidence weak, don't issue charge sheet)
3. **Suspension Pending Inquiry** (Optional):
   - If employee's presence may hamper investigation (witness intimidation) OR pose risk (violence, theft)
   - Suspension order: "You are suspended pending inquiry with immediate effect. During suspension, you will receive 50% of Basic + DA (subsistence allowance) as per Standing Orders."
   - Duration: Inquiry should be completed within 30-60 days (prolonged suspension courts frown upon)

### Step 2: Charge Sheet

**Format**:
```
[Company Letterhead]

Date: 15th March 2025

Mr. Rajesh Kumar
Warehouse Manager
Employee ID: WH12345

Subject: Charge Sheet for Misconduct

Dear Mr. Kumar,

This is to inform you that the Company has received information that you have
committed an act of misconduct on 10th March 2025, as detailed below:

CHARGE:

On 10th March 2025 at approximately 11:30 PM, you removed 10 cartons of finished
goods (Product: ABC Soap, 100 units per carton, total value ₹2,00,000) from the
warehouse without authorization and attempted to take them out of the factory
premises in your personal vehicle (Registration No. KA-01-AB-1234).

You were apprehended by the security guard (Mr. Suresh) at the main gate. Upon
inspection of your vehicle, the 10 cartons were recovered. The incident was
recorded on CCTV camera (Gate No. 2).

This act constitutes THEFT of company property, which is a grave misconduct under
Standing Order 25(b) of the Company's Certified Standing Orders.

EXPLANATION REQUIRED:

You are required to submit your written explanation within 48 hours (by 17th March
2025, 5:00 PM) as to why disciplinary action should not be taken against you.

If you fail to submit your explanation within the stipulated time, it will be
presumed that you have nothing to say in your defense and the Company will proceed
with further action.

Yours sincerely,

[Signature]
HR Manager
ABC Manufacturing Ltd

EMPLOYEE ACKNOWLEDGMENT:
I acknowledge receipt of this charge sheet on _______________

Employee Signature: _______________
Date: _______________
```

**Key Elements of Charge Sheet**:
1. **Specific Allegations**: Date, time, location, what exactly employee did (NOT vague like "you misbehaved")
2. **Evidence Preview**: What evidence exists (CCTV, witnesses, documents) - gives employee fair notice
3. **Standing Order Reference**: Which specific misconduct category violated
4. **Explanation Timeline**: 48 hours to 7 days (reasonable time, not too short)

### Step 3: Employee's Written Explanation

**Employee's Response Options**:

**Option A: ADMIT**:
```
Date: 16th March 2025

To,
The HR Manager
ABC Manufacturing Ltd

Subject: Explanation to Charge Sheet dated 15th March 2025

I acknowledge receipt of the charge sheet. I admit that I removed 10 cartons of
ABC Soap from the warehouse on 10th March 2025.

I deeply regret my actions. I was facing a severe financial crisis due to my
wife's hospitalization (cancer treatment requiring ₹5 lakh immediately). In
desperation, I took the decision to remove the goods with the intention of selling
them to arrange funds.

I realize this was wrong and I have betrayed the Company's trust. I sincerely
apologize and request the Company to consider my 5 years of dedicated service
and this one-time lapse in judgment.

I am ready to face any disciplinary action the Company deems fit.

Yours sincerely,
Rajesh Kumar
```

**Result**: If employee admits, inquiry may be dispensed with (management can directly pass punishment order based on admission). BUT many courts still require inquiry (to assess credibility of mitigating circumstances like "financial emergency").

**Option B: DENY**:
```
Date: 16th March 2025

To,
The HR Manager
ABC Manufacturing Ltd

Subject: Explanation to Charge Sheet dated 15th March 2025

I deny the allegations in the charge sheet. The facts are as follows:

1. On 10th March 2025, I was working late (till 11 PM) to complete month-end
   inventory reconciliation (as instructed by my superior, Mr. Sharma, Production
   Head).

2. At around 11:15 PM, Mr. Sharma called me and asked me to deliver 10 cartons
   of ABC Soap to a customer (XYZ Traders) whose truck had arrived at Gate No. 2
   for urgent delivery.

3. I loaded the 10 cartons in my car (as the delivery vehicle was occupied) and
   proceeded to Gate No. 2 as per Mr. Sharma's instructions.

4. At the gate, the security guard (Mr. Suresh) stopped me and accused me of
   theft. I tried to explain that this was an authorized delivery as per Mr. Sharma's
   instructions, but he did not believe me.

5. I have been falsely implicated in this case. I request the Company to verify
   with Mr. Sharma (Production Head) and XYZ Traders (customer) who had placed
   the urgent order.

I request a fair inquiry to establish the truth.

Yours sincerely,
Rajesh Kumar
```

**Result**: Proceed to full domestic inquiry (since employee denies charges and has raised defense - authorized delivery)

**Format**:
```
[Company Letterhead]

Date: 20th March 2025

Mr. Rajesh Kumar
Warehouse Manager

Subject: Inquiry Notice - Charges of Misconduct dated 15th March 2025

Dear Mr. Kumar,

This is with reference to the charge sheet dated 15th March 2025 and your written
explanation dated 16th March 2025.

Your explanation is not found satisfactory. Accordingly, a domestic inquiry will
be conducted to examine the charges against you.

INQUIRY DETAILS:

Date: 25th March 2025
Time: 10:00 AM
Venue: Conference Room A, Admin Block

INQUIRY OFFICER:
Mr. Vijay Reddy, General Manager - HR (an independent officer not involved in
the incident)

PRESENTING OFFICER (Management's Representative):
Mr. Anand Singh, Manager - HR

CHARGES:
As per charge sheet dated 15th March 2025 (theft of company property)

WITNESSES (To be examined by Management):
1. Mr. Suresh (Security Guard) - Detained you at gate, recovered goods

3. Mr. Ramesh (Stock Clerk) - Stock register showing unauthorized removal of goods

DOCUMENTS (To be produced by Management):
1. CCTV footage (Gate No. 2, 10th March 2025, 11:20 PM to 11:40 PM)

YOUR RIGHTS:
1. You may remain present during the inquiry and present your defense
2. You may cross-examine the witnesses produced by the Management
3. You may produce your own witnesses and documents in your defense
4. You may engage a representative (fellow employee, union representative) to
   assist you. NOTE: External legal counsel not permitted in domestic inquiry.
5. You may submit written arguments after the inquiry hearing

If you fail to attend the inquiry without valid reasons, the inquiry will proceed
ex-parte (in your absence) and a decision will be taken based on available evidence.

Please confirm your attendance by 22nd March 2025.

Yours sincerely,

[Signature]
HR Manager

EMPLOYEE ACKNOWLEDGMENT:
I acknowledge receipt of this inquiry notice.

Employee Signature: _______________
Date: _______________
```

### Step 5: Inquiry Hearing

**Procedure**:

```
DAY OF INQUIRY: 25th March 2025, 10:00 AM

INQUIRY OFFICER (IO): Mr. Vijay Reddy (General Manager - HR)
PRESENTING OFFICER (PO): Mr. Anand Singh (Manager - HR, representing Management)
CHARGED EMPLOYEE: Mr. Rajesh Kumar (Warehouse Manager)
EMPLOYEE'S REPRESENTATIVE: Mr. Kiran (Union Representative)

PROCEEDINGS:

10:00 AM - OPENING:
IO: "This inquiry is being conducted to examine the charges of misconduct against
    Mr. Rajesh Kumar as per charge sheet dated 15th March 2025. The charge is
    theft of company property (10 cartons of ABC Soap worth ₹2 lakh) on 10th
    March 2025."

IO to Employee: "Do you understand the charges against you?"
Employee: "Yes, sir."

IO: "How do you plead? Guilty or not guilty?"
Employee: "Not guilty, sir. I deny the charges. It was authorized delivery as
         per my superior's instructions."

IO: "Okay, we will examine the evidence. Presenting Officer, please call your
    first witness."

10:15 AM - MANAGEMENT'S CASE:

PO: "I call Mr. Suresh, Security Guard, as the first witness."

[Mr. Suresh enters, takes oath]

PO's Examination-in-Chief:
Q: What is your name and designation?
A: Suresh Kumar, Security Guard, Gate No. 2.

Q: Were you on duty on 10th March 2025 night shift?
A: Yes, sir. 8 PM to 8 AM shift.

Q: What did you observe around 11:30 PM?
A: I saw Mr. Rajesh Kumar's car (KA-01-AB-1234) approaching Gate No. 2. I stopped
   the vehicle for routine check as per protocol (all vehicles exiting factory
   after 11 PM must be checked).

Q: What did you find?
A: Upon inspection, I found 10 cartons of ABC Soap in the car's trunk. I asked
   Mr. Rajesh for the gate pass (authorization to remove goods). He could not
   produce any gate pass.

Q: What did you do then?
A: I detained him and informed the Duty Manager. The goods were seized and Mr.
   Rajesh was taken to the admin office for questioning.

PO: "No further questions."

IO to Employee's Representative: "You may cross-examine the witness."

Employee Representative's Cross-Examination:
Q: Mr. Suresh, did Mr. Rajesh tell you why he was taking the goods?
A: Yes, he said it was authorized delivery for a customer, as per Production
   Head's instructions.

Q: Did you verify with the Production Head (Mr. Sharma)?
A: No, sir. At 11:30 PM, Mr. Sharma had already left the factory. I could not
   reach him on phone.

Q: So you did not verify Mr. Rajesh's claim before detaining him?
A: No, sir. As per protocol, without gate pass, goods cannot leave the premises.

Q: Is it possible that Mr. Rajesh was speaking the truth about authorized delivery?
A: [Hesitates] It is possible, sir, but without gate pass I could not allow.

Employee Representative: "No further questions."

[Similar examination of 2nd witness: CCTV operator, 3rd witness: Stock clerk]

12:00 PM - LUNCH BREAK

1:00 PM - EMPLOYEE'S DEFENSE:

IO: "Mr. Rajesh, do you wish to present any witnesses or documents in your defense?"

Employee: "Yes, sir. I call Mr. Sharma, Production Head, as my witness. He will
         confirm that he instructed me to deliver the goods."

IO: "Has Mr. Sharma been summoned?"
Employee Representative: "We submitted the witness list yesterday (24th March),
                        requesting Mr. Sharma to be summoned."

IO: "Let me check... [Checks file] I see the witness request. HR, why was Mr.
    Sharma not summoned?"

PO: "Sir, Mr. Sharma is on leave this week (medical leave). We could not arrange
    his presence."

IO: "This is a serious lapse. The employee has a right to examine witnesses in
    his defense. Adjourn the inquiry to 1st April 2025 (next week) to allow
    Mr. Sharma's examination."

[Inquiry adjourned]

1st APRIL 2025 - RESUMED HEARING:

Employee's Witness: Mr. Sharma (Production Head)
[Takes oath]

Employee Representative's Examination:
Q: Were you present in the factory on 10th March 2025 night?
A: Yes, I worked till 11 PM that day.

Q: Did you instruct Mr. Rajesh to deliver 10 cartons of ABC Soap to any customer?
A: No, I did not.

Q: [Employee interjects]: "Sir, you called me at 11:15 PM and asked me to deliver
   to XYZ Traders!"
Sharma: "I left the factory at 11 PM. I did not call you at 11:15 PM."

Q: Do you have any record of a call to Mr. Rajesh at 11:15 PM?
A: [Checks phone] No, my call log shows last call was at 10:50 PM to my wife.

[Employee's defense collapses - key witness contradicts his claim]

2:00 PM - CLOSING ARGUMENTS:

PO (Management): "The evidence clearly establishes that Mr. Rajesh removed 10
                 cartons without authorization. His defense that it was authorized
                 delivery has been disproven by Mr. Sharma's testimony. We request
                 the Inquiry Officer to find the charges proven and recommend
                 dismissal."

Employee Representative: "While Mr. Sharma denies giving instructions, there is
                        no evidence of Mr. Rajesh's criminal intent. He may have
                        genuinely misunderstood or there may have been a
                        miscommunication. We request leniency - a warning or
                        suspension instead of dismissal."

IO: "The inquiry is now closed. I will submit my findings to the Management within
    7 days. Thank you."
```

### Step 6: Inquiry Report

**Format**:
```
DOMESTIC INQUIRY REPORT

Employee: Mr. Rajesh Kumar, Warehouse Manager (Employee ID: WH12345)
Charge: Theft of company property (10 cartons ABC Soap, ₹2 lakh) on 10th March 2025
Inquiry Officer: Mr. Vijay Reddy, General Manager - HR
Inquiry Dates: 25th March 2025, 1st April 2025

SUMMARY OF EVIDENCE:

Management's Case:
1. Mr. Suresh (Security Guard): Testified that he stopped Mr. Rajesh's car at
   11:30 PM, found 10 cartons in trunk, Mr. Rajesh could not produce gate pass.

2. CCTV footage: Shows Mr. Rajesh loading 10 cartons into his car at 11:20 PM
   from warehouse (no other person present).

3. Stock register: 10 cartons marked missing (unauthorized removal, no gate pass entry).

Employee's Defense:
1. Claimed authorized delivery as per Production Head Mr. Sharma's instructions
   (call at 11:15 PM).

2. Called Mr. Sharma as defense witness. Mr. Sharma testified: "I left factory
   at 11 PM, did not call Mr. Rajesh at 11:15 PM." Phone records corroborate
   (no call at 11:15 PM).

FINDINGS:

Charge: Removal of 10 cartons of ABC Soap without authorization on 10th March 2025

Evidence Analysis:
- CCTV footage clearly shows Mr. Rajesh loading goods into his car (PROVEN)
- Security guard's testimony: Mr. Rajesh could not produce gate pass (PROVEN)
- Stock register: No authorized gate pass issued for the 10 cartons (PROVEN)
- Employee's defense: Authorized delivery as per Mr. Sharma's instructions (DISPROVEN
  - Mr. Sharma denied giving such instructions, phone records show no call)

CONCLUSION: CHARGE PROVEN

The preponderance of evidence establishes that Mr. Rajesh Kumar removed 10 cartons
of company property without authorization on 10th March 2025. His defense that
it was authorized delivery has been conclusively disproven.

MITIGATING CIRCUMSTANCES:
Employee's representative pleaded for leniency citing:
- 5 years of service without prior misconduct
- Financial hardship (wife's medical treatment)

ASSESSMENT: Financial hardship, while sympathetic, does not justify theft of
company property. Theft involves criminal intent to permanently deprive the
owner of property and violates the fundamental trust between employer and employee.

RECOMMENDATION:
Given the gravity of the misconduct (theft, which is a criminal offense) and the
substantial value involved (₹2 lakh), I recommend DISMISSAL from service with
immediate effect.

The employee is not entitled to:
- Notice pay (dismissed for cause)
- Severance pay (dismissed for misconduct)
- Gratuity (forfeited under Section 4(6)(a) Payment of Gratuity Act as theft
  constitutes moral turpitude)

The employee is entitled to:
- Salary for days worked till date of dismissal
- Leave encashment for earned leave accrued (subject to deduction of ₹2 lakh
  value of stolen goods if goods not recovered)

Date: 5th April 2025

[Signature]
Vijay Reddy
Inquiry Officer
General Manager - HR
```

been conclusively disproven by Mr. Sharma's testimony and phone records.

PUNISHMENT:
In view of the gravity of the misconduct, the Management has decided to DISMISS

- You are NOT entitled to any severance pay.

FINAL SETTLEMENT:
You are entitled to receive the following in your full & final settlement:

1. Salary for the period 1st March 2025 to 10th April 2025 (40 days): ₹60,000

GROSS AMOUNT DUE: ₹96,923

NET AMOUNT DUE: NIL (₹96,923 - ₹2,00,000 = -₹1,03,077)

Since the deductions exceed the amounts due to you, you OWE the Company ₹1,03,077.
The Company reserves the right to pursue recovery through legal means.

HANDOVER:
You are required to hand over all company property (laptop, access card, documents)
to the HR department by 12th April 2025.

APPEAL RIGHTS:
If you are aggrieved by this order, you may:
1. File an appeal with the Managing Director within 15 days of this order (as

2. Raise an industrial dispute through your union or by approaching the Labour
   Commissioner for conciliation
3. File a claim before the Labour Court if conciliation fails

Yours sincerely,

[Signature]
Suresh Iyer
Chief Human Resources Officer

## Payment Entitlements Upon Termination for Cause

| **Payment Component** | **Termination WITH Cause (Misconduct)** | **Termination WITHOUT Cause (Retrenchment)** |
|-----------------------|-----------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| **Notice Pay** | NO (dismissed for cause, no notice required) | YES (1-3 months salary in lieu of notice per employment agreement) |
| **Salary for Days Worked** | YES (pro-rated till dismissal date) | YES (pro-rated till last working day) |
| **Leave Encashment** | YES (for earned leave accrued, company may deduct damages) | YES (all accumulated earned leave) |
| **Gratuity** | FORFEITED if misconduct involves moral turpitude (Section 4(6) Gratuity Act) | YES (₹20 lakh max, 15 days wages × years of service ÷ 26) |
| **Severance Pay** | NO (only for retrenchment, not misconduct) | YES (15 days wages × years of service per Section 25F Industrial Disputes Act) |
| **Bonus (Pending)** | Company discretion (typically forfeited for misconduct) | YES (statutory bonus if wage ≤₹21,000/month) |
| **Notice to Government** | NO (Section 25F not applicable) | YES (1 month notice to Labour Commissioner per Section 25F(c)) |

**Moral Turpitude Definition** (For Gratuity Forfeiture):
- **Acts involving moral depravity**: Theft, fraud, embezzlement, bribery, sexual harassment, violence, criminal offenses
- **NOT moral turpitude**: Poor performance, negligence (without criminal intent), insubordination (unless violent), absenteeism

## Proportionality of Punishment

**Supreme Court Principle** (B.C. Chaturvedi v. Union of India, 1995):
```
"Punishment must be commensurate with the gravity of misconduct. Even if misconduct
is proven, if punishment is disproportionately harsh, courts will interfere and
substitute a lesser punishment."
```

**Punishment Scale** (From Least to Most Severe):

1. **Warning** (Written warning, placed on record)
2. **Withholding Increment** (Annual salary increase withheld for 1-2 years)
3. **Suspension** (Without pay for 7-30 days)
4. **Demotion** (Reduction in grade/designation/salary)
5. **Termination with Notice** (60-90 days notice + severance + gratuity)
6. **Dismissal** (Immediate termination, no notice, gratuity may be forfeited)

**Proportionality Examples**:

| **Misconduct** | **Typical Punishment** | **Disproportionate (Likely Set Aside by Court)** |
|----------------|------------------------|---------------------------------------------------|
| **First-time late-coming** (15 mins late) | Warning | Dismissal (too harsh for minor infraction) |
| **Habitual late-coming** (15+ instances over 6 months, after 2 warnings) | Suspension 7 days OR withhold increment | Dismissal (courts may uphold if repeated warnings ignored) |
| **Single instance of insubordination** (refusal to work overtime once) | Warning OR 1-day suspension | Dismissal (too harsh, unless aggravating factors) |
| **Public insubordination** (shouting at manager in front of clients/team) | Suspension 14 days OR final warning | Dismissal (courts may uphold due to public nature, reputational harm) |
| **Theft of ₹500** (petty theft, first offense, 10 years service) | Dismissal + gratuity forfeiture (courts may reduce to termination with notice, allow gratuity for long service) | Dismissal + criminal prosecution (proportionate, but courts consider mitigating factors) |
| **Theft of ₹2 lakh** (substantial value) | Dismissal + gratuity forfeiture (proportionate) | Warning (too lenient, court unlikely to interfere if management dismisses) |
| **Sexual harassment** (proven by ICC) | Dismissal + gratuity forfeiture (proportionate per POSH Act) | Warning (too lenient, court will order dismissal per POSH Act mandate) |

## Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid

### Pitfall 1: Vague Charge Sheet

**❌ BAD CHARGE SHEET**:
```
"You have been found misbehaving with colleagues and not following company rules.
Explain why action should not be taken against you."
```

**Problem**: Too vague (which colleagues? what rules? when did this happen?)

**✅ GOOD CHARGE SHEET**:
```
"On 5th March 2025 at 3:00 PM, you used abusive language ('You are useless!',
'Get out of my sight!') towards Ms. Priya Sharma (Junior Executive) in the
presence of 5 team members during a team meeting in Conference Room B. This
constitutes disorderly conduct under Standing Order 25(g) and creates a hostile
work environment."
```

### Pitfall 2: Biased Inquiry Officer

**❌ BAD PRACTICE**: Reporting manager who witnessed misconduct conducts inquiry

**Problem**: Violates "Nemo Judex" principle (no one can be judge in own cause)

**✅ GOOD PRACTICE**: Independent inquiry officer (from different department, not involved in incident, not witness)

### Pitfall 3: Denying Cross-Examination

**❌ BAD PRACTICE**: Inquiry officer refuses to allow employee to cross-examine witnesses ("We don't have time for this")

**Problem**: Violates Principles of Natural Justice → Entire inquiry vitiated → Labour Court will set aside dismissal order

**✅ GOOD PRACTICE**: Allow full cross-examination (even if time-consuming, it's a legal requirement)

### Pitfall 4: Ex-Parte Inquiry Without Valid Reason

**❌ BAD PRACTICE**: Employee doesn't attend first inquiry date (due to medical emergency), employer proceeds ex-parte immediately

**Problem**: Courts require reasonable opportunity (if employee provides medical certificate, inquiry should be adjourned)

**✅ GOOD PRACTICE**: Adjourn inquiry if employee provides valid reason (medical, family emergency), give 1-2 adjournments, proceed ex-parte only if employee repeatedly defaults without reason

## Compliance Checklist

### For Employers (Conducting Domestic Inquiry)

- [ ] Preliminary investigation completed (sufficient evidence to proceed)
- [ ] Charge sheet issued with specific allegations (date, time, what employee did)
- [ ] Employee given 48 hours to 7 days to submit written explanation
- [ ] Inquiry notice issued if employee denies charges (inquiry date, time, venue, inquiry officer, witnesses, documents)
- [ ] Independent inquiry officer appointed (not involved in incident, not witness)
- [ ] Employee's right to cross-examine witnesses honored (full cross-examination allowed)
- [ ] Employee's witnesses examined (if employee requests, witnesses must be summoned)
- [ ] Inquiry report prepared (findings on whether charges proven, recommendation)
- [ ] Management's final order proportionate to misconduct (dismissal for grave misconduct, lesser punishment for minor offenses)
- [ ] Final settlement processed (salary + leave encashment, gratuity forfeiture if moral turpitude)

### For Employees (Defending Against Charges)

- [ ] Review charge sheet carefully (understand specific allegations)
- [ ] Submit written explanation within deadline (deny charges if false, admit with mitigating circumstances if true)
- [ ] Attend inquiry (failure to attend = ex-parte inquiry, decided in your absence)
- [ ] Cross-examine management's witnesses (challenge credibility, probe inconsistencies)
- [ ] Present own witnesses/documents (prove your defense)
- [ ] Engage representative (union leader, fellow employee for assistance)
- [ ] Submit written arguments after inquiry (summarize your defense)
- [ ] If dismissed, file appeal with management within 15 days (per company grievance policy)
- [ ] If appeal rejected, raise industrial dispute (approach Labour Commissioner for conciliation)

## Landmark Cases

### 1. Indian Bank v. M. Sadananda Bhat (SC 1994)

**Facts**:
- Bank employee (cashier) dismissed for cash shortage of ₹50,000
- Domestic inquiry conducted, employee found guilty
- BUT inquiry officer was Branch Manager (employee's direct superior + involved in detecting shortage)

**Employee's Challenge**: Inquiry vitiated due to biased inquiry officer

**Supreme Court Held**:
1. **Inquiry Officer Must Be Impartial**: Person conducting inquiry must not be involved in incident (violates "Nemo Judex" principle)
2. **Branch Manager as IO = Bias**: Branch Manager detected shortage, filed complaint → Cannot be inquiry officer (interested party)
3. **Vitiated Inquiry → Dismissal Set Aside**: Even if charges proven, procedural lapse vitiates entire inquiry → Employee reinstated with back wages

**Principle**: Domestic inquiry MUST follow Principles of Natural Justice (impartial inquiry officer mandatory)

### 2. B.C. Chaturvedi v. Union of India (SC 1995)

**Facts**:
- Government employee (clerk) committed minor financial irregularity (₹5,000 discrepancy in account books, no fraud/theft alleged)
- Domestic inquiry held, charges proven
- Punishment: Dismissal from service (lost pension, gratuity)

**Employee's Appeal**: Punishment disproportionate (₹5,000 mistake, 15 years service, first offense)

**Supreme Court Held**:
1. **Proportionality Test**: Punishment must be commensurate with gravity of misconduct
2. **Dismissal Disproportionate**: ₹5,000 accounting error (not fraud/theft) + 15 years unblemished service + first offense → Dismissal too harsh
3. **Substituted Punishment**: Reduction of 3 increments (salary increase withheld for 3 years) instead of dismissal

**Principle**: Courts will interfere if punishment grossly disproportionate, even if misconduct proven
