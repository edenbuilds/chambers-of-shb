# IL-LABOUR-STRIKES-LOCKOUTS

## Protocol ID
IL-LABOUR-STRIKES-LOCKOUTS

## Classification
- **Domain**: Labour Law
- **Sub-domain**: Industrial Relations
- **Type**: Operational Protocol
- **Complexity**: High
- **Version**: 1.0.0

---

## Purpose
This protocol provides comprehensive guidance on strikes, lockouts, and other forms of industrial action under the Industrial Disputes Act 1947, covering legality conditions, notice requirements, prohibition periods, payment during strike/lockout, and consequences of illegal industrial action.

---

## Core Framework

### 1. DEFINITIONS (Section 2, Industrial Disputes Act 1947)

#### 1.1 Strike
```
STRIKE (Section 2(q)):
"Cessation of work by a body of persons employed in any industry, acting in
combination, OR a concerted refusal, OR a refusal under a common understanding,
of any number of persons who are or have been so employed to continue to work
or to accept employment."

KEY ELEMENTS:
1. CESSATION OF WORK: Stoppage of work (complete or partial)
2. COLLECTIVE ACTION: Not individual refusal (body of persons)
3. COMMON UNDERSTANDING: Pre-planned, coordinated action
4. EMPLOYMENT RELATIONSHIP: Current or former employees

FORMS OF STRIKE:
- Total strike (complete stoppage)
- Partial strike (some workers, some departments)
- Pen-down strike (office workers refuse paperwork)
- Tool-down strike (workers present but don't use tools)
- Stay-in strike (sit-down strike, workers occupy premises)
```

#### 1.2 Lockout
```
LOCKOUT (Section 2(l)):
"Temporary closing of a place of employment, OR the suspension of work, OR the
refusal by an employer to continue to employ any number of persons employed by him."

KEY ELEMENTS:
1. EMPLOYER'S ACTION: Initiated by employer (not workers)
2. TEMPORARY: Not permanent closure (which requires different process)
3. SUSPENSION OF WORK: Workers willing to work, employer refuses
4. COLLECTIVE: Affects number of workers (not just one)

FORMS OF LOCKOUT:
- Total lockout (entire establishment closed)
- Partial lockout (specific departments/shifts)
- Preventive lockout (to pre-empt anticipated strike)
- Retaliatory lockout (in response to illegal strike/go-slow)
```

#### 1.3 Public Utility Service
```
PUBLIC UTILITY SERVICE (Section 2(n) + First Schedule):
Industries/services essential to community, requiring stricter strike/lockout rules.

FIRST SCHEDULE SERVICES:
1. Railway service (transport of goods/passengers)
2. Postal, telegraph, telephone services
3. Ports, docks, airports
4. Electricity, gas, water supply
5. Hospitals, medical services
6. Banking (scheduled banks)
7. Coal, oil, petroleum production/distribution
8. Defense production
9. Public transport (buses, metro, monorail)
10. Sanitation services
11. Fire services
12. Cement, steel (added by state governments in some states)

ADDITIONAL (state notifications):
- Educational institutions (in some states)
- IT/ITES services (Karnataka, some periods)
```

### 2. CONDITIONS FOR LAWFUL STRIKE

#### 2.1 Notice Requirements (Section 22)
```
IN PUBLIC UTILITY SERVICES:
- 14 DAYS NOTICE required to employer (before strike)
- Notice in writing, specifying:
  - Date of intended strike
  - Reasons for strike (grievances, demands)
  - Signed by workers or union
- Strike can commence only AFTER 14 days from notice + 6 weeks from notice date

CALCULATION:
Notice given: 1st January
Earliest strike date: 15th January (14 days) BUT within 6 weeks from 1st Jan
Latest strike date: 12th February (6 weeks from 1st Jan)
If no strike by 12th Feb → Notice lapses, fresh notice required

IN NON-PUBLIC UTILITY:
- No statutory notice required (but often required by Standing Orders or CBA)
- Good practice: Reasonable notice (7-14 days)

EXCEPTIONS (No notice required):
- Sympathetic strike (supporting another union's lawful strike)
- Protest strike (against gross injustice, sudden event)
  [But courts rarely uphold this exception without notice]
```

#### 2.2 Prohibition Periods (Section 23)
```
STRIKE IS ILLEGAL if commenced, continued during:

1. CONCILIATION PROCEEDINGS:
   - From date of conciliation officer appointment
   - Till 7 days after conclusion of conciliation proceedings

2. ARBITRATION:
   - During pendency of arbitration proceedings (if referred)
   - Till award is published

3. ADJUDICATION:
   - From date of reference to Labour Court/Industrial Tribunal/National Tribunal
   - Till one month after adjudication order is published
   - Even if award is against workers, no strike for 1 month

4. DURING PENDENCY OF SETTLEMENT:
   - If settlement registered under Section 12(3)
   - During settlement's validity period (typically 2-3 years)

TIMELINE EXAMPLE:
Dispute arises: 1st March
Conciliation starts: 10th March → NO STRIKE allowed
Conciliation fails: 15th May → NO STRIKE till 22nd May (7 days cooling period)
Referred to Labour Court: 1st June → NO STRIKE allowed
Award passed: 1st September → NO STRIKE till 1st October (1 month)
After 1st October: STRIKE ALLOWED (if other conditions met)
```

#### 2.3 Majority Support (Not Statutory, but Practical)
```
NOT REQUIRED BY LAW:
Industrial Disputes Act does not mandate ballot or majority support

TRADE UNION PRACTICE:
- Responsible unions conduct secret ballot before strike
- Majority (>50%) or supermajority (≥66%) required
- Democratic legitimacy strengthens strike

STANDING ORDERS (if applicable):
Some model Standing Orders require:
- General body resolution (for union-led strike)
- Notice to employer (even in non-public utility)

CONSEQUENCE OF MINORITY STRIKE:
- Legally valid strike (if notice given, not during prohibition)
- But practically weak (employer can continue with majority workers)
- Risk of backfire (majority workers resent disruption)
```

### 3. ILLEGAL STRIKES

#### 3.1 Grounds for Illegality
```
STRIKE IS ILLEGAL if:

1. NO NOTICE (in public utility service):
   ✗ Wildcat strike without 14 days notice
   ✗ Strike before 14 days elapse
   ✗ Strike after 6 weeks from notice (notice lapsed)

2. DURING PROHIBITION PERIODS (Section 23):
   ✗ During conciliation/arbitration/adjudication
   ✗ Within 1 month of award publication

3. IN VIOLATION OF SETTLEMENT/AWARD:
   ✗ Strike on issue already settled (binding settlement)
   ✗ Strike challenging arbitration award

4. GO-SLOW/WORK-TO-RULE (if amounts to strike):
   ✗ Deliberate slowdown affecting production
   ✗ Courts have held go-slow = strike (if collective, deliberate)

5. STRIKES FOR ILLEGAL OBJECTS:
   ✗ Political strike (not related to employment terms)
   ✗ Sympathetic strike for illegal cause
   ✗ Coercing third parties

6. GHERAO/SIT-IN (if involves wrongful confinement):
   ✗ Preventing management from leaving premises
   ✗ Occupying premises after work hours (trespass)
```

#### 3.2 Consequences of Illegal Strike
```
FOR WORKERS:

1. LOSS OF WAGES (Section 26):
   No wages payable for strike period (illegal strike)
   Even if employer voluntarily pays, cannot claim as right

2. DISCIPLINARY ACTION:
   - Warning, suspension (for first-time participants)
   - Dismissal (for ringleaders, repeat offenders)
   - Domestic inquiry required (principles of natural justice)

3. LOSS OF PROTECTION:
   - No reinstatement if dismissed for illegal strike
   - Industrial dispute remedy not available
   - Civil immunity under Trade Unions Act not applicable

4. CRIMINAL LIABILITY (rare):
   - Section 26 ID Act: Penalty for illegal strike (fine, imprisonment)
   - Rarely prosecuted (impractical to prosecute hundreds of workers)

FOR UNION:

1. LOSS OF RECOGNITION (in states with recognition laws):
   Maharashtra MRTU Act: Recognition can be withdrawn for unfair practice

2. CIVIL LIABILITY:
   Damages to employer for losses (rare, difficult to prove quantum)

3. CONTEMPT (if strike violates court order):
   Strike during injunction → Contempt of court (fine, imprisonment of leaders)

FOR LEADERS/INSTIGATORS:

1. DISMISSAL FROM SERVICE:
   Leading illegal strike = misconduct under Standing Orders
   Summary dismissal (with domestic inquiry)

2. CRIMINAL PROSECUTION:
   - Unlawful assembly (Section 141 IPC) if violence
   - Wrongful confinement (Section 342 IPC) if gherao
   - Rioting (Section 146 IPC) if property damage
```

### 4. LAWFUL STRIKES - WORKERS' RIGHTS

#### 4.1 Protection from Victimization
```
IF STRIKE IS LAWFUL:

1. NO DISMISSAL FOR PARTICIPATION:
   Employer cannot terminate workers merely for participating in lawful strike
   Exception: Misconduct during strike (violence, damage to property)

2. NO WAGE DEDUCTION (debatable):
   Section 26: "No wages during strike period"
   But if employer continues production with replacement workers, may argue breach
   Generally: No work, no pay principle applies even for lawful strike

3. NO ADVERSE ACTION:
   Cannot deny promotion, transfer to unfavorable location, discriminate

4. REINSTATEMENT AFTER SETTLEMENT:
   Settlement/award often includes reinstatement of strikers without penalty
```

#### 4.2 Payment During Lawful Strike (General Rule: NO PAY)
```
STATUTORY POSITION (Section 26):
"No workman shall be entitled to wages for period of illegal strike"
(Implies: Even lawful strike, no statutory entitlement to wages)

PRACTICAL POSITION:
- No work, no pay (general principle of contract law)
- Employer not obligated to pay during lawful strike
- Union may pay "strike pay" from union funds (nominal amount)

EXCEPTIONS (rare):

1. EMPLOYER'S FAULT:
   If strike caused by employer's illegal lockout or unfair practice
   → Labour Court may award compensation/back wages

2. SETTLEMENT/AWARD:
   Parties may agree in settlement to pay partial wages for strike period
   Example: "Employer agrees to pay 50% wages for 15-day strike period"

3. EMPLOYER'S VOLUNTARY PAYMENT:
   Some progressive employers pay wages to end strike quickly
   Not a legal obligation, purely business decision
```

### 5. LOCKOUTS

#### 5.1 Conditions for Lawful Lockout
```
SAME CONDITIONS AS STRIKE (Section 22-23):

1. NOTICE (in public utility):
   - 14 days notice to workers
   - Within 6 weeks from notice (same as strike)

2. NOT DURING PROHIBITION PERIODS:
   - Not during conciliation, arbitration, adjudication
   - Not within 1 month of award

3. NOT IN VIOLATION OF SETTLEMENT/AWARD

FORMS OF LAWFUL LOCKOUT:

1. DEFENSIVE LOCKOUT:
   In response to illegal strike, go-slow, gherao
   Employer protects property, equipment
   Courts uphold as legitimate management action

2. OFFENSIVE LOCKOUT (rare):
   To pressure workers during bargaining
   Legal if notice given and not during prohibition
   But commercially risky (production loss, customer dissatisfaction)

3. PREVENTIVE LOCKOUT:
   Anticipating violence, property damage
   Must have reasonable grounds (past incidents, intelligence)
   Courts scrutinize closely (cannot be pretext to avoid negotiations)
```

#### 5.2 Consequences of Illegal Lockout
```
LOCKOUT IS ILLEGAL if:
- No notice (in public utility)
- During conciliation/adjudication
- In violation of settlement/award

CONSEQUENCES FOR EMPLOYER:

1. PAYMENT OF WAGES:
   Section 25C: Compensation = 50% of Basic + DA for lockout period
   (Even if illegal lockout, minimum 50% compensation)

2. PROSECUTION:
   Section 26: Penalty for illegal lockout (fine)
   Rarely prosecuted

3. INDUSTRIAL COURT ORDERS:
   - Order to pay full wages (if lockout illegal and malafide)
   - Reinstatement of workers with back wages
   - Compensation for losses (medical emergency, loans due to no income)

4. UNFAIR LABOUR PRACTICE:
   Illegal lockout = ULP under Maharashtra MRTU Act
   Damages, penalty, prosecution
```

### 6. OTHER FORMS OF INDUSTRIAL ACTION

#### 6.1 Go-Slow
```
DEFINITION:
Deliberate slowdown in work pace to reduce output without complete stoppage

EXAMPLES:
- Assembly line workers reduce speed (100 units/hour → 60 units/hour)
- Checkers delay clearance of goods at port
- Bank employees process fewer customers

LEGALITY:
ILLEGAL if:
- Amounts to "strike" (collective, deliberate refusal to work normally)
- Courts: "Go-slow is a covert strike" (Burn & Co. Ltd. case)

EMPLOYER'S REMEDIES:
1. Treat as illegal strike → Disciplinary action
2. Defensive lockout (to protect against sustained losses)
3. Wage deduction (for work not done, proportionate)

WORKER'S ARGUMENT:
- "We are working, just slowly due to fatigue/poor conditions"
- Difficult to prove deliberate slowdown vs genuine reasons
- Employer must establish: Collective, deliberate, concerted action
```

#### 6.2 Work-to-Rule
```
DEFINITION:
Workers strictly follow all rules, procedures (usually waived for efficiency)

EXAMPLES:
- Pilots insist on 100% pre-flight checks (normally 80% due to time constraints)
- Factory workers refuse overtime (within rights, but disrupts production)
- Clerks follow all filing procedures meticulously (reduces output)

LEGALITY:
AMBIGUOUS:
- Workers argue: "We're following company's own rules"
- Employer argues: "Deliberate disruption, amounts to strike"

COURTS' VIEW:
- If genuine compliance: LAWFUL (workers entitled to follow rules)
- If deliberate disruption to coerce: ILLEGAL (amounts to strike)
- Fact-specific (burden on employer to prove mala fide intent)

EXAMPLE - Air India Pilots (2012):
Pilots sick leave en masse (collective, coordinated)
Court: Work-to-rule can be strike if collective refusal to work normally
```

#### 6.3 Gherao
```
DEFINITION:
Workers surround management, prevent movement till demands met

FORMS:
- Managers confined to office (cannot leave till sign agreement)
- Factory gate blockade (suppliers, customers cannot enter/exit)
- Sit-in at management cabin

LEGALITY:
ILLEGAL:
- Wrongful confinement (Section 342 IPC)
- Criminal intimidation (Section 506 IPC)
- Trespass (if after work hours)

SUPREME COURT VIEW (Pepsi Foods case):
"Gherao is illegal per se. Right to peaceful picketing exists, but not
wrongful confinement. Management entitled to police protection to exit."

CONSEQUENCES:
- Dismissal of participants (misconduct)
- Criminal prosecution (FIR for wrongful confinement)
- Injunction (court order to vacate premises immediately)
- Contempt if court order violated

DISTINCTION: GHERAO vs PICKETING
Picketing: Peacefully persuading others (workers, customers) - LAWFUL
Gherao: Confinement, obstruction - ILLEGAL
```

#### 6.4 Hunger Strike
```
DEFINITION:
Workers refuse food to protest employer action

LEGALITY:
LAWFUL:
- Right to protest (Article 19(1)(a) freedom of expression)
- Not "strike" under ID Act (no cessation of work)
- Provided on own time (not during work hours)

EMPLOYER'S POSITION:
- Cannot force-feed (personal liberty)
- Can terminate if absence from work (if hunger strike during work hours)
- Can seek police removal if on company premises (trespass)

EXAMPLE:
Workers on hunger strike at factory gate (after work hours)
→ LAWFUL protest
Workers on hunger strike inside factory (during work shift)
→ Absence from work, misconduct (subject to domestic inquiry)
```

### 7. PAYMENT OF WAGES DURING STRIKE/LOCKOUT

#### 7.1 General Principles
```
NO WORK, NO PAY:
Contract of employment is bilateral
Worker provides work → Employer pays wages
No work (strike/lockout) → No wages (general rule)

SECTION 26 ID ACT:
"No workman entitled to wages for period of illegal strike"
(Implication: Even lawful strike, no statutory right to wages)

EXCEPTIONS:

1. EMPLOYER'S ILLEGAL LOCKOUT:
   Section 25C: Compensation = 50% of Basic + DA for lockout period
   If lockout declared illegal by court → Full wages

2. SETTLEMENT/AWARD:
   Parties may agree to pay wages for strike period
   Example: "Ex gratia payment of ₹10,000 to all workers for 10-day strike"

3. CONTINUING WORKERS:
   Workers who did not participate in strike → Full wages
   Employer cannot deduct wages of non-strikers

4. PARTIAL STRIKE:
   If only some departments on strike, others working
   → Pay full wages to working departments
   → No wages to striking departments
```

#### 7.2 Calculation Examples
```
SCENARIO 1: ILLEGAL STRIKE (7 days)
Worker's monthly salary: ₹30,000 (for 26 days)
Daily wage: ₹30,000 ÷ 26 = ₹1,154
Strike period: 7 days

Wage deduction: 7 × ₹1,154 = ₹8,078
Salary paid: ₹30,000 - ₹8,078 = ₹21,922

SCENARIO 2: ILLEGAL LOCKOUT (15 days)
Worker's monthly salary: ₹40,000 (Basic ₹20,000 + DA ₹8,000 + HRA ₹12,000)
Lockout period: 15 days (declared illegal by Labour Court)

Minimum compensation (Section 25C): 50% of (Basic + DA)
= 50% × (₹20,000 + ₹8,000) ÷ 26 × 15 days
= 50% × ₹1,077 × 15 = ₹8,078

Labour Court order (full wages):
= (₹20,000 + ₹8,000 + ₹12,000) ÷ 26 × 15 = ₹23,077

SCENARIO 3: LAWFUL STRIKE, SETTLEMENT INCLUDES PARTIAL PAYMENT
10-day strike, settlement: "Employer pays 40% wages for strike period as gesture"
Worker's daily wage: ₹1,500
Total loss: 10 × ₹1,500 = ₹15,000
Settlement payment: 40% × ₹15,000 = ₹6,000 (ex gratia, not wages)

SCENARIO 4: PARTIAL STRIKE (50% workers on strike)
Total workforce: 200 workers
On strike: 100 workers (7 days)
Working: 100 workers (full attendance)

Payroll:
- 100 working workers: Full wages (₹30,000 × 100 = ₹30 lakhs)
- 100 striking workers: No wages for 7 days
  (₹30,000 - ₹8,077 deduction = ₹21,923 × 100 = ₹21.92 lakhs)
Total: ₹51.92 lakhs (vs normal ₹60 lakhs)
```

## Landmark Cases

### 1. **T.K. Rangarajan v. Govt. of Tamil Nadu (2003) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Government employees went on strike for wage revision. Dismissed for participating.

**Held**:
- **No fundamental right to strike** (even for government employees)
- Strike = breach of conduct rules (for govt employees)
- Dismissal upheld (no illegal termination)

**Impact**: Confirmed that right to form union (Article 19(1)(c)) does NOT include right to strike.

---

### 2. **Syndicate Bank v. K. Umesh Nayak (2006) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Bank employee participated in nationwide strike called by union. Dismissed for absence.

**Held**:
- Participation in illegal strike = misconduct
- Employer entitled to dismiss (after domestic inquiry)
- No reinstatement if strike illegal (during conciliation/adjudication)

**Impact**: Employees cannot claim protection for participating in illegal strikes.

---

### 3. **Punjab National Bank v. Workmen (1959) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Dispute over whether go-slow amounts to strike under ID Act.

**Held**:
- **Go-slow is a form of strike** if collective and deliberate
- Not merely working slowly, but concerted refusal to work normally
- Employer can treat as illegal strike and take disciplinary action

**Impact**: Established that covert forms of strike (go-slow, work-to-rule) are strikes under ID Act.

---

### 4. **Burn & Co. Ltd. v. Workmen (1959) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Workers engaged in go-slow, reducing output by 30-40%.

**Held**:
- Deliberate go-slow to reduce production = strike
- Workers cannot claim wages for go-slow period (no normal work done)
- Employer entitled to disciplinary action

**Impact**: Clarified employer's rights during go-slow tactics.

---

### 5. **Management of Hindustan Lever Ltd. v. Kamalakshi (1992) - Karnataka HC**
**Facts**: Workers gheraoed manager, confined for 8 hours till demands met.

**Held**:
- **Gherao is illegal per se** (wrongful confinement, criminal act)
- No protection under Trade Unions Act for illegal activities
- Dismissal of participants upheld

**Impact**: Confirmed that gherao, sit-in involving confinement are criminal acts, not protected industrial action.

---

### 6. **State of Haryana v. Charanjit Singh (2006) - Supreme Court**
**Facts**: Government employees on mass casual leave (coordinated, to disrupt work).

**Held**:
- Mass casual leave (if coordinated to disrupt) = strike
- Courts look at substance, not form
- "Strike by any other name is still a strike"

**Impact**: Employers can treat coordinated mass absenteeism as strike.

## Compliance Checklist

### For Workers/Trade Unions (Before Strike)

**Planning:**
- [ ] Identify genuine grievances (wages, conditions, unfair practices)
- [ ] Exhaust internal grievance mechanism first (bipartite discussions)
- [ ] Conduct secret ballot among members (democratic mandate)
- [ ] Majority support (≥50% or ≥66% as per union rules)

**Notice (if Public Utility Service):**
- [ ] Draft notice specifying date, reasons for strike
- [ ] Serve 14 days advance notice on employer (written, acknowledged)
- [ ] Ensure strike date is between 14 days and 6 weeks from notice

**Timing (Check Prohibition Periods):**
- [ ] NO conciliation proceedings pending
- [ ] NO arbitration pending
- [ ] NO adjudication before Labour Court/Tribunal
- [ ] NOT within 1 month of award publication
- [ ] NOT during validity of registered settlement (Section 12(3))

**During Strike:**
- [ ] Peaceful picketing only (no violence, intimidation)
- [ ] No gherao, wrongful confinement of management
- [ ] No damage to employer's property
- [ ] Allow willing workers to enter (no coercion)
- [ ] Maintain communication with employer (for negotiation)

**Documentation:**
- [ ] Minutes of general body meeting (decision to strike)
- [ ] Ballot results (if conducted)
- [ ] Copy of notice served to employer (with acknowledgment)
- [ ] Daily reports of strike participation, incidents

---

### For Employers

**Preventive Measures:**
- [ ] Address genuine grievances promptly (before escalation)
- [ ] Maintain open communication channels with union
- [ ] Ensure compliance with labour laws (wages, benefits, safety)
- [ ] Train supervisors in conflict resolution

**Upon Receiving Strike Notice:**
- [ ] Verify if public utility service (14-day notice mandatory?)
- [ ] Check if notice is valid (within 6 weeks window)
- [ ] Initiate conciliation (triggers prohibition under Section 23)
- [ ] Negotiate in good faith (to avoid strike)

**During Lawful Strike:**
- [ ] No victimization of striking workers (cannot dismiss merely for participation)
- [ ] Maintain essential services (if public utility)
- [ ] Protect non-striking workers (safe entry/exit from premises)
- [ ] Continue production with willing workers (if feasible)
- [ ] No wage payment for strike period (document daily attendance)

**During Illegal Strike:**
- [ ] Issue show cause notices to participants (domestic inquiry process)
- [ ] Proportionate disciplinary action:
  - Warning: First-time participants, short duration strike
  - Suspension: Repeated offenders
  - Dismissal: Leaders, instigators, violence/property damage
- [ ] Consider defensive lockout (if go-slow, gherao, risk to property)
- [ ] File police complaint (if violence, wrongful confinement, trespass)

**Lockout (if contemplated):**
- [ ] Give 14 days notice (if public utility service)
- [ ] Ensure not during conciliation/adjudication
- [ ] Document reasons (illegal strike, go-slow, financial distress)
- [ ] Pay minimum compensation (50% of Basic + DA during lockout)

**Settlement:**
- [ ] Draft settlement terms (wage revision, benefits, reinstatement)
- [ ] Execute in writing (signed by authorized representatives)
- [ ] Register with Labour Commissioner (Section 12(3) ID Act)
- [ ] Implement terms promptly (restore production, pay arrears)

## Common Pitfalls

### For Workers/Unions
1. **Striking during conciliation/adjudication**: Automatic illegality (Section 23). Wait for proceedings to conclude + 7 days/1 month.

2. **No notice in public utility service**: Illegal strike even if grievances genuine. Serve 14-day notice mandatorily.

3. **Gherao/violence during strike**: Loses legal protection. Criminal prosecution + dismissal risk. Stick to peaceful picketing.

4. **Minority strike without ballot**: Legally valid (if notice given) but practically weak. Majority workers resent disruption.

5. **Expecting wages during strike**: No work, no pay principle. Union may provide nominal strike pay from funds.

### For Employers
1. **Dismissing workers for lawful strike**: Illegal victimization. Cannot terminate merely for participation. Only misconduct during strike (violence) justifies dismissal.

2. **Illegal lockout without notice**: Pay 50% compensation minimum (Section 25C). If malafide, Labour Court may order full wages + damages.

3. **Unilateral change in conditions during strike**: Unfair labour practice. Cannot impose new terms while negotiations pending.

4. **Hiring replacement workers during strike**: Risky in India (unlike US). May be treated as unfair practice if prevents reinstatement post-settlement.

5. **Not documenting daily attendance**: Cannot prove who struck and for how many days. Maintain daily muster roll during strike/lockout.

## Conclusion

Strikes and lockouts are extreme measures in industrial relations, with significant legal and practical consequences. Key takeaways:

1. **Strike ≠ Fundamental Right**: Right to form union (Article 19(1)(c)) does NOT include automatic right to strike. Strike is a tool, subject to statutory regulations.

2. **Notice Mandatory (Public Utility)**: 14 days advance notice required in railways, banks, ports, electricity, hospitals, etc. Strike valid only between 14 days and 6 weeks from notice.

3. **Prohibition Periods (Section 23)**: NO strike during conciliation, arbitration, adjudication, or within 1 month of award. Violation = illegal strike.

4. **No Work, No Pay**: Even lawful strike, no statutory entitlement to wages (Section 26). Union may pay nominal strike pay. Employers can continue production with willing workers.

5. **Illegal Strike Consequences**: Disciplinary action (warning to dismissal), no reinstatement protection, criminal prosecution (rare), union loses recognition (in Maharashtra).

6. **Illegal Lockout Consequences**: Pay 50% compensation (Section 25C), Labour Court may order full wages if malafide, prosecution (rare).

7. **Go-Slow/Gherao = Strike**: Courts treat deliberate slowdown, work-to-rule as strikes. Gherao is illegal (wrongful confinement).

Both employers and workers benefit from constructive dialogue and genuine collective bargaining to avoid strikes/lockouts. Prevention through fair employment practices and responsive grievance mechanisms is far superior to disruptive industrial action.
